Bandung

Developed by Dutch colonialists in the 19th century as a high-altitude tea and coffee hub, Bandung's cool climate earned it the nickname 'Paris of Java'. Its extensive collection of tropical Art Deco architecture highlights a brief but intensely wealthy colonial golden age. Visitors today can clearly trace the detailed layers of this unique heritage throughout the preserved streets and monuments.

Grand Mosque of Bandung

Bandung's Grand Mosque occupies the western side of Alun-Alun, where successive buildings have served the city's Muslim community since the nineteenth century. Repeated reconstruction created the present form with a large dome, twin minarets and broad prayer halls. An open forecourt connects the mosque with the public square and the commercial streets around it. The mosque and square operate as one civic setting.

Gedung Sate

Completed in the 1920s, Gedung Sate was designed as a Dutch colonial government building and now houses West Java's provincial administration. A central tower topped by a skewer-like ornament gives the landmark its popular name. Classical symmetry, local roof forms, stone arcades and climate-responsive details combine European planning with architectural forms adapted to Bandung and its environment.

This significant event marked a milestone in world history by uniting 29 countries from both continents during a turbulent period of the Cold War.

Bandung Lautan Api Monument

Bandung Lautan Api Monument commemorates the March 1946 evacuation and deliberate burning of parts of southern Bandung by Indonesian forces and residents resisting renewed colonial control. A tall stylized flame rises above a public square in Tegallega. The memorial addresses a specific wartime strategy, civilian displacement and the city's role in Indonesia's independence struggle.

Gua Belanda

Gua Belanda consists of tunnels excavated under Dutch colonial rule within Djuanda Forest Park. Parts were associated with water and military infrastructure, then adapted for communications and defense during the Second World War. Dark passages and side chambers retain a damp, utilitarian appearance. Limited lighting keeps the original unfinished character visible inside the tunnel network.
