Ubud

Ubud became a cultural centre in upland Bali under Dutch colonial rule and twentieth-century royal patronage of the arts. Palace courtyards, Hindu temples, and rice-terrace landscapes record how Balinese dance, painting, and subak irrigation co-evolved in the Gianyar regency. Monkey forest sanctuaries and rice-terrace walks extend palace dance traditions through Gianyar uplands.

Penglipuran Village

Penglipuran is a highland Balinese village organized along a central stone-paved axis with walled family compounds, gateways, temples, bamboo groves, and communal spaces. Residents maintain customary rules connected with spatial order and ritual life while also managing heavy tourism.

Tegallalang Rice Terrace

Ceking Rice Terrace at Tegallalang follows a steep valley north of Ubud, where small plots, irrigation channels, palms, paths, cafes and photo businesses occupy working agricultural land. The terraces reflect local subak water management, although their exact age cannot be reduced to a claim from the eighth century. Farming, entry arrangements and commercial structures change across separately managed sections.

Sacred Monkey Forest Sanctuary

The Sacred Monkey Forest Sanctuary in Ubud is a dense, natural forest reserve that is a habitat for hundreds of long-tailed macaques. The sanctuary contains three ancient Hindu temples covered in moss and detailed stone carvings. It represents the Balinese principle of Tri Hita Karana, emphasizing harmony between humans, nature, and the divine.
