Tainan

= Archaeological excavations in Zuojhen District suggest that the Tainan region has been inhabited for at least 20,000 to 31,000 years. The indigenous Siraya tribe dominated the region by the 16th century. The Sakam people of the Sinkan sub-tribe inhabited the area of the present-day city. Other Sirayan sub-tribes, including the Soelangh, Mattauw and Baccloangh inhabited the surrounding area. By the late 16th century, Chinese merchants and fishermen had set up several bases along the west coast of the island, including a sandbar across the Taikang Inner Sea (Chinese: 臺江內海; Pe̍h-ōe-jī: Tâi-kang lāi-hái) off the bay of Sakam village (modern-day Fort Provintia). The Chinese adopted Taioan (modern-day Anping) as the name of the sandbar. Slightly north of Taioan, along the shoreline near Bassemboy (北線尾; Pak-siàn-bóe), Japanese traders established bases for trade with China.

Hayashi Department Store

Hayashi Department Store opened in 1932 during Japanese rule and was one of Tainan's early modern retail buildings with an elevator. Restored after decades of changing use, it now contains Taiwanese design goods, food, exhibitions, and a rooftop shrine and terrace; wartime damage remains visible in places, while the current shop mix differs from the original department-store operation.

National Museum of Taiwan Literature

The National Museum of Taiwan Literature is a grand former government building that houses literary artifacts from native and colonial writers. It is located in the West Central District of Tainan, making it a convenient stop for tourists near the Tainan Train Station. The district is home to many other famous attractions such as Hayashi Department Store and Confucius Temple, making it a popular choice for sightseeing.

Eternal Golden Castle

Built in the 1870s as a coastal defense work, Eternal Golden Castle in Tainan now lies partly ruined after disasters and brick theft, though wall sections wrapped in banyan roots survive. Courtyards and remaining masonry form the walk when grounds are open. Roots and brick share the same broken circuit.

Chihkan Tower

Chihkan Tower occupies the site of Fort Provintia, built by the Dutch East India Company in 1653 and taken by Koxinga's forces in 1661. Later Qing-era pavilions, tablets, shrines and reconstruction now stand above portions of the original brick foundations. The layered complex records colonial trade, regime change.

Tainan Grand Mazu Temple

Tainan's Grand Mazu Temple, or Great Queen of Heaven Temple, is a seventeenth-century sanctuary to Mazu, protector of seafarers. Anping Old Street follows lanes near Fort Zeelandia where maritime trade and settlement developed under Dutch, Zheng, Qing and Japanese rule. Shops, temples, food stalls, old houses and narrow passages create a commercial heritage district. From about 1668, Anping Kaitai Tianhou Temple (Anping Mazu Temple) has honored the sea goddess Matsu through moves and renovations that kept the cult active. Exterior and main halls can usually be seen outside services. The temple anchors Anping's maritime devotion near the old street.

Anping Fort

Anping Fort marks the remains of Fort Zeelandia, the Dutch East India Company's seventeenth-century stronghold on Taiwan. Brick foundations, excavated walls, later monuments, a museum and a lookout tower explain the site, but little of the original upper fortress survives. Qing demolition, shoreline change, Japanese-era construction and modern restoration all shaped the present complex.

Yuguang Island

Yuguang Island lies off Tainan's Anping coast as a small barrier islet linked by causeway and local ferry routes. Sandy paths, coastal scrub, and low shoreline look back toward Anping Harbor and the Taiwan Strait. Walkers come for sea breezes and sunset light rather than large monuments, using the island as a quiet counterpoint to the denser historic streets on the mainland side.

The former Tait & Co. Merchant House stands in Anping as a remnant of foreign trading activity after treaty-port opening in the nineteenth century. Koxinga's Shrine

Koxinga's Shrine commemorates Zheng Chenggong, whose forces expelled the Dutch from Taiwan in 1662 and established a short-lived regime based in Tainan. The present complex largely reflects later rebuilding, with ceremonial gates, halls, statues, tablets and a museum. Official narratives have changed under Qing, Japanese, Nationalist and local governments, making the shrine a study in political memory as well as devotion.

Taijiang National Park

From the Yanshui River estuary to the Zengwen River, Taijiang National Park covers land and sea with mangroves, wetlands, and aquaculture ponds once part of the Inner Sea. Black-faced spoonbills and other scarce wildlife use the watery habitats. The park reads as a coastal working wetland rather than a mountain reserve.

Tiantan Tiangong Temple

Tiantan Tiangong Temple is dedicated to the Jade Emperor, a high deity in Chinese folk religion. The Tainan temple developed from an earlier site connected with Zheng-period state worship and was rebuilt through later dynasties. Courtyards, carved beams, plaques, incense, and ritual halls support active ceremonies, while its local claim as Taiwan's first temple to heaven requires careful historical definition.

Hutoupi

Hutoupi Reservoir in Xinhua is among Taiwan's older engineered lakes, created during the nineteenth century and later expanded under Japanese rule. Wooded shores, paths, bridges, picnic areas and recreational facilities surround the water. The managed landscape combines irrigation history with leisure use, showing how a practical reservoir became a public park.

The Spring plaza at Nanmen Park in Tainan opens 1.54 hectares below street level, with a sunken park and public wading pool ringed by tree-dotted islets. The space reused an underground mall parking structure from the 1980s. Paths and shade now replace cars on the lowered floor.

五妃廟

The Temple of the Five Concubines (五妃廟) in Tainan commemorates five Ming loyalist concubines who died in 1683. The courtyard shrine and spirit tablets form a compact memorial complex. It sits in southern Tainan and is open as a small historic temple for quiet visits.
