Beijing

Beijing has served as a national capital for Liao, Jin, Yuan, Ming, and Qing dynasties. The Forbidden City, Temple of Heaven, and hutong lanes record imperial ritual and court life, while Tiananmen Square and twentieth-century avenues reflect republican and revolutionary transformations of the North China plain. Summer Palace gardens and Ming-era wall segments complement imperial monuments on the North China plain.

Temple of Heaven

The Temple of Heaven is a known temple complex dating back to 1420, known for its distinctive circular buildings set in a popular park. It's located in the Dongcheng district of Beijing, which is an ideal area for accommodations due to its proximity to historical sites like the Forbidden City and Tiananmen Square. The district also offers attractions such as Jingshan Park for nature lovers and the Wangfujing pedestrian mall for shopping enthusiasts.

Tiananmen Square

Tiananmen Square spreads as a vast paved plaza in central Beijing used for national ceremonies and political gatherings. The space has hosted major events in modern Chinese history and remains tied to state ritual. Surrounding civic buildings frame the open hardscape where crowds assemble for anniversaries and official occasions.

Tiananmen

Facing Tiananmen Square, the Gate of Heavenly Peace forms the southern entrance to Beijing's former imperial city and the approach to the Forbidden City. The present structure dates mainly from the seventeenth century, although earlier gates occupied the site under the Ming dynasty; mao Zedong proclaimed the People's Republic of China from its rostrum in 1949, giving the gate a central role in modern state ceremony.

Summer Palace

Lakefront halls, temples, and painted corridors fill the Summer Palace grounds northwest of central Beijing. Classical Chinese garden design ties craftsmanship to water, rockeries, and planted slopes. When open, routes move through courtyards and lakeside galleries rather than a single sealed museum wing.

Yuanmingyuan Park

Yuanmingyuan Park, located in northwestern Beijing, is home to the ruins of the Old Summer Palace. Once an imperial residence known for its architecture and gardens, it was looted and destroyed during the Second Opium War in 1860. The palace was known for its vast art and antiquities collection. Today, visitors can explore the ruins of Dashuifa Site, a reminder of the palace's history.

Prince Gong Mansion

Prince Gong's Mansion, built in 1777 in central Beijing, is a preserved princely residence now open as a museum. Courtyards, gardens, ponds, and formal halls show Qing-era layout, and a theatre and shrine spaces illustrate how elite households combined private life with ritual use.

Beihai Park

A broad lake and wooded island shape Beihai Park, an imperial garden developed through the Liao, Jin, Yuan, Ming and Qing periods. The White Dagoba crowns Jade Flower Island, while temples, pavilions, painted corridors and classical rockwork line the shores; opened as a public park in 1925, the grounds preserve court landscaping traditions beside Beijing's former palace district.

Jingshan Park

Jingshan's five artificial peaks rise immediately north of the Forbidden City on soil excavated from palace moats and nearby waterways. Pavilions mark the ridge, with the central summit providing a direct view along the imperial complex's north-south axis; the park also contains the site associated with the death of the Chongzhen Emperor in 1644, when Ming rule in Beijing collapsed.

The Palace Museum

The Palace Museum, a massive palace complex and museum, is known for its historical significance and contemporary Chinese art. Established in 1925, it houses an extensive collection of antiquities alongside groundbreaking modern artworks. With over 80,000 daily visitors, the museum offers tours, art exhibits, and dining options. The inventory has grown to 1,860,000 items since its establishment and continues to expand with new exhibitions and thematic shows.

Meridian Gate

Built as the imperial palace's principal ceremonial entrance, its U-shaped platform supports a central tower and four corner pavilions where court rituals and proclamations once took place. Five passageways pierce the massive Meridian Gate at the southern edge of the Forbidden City. Museum entry now passes beneath the gate, but the elevated halls and side wings remain important architectural details in their own right.

National Stadium

Beijing's National Stadium, nicknamed the Bird's Nest, was built for the 2008 Olympics and still hosts concerts and sports events. Interlocking steel columns form the outer bowl that made the design famous worldwide. After the Games the arena stayed in use as a large public venue on the Olympic Green.
