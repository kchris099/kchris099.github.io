Xi'an

Xi'an, ancient Chang'an, served as capital for the Western Han, Sui, and Tang dynasties and as a terminus of the Silk Road. City walls, bell and drum towers, and the Great Mosque mark imperial ritual, Buddhist pilgrimage, and Muslim merchant quarters in northwest China's greatest medieval metropolis. The historic centre remains one of China's clearest windows onto Silk Road urban life.

Great Mosque of Xi'an

Courtyards of the Great Mosque sit beside craft streets in Xi'an's Muslim Quarter. Nearby food stalls sell roasted beef, fish, and pancakes typical of the district's Hui cooking. The mosque exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside prayer times for respectful walk-throughs.

Bell Tower of Xi'an

Xi'an's Bell Tower, rebuilt in the Ming period around 1384, stands at the crossing of the city's main axes inside the old walls. The wooden pavilion on a brick base once marked time for the imperial city, and underpasses now carry traffic while visitors climb for views over the drum tower and Muslim Quarter.

Ancient City Wall

Xi'an's Ming Dynasty city wall still rings the old urban core with thick brick ramparts, gates, and corner towers. A wide paved walk along the top lets visitors circle on foot or by bicycle above the moat and traffic below. From the parapet the view shifts between tiled roofs inside the wall and the modern skyline beyond the gates.

Fortifications of Xi'an

Ming builders raised Xi'an's city wall around 1370 as a continuous defensive circuit about 13.7 kilometers long. A dry moat still rings much of the masonry. When open, people walk the wall-top route along battlements and towers that enclose the old city grid.

Small Wild Goose Pagoda

An eighth-century brick pagoda rises inside the Jianfu Temple grounds, with an adjacent park around its base. Climbers can reach upper levels for city views when access is allowed. Ming and Qing rebuilds renewed temple fabric around the pagoda without erasing its Tang-period core.

06 AdmissionCNY40

Giant Wild Goose Pagoda

Rebuilding and repairs produced the present seven-story brick tower within the Da Ci'en Temple complex in Xi'an. Tang emperor Gaozong authorized an early Giant Wild Goose Pagoda in 652 to hold Buddhist texts and images brought from India by the monk Xuanzang. The square structure, surrounding halls, and translation history connect the site with the movement of Buddhist scholarship along Asian trade routes.

Shaanxi History Museum

Shaanxi History Museum opened in 1991 in a modern complex modeled on Tang palace architecture. Its collections trace regional history from prehistoric settlement through imperial capitals, with bronzes, ceramics, tomb murals, coins, sculpture and objects excavated around Xi'an. The museum's scale and chronology reflect Shaanxi's role at the center of several Chinese dynasties.
