Explore Lhasa

A Brief History

Lhasa is situated in the Himalayas and is the administrative capital of the Tibet Autonomous Region. The city rose to prominence in the seventh century when Songtsen Gampo unified Tibet and moved his capital there. It subsequently developed into the principal religious and political center of Tibetan Buddhism. The urban landscape is marked by historic monasteries, temples, and palaces that reflect centuries of religious devotion and governance.

Top Sights & Activities

Potala Palace

Potala Palace, located on Red Hill in Lhasa, is a grand complex with hundreds of rooms and shrines. Initially built in the 7th century and expanded over time, it became the largest integrated palatial architecture in Lhasa. The palace houses numerous treasures such as frescos, statues, Buddhist scriptures, silver and gold objects, and more. It also attracts countless faithful pilgrims.

¥60

Norbulingka

Norbulingka, also known as the Treasure Park, is a fortified historic site in Lhasa, China. Norbulingka, also known as the Treasure Park, served as the summer palace for the Dalai Lamas. Initially constructed with just one palace in the 1740s, it has expanded over nearly 300 years to include five sections and over 370 rooms. Walls, courtyards, or towers form the main visitor route when the grounds are open.

¥85

Jokhang Temple

Jokhang Temple, an ancient Buddhist temple in Tibet, was built over 1,300 years ago by King Songtsen Gampo. It is the most sacred building in Tibet and houses the revered Jowo Rinpoche statue of Buddha. Situated at an altitude of 3,600 meters, it is surrounded by Barkhor Street where pilgrims perform ritual kora around the temple.

Free

Sera Monastery

Sera Monastery is a religious building in Lhasa, China. Records associated with the site include 1419. At the foot of the majestic Serawoze Mountain, Sera Monastery is a Tibetan Buddhist site that dates back to 1419 and spans an 28 acres. This revered monastery is one of the Great Three monasteries of the Gelugpa School, alongside Ganden and Drepung Monasteries. The exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside service times.
