Lhasa

Lhasa is situated in the Himalayas and is the administrative capital of the Tibet Autonomous Region. The city rose to prominence in the seventh century when Songtsen Gampo unified Tibet and moved his capital there. It subsequently developed into the principal religious and political center of Tibetan Buddhism. The urban landscape is marked by historic monasteries, temples, and palaces that reflect centuries of religious devotion and governance.

Potala Palace

White and red palace blocks climb Red Hill above Lhasa in the Potala Palace complex. Earlier royal construction began in the seventh century, while the present form grew mainly from seventeenth-century work under the Fifth Dalai Lama and later additions. Chapels, assembly halls, tomb stupas; murals, manuscripts, statues and former administrative rooms record its roles as religious seat, residence and government center.

Norbulingka

Known as the Treasure Park, Norbulingka began as a single palace in the 1740s and grew into the Dalai Lamas' summer residence. Nearly three centuries of expansion produced five sections and more than 370 rooms set in garden grounds. When open, routes pass walls, courtyards, and palace halls across the parkland.

Jokhang Temple

Jokhang Temple, an ancient Buddhist temple in Tibet, was built over 1,300 years ago by King Songtsen Gampo. It is the most sacred building in Tibet and houses the revered Jowo Rinpoche statue of Buddha. Situated at an altitude of 3,600 meters, it is surrounded by Barkhor Street where pilgrims perform ritual kora around the temple.

Sera Monastery

Founded in 1419 at the foot of Serawoze Mountain, Sera Monastery is one of the three great Gelug monasteries of Lhasa with Ganden and Drepung. The complex covers roughly 28 acres of assembly halls, colleges, and monk residences. Afternoon debate courtyards and chapel visits define a typical public route through the active monastic campus.
