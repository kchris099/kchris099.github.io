Yerevan

Yerevan's recorded settlement dates to the eighth century BCE on the Ararat plain. Russian and Soviet planning left wide boulevards and pink tuff apartment blocks, while medieval churches and the Cascade complex reflect Armenia's Christian heritage and post-Soviet cultural revival as national capital. Genocide memorial precincts and Ararat viewpoints extend pink-tuff boulevards across the volcanic plain.

Republic Square

Designed by Alexander Tamanian, Republic Square was once Lenin Square and held a Lenin statue until 1991. Neoclassical government and museum buildings frame a broad paved oval used for parades, concerts, and the evening fountain show. The renaming after independence kept the axial plan while removing Soviet monumental sculpture.

Vernissage Market

Open-air stalls at Vernissage Market in Yerevan sell souvenirs, antiques, canvas paintings, Orthodox icons, coffee gear, handicrafts, and local foods. Despite the gallery-sounding name, the mix stays eclectic and street-level. Trading runs through regular market hours along the outdoor lanes.

Saint Gregory The Illuminator Cathedral

Consecrated in 2001, Saint Gregory the Illuminator Cathedral commemorates 1,700 years since Armenia adopted Christianity as a state religion. The complex includes the main cathedral and chapels dedicated to King Tiridates III and Queen Ashkhen; pale tuff walls, angular towers, and a broad forecourt give it a modern interpretation of Armenian church architecture.

40.1722° N / 44.5172° E

04 AdmissionFree

Sasuntsi Davit Statue

In Yerevan, Armenia, stands the Sasuntsi Davit Statue, a copper equestrian statue created by Yervand Kochar in 1959. This landmark depicts David of Sassoun, the protagonist of the Armenian national epic Daredevils of Sassoun. The idea to erect this statue emerged in the late 1930s and it has become a symbol of Armenian heritage and history.

40.1553° N / 44.5095° E

Charles Aznavour Square

Charles Aznavour Square honors the singer's aid to Armenia after the 1988 Spitak earthquake, with a central pool ringed by twelve zodiac signs. The paved plaza sits among Yerevan theatres and hotels, used for evening gatherings and photos. Water, stone paving, and the dedication together make a civic memorial rather than a landscaped garden.

Holy Mother of God Katoghike Church

Katoghike Church of the Holy Mother of God keeps a small stone footprint in central Yerevan with roots traced to the Arsacid period. Mountains ring the city beyond the older streets around the chapel. Exterior walls and the main interior remain viewable outside service times for those walking the downtown core.

Armenian National Opera and Ballet Theatre

Alexander Tamanian designed Yerevan's circular opera complex as two performance halls sharing one monumental structure. Opened in the 1930s and later expanded, it houses the Armenian National Academic Theatre of Opera and Ballet and the Aram Khachaturian Concert Hall. Stone arcades face Freedom Square, where the building anchors a major civic and cultural gathering space.

40.1859° N / 44.5151° E

Freedom Square

In downtown Yerevan, Freedom Square, also known as Liberty Square, is a busy town square that forms part of the Yerevan Opera Theater complex. Situated between Swan Lake and Opera Park, it is bordered by four streets: Mashtots Avenue, Sayat Nova, Tumanyan Street, and Teryan Street. This area is surrounded by attractions such as the Yerevan State Puppet Theatre and the Arno Babajanyan Statue.

40.1854° N / 44.5150° E

09 AdmissionFree

Mother Armenia Monument

Sculptor Ara Harutyunyan's 51-meter Mother Armenia statue rose in Victory Park in 1967 above Yerevan. Clear days open views across the city toward Mount Ararat from the memorial terrace. The towering figure anchors the park's commemorative axis in an open public setting.

40.1951° N / 44.5247° E

10 AdmissionFree

Blue Mosque

Blue glazed tiles, a slender minaret and a shaded courtyard distinguish Yerevan's eighteenth-century Blue Mosque. Built during Persian rule, the complex later passed through secular uses before restoration returned it to religious and cultural activity; prayer halls, small exhibition rooms and garden paths preserve an Iranian architectural presence within the modern city center.

40.1782° N / 44.5055° E
