Yangon

British annexation of Lower Burma made Rangoon a grid-planned colonial capital with docks facing the gilded Shwedagon Pagoda. Independence, military rule, and the 2006 move of government to Naypyidaw left downtown full of ageing colonial façades and street markets around that Buddhist axis. Circular railway rides still loop past colonial brick blocks and neighbourhood markets.

Shwedagon Pagoda

Shwedagon's gilded central stupa dominates Singuttara Hill above Yangon, surrounded by prayer halls, planetary posts, smaller shrines, bells, and pavilions. Buddhist tradition assigns the site antiquity and relics of several Buddhas, while archaeological dating is later and more uncertain, and repeated rebuilding and donations created the present complex, which remains Myanmar's foremost active pilgrimage center.

Chaukhtatgyi Buddha Temple

A vast reclining Buddha fills Chaukhtatgyi's covered hall, its painted face, glass eyes, decorated feet and extended body visible from a long platform. The current image dates mainly from twentieth-century rebuilding after an earlier statue was demolished. Devotees, monks and local families use the Yangon temple daily.

The Secretariat Yangon

Henry Hoyne-Fox designed the Victorian brick Ministers' Building between 1889 and 1905 for British colonial government. After independence it housed Myanmar's main ministers' offices until the capital shifted to Nay Pyi Taw in 2005. The abandoned complex still dominates a central Yangon block.

16.7753° N / 96.1658° E

04 AdmissionFree

St Mary's Cathedral

Built in the early twentieth century under architect Joseph Cuypers, it became the seat of the Roman Catholic archdiocese; wartime damage and later repairs altered parts of the building, while Mass and parish life continue within Myanmar's large Catholic church. Red-brick walls, pointed arches, twin spires, and stained glass distinguish St. Mary's Cathedral in central Yangon.

16.7786° N / 96.1647° E

05 AdmissionFree

Sule Pagoda

A gilded octagonal stupa at a major Yangon junction, Sule Pagoda has long worked as both shrine and civic landmark. Tradition assigns it deep age in Burmese religious memory. Modern pro-democracy gatherings have also used the traffic circle around its base.

Bogyoke Aung San Market

Nearly a century old, Bogyoke Aung San Market fills a colonial hall with antiques, jewellery, art, cloth, clothing, and food near Sule Pagoda and night-market streets. Travellers often buy Burmese textiles and jewellery here. Covered aisles keep trade going through the day.

Karaweik Palace

Karaweik Palace, situated on the Karaweik Lake in Yangon, is a must-visit destination for those seeking an authentic experience of Myanmar's cultural and culinary heritage. The palace is known for its traditional Pann Sel Myo and the Karaweik mythical bird design that adorns its structure.

08 AdmissionFree

Thiri Mingala Kabar Aye Pagoda

Kabar Aye Pagoda was built in the early 1950s as part of preparations for the Sixth Buddhist Council in Yangon. A broad circular form, central relic chamber, Buddha images, and surrounding religious facilities express its modern peace dedication; vendor stalls and conference-related structures occupy the wider compound, which differs from the city's much older royal stupas.

16.8569° N / 96.1545° E

09 AdmissionFree

Swe Taw Myat Pagoda

Swe Taw Myat is known for housing a tooth relic associated with the Buddha, drawing worshippers who circle gilded halls and surrounding stupas. Offerings continue at secondary shrines across the compound. The complex works as an active pilgrimage stop on Yangon's pagoda routes.

16.8753° N / 96.1494° E

10 AdmissionFree

Kyauk Taw Gyi (White Marble Buddha)

A large seated Buddha carved from a single block of white marble anchors Kyauk Taw Gyi near Yangon's northern edge. Transporting and installing the image in the early twenty-first century became a major state religious project. A high hall, tiled approaches, smaller shrines, and active offerings surround the figure, while official naming varies between Kyauk Taw Gyi and Lawka Chantha Abaya Labha Muni.

16.8847° N / 96.1232° E
