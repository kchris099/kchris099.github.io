Bagan

Bagan was the capital of a powerful Burmese kingdom from the 9th to 13th centuries, when rulers sponsored thousands of Buddhist temples across the Irrawaddy plain. Mongol pressure and shifting politics ended its imperial role, but the brick monuments remained. Earthquakes in 1975 and 2016 damaged many structures; conservation continues. Hot-air balloons, river views, and temple circuits now organise visits across the archaeological zone.

Dhammayangyi Temple

Dhammayangyi Temple is an imposing 12th-century structure in Bagan, featuring shrines, Buddha statues, and walkways. Commissioned by King Narathu to atone for his sins of assassinating his family members, the temple's eccentricity is reflected in its finely set brickwork and unfinished construction.

Lawkananda Pagoda

Lawkananda Pagoda, a tiered gilded pagoda, was originally built in the 11th century by King Anawrahta and has undergone renovations over the years. It is known for enshrining a replica of Buddha's tooth. Perched on a hill near the shipyard, it offers views of the Ayeyarwaddy River. The recent re-gilding adds to its allure, making it a popular spot for both locals and tourists.

Bupaya

Bupaya is a sacred pagoda located on the bank of the Ayeyarwaddy River, featuring a bulb-shaped dome. It was originally built by the 3rd King of Pagan and is said to be the oldest stupa in Bagan. The design of Bupaya reflects the ancient Pyu style, with its cylindrical form and gourd-like shape.

Shwesandaw Pagoda

Shwesandaw Pagoda, a Buddhist temple dating back to 1057 AD, is known for its five terraces that offer views of the surrounding landscape. Travellers can climb these terraces using steep stairs and a steel bannister for support. Standing at over 320 feet tall, it is one of the highest temples in Bagan and provides panoramic views of the Ayeyarwady River and nearby landmarks such as Thatbyinnyu Temple.

The Bagan Golden Palace, located on the left bank of the Ayeyarwady River in Central Myanmar, was the capital during the peak of the Myanmar Empire.
