Almaty

Almaty served as capital of Soviet and independent Kazakhstan until 1997 and remains the country's largest city at the foot of the Trans-Ili Alatau. Tsarist-era wooden villas, Stalinist blocks, and post-Soviet towers line wide streets laid out after earthquakes in the 19th century. Panfilov Park, the Zenkov Cathedral, and mountain resorts record its role as a cultural, financial, and gateway city to the Tian Shan.

Almaty Central Mosque

Almaty's Central Mosque combines white marble, blue and gold domes, calligraphy, courtyards, and a tall minaret near the Green Bazaar district. Completed in the late twentieth century, it became a major congregational mosque for the city. The complex reads clearly from surrounding avenues and plazas.

Almaty Zoo

Almaty Zoo opened in 1937 at the foot of the Trans-Ili Alatau on roughly twenty-one hectares in eastern Almaty. Mammals, birds, and reptiles occupy enclosures against a mountain backdrop. It ranks among Kazakhstan's older zoological parks and remains a standard family outing in the city.

Ascension Cathedral

Ascension Cathedral rises in Panfilov Park as a colorful wooden Russian Orthodox church completed in the early twentieth century. Architect Andrei Zenkov is closely associated with its earthquake-resistant construction, while painted interiors and an iconostasis support active worship; the building survived the 1911 earthquake and later Soviet secular use before being returned to the church.

28 Panfilov Guardsmen Park

Panfilov Park combines mature trees, paths, the Ascension Cathedral, military memorials, and civic gathering spaces in central Almaty. Its name honors soldiers of the Panfilov Division associated with the defense of Moscow in 1941, though aspects of the famous wartime account have been historically disputed. The eternal flame and memorial sculpture remain central to public remembrance.

Abilkhan Kasteev State Art Museum

The Abilkhan Kasteev State Museum of Arts holds Kazakhstan's principal public collection of fine and decorative art; founded in the Soviet period and later named for painter Abilkhan Kasteev, it documents both national and regional artistic development. Galleries cover Kazakh painting, sculpture, textiles, metalwork, Russian art and selected European or Asian works, with temporary exhibitions extending the permanent displays.
