Isfahan

Isfahan served as Safavid capital from 1598, when Shah Abbas I rebuilt Naqsh-e Jahan Square with mosques, bazaars, and polo grounds. Persian garden design, tilework, and bridge architecture along the Zayandeh River record how the dynasty linked Shi'a pilgrimage routes to Silk Road commerce. Khaju Bridge arcades and Jolfa Armenian churches extend Safavid urban design along the Zayandeh River.

Si-o-Se Pol Bridge

Built in 1602 by Allahverdi Khan Ondiladze over the Zayandeh River, the Bridge of 33 Arches stretches nearly 1,000 feet and about 45 feet wide as a double-deck stone crossing. Walkers use the lower and upper arcades for evening air. Sunset light fills the arches, and the bridge remains Isfahan's principal Safavid river span.

Holy Bethlehem Church

Khaje Petros, a wealthy Armenian merchant, commissioned Bethlehem Church in Isfahan's New Julfa quarter in the early seventeenth century. A high double-shell dome, wall paintings, gilded decoration, inscriptions, and biblical scenes fill the Armenian Apostolic interior; the church reflects the community displaced to Isfahan under Shah Abbas I, while height rankings among nearby domes add little historical value.

32.6365° N / 51.6577° E

03 AdmissionFree

Khajoo Bridge

Khajoo Bridge, built in 1650 by Shah Abbas II, is a example of Persian bridge architecture with its 24 arches and royal pavilion. This graceful structure on the Zayandeh Rud River has stood for over 350 years and still is a recreational center for social gatherings and cultural exchanges. The bridge's detailed decorations, including paintings and tiles, reflect the grandeur of the Safavid Dynasty.

32.6368° N / 51.6833° E

Fire Temple of Isfahan

Atashgah rises on a rocky hill west of Isfahan as the remains of a fortified complex traditionally linked with Zoroastrian fire worship. Mud-brick walls, circular summit ruins, terraces, and commanding views survive in heavily eroded form. Dates range across late antique and earlier interpretations, so calling every remain a securely identified Sasanian temple overstates what archaeology can confirm.

Monar Jonban

A pair of shaking minarets crowns an Ilkhanid-era tomb hall, viewed from the courtyard when the shrine complex is open. The monument sits on Isfahan's western side toward the Najafabad approach. Visitors come for the engineered sway of the towers as much as for the prayer hall below.

Shah Abbas Mosque

Shah Abbas I commissioned the great mosque on Isfahan's Naqsh-e Jahan Square in the early seventeenth century, and a bent entrance passage turns the building toward Mecca despite the square's different alignment, demonstrating Safavid urban planning. Turquoise domes and monumental iwans frame the courtyards, while prayer halls, calligraphy and dense tilework develop the interior sequence.

32.6552° N / 51.6780° E

Aali Qapu Palace

Built around 1597 by Shah Abbas after the capital moved from Qazvin, this six-story palace on Naqsh-e Jahan Square served Safavid sultans as a governmental and residential base. Walls, courtyards, and elevated halls form the route when grounds are open. Music rooms and terrace views overlook the square toward Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque.

32.6570° N / 51.6766° E

08 AdmissionIRR 2,500,000

Sheikh Lotfollah Mosque

Shah Abbas commissioned the royal mosque on Naqsh-e Jahan Square as a place of learning, teaching, and prayer; construction took eighteen years. The dome faces Ali Qapu across the square. Tilework fills the prayer hall without a large open courtyard, giving the mosque a more intimate royal character than the Shah Mosque nearby.

32.6574° N / 51.6786° E

09 AdmissionIRR 2,500,000

Chehel Sotoon Palace

Also called the Forty Column Palace, this seventeenth-century pleasure palace hosted Shah Abbas II receptions inside a classic Persian garden. Reflected columns in the pool double the porch count that gives the palace its name. Walls and garden courts form the walking route when the grounds are open to visitors.

32.6573° N / 51.6717° E

Jame' Mosque of Isfahan

The Jame' Mosque of Isfahan, also known as Masjed-e Jame, is a notable building that has been continuously built, rebuilt, and renovated since the 9th century. It holds a significant influence on mosque architecture in Iran and Central Asia. Many international tourists consider it to be the most site in Iran. The main building has undergone construction and renovations from the 8th century to the end of the 20th century.
