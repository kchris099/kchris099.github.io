Isfahan

Isfahan served as Safavid capital from 1598, when Shah Abbas I rebuilt Naqsh-e Jahan Square with mosques, bazaars, and polo grounds. Persian garden design, tilework, and bridge architecture along the Zayandeh River record how the dynasty linked Shi'a pilgrimage routes to Silk Road commerce. Khaju Bridge arcades and Jolfa Armenian churches extend Safavid urban design along the Zayandeh River.

Si-o-Se Pol Bridge

Si-o-Se Pol Bridge, also known as the Bridge of 33 Arches, is a historic landmark in Isfahan, Iran. Built in 1602 over the Zayandeh River by Allahverdi Khan Ondiladze, this double-deck stone bridge spans nearly 1,000 feet and is 45 feet wide. It has become a popular destination for tourists from around the world and a romantic spot for couples to enjoy sunset strolls.

Holy Bethlehem Church

Holy Bethlehem Church, also known as Bedkhem Church, is a significant Armenian Apostolic church located the Jolfa district of Isfahan, Iran. Built in 1627 by the Armenian merchant Khaje Petros during the Safavid era, this church was created to serve the local Armenian community relocated by Shah Abbas. Its striking rectangular design is crowned with an dome that is the tallest among twelve nearby churches.

Khajoo Bridge

Khajoo Bridge, built in 1650 by Shah Abbas II, is a example of Persian bridge architecture with its 24 arches and royal pavilion. This graceful structure on the Zayandeh Rud River has stood for over 350 years and still is a recreational center for social gatherings and cultural exchanges. The bridge's detailed decorations, including paintings and tiles, reflect the grandeur of the Safavid Dynasty.

The Fire Temple of Isfahan, also known as Atashgah, is an ancient Zoroastrian complex located on a hilltop outside the city. Dating back to the Sassanian era, this temple was used for worshiping fire, a central element in the Zoroastrian religion. The site offers scenic views and provides insight into the religious practices of ancient Persia.
