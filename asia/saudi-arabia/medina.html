Medina

The Medina Province shares its historical background with the city of Medina, including major events in early Islamic history such as the Battle of Badr and the establishment of Quba Mosque, the first mosque in Islam. The province was created in 1925 following the Saudi conquest of the Hejaz and the absorption of the Kingdom of Hejaz into the Sultanate of Nejd, forming the Kingdom of Hejaz and Nejd in 1926, which was later renamed the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia in 1932. The modern provincial boundaries were defined later. At the start of the 2020s, Medina launched the Humanizing Medina program, led by Fahd Al-Buliheshi, the Municipal Commissioner of Medina, under the supervision of the Medina Development Authority. The program aims to make the city and the surrounding province more walkable, sustainable, and accessible for residents and visitors.

Al Masjid-e-Nabawi

The Prophet's Mosque in Medina grew from the community mosque established by Prophet Muhammad after the Hijra into a vast pilgrimage complex expanded by successive rulers. The Green Dome marks the chamber containing his tomb and those of Abu Bakr and Umar. Prayer, visitation, crowd control, and sacred etiquette govern the site, whose religious importance cannot be captured by architecture alone.

The Green Dome

The Green Dome rises above the burial chamber within the Prophet's Mosque in Medina. Its present color and form result from later Ottoman-era work over a structure built centuries after the original mosque, covering the tombs associated with Prophet Muhammad, Abu Bakr and Umar. The dome is seen from the mosque courtyards, while devotional access to the chamber is strictly managed.

Masjid al-Qiblatayn

Masjid al-Qiblatayn marks the place where Prophet Muhammad received the command to turn the qibla from Masjid al-Aqsa toward the Kaaba. The mosque remains a key stop on Islamic history circuits in Medina. Exterior and main interior spaces stay viewable outside prayer times.

The Seven Mosques

Located against the rugged cliffs of Medina, The Seven Mosques is a complex that holds deep historical and spiritual significance for Muslims. This site commemorates the Battle of the Trench, an essential event in Islamic history. Among its notable structures is Al-Fath Mosque, where the Prophet Muhammad prayed for victory during this battle.

Cave of Bani Haram

A small cave on Mount Sela is traditionally associated with the Battle of the Trench and with accounts of Prophet Muhammad praying or sheltering during the campaign. Its significance comes from religious memory connected with Medina's early Islamic history. The surrounding rocky terrain helps place the tradition within the defensive landscape west of the old city.

Masjid Quba

Quba Mosque stands south of central Medina on the site associated with an early mosque founded by Prophet Muhammad after the Hijra. Repeated expansion has transformed the early structure into a large modern complex serving worshippers and pilgrims. Masjid Quba, located south of Madinah, holds a special place in the hearts of Muslims as it is an early mosque built by Prophet Muhammad.

King Fahad Central Park

About five kilometers south of the Prophet's Mosque, King Fahd Central Park spreads across roughly 4.5 square kilometers with playgrounds, food courts, and outdoor play areas. Weekends draw heavy crowds, with police presence for order. Paths and lawns organize the large recreational ground.

مزرع سلمان الفارسي رضي الله عنه

The farm associated with Salman al-Farisi lies among Medina's historic agricultural plots linked in local tradition to the Companion of the Prophet. Palm groves, irrigation channels, and simple rural structures recall the oasis farming that supported the early city beyond the mosque precincts. Visits center on the planted grounds and the named remembrance of Salman rather than on a large museum complex.

Al-Rashid Mega Mall

Al Rashid Mega Mall serves Medina as an enclosed retail and leisure center with shops, restaurants, family entertainment and everyday services. Decorative interior elements and broad circulation areas organize the commercial floors, while the surrounding roads connect the complex with residential districts. Its role is primarily local and practical, providing shopping and indoor recreation within the modern city. The enclosed setting offers relief from Medina's heat.

Archers' Hill Uhud

Archers' Hill rises on the Uhud battlefield north of Medina, the ridge where Muslim archers were posted during the Battle of Uhud. Slopes of rock and scrub look across the plain toward Mount Uhud itself, with markers explaining the engagement's positions. Pilgrims and history visitors climb for the vantage and the documented link to early Islamic military history, not for landscaped park amenities.

Uhud Mountain Cave

Rock openings on the slopes of Mount Uhud are visited in connection with the Battle of Uhud, fought in 625, although precise historical associations with individual caves are uncertain. The rough, exposed terrain and informal paths make the broader battlefield landscape more significant than any single opening in the rock. Views across the mountain help place the early Islamic battle sites within their natural setting north of the city.

Uhud Mountain

Mount Uhud forms a long rocky ridge north of Medina and overlooks the battlefield where Muslim and Meccan forces fought in 625. Martyrs' graves, Archers' Hill, mosques and interpretive sites lie across the plain below. Uhud Mountain, located in Medina, is a significant historical site where the Battle of Uhud took place in 624 CE.

Al-Ghamama Mosque stands southwest of the Prophet's Mosque on a site traditionally linked to Eid and rain prayers led by Prophet Muhammad. The present Ottoman-era building has dark stone walls, domes, arches, and a compact prayer interior shaped by later restoration.

Abu Bakr Mosque stands near Al-Ghamama in the historic prayer-ground area southwest of the Prophet's Mosque. Quba Front

Quba Front covers the open approaches and plaza edge around Quba Mosque on Medina's southern side, where roads and pedestrian paths meet the mosque precinct. The setting ties everyday neighborhood traffic to one of Islam's earliest congregational sites. Shade structures, paving, and views toward the mosque domes define the stop more than a separate monument with its own interior rooms.

Dhul Hulaifah Miqat Mosque

Located a large complex adorned with lush trees, the Dhul Hulaifah Miqat Mosque, also known as Shigarh Mosque or Abyar Ali Mosque, stands out as a significant landmark in Madinah. Spanning an 6000 square meters, this mosque is steeped in history and tradition. It marks the last stop of Prophet Muhammad before began on his Umrah journey after signing a peace treaty.

The Aziziyah Overlook

The Aziziyah Overlook sits in Medina's Aziziyah district as a raised viewpoint over residential blocks and routes leading toward the Prophet's Mosque area. Railings and open platforms make the city layout readable at dusk when lights come up across the basin. Local walking routes include the terrace for orientation rather than for a ticketed exhibit hall.
