Leh

Leh was established as the capital of the Himalayan kingdom of Ladakh in the seventeenth century by King Sengge Namgyal. It served as an important stop on the ancient trade routes connecting Tibet, Kashmir, and Central Asia. The city's architecture is dominated by the nine-story Leh Palace and numerous Buddhist monasteries. Today, Leh remains a vital center of Tibetan Buddhist culture and a gateway for high-altitude trekking and exploration.

Leh Palace

Leh Palace, or Lhachen Palkhar, crowns a ridge above Leh as a nine-storey royal residence begun under King Sengge Namgyal in the seventeenth century. Its massing deliberately recalls Tibet's Potala Palace. From the upper terraces the Indus valley and surrounding monasteries open below the old town.

Shanti Stupa

Shanti Stupa above Leh was inaugurated in 1991 through Japanese and Ladakhi Buddhist cooperation, including Bhikshu Gyomo Nakamura and Kushok Bakula. White domes and Buddha statues sit near 4,267 meters with wide views over the Indus valley. Pilgrims and evening walkers share the hilltop plaza.

Ancient palace Shey

Shey Palace and its adjoining gompa sit above the Indus south of Leh, once a royal seat of Ladakhi kings. Whitewashed walls, a fortress-like massing, and temple rooms with a large Buddha statue define the visit. Courtyards and stair routes climb from the valley road toward the ridge-top complex.

Leh Main Market

Below the old palace, Leh Main Market forms the commercial center of the modern town through pedestrian lanes, shops; cafes, produce sellers, banks, craft outlets and public seating. Redevelopment has reshaped the central street, while Tibetan, Ladakhi, Kashmiri and tourist economies meet in the surrounding lanes.

Zorawar Fort

General Zorawar Singh raised this nineteenth-century fort about a half-hour trek from Leh town, tying it to regional military campaigns. Walls, courtyards, and towers form the walking route when grounds are open. The stone work sits in the high desert above the Indus valley, and history-minded travellers treat it as a short climb from the city core.

Offside cafe

Offside Cafe in Leh is a casual eatery with outdoor seating oriented toward mountain views and, from some tables, aircraft movements at the nearby airport. The neighborhood mixes monastery and temple approaches with everyday cafes. Travellers use it as a rest stop between high-altitude sightseeing rather than as a monument.

Khardungla Pass

Khardung La crosses the Ladakh Range on the road between Leh and the Nubra Valley. Modern surveys place its elevation below the 5,602-meter figure once displayed on signs, and other motorable passes rise higher. Thin air, snow, military traffic and changing road conditions define the crossing, which still provides an important route through a stark high-altitude landscape.

34.2786° N / 77.6047° E

Leh market

Leh Market spreads through pedestrian streets below Leh Palace, where shops sell produce, clothing, trekking supplies, Tibetan and Ladakhi crafts, and everyday household goods. Kashmiri Gali and smaller lanes connect the main commercial area with Chubi and routes toward the palace. Cafes and public seating now share space with long-standing trading activity at the center of the town.

Phyang Monastery

Phyang Monastery, founded in 1515 in a valley near Leh, holds brightly painted frescoes, scriptures, ritual objects, and a small museum of traditional weapons. The Drukpa Buddhist gompa climbs a hillside above village fields. Festival dances and daily prayer keep the complex in active religious use.
