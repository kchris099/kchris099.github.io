Delhi

Delhi layers Rajput, Sultanate, Mughal, and British imperial capitals on the Yamuna River. Red Fort, Humayun's Tomb, and Qutub Minar record successive conquests and architectural patronage, while New Delhi's ceremonial axis reflects twentieth-century colonial planning and independent India's government seat. Lotus Temple and Lodi Garden tombs extend Mughal and colonial layers across the capital's metropolitan sprawl.

Red Fort

The Red Fort, located in Old Delhi, India, is a 17th-century Mughal fortress made of red sandstone. It was the main residence of the Mughal Emperors and was commissioned by Emperor Shah Jahan in 1638. The fort's design is credited to Ustad Ahmad Lahori, who also built the Taj Mahal.

Jama Masjid

Jama Masjid in Old Delhi is a seventeenth-century Friday mosque of red sandstone and marble built under Shah Jahan. Three great gates climb to a vast courtyard and striped minarets above the bazaar. From the eastern steps the Jama Masjid faces the Red Fort across the old city streets.

03 AdmissionFree

Fatehpuri Masjid

At the western end of Chandni Chowk, Fatehpuri Masjid anchors Old Delhi's market axis opposite the Red Fort direction. Fatehpuri Begum, one of Shah Jahan's wives, commissioned the red-sandstone mosque in 1650. A broad courtyard, prayer hall, fluted dome, minarets, and surrounding commerce reveal a Mughal religious foundation that remains active through daily and Friday worship.

28.6566° N / 77.2228° E

Agrasen ki Baoli

Sixty meters of descending arches cut a dry stone shaft through central Delhi, each tier lined with niches that deepen the shade. Tradition credits Maharaja Agrasen, with later medieval rebuilding; the Archaeological Survey now protects the empty well. Glass office towers press close above the quiet sunken corridor, so the baoli feels like a sudden drop into older street levels.

Gurdwara Bangla Sahib

Near Connaught Place a gold-tinted dome rises over a broad sarovar, where devotees wash and queue toward the marble sanctum. Tradition links the compound with Guru Har Krishan. Langar halls feed continuous lines of worshippers and city workers, and the sacred pool keeps the courtyard cool even when traffic presses the surrounding streets.

Cathedral Church of The Redemption (C.N.I.)

Located Delhi, the Cathedral Church of The Redemption, also affectionately known as Viceroy Church, is a example of 1930s British colonial architecture. Its design draws inspiration from known architects Lutyens and Palladio, showing an elegant blend of red sandstone exteriors that reflect simplicity and class.

07 AdmissionFree

Rashtrapati Bhavan

Rashtrapati Bhavan was designed by Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker as the Viceroy's House, then became the residence and offices of India's president after independence. Completed in 1929, the vast estate combines classical planning with Indian stonework, chhatris, screens and garden traditions. Museum circuits, ceremonial halls and Amrit Udyan follow separate booking and security arrangements.

Rashtrapati Bhavan Museum New Delhi

The Rashtrapati Bhavan Museum in New Delhi, inaugurated in 2014 by President Pranab Mukherjee, displays presidential memorabilia, gifts of state, and rooms interpreting the estate's history. Galleries occupy converted stable and kitchen wings beside the presidential complex. Timed entry guides visitors through the exhibition sequence.

09 AdmissionFree

Talkatora Garden

Talkatora Garden west of central Delhi combines Mughal-era tank remnants with later landscaped lawns, rose beds, and the Talkatora Stadium complex. Paths and shade trees draw evening walkers. The garden's name recalls the carved reservoir that once stored water for the surrounding ridge.

10 AdmissionFree

Shri Laxmi Narayan Temple (Birla Mandir)

Shri Laxmi Narayan Temple, also known as Birla Mandir, is a large Hindu temple complex spanning over 3 hectares. Situated near Connaught Place in New Delhi, it was constructed by the known Birla family and inaugurated by Mahatma Gandhi in 1939. The temple is dedicated to Goddess Laxmi and Lord Vishnu, collectively known as Laxminarayan.

28.6327° N / 77.1990° E

11 AdmissionFree

Coronation Park

Coronation Park in north Delhi marks the ground of the 1911 Delhi Durbar, where George V proclaimed the new capital. Lawn, trees, and relocated statues of British monarchs occupy the commemorative field. Paths cross open grass rather than a palace compound, recalling imperial ceremony on the city's edge.
