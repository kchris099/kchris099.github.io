Jodhpur

Rao Jodha of the Rathore clan founded Jodhpur in 1459 and raised Mehrangarh on a rock ridge above the Marwar plain. The indigo-washed old town and courtly palaces grew from that desert kingdom's trade toward Gujarat and Delhi. Stepwells and zenana courtyards are the domestic side of the same Rajput capital. Umaid Bhawan palace, finished in the twentieth century, extended Rathore display into modernity.

Umaid Bhawan Palace, Jodhpur

Umaid Bhawan Palace was built between 1928 and 1943 for Maharaja Umaid Singh during a period of drought and economic distress in Marwar. Henry Vaughan Lanchester's sandstone design combines classical massing with Indian details and Art Deco interiors. The complex now contains a royal residence, hotel, and museum.

Umaid Bhawan Museum

Part of Umaid Bhawan Palace holds museum galleries of maharaja antiques and court artwork tracing the Suryavanshi line, while other wings remain a hotel and royal residence. Rooms display furniture, arms, and paintings from the palace household. The sandstone complex sits above Jodhpur as a twentieth-century royal landmark split between public galleries and private use.

03 AdmissionFree

Ganesh Temple Ratanada

A hill in Jodhpur's Ratanada district holds an active Ganesh temple reached by steps and roads from surrounding neighborhoods. The sanctuary, smaller shrines and elevated city views draw local worshippers, with especially large congregations on Wednesdays. Renovation has changed parts of the complex, but daily devotion remains its central purpose. The hilltop approach is part of the devotional experience.

04 AdmissionFree

Toorji Ka Jhalra Bavdi

Tiered arches and niches step down a deep shaft to water in old Jodhpur, shaped by eighteenth-century court patronage as a public waterwork. Photographers and bathers share the geometric well near the blue-painted lanes. Stone symmetry fills the view from the street rim, and the baoli remains an active neighborhood landmark rather than a sealed ruin.

05 AdmissionFree

Ghanta Ghar Jodhpur

Ghanta Ghar, Jodhpur's 1911 clock tower, rises above Sardar Market with carved sandstone detailing in a Rajput idiom. The tower orients shoppers among spice, textile, and souvenir lanes. From the square, lanes climb toward the Mehrangarh fort mass on the rock above the blue-washed houses.

26.2951° N / 73.0240° E

06 Admission₹800

Mehrangarh Fort

Rao Jodha founded Mehrangarh in the fifteenth century on a steep rock above Jodhpur and later rulers expanded its gates, palaces, courtyards and ramparts. Museum rooms display arms, paintings, textiles, palanquins and royal interiors, while cannon positions overlook the blue-toned old city. Lifts and tours support access.

26.2982° N / 73.0186° E

07 Admission₹100

Jaswant Thada

White marble cenotaphs completed in 1899 honor Maharaja Jaswant Singh II and mark the royal cremation ground about one kilometer northeast of Mehrangarh. Sunlight turns the thin stone translucent along carved screens and domes. Paths among the memorials look back toward the fort ridge, linking the quiet marble terrace to Jodhpur's principal citadel.

26.3041° N / 73.0254° E

08 AdmissionFree

Bheru Bagh Parshvnath Jain Shvetamber Teerth

Dedicated to Parshvanath, this Shvetambara complex gathers marble carving across shrines used by Jodhpur's Jain community for worship and pilgrimage. Detailed stonework covers pillars and ceilings inside the quiet courtyards. Devotees treat the teerth as a spiritual center within the city, with ritual activity continuing through the carved halls.

09 AdmissionFree

Pragat Shri Santoshi Mata Mandir Dham

Pragat Shri Santoshi Mata Mandir sits on a hill above Jodhpur, drawing devotees especially on Fridays associated with the goddess. The climb ends at a shrine with views across the blue rooftops and surrounding plain. Women pilgrims form a large share of the weekly crowd.

26.3269° N / 73.0513° E

10 Admission₹30

Machia Biological Park, Jodhpur

Machia Biological Park near Kaylana Lake keeps desert and regional wildlife in managed enclosures linked by walking paths and visitor transport. Deer, antelope, big cats, reptiles, birds and small mammals may be represented, although collections change with transfers and animal care.
