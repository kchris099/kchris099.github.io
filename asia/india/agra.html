Agra

Agra flourished as the Mughal capital under Akbar, Jahangir, and Shah Jahan in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries. The Taj Mahal, Agra Fort, and riverside gardens embody imperial tomb architecture and riverfront palace design on the Yamuna, linking Indo-Islamic artistry to trans-regional trade. Marble inlay workshops and riverside gardens still frame views of the Taj from the Yamuna's west bank.

Taj Mahal

Taj Mahal, a 17th-century Mughal-style marble mausoleum located in Agra, India, is a symbol of love and architectural mastery. Built by Emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal, it is described as an "elegy created in marble" and an "expression of true love." The monument's symmetrical gardens, minarets, and mosque add to its grandeur.

Agra Fort

Agra Fort is a Mughal red-sandstone fortress on the Yamuna, expanded under Akbar and later emperors as a royal residence and military stronghold. Courts, audience halls, and marble pavilions open along the river-facing walls. Views from the ramparts take in the Taj Mahal downstream.

Diwan-E-Aam

Diwan-i-Am inside Agra Fort is the public audience hall where Mughal emperors heard petitions. Cloisters line three sides of a courtyard, with white lime-polished arches and a triple-arched royal canopy set with pietra dura. The open hall sits among the fort's red sandstone courts rather than as a freestanding palace.

The Shish Mahal (The Glass Palace)

Shah Jahan added this 17th-century mirrored pavilion inside Agra Fort as an imperial bath. Restored glass and mirror work line the chambers with reflected light. Routes through the fort's courtyards pass the pavilion when the complex is open to ticketed circuits.

Amar Singh Gate

Amar Singh Gate, also called Akbar Darwaza, is the pink-sandstone ceremonial entrance into seventeenth-century Agra Fort. Massive bastions and a bent approach forced attackers to slow before the inner courts. The gate stands near the fort railway station, a short distance from the Taj Mahal across the Yamuna plain.

Shri Mankameshwar Temple, Agra

Shri Mankameshwar Temple is an ancient Hindu temple dedicated to Lord Shiva, situated near the Jama Masjid in Rawatpara, Agra. It is believed that the Shiva Linga inside the temple was created by Lord Shiva himself. The temple holds great mythological significance, with legends stating that it was built by Lord Ram and prayed at by Goddess Sita. Visitors are drawn to its peaceful atmosphere and carvings.

Itmad-ud-Daula

Itmad-ud-Daulah's Tomb, often nicknamed the Baby Taj, sits on the east bank of the Yamuna near central Agra. Nur Jahan commissioned the white marble mausoleum for her father, with inlay work that later informed the Taj Mahal. Compact gardens and pierced screens surround the central tomb chamber.

I LOVE AGRA photo point

The 'I LOVE AGRA' photo point is a popular modern installation located near the Taj Mahal. Featuring large, brightly colored letters, it provides a fun and well known backdrop for tourists to capture memories of their visit to the historic city. The site is frequently busy with visitors looking for a typical souvenir photograph.

Gurudwara Guru Ka Taal Agra

Gurudwara Guru Ka Taal in Agra marks the place associated with Guru Tegh Bahadur's detention under Aurangzeb and recalls visits by several Sikh Gurus. The white gurdwara complex includes a sarovar and prayer halls used by pilgrims. Langar and daily services keep the site active beyond sightseeing hours.

Sikandra Fort

The name Sikandra Fort commonly refers to Akbar's Tomb, a Mughal funerary complex northwest of Agra. A monumental red-sandstone gateway leads into geometric gardens with marble inlay, subsidiary buildings and the emperor's cenotaph. Akbar planned the site before his death in 1605, and Jahangir completed the dynastic memorial. The garden plan guides movement toward the tomb.
