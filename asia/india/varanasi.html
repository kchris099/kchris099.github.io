Varanasi

Known as Kashi, Varanasi ranks among the oldest continuously inhabited cities on the Ganges. Pilgrims use the ghats for ritual bathing and cremation while silk weavers work lanes behind the riverfront temples. Evening aarti at Dashashwamedh Ghat is the public ritual that most clearly expresses that sacred urban order. Buddhist Sarnath a few kilometres north ties the same sacred landscape to the Buddha's first sermon.

Shri Kashi Vishwanath Temple

Kashi Vishwanath Temple is Varanasi's principal Shiva shrine on the Ganges side of the old city, rebuilt in the eighteenth century under Ahilyabai Holkar and expanded with later corridors. Gold-plated spires and tightly controlled entry define the visit. The temple anchors Hindu pilgrimage to Kashi.

Shree Annapurna Mandir

Annapurna Temple near Kashi Vishwanath honors the goddess of nourishment within Varanasi's dense sacred core. The present shrine is associated with Maratha patronage in the eighteenth century, while ritual distribution of food and special festival display of the goddess's golden image reinforce its meaning.

Manikarnika Ghat

Manikarnika Ghat is one of Varanasi's principal cremation grounds, where funeral pyres, priests, wood carriers, mourners and river traffic continue around the clock. Hindu traditions connect death here with liberation, while wells, shrines, platforms and narrow lanes form a working ritual landscape.

Nepali Temple

The Nepali Temple, also called Kathwala Temple, stands near Manikarnika Ghat in Varanasi with carved wooden architecture brought in a Nepalese style. The small shrine sits above the cremation ghat lanes. Exteriors and courtyard approaches are the usual view for non-worshipping visitors.

Dhamek

Dhamek Stupa rises at Sarnath as a massive cylindrical monument marking the area where Buddhist tradition places the Buddha's first teaching. Mauryan foundations and later enlargements produced the present brick core with carved stone bands of geometric and floral patterns; it stands within excavated monasteries and shrines, while the exact ancient event location remains a matter of religious and archaeological interpretation.

Ashoka Pilar, Sarnath,(Varanasi)

The Ashoka Pillar remains at Sarnath Deer Park, about ten kilometers north of Varanasi, include the polished shaft and the four-lion capital that became India's national emblem. The fragment stands among monastic ruins of the deer park. A museum nearby holds related carved capitals and finds.

Archaeological Buddhist Remains of Sarnath

Sarnath's archaeological zone contains monastery foundations, stupas, temples, pillars, drainage and sculpture fragments developed across many centuries of Buddhist occupation. Paths connect Dhamek Stupa, the remains of the Ashokan pillar and later structures in an open park.

Chaukhandi Stupa Sarnath Varanasi

Chaukhandi Stupa began as a terraced brick monument marking the approach to Sarnath and the traditional place where the Buddha met his first disciples. A Mughal-period octagonal tower was later added to commemorate a royal visit. Its layered mound stands apart from the main excavated complex, demonstrating how Buddhist memory and later imperial commemoration accumulated on the same structure.

Assi Ghat, (Varanasi)

Assi Ghat Varanasi is a significant riverfront gathering place where religious festivals, performances, and yoga classes take place. It is ghats in the area and holds cultural importance as it marks the meeting point of River Assi with the Ganges. Pilgrims visit to bathe in its waters and worship a Shiva lingam beneath a pipal tree. The ghat comes alive in the evenings with hawkers and entertainers.

Chet Singh Ghat, (Varanasi)

Chet Singh Ghat on the Ganga in Varanasi combines a riverside landing with the fortified palace associated with Raja Chet Singh. Stone steps and riverside walls see fewer crowds than the central cremation ghats. The stretch suits quieter walks along the river facade.

Shri Satyanarayan Tulsi Manas Temple, (Varanasi)

Tulsi Manas Temple is a modern white-marble shrine opened in the 1960s near Durga Kund in Varanasi. Its walls carry verses and scenes from Tulsidas's Ramcharitmanas, while mechanical tableaux, gardens and sanctuaries center on Rama, Sita, Lakshmana and Hanuman. The complex commemorates a literary and devotional tradition, and it does not occupy Tulsidas's original residence. Carved text links architecture with devotional literature.

Shri Sankat Mochan Hanuman Temple, (Varanasi)

Sankat Mochan Hanuman Temple is a major Varanasi shrine traditionally linked with the poet Tulsidas. Courtyards, queues, offerings, devotional music and monkeys surround worship of Hanuman as the remover of troubles. The modern complex has been rebuilt and secured after a 2006 bombing. Daily religious practice remains the defining activity within the grounds.

Dashashwamedh Ghat

Dashashwamedh Ghat occupies a central bend of Varanasi's riverfront, with broad steps used for bathing, ritual, boat boarding, festivals and evening Ganga aarti. Priests perform the public ceremony with lamps, bells, incense and coordinated movement before large crowds. Morning worship and everyday river work remain equally important, while event security can reshape access to the ghat.

Dr. Rajendra Prasad Ghat, (Varanasi)

Named for India's first president, Dr. Rajendra Prasad Ghat forms part of the continuous stone riverfront beside Dashashwamedh Ghat; early light and river traffic define the visit more than a single monument. Steps support bathing, boat access, ceremonies, and movement between temples and lanes above.

Man Singh Observatory

Also called Jantar Mantar, this early eighteenth-century observatory sits at Manmandir Ghat with large stone instruments for celestial measurement. Approaches are usually short walks from road level above the river. The instruments share the ghat edge with palace fabric, tying astronomy architecture to Varanasi's riverfront royal quarter.

Kaal Bhairav Temple in Varanasi honours Bhairava as the city's fierce guardian deity. Devotees offer alcohol and flowers in a compact sanctum reached through old-city lanes. Mahamrityunjay Mahadev Temple in Varanasi is a Shiva shrine associated with the Mahamrityunjaya mantra and healing vows. The compound holds a sanctum, bells, and queues of devotees seeking protection and recovery. Street approaches pass through residential lanes away from the main ghat front.

Namo Ghat

Namo Ghat is a newer developed stretch of Varanasi's riverfront with wider promenades, planting, and boat access along the Ganga. The landscaped landing aims to ease crowding on older central ghats. Evening walkers and boat passengers use the open embankment as a modern shoreline park.

Ramnagar Fort, (Varanasi)

Ramnagar Fort was built in the eighteenth century by Kashi Naresh Balwant Singh on the Ganges opposite Varanasi. Sandstone courtyards, residential wings, temples, ceremonial spaces and a museum of carriages, arms, clocks. Costumes and royal objects remain tied to the former princely family. Dussehra traditions and river views provide local context, while Chunar Fort is a separate site.

Shri Batuk Bhairav Temple, (Varanasi)

Batuk Bhairav Temple honors a child form of Shiva within Varanasi's network of Bhairava shrines, and legends describe the deity as a guardian. A compact sanctuary, oil offerings, protective rites, and steady local worship draw devotees, including communities connected with Tantric traditions.
