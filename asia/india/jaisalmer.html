Jaisalmer

Rawal Jaisal established the desert fortress in 1156 on caravan routes across the Thar. Merchant havelis in golden sandstone were paid for by that overland trade. When maritime routes undercut the caravans, isolation left the fort city largely unmodernised inside its walls. Living families still occupy parts of the fort, unusual among Rajasthan's hill strongholds.

BADA BAGH

Bada Bagh, about six kilometers from Jaisalmer, holds a garden of carved cenotaphs for Bhatti rulers and their families. Domed chhatris rise among wind turbines on the rocky plain. Late light on the sandstone memorials makes the site a common stop between the fort and desert camps.

Jaisalmer Fort

Jaisalmer Fort rises from Trikuta Hill as a twelfth-century Rajput citadel built in yellow sandstone. Palaces, Jain temples, houses, shops, lanes, gates and defensive walls remain part of an inhabited urban quarter within the Hill Forts of Rajasthan property. Water use, drainage, tourism and conservation place unusual pressure on the living fort, whose golden color changes with the desert light.

SHOPPING IN JAISALMER -- JAI SHANKAR HANDICRAFTS

Jai Shankar Handicrafts operates near the fort parking area on Dhibba Para Road in Jaisalmer. The shop concentrates on embroidered textiles and patchwork assembled from garments, bags and decorative fabrics associated with desert communities, alongside clothing and household pieces. Its setting close to the fort makes it a commercial craft stop, with custom work and available stock depending on the business.

Burj Al Jaisalmer is a tented desert camp on the Sam sand dunes west of Jaisalmer. Guests use camel rides across the dunes by day and folk music performances after dark. The camp layout groups luxury tents around dining and performance clearings on the sand.

05 AdmissionFree

Gadisar Lake

Gadisar Lake, constructed in the 14th century by Rawal Gadsi Singh, is a man-made rainwater lake surrounded by small temples. It is a water source for the people of Jaisalmer and is known for its scenic tranquility. The lake attracts tourists and offers a peaceful retreat for architecture lovers, culture enthusiasts, and bird watchers.

26.9086° N / 70.9231° E

Gadisar Lake Jaisalmer

Gadisar's shoreline walks and boat launches sit below Jaisalmer's fort ridge, where temples and ghats meet the reservoir. District roads reach the water from town, and travellers often combine the lake with nearby parks along the same approach. Stone steps and small shrines frame the water's edge, giving the lake a ceremonial rather than purely recreational character.

WelcomHeritage Mandir Palace

Built for royalty in the twentieth century, this palace hotel sits a short walk from Jaisalmer Fort and Bhatia Market. Bright rooms and suites share the historic shell with restaurants, an outdoor pool, and spa services. Guests use the property as both lodging and a sandstone architectural stop inside the old town's hotel quarter.

Nathmal Ki Haveli

Nineteenth-century Diwan Mohata Nathmal, a state prime minister, occupied this mansion whose twin facades carry dense sandstone elephants and lattice work. The privately held haveli still dominates a lane below the fort. Carvers left slightly mismatched sides that reward close looking, and the street front remains one of Jaisalmer's richest carved house elevations.

26.9159° N / 70.9127° E

Patwa Haveli Foundation - Jaisalmer

Patwon Ki Haveli is a group of five nineteenth-century mansions commissioned by the wealthy Patwa merchant family outside Jaisalmer Fort. Dense sandstone carving, projecting jharokhas, courtyards, painted rooms, and narrow stairs reveal elite domestic life and trading wealth.

26.9163° N / 70.9146° E

Jaisalmer War Museum

About eleven kilometers from town, galleries document Indian Army campaigns with vehicles, weapons, and narrative displays. An Honour wall lists Param Vir Chakra and Maha Vir Chakra recipients by name. Public rooms open on posted hours, and the desert-edge site focuses on military history rather than fort tourism inside the old city.
