Hampi

As capital of Vijayanagara in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, Hampi ranked among the era's richest cities. A coalition of Deccan sultanates sacked it in 1565. The boulder-strewn ruins of temples and royal enclosures are what survive of that imperial Hindu metropolis on the Tungabhadra. Vitthala temple's stone chariot and musical pillars are among the most photographed imperial fragments.

The Stone Chariot (Hampi)

Carved from stone to resemble a ceremonial temple chariot, Hampi's Stone Chariot stands in the courtyard of the Vijaya Vittala Temple. Garuda was worshipped in the small shrine, while wheels, elephants, relief panels, and layered masonry demonstrate Vijayanagara craftsmanship; modern conservation has fixed the wheels and limits contact, and transport from the parking area is a practical matter outside the monument's description.

Shree Vijaya Vitthala Temple

Fifteenth-century Vijayanagara builders raised a broad temple compound around halls and pillared courts near the Tungabhadra. In the courtyard stands a stone chariot shrine once fitted to move on rollers. Exterior platforms and main interior spaces usually remain viewable outside service times, and the ruined complex still reads as a ceremonial core of the empire's capital at Hampi.

Virupaksha temple

Began on a journey to Hampi is incomplete without exploring the Virupaksha Temple. This ancient Hindu temple, dedicated to Lord Shiva in his form as Virupaksha, is a evidence of the rich spiritual heritage of the region. Its detailed architecture and historical significance make it a must-visit site for travelers seeking to immerse themselves in India's cultural history.

04 Admission₹40

Group of Monuments at Hampi

Hampi's property spreads across a broad landscape of temples, royal enclosures, markets, waterworks, fortifications, boulder hills and settlements once central to the Vijayanagara Empire. Monuments date from different periods and cannot be understood as one compact ruin. Roads, footpaths, river crossings and active worship connect the sacred and courtly zones across many kilometres.

05 AdmissionFree

Kadalekalu Ganesha

A granite monolith about fifteen feet high fills a small shrine chamber on the Hampi hills, the elephant-headed form carved from a single boulder. Craftsmen of the Vijayanagara period shaped the figure that locals call Kadalekalu for its chickpea-like belly. Pilgrims climb to the chamber among rock outcrops near Virupaksha, where the statue dominates the modest enclosing walls.

06 AdmissionFree

Hemakuta Hill Temple

Scattered shrines and early Vijayanagara ruins crown Hemakuta Hill above Virupaksha, with open rock platforms that catch sunrise and sunset light. Photographers gather for wide views across boulder fields and temple towers inside the UNESCO zone. Short climbs lead between small temples and unfinished halls, and the hilltop remains a quiet counterpoint to the busier riverside clusters below.

Saasivekaalu Ganesha

Saasivekaalu Ganesha is a monolithic stone Ganesha at Hampi, named for a belly shaped like a mustard seed. The open-air shrine sits among boulder hills of the Vijayanagara ruins. Devotees leave offerings at the carved figure while travellers walk the short path from nearby temple clusters.

08 AdmissionFree

Hampi Shri Krishna Temple

Krishnadevaraya commissioned Hampi's Krishna Temple after a sixteenth-century military victory, installing an image of Balakrishna later removed to Chennai. A high gateway opens to pillared halls, subsidiary shrines, kitchens, and a temple tank, while a long market street extends outside.

09 AdmissionFree

Hampi Shri Lakshmi Narasimha Temple

A 6.7-meter granite Narasimha, Vishnu's man-lion form, sits cross-legged in an open enclosure near Virupaksha. The monolith still dominates the platform despite damage to attendant figures around the base. Exterior approaches and the main viewing space usually stay open outside service times, and the statue remains one of Hampi's most recognizable carved images from the imperial period.

10 AdmissionFree

Hampi Bazaar

A long stone-paved street extends east from Virupaksha Temple through Hampi Bazaar, once lined with pillared shops and ceremonial structures serving the sacred center. Modern settlement changed after archaeological clearances, while a monolithic Nandi remains in a pavilion near the far end. The surviving street records trade, pilgrimage and contested heritage management.

11 Admission₹40

Lotus Mahal

Within Hampi's Zenana Enclosure, Lotus Mahal uses cusped arches, projecting balconies, domed forms, and open bays to create a cool two-storey pavilion. Vijayanagara stone construction combines with forms often described as Indo-Islamic. Its exact function remains uncertain, possibly involving courtly leisure or administration, so claims.

15.3203° N / 76.4713° E

12 Admission₹600

Elephant Stables

The Elephant Stables at Hampi are a long sixteenth-century Vijayanagara range of eleven domed chambers built to house royal elephants. Arched bays face a lawn near the Zenana enclosure and Lotus Mahal. The Indo-Islamic roof line marks the ceremonial quarter of the ruined capital.

15.3211° N / 76.4727° E

13 AdmissionFree

Hazara Raama Temple

Dedicated to Rama, this fifteenth-century temple near the Lotus Mahal zone covers walls with dense friezes of animals, deities, and courtly scenes. Reliefs also recall Krishna narratives within the same carved program. Exterior courts and principal interior spaces usually remain viewable outside service times, and the small compound sits among the royal-center ruins rather than the riverside temple belt.

15.3178° N / 76.4688° E

14 Admission₹40

Royal Palace

In Hampi's royal enclosure near the Lotus Mahal, palace platforms and foundation lines mark where Vijayanagara rulers kept courts and residences. Surviving walls, courtyards, and tower bases form the walking route when the grounds are open. The stone outlines sit among audience halls and enclosure walls rather than a standing Tudor mansion, reading as an archaeological palace quarter.

15 AdmissionFree

Underground Shiva Temple

The Underground Shiva Temple in Hampi is a fascinating 14th-century Hindu temple dedicated to Lord Shiva. Situated below ground level, the core areas of the temple are typically submerged, making access to the inner parts restricted. Visitors can experience the marvel of wide steps leading to the interior, where a massive hall with cubical pillars supporting the roof awaits.

15.3179° N / 76.4640° E

16 AdmissionFree

Sanapur Lake

North of the main Hampi ruins, Sanapur Lake spreads a quiet reservoir shore used for walking paths and small boat launches. District roads reach the water from surrounding villages, and travellers often pair the stop with nearby parks along the same route. The shoreline stays calmer than the boulder temples, with open water reflecting the low hills that rim the basin.
