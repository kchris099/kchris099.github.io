Amritsar

Guru Ram Das founded Amritsar in 1577 around the pool that became the Golden Temple complex. Mughal and later British conflict, including the 1919 Jallianwala Bagh massacre, scarred the sacred city. For Sikhs worldwide it remains the primary pilgrimage centre of the faith. The Akal Takht within the complex asserts Sikh temporal authority beside the Harmandir Sahib.

Sri Harmandir Sahib

Sri Harmandir Sahib, also known as the Golden Temple, is a central place of worship for Sikhs worldwide. The temple is made of white marble covered in gold leaf and is surrounded by the Amritsar Sarovar, with a causeway called the Guru's Bridge leading to its entrance. Inside, devotees can find the Guru Granth Sahib, the holy scripture of Sikhism.

Gurudwara Ber Baba Budha Sahib

Gurudwara Ber Baba Budha Sahib centers on traditions associated with Baba Buddha, an early Sikh figure who served successive Gurus. A revered ber tree anchors the pilgrimage complex. The prayer hall and sarovar support worship throughout the year. Langar service and festival gatherings sustain the active religious community on the outskirts of Amritsar.

Sri Akal Takht Sahib

Akal Takht Sahib, founded in 1606 beside the Harmandir Sahib in Amritsar, is one of the five seats of temporal authority in Sikhism. The building faces the holy tank and holds scriptures used in Sikh jurisprudence and assembly. Together with the Golden Temple complex it anchors the religious and political heart of the faith.

Gobindgarh Fort

Gobindgarh Fort, a historic fortress dating back to the 17th century, is a significant part of Amritsar's heritage. Spanning across 43 acres, this fort has an immersive experience through infotainment shows such as light and sound displays, a museum showing its history, and exhibitions. The fort also hosts cultural performances including bhangra and gatka shows.

31.6269° N / 74.8601° E

05 AdmissionFree

Golden Gate (Sri Amritsar City Gateway)

The Golden Gate, or Sri Amritsar City Gateway, marks a ceremonial entrance on approaches into Amritsar with tall arched portals and decorative cladding. The form borrows palace-gate scale to signal arrival in the city rather than to fortify a wall. Traffic and welcome signage concentrate around the span.

06 AdmissionFree

Jallianwala Bagh

A walled garden holds the memorial to the 13 April 1919 massacre. Narrow entrances, bullet-marked walls, and a central monument keep attention on that history. The site sits on the Heritage Mile near the Golden Temple and the Partition Museum for linked visits.

31.6206° N / 74.8803° E

07 AdmissionFree

Martyrs Well

Martyrs' Well stands inside Jallianwala Bagh as a direct memorial to people who jumped into it while British troops fired on the enclosed crowd on 13 April 1919. A protected structure and interpretive panels mark the site within the larger memorial garden. Its meaning depends on the massacre's violence and colonial repression, not on emotional instructions or dramatic embellishment.

31.6207° N / 74.8809° E

Sadda Pind Amritsar

Sadda Pind on Amritsar's outskirts recreates a Punjabi village streetscape with mud-plaster houses, craft stalls, and food courts. Artisans demonstrate pottery and phulkari work while folk shows run on a small stage. The park packages rural culture as a half-day visit rather than a living village.

Gurudwara Chheharta Sahib

Gurudwara Chheharta Sahib stands about eight kilometers from Amritsar in Chheharta village. Tradition links its well to Guru Arjan Dev, dug to mark the birth of Guru Hargobind. The gurdwara complex and sarovar draw pilgrims who combine the stop with other Sikh sites around the city.

Ahuja Lassi

Long associated with Amritsar's old commercial streets, Ahuja Lassi is a compact refreshment shop centered on thick Punjabi lassi. Sweet and salted yogurt drinks are prepared in traditional cups and served through a fast, informal counter system. The business offers a focused example of a local food custom within the city's busy market area. The narrow menu keeps attention on the drink itself.

I.D.H Market

I.D.H. Market forms a dense wholesale and retail district near Amritsar's bus stand. Clothing, stationery and cosmetics occupy many shops, while household items, food and small electronics broaden the trade. Loading vehicles and narrow passages create frequent congestion across a commercial zone spread over several lanes and without a single formal entrance.
