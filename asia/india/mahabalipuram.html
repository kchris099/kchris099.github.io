Mahabalipuram

Pallava kings in the seventh and eighth centuries cut rathas, the Shore Temple, and the Arjuna's Penance relief into coastal granite while Mamallapuram traded toward Southeast Asia. UNESCO conservation now steadies monuments worn by wind and surf. Living stone-carving workshops in the modern town continue a craft tied to that courtly port. Chinese pilgrim accounts and Southeast Asian inscriptions corroborate the port's early reach.

Five Rathas (Pancha Rathas)

The Five Rathas, or Pancha Rathas, are monolithic granite temple carts at Mahabalipuram, each carved from a single boulder and named for the Pandava brothers and Draupadi. Pallava sculptors left unfinished roofs and shrine forms standing in open sand. The group reads as an outdoor workshop of temple types more than a single enclosed sanctuary.

02 AdmissionFree

Tiger Cave

Tiger Cave at Saluvankuppam, about five kilometers from Mahabalipuram, is a Pallava rock-cut shrine whose entrance is framed by carved tiger heads. An old Shiva shrine and open shore ground share the coastal site. Families use the shade and carvings as a short stop combined with beach air.

12.6559° N / 80.2090° E

03 Admission₹40

Group of Monuments at Mahabalipuram

Mahabalipuram's group brings together seventh- and eighth-century Pallava monuments spread across the coastal town. Shore temples, rock-cut caves, monolithic rathas, structural shrines, inscriptions and the great open-air relief occupy separate clusters linked by streets and paths. Sea exposure, weathering, active worship and later settlement all shape an archaeological landscape that cannot be reduced to one enclosure.

12.6165° N / 80.1988° E

04 Admission₹600

Mahabalipuram Shore Temple

Twin shrines face the Coromandel surf among rathas, mandapas, and open-air rock reliefs that make up the wider monument group. The Shore Temple's weathered towers meet spray and wind at the water's edge. Nineteenth-century British planning shaped the modern town nearby, while the temple itself remains the coastal focus of the Pallava stone ensemble.

12.6165° N / 80.1993° E

05 AdmissionFree

Mahabalipuram Beach

Mahabalipuram Beach, located south of the city of Mahabalipuram, offers a setting with its white sand and green landscape. Travellers can explore an old ruined Dutch fort and cemetery nearby, showing fine stone work and tombstones dating back to the arrival of European powers in India. The beach is known for pure white sand and offers opportunities for swimming, surfing, and enjoying views of the 8th-century Shore Temple.

06 AdmissionFree

Krishna's Butter Ball

Krishna's Butter Ball is a giant natural granite boulder at Mahabalipuram balanced on a sloping rock face. The round mass appears ready to roll yet stays fixed above the path. Travellers gather at its base for photographs between visits to nearby shore temples and caves.

12.6191° N / 80.1925° E

07 AdmissionFree

Varaha Cave

Varaha Cave Temple at Mahabalipuram is a seventh-century Pallava rock-cut shrine with life-sized reliefs of Varaha, Gaja Lakshmi, and other Hindu themes. Columns and a shallow mandapa are cut directly from the cliff. Soft light on the carved panels rewards a slow look inside the small hall.

12.6181° N / 80.1923° E

08 AdmissionFree

Descent of the Ganga River and Arjuna's Penance

Arjuna's Penance, also read as the Descent of the Ganga, is a vast Pallava rock relief at Mahabalipuram covering a cleft cliff face. Gods, animals, and ascetics crowd the carved surface around a natural fissure suggesting falling water. The open-air sculpture sits among the town's other shore monuments.

12.6178° N / 80.1927° E

09 AdmissionFree

Krishna Mandapam

Krishna Mandapam at Mahabalipuram is a rock-cut cave temple with reliefs of Krishna lifting Govardhana and related pastoral scenes. Pallava carvers cut the shallow hall from living rock near the main monument cluster. The mandapa rewards close viewing of the narrative panels more than a long climb.

12.6174° N / 80.1926° E

10 AdmissionFree

Arulmigu Sri Sthala Sayana Perumal Temple

Located in Mahabalipuram, the Arulmigu Sri Sthala Sayana Perumal Temple is an ancient and revered shrine dedicated to Lord Vishnu. Constructed in the Dravidian style of architecture, it is known for its painted stone statues and shore location. Arulmigu Sri Sthala Sayana Perumal Temple remains an active place of worship and a documented historic building in Mahabalipuram, India.
