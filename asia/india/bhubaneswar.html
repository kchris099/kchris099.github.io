Bhubaneswar

Bhubaneswar stands near the ruins of Sisupalgarh. Dhauli, near Bhubaneswar was the site of the Kalinga War (c. 262-261 BCE), in which the Mauryan emperor Ashoka invaded and annexed Kalinga. A complete edicts of the Mauryan Emperor, Ashoka, dating from between 272 and 236 BCE, remains carved in rock, 8 kilometres (5.0 mi) to the southwest of the modern city. After the decline of the Mauryan empire, the area came under the rule of Mahameghavahana dynasty, whose most well-known ruler is Kharavela. His Hathigumpha inscription is located at the Udayagiri and Khandagiri caves near Bhubaneswar.

Nandankanan Zoological Park

Nandankanan combines a zoological park and botanical landscape beside Kanjia Lake on the edge of Bhubaneswar. Established in 1960 within forest habitat, it supports animal care, breeding, education, safari zones, gardens and research. Species on display and transport services change, while heat, large distances and managed enclosures make it different from the romanticized idea of animals roaming an untouched forest.

Lingaraj Temple

Lingaraj Temple dominates Bhubaneswar's old sacred quarter with a high curving tower and a dense complex of smaller shrines. Built mainly in the eleventh century, its form shows mature Kalinga architecture and supports worship of Harihara, combining Shiva and Vishnu traditions.

Ananta Basudev Temple

Ananta Vasudeva Temple stands near Bindu Sagar as Bhubaneswar's principal medieval Vaishnava shrine. Traditionally dated to the thirteenth century and linked with Queen Chandrika, it follows Kalinga temple forms while housing Krishna, Balarama and Subhadra. A large ritual kitchen prepares offerings for worshippers, tying architecture to continuing food, festival and pilgrimage practices.

Udayagiri and Khandagiri Caves

Udayagiri and Khandagiri contain rock-cut caves created for Jain ascetics around an early century BC under the Mahameghavahana rulers. Inscriptions, reliefs, cells, water channels and multi-level facades occupy two low hills, with the Hathigumpha inscription providing key evidence for King Kharavela. Stairs, exposed rock and later religious use complicate the idea of a single preserved monastery.

Rani Gumpha

Within the Udayagiri cave complex, this rock-cut residence of historical and religious note lost its lower-storey pillared hall to collapse. Carved chambers still open along the hillside. Approaches follow the archaeological paths through the twin hills.

Dhauli Shanti Stupa

Dhauli Shanti Stupa, the Peace Pagoda above the Daya River near Bhubaneswar, was built in 1972 by Japanese and Indian Buddhist groups. White domes and Buddha figures overlook the plain linked to Ashoka's remorse after the Kalinga War. Nearby rock edicts tie the modern stupa to that ancient turning point.

ASHOKAN ROCK EDICT

Inscriptions at Udayagiri and Khandagiri carry Ashokan texts with English translations posted for readers beside the rock faces. The carved surfaces sit in an open archaeological setting. The edicts document early historic rule on the same hills as the caves.

Rajarani Temple

Warm-toned sandstone gives Rajarani Temple its modern name and shapes this eleventh-century Kalinga monument in Bhubaneswar. A curving tower rises above clustered miniature spires, guardians, musicians and sculpted couples carved across the exterior. The temple no longer centers on an active cult image, allowing its architecture and stonework to define the visit. Exterior sculpture carries most of the temple's detail.

Mukteswara Temple

A tenth-century Shiva temple is noted for its carved torana arch and compact Kalinga spire. Fine sandstone reliefs cover the sanctuary walls with early Odishan ornament. The small compound sits among other early temples near Rajarani in the old temple belt.

Chausathi Yogini Temple

The 64 Yogini Temple, also known as Chausath Yogini Temple, is a unique circular temple dating back to the 9th century. Situated near the Bhargavi River in Hirapur, it is one of the four Chausath Yogini Temples in the country. Built during the Kalachuri kingdom, this historical site resembles the architecture of Khajuraho.

Bhaskareswara Temple

An unusually tall stepped base around a large lingam distinguishes Bhaskareswara Temple from other compact medieval Shiva shrines in Bhubaneswar. Built during the later phase of Kalinga temple development, its circular visual emphasis and raised approach have encouraged comparisons with earlier Buddhist forms, though that interpretation is debated. Active worship continues within a monument whose proportions differ sharply from nearby temple towers.

20.2445° N / 85.8528° E

17 AdmissionFree

Brahmeswara Temple

Brahmeswara Temple was completed around AD 1060 during Somavamsi rule and is traditionally linked with Queen Kolavati. A central Shiva shrine and four subsidiary temples create a panchayatana plan. Carved doorways, musicians, guardians and exterior figures show the developed stonework of Bhubaneswar's medieval temple tradition within a compact walled compound.
