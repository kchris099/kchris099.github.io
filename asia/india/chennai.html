Chennai

Chennai, formerly Madras, was established in 1639 when the British East India Company built Fort St. George, one of the first major English settlements in India. It grew into a major naval base and administrative center for the Madras Presidency. The city played an important role in the Carnatic Wars and the development of the Indian railway system. Today, Chennai is a major cultural and economic hub, known for its Dravidian architecture, classical music traditions, and expansive Marina Beach.

Dr.M.G.R. and Dr.J.Jayalalitha Memorial

The MGR and Jayalalithaa Memorial complex occupies part of Chennai's Marina Beach, marking the burial places of two Tamil Nadu chief ministers and film-linked political leaders. Monumental arches, landscaped paths, symbolic forms, security areas, and steady public visitation reflect the visual culture of Dravidian politics. Ceremonies and crowd controls can alter access, especially on anniversaries and election-related occasions.

Perarignar Anna Memorial

N. Anna Memorial commemorates C. Annadurai, an early chief minister of Tamil Nadu from the Dravidian movement, at his burial place on Marina Beach. A formal approach, memorial platform, inscriptions, and landscaped grounds create a civic site used for remembrance and political homage. Its design and history are distinct from the neighboring MGR and Jayalalithaa complex, despite their shared waterfront setting.

Santhome Cathedral Basilica

Santhome Cathedral Basilica stands over the traditional burial place of the apostle Thomas in Chennai's Mylapore district. Portuguese clergy established an earlier church here, while the present white neo-Gothic building was largely rebuilt under British rule in the late nineteenth century. A high spire, stained glass, nave, crypt, and active Catholic worship link local Christian tradition with later colonial architecture.

Nanganallur Anjaneyar Temple

Nanganallur Anjaneyar Temple centers on a tall monolithic granite image of Hanuman within a modern Chennai shrine complex. Active devotion, especially during Hanuman celebrations, gives the site its religious importance.

St Thomas Mount National Shrine, or Parangimalai, crowns a hill south of Chennai with a 1523 Portuguese-era church tied to the apostle Thomas tradition. Steps and a road climb to the shrine and a wide view toward the airport and city. Pilgrims and day visitors share the terrace around the church.

Arulmigu Marundeeswarar Temple

Marundeeswarar Temple at Thiruvanmiyur is a long-lived Shiva complex with Chola-period inscriptions, later towers, pillared halls, and a broad ritual courtyard. Tradition associates Shiva here with teaching medicinal knowledge to the sage Agastya, giving the deity the title Lord of Medicines. The belief remains part of worship, while architectural evidence records repeated expansion.

Besant Nagar Beach

Besant Nagar Beach extends along Chennai's southern waterfront with a broad sandy shore and a promenade used for walking, food stalls and evening gatherings. The Karl Schmidt Memorial stands near the water, while churches and temples occupy surrounding streets. Seasonal currents, surf conditions and erosion shape use of the beach. The shoreline and neighborhood give the area its lasting public character.

Mahatma Gandhi Statue

On Chennai's Marina Beach, a bronze Mahatma Gandhi strides forward with a staff in hand. Sculptor D. P. Roy Choudhury created the work, installed in the late 1950s as part of the waterfront's sequence of civic monuments. The figure's energetic pose differs from more static memorial portraits and places the independence leader within a broad public promenade used for ceremonies and daily recreation.

Vivekananda House

A circular pink building on Marina Beach Road preserves the former Ice House where imported ice was stored during the nineteenth century. Swami Vivekananda stayed here in 1897, and the renamed Vivekananda House now uses photographs, rooms, multimedia, and devotional displays to interpret his life and teaching. Its commercial origins and later spiritual association are both essential to the site's history.

Kannagi Statue

Kannagi stands on Chennai's Marina promenade holding an anklet, the object central to her demand for justice in the Tamil epic Silappatikaram. The modern bronze statue gives public form to a literary heroine associated with fidelity, anger, and moral authority; removal and reinstatement during political disputes made the monument part of contemporary Tamil identity as well as ancient narrative memory.

Chennai Snake Park

Chennai Snake Park began as a reptile conservation and public-education facility beside Guindy National Park. Enclosures contain snakes, crocodilians, turtles and other reptiles, supported by identification panels and demonstrations that have changed with animal-welfare standards. Species lists and programs vary, while its institutional purpose is better described through conservation, rescue awareness and interpretation than through family-entertainment language.

Thousand Lights Shia Mosque

Thousand Lights Mosque developed in Chennai's Royapettah district from an early nineteenth-century Shia assembly complex. Its name is traditionally linked with the many oil lamps used to illuminate large gatherings. Later rebuilding added domes, minarets, prayer halls, and spaces for Muharram observances. The mosque is an active religious center, with separate arrangements for worship and casual entry.

Victoria Public Hall

Victoria Public Hall was completed in the late nineteenth century beside Chennai Central as a civic venue commemorating Queen Victoria's jubilee. Robert Chisholm's red-brick design combines arcades, a clock tower, and Indo-Saracenic details suited to meetings, theater, and public ceremonies.
