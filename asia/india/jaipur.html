Jaipur

Jaipur was planned in 1727 by Maharaja Sawai Jai Singh II as a fortified Rajput capital with a grid of bazaars and palaces painted pink for a later royal visit. Hawa Mahal, Jantar Mantar, and Amber Fort mark astronomy, court ritual, and desert trade in Rajasthan's principal city. City Palace courtyards and Nahargarh ridge forts still frame Rajput power above the Aravalli hills.

Shri Khole Ke Hanuman Ji Temple, Jaipur

Shree Khole Ke Hanuman Ji ka Mandir, located in Laxman Dungri, Jaipur, is a historic temple dedicated to Lord Hanuman. It is considered one of the famous temples in Jaipur and is a must-visit spot for tourists. The temple's inner sanctum can accommodate up to 500 devotees and offers panoramic views of the green surroundings from its location atop a hillock on the Delhi bypass.

Jal Mahal

Sawai Madho Singh's eighteenth-century sandstone pavilion rises from Man Sagar Lake, its lower stories meeting the water while upper galleries catch lake breezes. Built as a summer retreat for the court, the palace is usually viewed from the shore rather than entered room by room. Red walls reflect in the lake, fixing the Water Palace as a fixed point on Jaipur's northern approach.

Birla Mandir, Jaipur

Birla Mandir in Jaipur is a modern white marble temple to Lakshmi-Narayan with three domes marking different paths of Hindu practice. Idols carved from single stone blocks stand in the sanctums. The hilltop site overlooks the city while remaining an active family place of worship.

Moti Doongri Fort

Moti Doongri Fort crowns a small Jaipur hill above the popular Ganesh temple, but the hilltop residence is generally not open as a public monument. The present castle-like building was remodeled in a European style for the former royal family. Its silhouette and private status is distinct from the active temple below, which has separate history, access, and religious significance.

Moti Dungri Ganesh Temple occupies a hillock below Moti Dungri fort in Jaipur, drawing devotees to a brightly painted Ganesh shrine. Hawa Mahal

Maharaja Sawai Pratap Singh completed the five-story Palace of Winds in 1799, facing the street with a screen of small jharokhas that pull cool air through the chambers. The pink facade reads as a honeycomb from the bazaar below. Royal women once watched processions through those openings without appearing in public, and the same lattice still defines the building's street presence.

The City Palace

Jaipur's City Palace grew from the eighteenth-century court of Sawai Jai Singh II into a large complex still connected with the former royal family. Gates, courtyards, audience halls, armories, textiles, paintings; carriages and decorated rooms combine Mughal and Rajput planning.

Tripolia Gate

Tripolia Gate is an eighteenth-century triple-arched entrance on the south side of Jaipur's City Palace, giving its name to Tripolia Bazaar. The stacked pols form a single ceremonial structure rather than separate towers. Night lighting picks out the sandstone facade above the market street.

Isarlat Sargasooli

Also called Sargasuli, this eighteenth-century minaret rises above busy Jaipur markets with stairs leading to high platforms. From the top, views take in Nahargarh Fort, City Palace, Jantar Mantar, Hawa Mahal, and the old Vidhan Sabha across the Pink City grid. Street commerce presses the base while the tower itself stays a vertical pause in the commercial lanes.

Shri Radha Gopinath Ji Temple, Jaipur

Tradition holds that the temple's Krishna idol was crafted by Vajranabha, Krishna's grandson, and moved from Vrindavan during Mughal-era upheavals. The deity has occupied this Jaipur seat since the eighteenth century. Exterior courts and main halls usually remain viewable outside service times, and the shrine sits among the Hawa Mahal district's older religious addresses.

Sisodia Rani ka Bagh

Sawai Jai Singh II created Sisodia Rani ka Bagh in the eighteenth century for a queen from the Sisodia house of Mewar. Terraced gardens, channels, fountains, painted pavilions, and small temples climb a narrow valley east of Jaipur. Restoration and water supply affect the appearance of its cascades, while courtly retreat and dynastic marriage provide firmer context than a generic monument to romantic love.

Also called Amer Fort, the hill palace mixes Hindu and Muslim design in courtyards built from pale yellow, rose, and white stone that read amber from a distance. Elephant approaches and steep ramps climb toward audience halls and mirrored chambers. The fort's colored masonry and layered courts sit above the lake approach on Jaipur's northeastern ridge.

Amer Fort

Amer Fort rises above Maota Lake outside Jaipur, begun in the late sixteenth century under Raja Man Singh. Rajput and Mughal courts share the palace sequence, from Diwan-i-Am to the mirrored Sheesh Mahal. Steep paths and elephant routes climb to courtyards overlooking the Aravalli ridge.

Panna Meena ka Kund

Near Amer Fort, a geometric stepwell catches rainwater with symmetrical stairs on every side. After monsoon rains the water mirrors the lattice of steps, and photographers gather along the upper rim. The well sits just off the fort approach roads, compact enough that the stair geometry fills the entire view from ground level.

Jaigarh Fort

Jaigarh Fort extends along the Aravalli ridge above Amber as an eighteenth-century military complex built under Sawai Jai Singh II. Ramparts, watchtowers, water reservoirs, workshops, palaces and temples document defense and state production. The large Jaivana cannon remains within the fort, where its casting history and setting illustrate the technical resources of the Jaipur court.

Nahargarh Fort

Nahargarh Fort, built in 1734 above Jaipur, is part of a trio of forts along with Jaigarh and Amer. It was constructed to protect the city and has a rich military history. The fort also houses a wax museum featuring sports stars, freedom fighters, and Bollywood celebrities. Visitors can began on a cycling expedition to explore the fort in a unique way while enjoying views of Jaipur and Man Sagar Lake.

Gaitor Ki Chhatriyan

Gaitor Ki Chhatriyan is the royal cremation ground of Jaipur's Kachwaha rulers, a valley of marble and sandstone cenotaphs with carved domes. Each chhatri marks a maharaja with pillared pavilions open to the air. The cluster lies north of the city toward the hills, quieter than the palace museums.
