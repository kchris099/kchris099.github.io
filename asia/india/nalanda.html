Nalanda

= Archaeological excavations at sites near Nalanda, such as the Juafardih site about three kilometres away, have yielded black ware and other items. These have been carbon dated to about 1200 BCE. This suggests that the region around Nalanda in Magadha had a human settlement centuries before the birth of the Mahavira and the Buddha. Early Buddhist texts state that Buddha visited a town near Rajagriha (modern Rajgir - the capital of Magadha) called Nalanda on his peregrinations. He delivered lectures in a nearby mango grove named Pavarika and one of his two chief disciples, Shariputra, was born in the area and later attained nirvana there. These Buddhist texts were written down centuries after the death of the Buddha, are not consistent in either the name or the relative locations.

Viswa Shanti Stupa Rajgir

Viswa Shanti Stupa Rajgir is a religious building in Nalanda, India. Perched atop the Gridhakuta Hill at an altitude of 400m, Viswa Shanti Stupa Rajgir is a modern white stupa built by the Japanese as a symbol of world peace. Accessible by cable cars, this site also includes a temple for prayers dedicated to universal peace. The exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside service times.

Gridhrakuta, also known as Vulture Peak or Vultures Peak, is a significant pilgrimage site for Buddhists located in the Nalanda district of Bihar. It is believed to be ancient sites where Buddha preached his Lotus Sermon and used to meditate. The peak resembles the shape of a vulture's head due to natural stone formations.

03 AdmissionFree

Swarn Bhandar

Swarn Bhandar is a visitor site in Nalanda, India. Carved from a single massive rock during the 3rd or 4th century CE by Jain sage Vairadeva, these two artificial caves served as meditation spaces and showcase detailed carvings and inscriptions that reflect their rich heritage. The main structures and grounds are the principal points of interest.

04 AdmissionFree

Saptaparni Cave

Saptaparni Cave, also known as Saptaparni Guha or Sattapanni Guha, is a significant historical and spiritual site located in Rajgir, Bihar. It is believed to be the place where the first Buddhist council was held after Lord Buddha's Parinirvana. The cave gets its name from the seven Saptaparni trees that once shaded its entrance.
