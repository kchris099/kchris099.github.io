Kolkata

Developed by the British East India Company from three riverside villages in 1690, Kolkata served as the capital of British India until 1911. Its grand colonial architecture and profound literary heritage cement its legacy as the historical vanguard of Indian modernization. Visitors today can clearly trace the detailed layers of this unique heritage throughout the preserved streets and monuments.

Howrah Bridge

The Howrah Bridge, also known as Rabindra Setu, is a busy cantilever road bridge that spans the Hooghly River, connecting Kolkata and Howrah in Bengal. Built by the British and opened in 1943, it is one of the busiest bridges in India. Despite its age, it is referred to as the 'New Howrah Bridge' and is an long running symbol of Kolkata.

Dakshineswar Kali Temple

Rani Rashmoni founded Dakshineswar Kali Temple in 1855 on the eastern bank of the Hooghly. The nine-spired shrine to Bhavatarini Kali faces a large courtyard lined with twelve Shiva temples, a Radha-Krishna shrine, bathing ghats, and rooms associated with Ramakrishna. Heavy pilgrimage and daily ritual define the complex, whose river setting also connects with Belur Math by ferry.

James Princep Monument

James Prinsep Monument, also known as Prinsep Ghat, is a 1841 structure located on the banks of River Hooghly. The monument has Greek and Gothic architectural details and is set in a parklike environment with views of the nearby bridge. Visitors, including architecture enthusiasts and locals alike, are drawn to its regal charm. It's an ideal spot for leisurely activities such as watching the sunrise or sunset against the backdrop of the Howrah Bridge.

Vijaydurg Fort, Kolkata

Vijaydurg Fort occupies a rocky headland around a sheltered creek, where walls and bastions enclose a maritime stronghold developed under several rulers. The Maratha navy expanded its defenses and used the sea approaches strategically. Gateways, reservoirs and ruined structures record military administration and coastal warfare. The relationship between fortifications, anchorage and open water explains the site's former naval importance.

Belur Math on the Hooghly's west bank is the headquarters of the Ramakrishna Math and Mission. The 1938 Ramakrishna Mandir blends temple motifs from several faiths among gardens and riverside paths. Quiet grounds and monastic halls face the river toward Kolkata.

Zoological Garden Alipore

Alipore Zoological Gardens opened to the public in the 1870s and became one of India's earliest formally organized zoos. Historic enclosures, lakes, trees, bird areas, reptiles and modernized habitats occupy a compact Kolkata site. Its history includes the long-lived Aldabra tortoise Adwaita.

Rabindra Sarobar is a large artificial lake and public landscape in south Kolkata, created from excavated land and later named for Rabindranath Tagore. Nakhoda Masjid

Kolkata's Birla Mandir was built over several decades and opened in the 1990s as a modern Hindu temple dedicated chiefly to Radha and Krishna. Calcutta High Court

Calcutta High Court began operating in 1862 and occupies a Gothic Revival building designed by Walter Granville. Pointed arches, towers, tracery, and a long red facade draw inspiration from European civic architecture, including the medieval cloth hall at Ypres. The court still handles active legal proceedings, so security and institutional business govern access.

St. John's Church

St. John's Church was completed in 1787 as an early Anglican parish church for British Calcutta and later functioned as the city's cathedral before St. Paul's opened. A columned neoclassical building, memorial tablets, paintings, and a surrounding burial ground preserve colonial records, including monuments linked with the Black Hole narrative and East India Company officials. Those memorials require critical historical context.

Lok Bhavan, Kolkata

A domed central block, curved wings, ceremonial gates, and lawns follow a Palladian plan adapted to Bengal. Known historically as Government House and Raj Bhavan, Kolkata's gubernatorial residence was completed in the early nineteenth century for the British governor-general, and renaming to Lok Bhavan reflects current political usage, while the functioning official residence is normally viewed from outside.

Writers' Buildings

Writers' Building, originally constructed in 1777 as the headquarters of the British East India Company, now is the offices for the West Bengal state government. This Greco-Roman landmark has a scandalous history, including its construction and subsequent usage by Governor-General Marquess Wellesley. Situated at the northern end of Dalhousie Square, it was initially used to house junior writers of the East India Company, hence its name.

Jorasanko Thakurbari preserves the Tagore family's Kolkata home and the birthplace of Rabindranath Tagore. Courtyards and domestic rooms hold photographs, manuscripts and paintings connected with the family. Exhibits also address reformers, musicians and artists within their wider circle, placing the house inside the cultural networks of the Bengal Renaissance.

Acharya Jagadish Chandra Bose Indian Botanic Garden

Founded in the late eighteenth century at Shibpur, the Indian Botanic Garden supported colonial plant collection, economic botany and scientific exchange. The Great Banyan, palms, orchids, ponds, avenues and riverside habitats occupy extensive grounds across the Hooghly from Kolkata; species counts change with taxonomy and loss, while experiments with tea and other crops reveal the garden's commercial imperial history.
