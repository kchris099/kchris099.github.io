Fatehpur Sikri

Archaeological evidence points to settlement of the region since the Painted Grey Ware period. According to historian Syed Ali Nadeem Rezavi, the region flourished under Sunga rule and then under Sikarwar Rajputs, who built a fortress when they controlled the area from 7th to 16th century, until the Battle of Khanwa (1527). The area later came under the rule of the Delhi Sultanate and many mosques were built at the place which grew in size during the period of the Khalji dynasty. = At the site of Birchhabili Tila, located on the southeast bank of what used to be a large lake, excavation indicated the presence of a settlement as far back as the 6th century CE. The oldest layer, dating to the 6th through 9th centuries, contained various remains of buildings, including brick walls, building foundations, one dressed sandstone pillar, and a man-made pit also made of sandstone.

Fatehpur Sikri Fort

Fatehpur Sikri is a fortified Mughal city developed by Akbar in the late sixteenth century. Red-sandstone palaces, audience halls, courtyards, mosques, service areas and water structures form the former imperial capital. The Jama Masjid and Buland Darwaza occupy a religious precinct beside the palace complex, whose brief political life remains central to its history.

Panch Mahal

Five stacked colonnades taper toward an open pavilion on the western side of Akbar's Fatehpur Sikri complex. Courtiers used the airy tower for entertainment, catching breezes through successive perforated floors like a Persian wind tower rather than a solid Rajasthan palace block. The structure sits in an open plaza among palace courts, its silhouette readable from far across the sandstone platform.

Diwan-e-Khas

Diwan-i-Khas at Fatehpur Sikri is Akbar's private audience hall, a compact red sandstone pavilion sometimes called the Jewel House. A central carved column once supported a throne platform linked by bridges to corner galleries. Courtiers gathered here for confidential counsel apart from the public Diwan-i-Am.

27.0978° N / 77.6661° E

Jodha Bai's Palace

Within Fatehpur Sikri Fort, a residential palace block gathers courtyards, screened galleries, and enclosing walls linked to the imperial women's quarters. Routes through the compound pass walls and towers when the grounds are open, and the palace sits near Salim Chishti's tomb and Buland Darwaza on the same ridge. Red sandstone rooms open onto internal courts rather than street fronts.

Buland Darwaza Fatehpur Sikri

Buland Darwaza forms the monumental southern entrance to Fatehpur Sikri's Jama Masjid. Akbar commissioned the high sandstone gateway around the turn of the seventeenth century to commemorate campaigns in Gujarat. Broad steps, geometric inlay, calligraphy, domed kiosks, and a vast central arch create a ceremonial threshold; the nearby tomb of Salim Chishti is a separate marble shrine within the mosque court.

27.0942° N / 77.6630° E

06 AdmissionFree

Jama Masjid

Emperor Akbar commissioned this Friday mosque in the 1570s, anchoring Fatehpur Sikri with a vast courtyard that can hold thousands at prayer. Domes and arcades frame the open sahn, and the Buland Darwaza approach marks the mosque's monumental southern entry. Worship still fills the courts on Fridays, while quieter hours show the red sandstone detailing of early Mughal court architecture.

27.0948° N / 77.6622° E
