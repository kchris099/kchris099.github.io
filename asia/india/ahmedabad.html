Ahmedabad

Ahmedabad was founded in 1411 on the Sabarmati River as the Gujarat Sultanate's capital. Pols, stepwells, and mosques with carved stone screens illustrate Indo-Islamic merchant architecture, while Gandhi's ashram links the city to India's independence movement and modern textile industry. Sabarmati riverfront walks and pol neighbourhoods preserve sultanate-era merchant lanes beside modern Gujarat industry.

Teen Darwaja

Teen Darwaza has framed a principal approach between Bhadra Fort and old Ahmedabad since the early fifteenth century. Three stone arches once carried royal processions and now open onto a dense market street of shops, carts, signs, and traffic; carving and upper chambers survive above the everyday commerce, illustrating how Sultanate architecture remains embedded in a heavily used urban route.

Jama Masjid (Sunni)

Ahmad Shah I commissioned Ahmedabad's Jama Masjid in 1424 near the royal precinct. A broad courtyard leads to a yellow-sandstone prayer hall supported by many columns, with carved screens, domes, and a central ablution tank. Hindu and Jain craft traditions influenced its stonework, while daily prayer and Friday congregations keep the mosque active.

Rani no Hajiro

Rani no Hajiro encloses the tombs of queens and female relatives from Ahmedabad's Gujarat Sultanate. Stone screens, carved pillars, raised cenotaphs, and a quiet inner court contrast with the clothing and jewelry markets crowding the surrounding lanes near Manek Chowk.

Sarkhej Roza

Sarkhej Roza, completed around 1451, is a mosque and tomb complex on Ahmedabad's western fringe where Gujarat Sultanate builders mixed Indo-Islamic forms. Stone pavilions, a large tank, and royal tombs share a quiet courtyard away from the old city markets. The complex reads as a suburban royal retreat rather than a downtown Friday mosque.

Jhulta Minar, Bibiji Masjid

Bibiji Masjid's surviving gateway and paired minarets stand in Gomtipur, east of central Ahmedabad. Stone carving covers the slender towers, which are commonly grouped among the city's Jhulta Minar, or Shaking Minarets, because vibration in one was reported to affect the other. The ruined mosque preserves a distinctive fragment of Gujarat Sultanate architecture, while access to the upper levels is restricted.

Kamla Nehru Zoological Garden

Kamla Nehru Zoological Garden, also called Kankaria Zoo, opened in 1951 beside Kankaria Lake in Ahmedabad. The roughly 117-acre grounds hold mammals, more than two thousand birds, and about 140 reptiles in conventional zoo enclosures. Paths and shade trees structure family circuits around the lake edge.

Kankaria Lake

Sultan Qutb-ud-Din created Kankaria Lake in the fifteenth century as a large polygonal reservoir south-east of old Ahmedabad, and modern recreation has transformed the historic tank. A circular promenade now links gardens, food stalls, a zoo, children's facilities, boating, and event spaces around the water.

Bai Harir Sultani Vav

Dai Harir, an influential woman at the court of Sultan Mahmud Begada, commissioned the stepwell now known as Bai Harir or Dada Harir Vav around the end of the fifteenth century. Flights of stairs descend through carved stone galleries, columns and landings toward the water level. Changing light, geometric ornament and the nearby mosque and tomb reveal a practical water structure tied to patronage and piety.

Hutheesing Jain Temple

Completed in 1848, Hutheesing Jain Temple was commissioned by a wealthy merchant family and finished under the direction of his widow, Harkunvar. White marble, a domed courtyard, carved columns, subsidiary shrines and a sanctuary dedicated to Dharmanatha express nineteenth-century Jain craftsmanship. Worship continues, with ritual boundaries and photography governed by temple practice.

Sabarmati Ashram

Mahatma Gandhi lived and worked at Sabarmati Ashram from 1917 until the 1930 Salt March, using the riverside settlement as a base for experiments in communal life and nonviolent politics. Hriday Kunj, archives, letters, photographs, spinning tools, and museum galleries document residents as well as Gandhi. Quiet courtyards and simple buildings preserve an institutional community, not merely a personal residence.
