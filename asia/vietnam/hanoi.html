Hanoi

Hanoi became Vietnam's capital under Ly and later dynasties on the Red River delta. Temple of Literature, French colonial opera house, and Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum record Confucian examination culture, colonial urbanism, and twentieth-century independence struggles. Hoan Kiem Lake promenades and Old Quarter guild streets extend dynastic and colonial layers around the Red River.

Hanoi Opera House

Built in 1911 with French nineteenth-century influences, Hanoi Opera House shows ivory-toned columns, Gothic-inspired domes, and neoclassical windows on its facade. Ticketed performances fill the auditorium. The colonial-era house still anchors the downtown opera square.

Ho Chi Minh's Mausoleum

Ho Chi Minh's Mausoleum stands in Hanoi's Ba Dinh Square as the guarded memorial containing the embalmed body of the revolutionary leader and former president. Monumental stone architecture, ceremonial lawns and military procedures shape the site. Entry follows strict hours, dress, security, photography and closure rules, while the surrounding presidential complex and museum operate separately.

One Pillar Pagoda

Near Hanoi's Ba Dinh complex, One Pillar Pagoda consists of a small wooden sanctuary raised on a single stone support above a lotus pond. The present building reconstructs an eleventh-century Buddhist temple damaged and rebuilt several times. Its compact plan evokes a lotus rising from water, and the surrounding courtyard continues as an active devotional site within a heavily visited civic district.

Ho Chi Minh’s Stilt House

Ho Chi Minh's Stilt House stands within Hanoi's Presidential Palace grounds as a modest wooden residence associated with the leader's life from the late 1950s. Rooms, furnishings, gardens, paths and nearby administrative buildings form a carefully managed memorial route. The house is viewed from controlled walkways, with state ceremonies, security and maintenance affecting access.

Presidential Palace

The Presidential Palace in Hanoi, a 1900s French Colonial masterpiece, is the official residence and workplace of Vietnam's president and vice president. Recognized as one of the world's most palaces by Architectural Digest, this neoclassical landmark was originally built as the residence for the Governor-General of Indochina. Designed by Auguste Henri Vildieu, it is a luxurious symbol of colonial architecture in Hanoi.

Quan Thanh Temple

Quan Thanh Temple stands near Hanoi's West Lake as a Taoist sanctuary dedicated to Tran Vu, a protective deity associated with the north. The complex includes gates, courtyards, altars, carved details and a large bronze statue within a site repeatedly rebuilt since its early foundation. Worship, offerings, festivals, restoration and dress guide conduct inside the active temple.

Tran Quoc Pagoda

Tran Quoc Pagoda occupies a small peninsula on Hanoi's West Lake and traces its foundation to the sixth century, though buildings have moved and been rebuilt repeatedly. A tall red stupa, courtyards, shrines, trees and lake views define the present Buddhist complex. Worship and monastic activity take priority, while festivals, crowds, weather and limited space affect access.

Imperial Citadel of Thang Long

The Imperial Citadel of Thang Long preserves archaeological layers and surviving structures from the long political center of Hanoi; restoration and research continue across the historic site, while temporary exhibitions and restricted zones affect the visitor route. Temple Of Literature

Hanoi's Temple of Literature was founded in the eleventh century and later housed an academy associated with Confucian scholarship and imperial examinations. Gates, courtyards, ponds, pavilions, altars and stone stelae honoring doctoral graduates form the complex. Ceremonies, student visits, conservation and crowds shape access, while later restoration has altered portions of the historic fabric.

Hanoi Old Quarter

Hanoi Old Quarter is a historic trading district with narrow lanes, a night market, and traditional tube houses. The Hanoi Old Quarter Cultural Centre offers interactive displays and exhibitions showing the city's evolution from a small trading port in 1010AD. Nearby Hoan Kiem Lake provides a peaceful escape with Ngoc Son Temple on its northern edge. Residents practice tai chi by the lake in the mornings, and on weekends, there's a funfair vibe due to traffic bans.

St. Joseph's Cathedral (Nhà Thờ Lớn Hà Nội) is Hanoi's neo-Gothic Catholic cathedral, consecrated in 1886 at the edge of the Old Quarter. Twin west towers and a stone nave define the exterior. The square in front is a common meeting point, and the interior is open around Mass times.
