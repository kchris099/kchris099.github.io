Huế

Huế was imperial capital of the Nguyen dynasty from 1802, with a walled citadel and tombs along the Perfume River. Royal pavilions, mandarin courts, and Buddhist pagodas record how Vietnamese rulers adapted Chinese court ritual in central Vietnam. Royal tomb avenues and Perfume River boat landings extend Nguyen imperial ritual into the central coast hinterland.

Thiên Mụ Pagoda

Thiên Mụ Pagoda, founded in 1601, rises in seven storeys above the Perfume River west of Huế. Landscaped courtyards and subordinate halls surround the brick tower. The riverside monastery remains one of the city's principal Buddhist sites and a stop on riverboat itineraries.

Hue Historic Citadel

Hue Citadel refers to the broad fortified enclosure built for the Nguyen dynasty, including walls, moats, gates, administrative areas, residential quarters and the Imperial City within. War, weather, demolition and restoration have changed much of the complex.

03 Admission₫200,000

Kiến Trung Palace

Kien Trung Palace was the setting for Emperor Bao Dai's abdication, marking the end of Vietnam's monarchy; wartime damage led to later restoration of the pavilion. Walls and courtyards form the usual route when open. The rebuilt halls sit inside the broader imperial enclosure.

16.4710° N / 107.5766° E

04 Admission₫200,000

Hue Imperial City

The Imperial City forms the ceremonial and governmental core inside Hue's larger citadel, laid out for the Nguyen dynasty behind additional walls and gates. Palaces, courtyards, temples, residences, gardens and ruins show both imperial planning and severe wartime damage. Restoration continues across the historic area, while weather, construction and events can redirect visitor routes.

16.4695° N / 107.5774° E

05 Admission₫200,000

Thái Hòa Palace

Thai Hoa Palace served as the principal throne hall of the Nguyen emperors within Hue's Imperial City, and repeated restoration has protected the fragile building, while conservation projects may close the interior or alter circulation around the hall. A formal courtyard and axial approach lead to a timber structure decorated with lacquer, gilding, dragons, and imperial motifs used for major ceremonies.

16.4687° N / 107.5784° E

Mausoleum of Emperor Minh Mang

Emperor Minh Mang's mausoleum arranges courtyards, pavilions, and lakes on a forested site south of Hue. Symmetrical axes and stone guardians reflect the emperor's Confucian taste. The complex is one of several Nguyen tombs spaced along the Perfume River hinterland.

16.3875° N / 107.5702° E

07 Admission₫150,000

Mausoleum of Emperor Khai Dinh

On Chau Chu Mountain near Hue, Emperor Khai Dinh's tomb was completed after his 1925 death. Concrete and tile mix European and Vietnamese forms along a steep hillside climb of terraces. Interior mosaics of porcelain and glass cover walls around the royal effigy in the burial chamber.

16.3990° N / 107.5903° E

08 Admission₫20,000

Hồ Thuỷ Tiên

Ho Thuy Tien is an unfinished water park outside Hue whose empty slides and dragon lake draw photographers. Official access is restricted, and security may turn people away. Those who enter find weathered concrete attractions reclaimed by vegetation.

16.4079° N / 107.5786° E

09 Admission₫150,000

Mausoleum of Emperor Tu Duc

Emperor Tu Duc's mausoleum spreads through a wooded landscape southwest of Hue with ponds, pavilions, temples, courtyards, stelae and tomb structures. The emperor used parts of the complex as a retreat during his lifetime, giving it functions beyond burial; restoration, humidity, uneven paths and separated architectural zones affect the route, while the ruler's actual burial place remains uncertain.

16.4331° N / 107.5645° E

10 AdmissionFree

Vong Canh Hill

Vong Canh Hill, situated near Tu Duc Tomb in Hue, offers views of the Huong River, surrounding forests, and distant mountains. The area is also home to old bunkers and is a great spot for hiking. Visitors can enjoy the landscape from this elevated vantage point, taking in the mausoleum areas and the meandering Huong River. The hill has been transformed into a tourist park spanning over 219 hectares, easily accessible by both land and waterway.

16.4272° N / 107.5649° E

11 AdmissionFree

Chùa Từ Hiếu

Từ Hiếu Pagoda sits in pine hills west of Huế as a Buddhist monastery linked with Thích Nhất Hạnh's early training years. Gates, courtyards, and ancestral shrines stand above tombs of Nguyễn-dynasty eunuchs. Footpaths through the trees keep the grounds quiet for day visits.

16.4389° N / 107.5720° E

12 Admission₫50,000

An Dinh Palace

An Dinh Palace stands beside the An Cuu River as an early twentieth-century residence associated with Emperor Khai Dinh and later Bao Dai's family. European-influenced facades, decorated reception rooms, murals and surviving grounds differ from Hue's older timber palaces. War, changing ownership and restoration altered the property, while exhibitions and conservation determine which rooms are open.

16.4567° N / 107.5983° E

13 AdmissionFree

Nhà Thờ Chính Tòa Phủ Cam

Phủ Cam Cathedral, seat of the Archdiocese of Huế, mixes modern concrete arches and stained glass with stone, wood, and terracotta. Nearly four centuries of Catholic presence in the former capital stand behind the present building. It oversees dioceses across central Vietnam from this hilltop parish.

16.4526° N / 107.5878° E
