Ho Chi Minh City

Ho Chi Minh City, formerly Saigon, developed on the Saigon River as a Khmer port before Vietnamese settlement grew in the 17th century. French colonial rule from the mid-19th century left boulevards, Notre-Dame Cathedral, and the Central Post Office. The city was the capital of South Vietnam until 1975. Markets such as Ben Thanh and tunnels at Cu Chi nearby record wartime and commercial history.

Statue of Ho Chi Minh

A bronze statue of Ho Chi Minh stands before Ho Chi Minh City Hall on Nguyen Hue Boulevard, depicting the revolutionary leader greeting the public. The monument forms part of a landscaped civic square used for ceremonies, festivals and evening gatherings. Security, events, crowds and municipal restrictions affect the area, while the historic city hall behind it is generally not open for casual interior visits.

Independence Palace

Independence Palace occupies the site of South Vietnam's former presidential residence and government headquarters. The present 1960s building contains reception rooms, offices, bunkers, communications equipment, ceremonial spaces, and grounds associated with the end of the Vietnam War in 1975. Restoration and state interpretation shape the route, while temporary closures and official events affect access.
