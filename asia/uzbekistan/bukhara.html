Bukhara

Bukhara served as a Silk Road emporium and Samanid capital, accumulating mosques, madrasas, and covered bazaars around the Po-i-Kalyan complex. Ark fortress walls and minarets record how Central Asian rulers blended Persian Islamic design with caravan trade wealth. Trading domes and Lyabi-Hauz pools still channel visitors through the medieval Silk Road core.

Chor Minor Monument

Built in 1807 by a Turkmen merchant, Chor Minor (the madrasah of four minarets) rises in northeast Bukhara with an entrance tower and four turquoise-capped turrets. The small madrasa form is unusual for the city. Walkers approach from neighborhood lanes around the monument.

Ismail Samani Mausoleum

The Ismail Samani Mausoleum in Bukhara dates from around the turn of the tenth century and is among the region's earliest surviving Islamic monuments. A compact cube supports a dome through detailed brickwork arranged in geometric and woven patterns that change with the light; the tomb stands in a park outside the densest old-city lanes, while conservation limits contact with its historic fabric.

39.7770° N / 64.4006° E

Ark of Bukhara

Bukhara's Ark rises on an artificial mound as the fortified residence and administrative center used by successive rulers. Gates, walls, courtyards, mosques, halls and museum rooms represent part of a complex repeatedly destroyed, rebuilt and heavily damaged in the early twentieth century.

39.7780° N / 64.4098° E

04 AdmissionFree

Bolo Hauz Mosque

Opposite Bukhara's Ark, Bolo Hauz Mosque faces a historic pool that once formed part of the Registan ensemble. An early eighteenth-century prayer hall is fronted by a tall wooden portico whose painted columns and carved ceiling reflect later additions; the mosque remains a religious site, while water levels, restoration, services, and local use affect how the courtyard and interior are seen.

39.7778° N / 64.4073° E

Kalan Minaret

Built in 1127 under Karakhanid ruler Arslan Khan, the Kalan Minaret rises about forty-eight meters beside Bukhara's Po-i-Kalyan complex. Bands of patterned brick wrap the tapering shaft, once used as a watchtower and call-to-prayer tower. The minaret still anchors views across the old city's low roofs.

39.7757° N / 64.4151° E

Mir-i-Arab Madrasa

Mir-i-Arab Madrasa faces the Kalon Mosque across a shared courtyard in Bukhara's Po-i-Kalyan ensemble. Blue tile work and a large lecture hall mark the working religious school. Together with the Kalyan Minaret, the trio defines the city's main Friday complex.

39.7760° N / 64.4154° E

07 AdmissionFree

Kalan Mosque

Kalan Mosque anchors Bukhara's Po-i-Kalyan ensemble beside the Kalan Minaret and Mir-i Arab Madrasa. Rebuilt in the sixteenth century, its vast courtyard is enclosed by repeated arches leading toward a domed prayer hall decorated with tile and brick. The complex remains associated with worship, so prayer times, religious study, restoration, and visitor management govern access.

39.7759° N / 64.4136° E

08 AdmissionUZS 20,000

Ulugbek Madrasah

Ordered by Ulugh Beg and completed in 1420, Ulugbek Madrasah in Bukhara later gained small shops inside former student cells. Exterior tiles, a fluted dome, stained windows, and tombs still read as Timurid fabric. The grandson of Amir Temur intended the school for scientific as well as religious study.

39.7768° N / 64.4175° E

09 AdmissionFree

Labi Hovuz

Labi Hovuz centers on a rectangular pool framed by madrasas and mulberry trees in Bukhara's old city. Cafes and craft stalls ring the water where residents gather in the evening. The square works as a social living room between the denser covered bazaars.

39.7730° N / 64.4208° E

Nodir Devonbegi Madrasah

Nodir Devonbegi began as a 1622 caravanserai before conversion into a madrasa beside Labi Hovuz. It forms part of the Lyabi-Khauz ensemble with the Kukeldash madrasa. A tiled portal and courtyard cells now host craft displays more often than full-time students.

39.7731° N / 64.4214° E
