Samarkand

Samarkand was a capital of Timur's empire in the 14th century and a major Silk Road hub linking China with Persia and the Mediterranean. The Registan ensemble, Bibi-Khanym Mosque, and Shah-i-Zinda necropolis display turquoise-tiled madrasas and observatories from Timurid and Ulug Beg's patronage. UNESCO-listed monuments record centuries of Islamic scholarship, caravan trade, and Uzbek national heritage.

Amir Temur Mausoleum Gur-i Amir Сomplex

Gur-i Amir centers on the domed mausoleum where Timur, several relatives and members of his dynasty were buried in Samarkand. A ribbed turquoise dome rises above an interior of gilding, carved stone, tile and cenotaphs, while the actual graves lie below; the complex began for Timur's grandson and later became a dynastic memorial, with restoration shaping much of its present brilliance.

Amir Temur Monument

The Amir Temur Monument, located at a busy roundabout in Samarkand, is an sight to behold. The giant-sized statue of Amir Temur stands at the center of the road and is a landmark at the entrance of Rukhobod historical landmark and Amir Temur complex. Tourists often gather here to take photos before began on their journey through the city's historical sites.

Registan Square

Called the Sandy Place, Registan Square faces Samarkand's three great madrasa facades across an open paved court. The city around it still practices embroidery, silk weaving, copper work, ceramics, and wood carving in nearby workshops. Evening light shows sometimes wash the tiled fronts.

04 AdmissionUZS 100,000

Sherdor Madrasa

Seventeenth-century Sherdor Madrasa stands on Registan Square opposite Ulugh Beg's earlier school. Tiger-lion mosaics on the portal give the building its name in local usage. With Tilya-Kori, the three facades enclose Samarkand's most photographed public square in a single ensemble.

05 AdmissionUZS 100,000

Tilya-Kori Madrasa

Tilya-Kori Madrasa, finished in 1660, closes Registan's northern side with a gold-leafed mosque interior. A courtyard and lecture wings complete the teaching complex behind the tiled facade. The gilded prayer hall explains the name, often rendered as 'decorated with gold' in guide literature.

06 AdmissionUZS 100,000

Ulugh Beg Madrasa

Ulugh Beg Madrasa occupies one side of Samarkand's Registan, built in the early fifteenth century under the astronomer-ruler Ulugh Beg. A tall tiled portal leads to a courtyard once surrounded by student cells and teaching rooms for theology and sciences; later restoration reconstructed damaged elements, while shops and cultural programs now share space with the surviving educational architecture.

07 AdmissionUZS 50,000

Shah-i-Zinda necropolis

Shah-i-Zinda climbs a Samarkand hillside as a corridor of mausoleums tied by tradition to early Islamic burial sites, with Timurid tile portals lining the narrow walk. Interpretive signs guide visitors among tombs and restored facades. Steps and passages link the upper and lower shrine clusters.

08 AdmissionFree

Hazrat Khizr Mosque

Hazrat Khizr Mosque stands on elevated ground near Shah-i Zinda and the Afrasiab area, overlooking parts of Samarkand. The present building largely reflects nineteenth- and twentieth-century reconstruction of a much older sacred site, with a columned porch, decorated ceiling, dome, and minaret. It remains associated with worship and memorial use, while the nearby presidential mausoleum adds modern political significance.

Siyob Bozori

Siyob Bazaar operates beside Bibi-Khanym Mosque as one of Samarkand's principal food markets. Covered rows concentrate bread, fruit and vegetables, while nuts, sweets and spices fill adjoining stalls. Meat and household goods serve local shoppers beyond the visitor trade. Product mix and crowding change through the day, but the market's central role remains ordinary urban commerce.

39.6620° N / 66.9799° E

Bibi-Khanym Mosque

Bibi-Khanym Mosque was built around the turn of the fifteenth century as a monumental congregational mosque associated with Timur's imperial capital; extensive reconstruction has reshaped the complex. Enormous portals, domes, courtyards, and tiled surfaces display the ambition of the project, but structural failures and earthquakes left major ruins.
