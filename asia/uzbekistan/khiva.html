Khiva

Khiva's Itchan Kala inner town preserves Kunya Ark, madrasas, and minarets from Khorezm khanate rule on the Amu Darya delta. Turquoise tilework and mud-brick fortifications record how oasis cities sustained Silk Road exchange in western Uzbekistan. Islam Khoja minaret views and craft workshops animate the mud-brick Itchan Kala within the Kyzylkum fringe.

Toshhovli Palace

Toshhovli Palace was built in nineteenth-century Khiva for Allakuli Khan as a residence, ceremonial center and administrative compound. Courtyards organize reception halls, private quarters, carved wooden columns, painted ceilings and blue-and-white tile panels suited to the enclosed desert setting; restoration and museum circulation reveal selected rooms, while the large complex's original household functions require more context than decorative detail alone.

02 AdmissionUZS 100,000

Islam Khoja Minaret

Islam Khoja Minaret rises above Khiva's Itchan Kala in bands of glazed tile and patterned brick completed in the early twentieth century; its narrow shaft belongs to a madrasa and mosque complex associated with reforming official Islam Khoja. A steep internal stair may provide access to a high viewpoint, but capacity, restoration, heat, and safety restrictions can close the climb.

41.3766° N / 60.3601° E

03 AdmissionUZS 250,000

Juma Mosque

Known as the Friday Mosque, Juma Mosque in Khiva has hosted communal Friday prayer since origins traced to the tenth century, with later renovations reshaping the hypostyle hall. Exterior and main interior spaces can be viewed outside service times when open. Wooden columns fill the prayer hall in dense rows.

41.3775° N / 60.3599° E

Itchan Kala

Itchan Kala forms the walled inner city of Khiva, where earthen ramparts enclose a dense historic district. Mosques and madrasas rise among palaces and mausoleums, while houses, markets and narrow lanes preserve the scale of urban life, and several centuries of rebuilding are visible across the fabric, and many structures now support museums, hotels or other tourism uses.

Kalta Minor Minaret

Kalta Minor was begun in the 1850s for Muhammad Amin Khan but left unfinished after rising part of its intended height. Broad proportions and dense bands of turquoise, blue, and white tile give the truncated minaret a distinctive presence near Khiva's western gate; its original projected height remains debated, while the exterior is viewed as part of the surrounding madrasa ensemble.

41.3783° N / 60.3580° E

06 AdmissionUZS 250,000

Kuhna Ark

Kuhna Ark served as Khiva's fortified royal citadel, containing reception halls, a mosque, mint, harem areas, courtyards and defensive spaces developed over several centuries. Carved columns, tilework, raised viewing areas and restored rooms illustrate court ceremony and administration. Much of the present fabric reflects rebuilding, while museum routes and conservation determine which sections can be entered.

41.3794° N / 60.3579° E
