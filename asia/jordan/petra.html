Petra

Petra was the Nabataean capital carved into sandstone canyons from the fourth century BCE, controlling incense routes to the Red Sea. Treasury facades, theatre, and royal tombs record Hellenistic-Arab trade wealth before Roman annexation and later Byzantine and Islamic phases in the Wadi Musa valley. High-place hikes and Bedouin routes extend Nabataean tomb façades through sandstone ridges above Wadi Musa.

Petra By Night

No visit to Petra is truly complete without experiencing the enchanting "Petra By Night." This event transforms the ancient city into a dreamlike landscape, as thousands of candles flicker in the darkness, casting a warm glow over its historic structures. Underneath a canopy of stars, be immersed in an atmosphere that feels both surreal and magical.

AlDayr

Al-Deir, the Monastery, is a rock-cut Nabataean facade reached by a long stair climb above Petra's central valley. The monumental doorway and urn-topped pediment face a wide terrace cut from sandstone. Late light warms the cliff face that crowns many visits to the ancient city.

The Treasury

Nabataean builders likely created it as a royal or elite tomb around an early century BCE or CE, despite the later name 'Treasury.' Columns, pediments, sculpted figures, and a shadowed interior chamber demonstrate Hellenistic influence adapted to Petra's rock-cut funerary tradition. Al-Khazneh appears at the end of Petra's Siq as a two-story facade carved into rose-colored sandstone.

30.3221° N / 35.4517° E

Al-Siq

The Siq follows a natural fault narrowed and reshaped by water between high sandstone walls on the approach to Petra. Nabataean channels, dams, niches, paving, and carved remains line the roughly one-kilometer passage. Its final opening frames the Treasury, but the route itself is both geological formation and engineered entrance.

Petra Visitor Center

The Petra Visitor Center is the ticket and orientation hub at the entrance to the main archaeological park. Guests collect entry tickets, maps, and licensed guides before walking toward the Siq and the Treasury. Displays introduce Nabataean history and site rules. Little Petra and outlying agricultural remains are reached on separate routes beyond the main canyon walk.

Petra

Petra spreads across valleys and ridges once occupied by the Nabataean capital and later Roman and Byzantine communities, and heat, elevation, distances and uneven terrain make route planning important, while the site's significance lies in an entire urban landscape. Rock-cut tombs, temples, water channels, streets, churches, domestic remains and mountain paths extend far beyond the Treasury.

Great Temple

Petra's Great Temple occupies a large terraced complex beside the Colonnaded Street. Archaeological evidence suggests that civic or ceremonial functions may have accompanied or replaced religious use despite the modern name. Stairways, column fragments, courtyards, seating and decorated architectural pieces reveal a substantial freestanding Nabataean building within the city's central public area.

Qasr al-Bint - Temple of Dushara

Qasr al-Bint is a significant part of the Petra complex, featuring temple remains with floral reliefs and columns. Visitors can explore other nearby attractions such as The Monastery, Temple of Dushares, and Renaissance Tomb. The area also offers opportunities to observe archaeologists at work preserving the ruins, there are old Nabatean baths, a museum, and a restaurant for visitors to enjoy.

Nabatean Theatre

Petra's Nabataean Theatre is carved largely from living rock along the main processional route into the city center. Concentric seating looks toward a stage area once used for public gatherings. Walkers pass it between the Siq and the denser tomb facades deeper in the valley.

Mousa's Spring

A spring in Wadi Musa is traditionally identified with the biblical and Qur'anic story of Moses bringing water from rock. A modest building encloses the flowing source near the road into Petra. The water has long supported local settlement, though religious identification rests on tradition.
