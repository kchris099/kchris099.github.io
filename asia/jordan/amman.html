Amman

Amman expanded from ancient Rabbath-Ammon and later Roman Philadelphia into the capital of Transjordan after 1921. Ottoman villas, Hashemite palaces, and 20th-century apartment blocks spread across jebels above the Roman theatre. Palestinian and regional migration swelled the population after 1948 and again in recent decades. Municipal archives, parish records, and surviving architecture help explain how the city connected to wider regional trade routes, court patronage, and modern civic rebuilding after industrial and wartime change.

The Hashemite Plaza

The plaza hosts folkloric dance and traditional Jordanian music among other public programs through the year. Open pavement and surrounding facades define the civic floor even when crowds thicken. It works as a downtown gathering space rather than a gated monument yard.

Roman Theater

Amman's Roman Theater was cut into a hillside during the second century CE, facing north to reduce direct sun. Its steep seating accommodated several thousand spectators and still supports occasional cultural events. Small museums of Jordanian folklore and popular traditions occupy rooms beside the stage; the Citadel is a separate hilltop archaeological complex across downtown.

Amman Citadel

On the hill above downtown, the citadel holds Jordan's Archaeological Museum with finds from sites across the country. Below lies Al-Balad, with rug shops, kunafeh counters, Hashem's hummus and falafel, za'atar sellers, and perfume stalls. Walls and courtyards structure the hilltop walk.

04 AdmissionFree

Roman Temple Of Hercules

Two tall columns and a massive podium remain from the Roman Temple of Hercules on Amman's Citadel. An inscription links construction with the governorship of Geminius Marcianus in the second century CE. Fragments of a colossal marble statue, including a hand, suggest an ambitious cult image, while the plateau also contains Bronze Age, Umayyad, and later remains.

05 AdmissionFree

Umayyad Palace

The Umayyad Palace occupies the northern part of Amman's Citadel with an audience hall, courtyard, residential units, mosque, and administrative spaces from the early eighth century. A modern wooden dome covers the monumental entrance hall, based on an interpretation of its original form. Earthquake damage ended much of the complex's use, leaving stone foundations across the hilltop.

Grand Husseini Mosque

The Grand Husseini Mosque, located in Amman, Jordan, was built by King Abdullah I on the site of a mosque constructed by Omar Bin Al-Khattab around 640 CE. The mosque is a focal point for Friday afternoon protests and is crowded with worshippers on most days. Surrounding the mosque are busy markets where various items can be purchased from street vendors.

07 AdmissionFree

Roman Nymphaeum

Amman's Nymphaeum was a large Roman public fountain built near the city's main street and watercourse in the second century CE. Semicircular basins, niches and masonry once supported statues and flowing water within a densely built civic district. Restoration has stabilized the surviving structure, which illustrates the public water architecture of Roman Philadelphia. The remains occupy a low area below Amman's modern streets.

31.9503° N / 35.9361° E

King Abdullah I Mosque

King Abdullah I Mosque is recognized by a broad blue mosaic dome and two slender minarets in central Amman. Completed in the late twentieth century, the complex includes a large prayer hall, smaller facilities, and Islamic cultural functions. Outside prayer times the exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable.

City Mall

City Mall opened in 2006 as a large indoor shopping complex in Amman covering about 160,000 square meters. Fashion, jewelry, and electronics stores share floors with restaurants, a cinema, and multi-level parking. It functions as a major west-Amman retail hub for residents and visitors alike.

Abu Darwish Mosque

12 AdmissionFree

Raghadan Palace

Raghadan Palace forms part of the Hashemite royal court complex on a hill above Amman. Built in the 1920s for Emir Abdullah, it combines local stone with formal reception and administrative spaces used for state occasions. Security restrictions prevent ordinary sightseeing inside, so the palace is generally understood from distant city views and its role in Jordan's modern monarchy.

13 AdmissionFree

Amman Railway Station

Amman Railway Station preserves steam locomotives from the Hejaz line that once linked Ottoman domains toward the Arabian pilgrimage routes and played a role in the 1918 Arab Revolt. Sheds and tracks display working and retired engines. The site interprets Jordan's rail heritage beside the modern capital.

14 AdmissionFree

Cave Of The Seven Sleepers (Ashabul Kahf)

A cave and associated religious complex at Al-Rajib, southeast of Amman, are traditionally linked with the Qur'anic story of the Companions of the Cave. Archaeological tombs, a prayer space, inscriptions and later structures support long local veneration. Several places across the region claim the same tradition, so the identification remains rooted in devotion and remains historically uncertain.

31.8989° N / 35.9738° E

15 AdmissionFree

Rainbow St.

Rainbow Street follows the ridge of Jabal Amman through an older residential and diplomatic district. Restored houses now contain cafes, restaurants, galleries, shops, cultural spaces, and viewpoints toward downtown. Weekend markets and evening crowds vary by season, while side streets reveal twentieth-century villas and stairs descending into Amman's surrounding valleys.
