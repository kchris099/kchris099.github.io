Seoul

Seoul has served as Korea's capital in different forms for more than six centuries, beginning with the Joseon dynasty's founding city of Hanyang in 1394. Royal palaces, gates, government avenues, and later occupation-era and postwar reconstruction shaped the urban core. Today's city combines restored historical districts with transport corridors, riverside public space, and high-rise development.

Gyeongbokgung Palace

Gyeongbokgung was founded in 1395 as the main royal palace of the Joseon dynasty. It was burned during the Imjin War, rebuilt in the 19th century, and later altered under Japanese rule. Ongoing restoration has returned major halls, gates, and courtyards to public use.

Gwanghwamun Gate

The broad central gate of Gyeongbokgung faces Gwanghwamun Square and once marked the formal approach to the Joseon royal palace. War, colonial relocation, demolition and several reconstructions altered both its position and fabric; the latest restoration returned it to a historically researched alignment in 2010. Stone platforms, guard chambers and a double-roofed pavilion frame ceremonial processions and daily palace entry.

The statue of King Sejong stands in Gwanghwamun Square and commemorates the 15th-century ruler associated with the creation of Hangul and many scientific and administrative reforms. The monument and its underground exhibition spaces connect public history with one of Seoul's main civic axes.

Seokchon Lake

Two connected basins make up Seokchon Lake beside Lotte World in Jamsil, following a former channel of the Han River reshaped during urban development. Central Seoul's Cheonggyecheon follows a historic watercourse uncovered during a major urban project completed in 2005. Paths descend below street level beside bridges, stonework and planted banks. Changdeokgung

Changdeokgung became the favored working palace of many Joseon rulers after its early fifteenth-century construction. Rebuilt after invasion and fire, its halls follow the terrain more closely than the formal plan of Gyeongbokgung, while the rear garden extends into wooded valleys. Heritage recognition reflects this relationship between architecture and landscape across buildings from several periods.

Seoul City Wall Trail

Hanyangdoseong traces the defensive wall built around Joseon's capital across four mountains and the lower city. Surviving masonry, reconstructed sections, gates and archaeological breaks form several walking routes. Steep grades and road crossings vary by section, while museums and markers explain how construction methods, boundaries and urban growth changed the wall over six centuries.

Seolleung and Jeongneung Royal Tombs

Royal burial grounds occupy a wooded enclave in Gangnam at Seolleung and Jeongneung. The three mounds contain King Seongjong, Queen Jeonghyeon and King Jungjong, with spirit roads, guardian figures, red gates and ritual buildings arranged according to Joseon funerary principles. They belong to the historic Royal Tombs of the Joseon Dynasty, while protected zones keep the burial mounds themselves beyond casual foot traffic.

Sungnyemun Gate (Namdaemun)

Sungnyemun once controlled the main southern approach through Seoul's city wall and remains National Treasure No. 1. A 2008 arson fire destroyed much of its timber pavilion, leading to a detailed reconstruction completed in 2013 above the surviving stone base. Modern roads isolate the gate from its former wall line, but recent pedestrian improvements make its layered masonry, painted woodwork, and defensive form easier to examine.
