Gyeongju

Gyeongju was capital of the Silla kingdom from the first millennium CE, unifying much of the Korean peninsula. Tumuli parks, Bulguksa temple, and Seokguram grotto record Buddhist patronage and aristocratic burial culture in the Kyongju basin of North Gyeongsang. Cheomseongdae observatory and royal tomb parks scatter Silla-era monuments across the Kyongju basin.

Bulguksa

Bulguksa is a visitor site in Gyeongju, South Korea. Constructed in 774, the Buddhist temple complex situated on Mount Toham has two pagodas. Although it attracts many tourists, who flock to admire its architectural marvels and attend active prayers performed by worshippers, it still maintains a quiet setting. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Donggung Palace & Wolji Pond

Donggung Palace and Wolji Pond is a Silla-era royal secondary palace site at the eastern foot of Mount Namsan in Gyeongju. The pond, built in 674 AD and enlarged during the reign of King Gyeongbok, anchors a garden network of small islands, pavilions, and connecting causeways. The eastern palace buildings, banquet halls, and stone pagodas were excavated and reconstructed in the 1980s and now form an evening floodlit visitor route promoted by the city tourism office.

Cheomseongdae Observatory

Cheomseongdae Observatory is a cylindrical structure made of 362 stones, symbolizing the solar year. It is located in Gyeongju, the ancient capital of the Silla kingdom in Korea. This city was once a busy cultural and commercial hub with significant archaeological remains. The observatory is part of the UNESCO World Heritage Site and is just one of many historical treasures found in Gyeongju, including Daereungwon Tomb Complex and Bulguksa Temple.

35.8347° N / 129.2190° E

05 AdmissionFree

Woljeonggyo Bridge

Woljeonggyo Bridge is a reconstructed Silla Dynasty bridge that offers a sight, especially at night when it is illuminated and reflects on the water. It has historical significance as it was part of the love story of a Buddhist monk and a princess, as well as serving as a gateway connecting royal palaces. The original bridge was built over 1000 years ago but sadly burnt down; however, in 2018, the largest wooden bridge in Korea was reconstructed.

The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Cheonmachong

Cheonmachong, also known as the Heavenly Horse Tomb, is a famous Silla burial mound dating back to the 5th or 6th century. It was unearthed in 1973 and is one of the few tombs open to the public. The tomb is believed to belong to a high-class nobleman, although the owner's identity remains unconfirmed.

35.8386° N / 129.2105° E

08 AdmissionFree

Bunhwangsa Temple and Stone Brick Pagoda

Bunhwangsa Temple, constructed in A.D. 634 during the Silla dynasty, has its original stone tower and adorned pillars that have withstood the test of time. Despite losing many buildings and relics to invasions in the late 16th century, it still holds the Mojeon Stone Tower as National Treasure No.30. The temple's old well, though no longer in use since the 1980s, adds to its historical charm.

35.8405° N / 129.2344° E

Gyeongju World

Gyeongju World is a major amusement park located in the historic city of Gyeongju. It offers a wide variety of attractions, including the 'Draken,' one of South Korea's most thrilling dive roller coasters, as well as family-friendly rides and a large water park (California Beach) in the summer. It provides modern entertainment contrasting with the city's ancient historical sites.
