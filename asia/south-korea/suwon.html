Suwon

= Suwon has been inhabited since at the latest the early Bronze Age: artifacts such as pottery, sculptures, and arrowheads dating from the Bronze Age to the early Iron Age have been found in the area, including on Yeogisan, which is now a monument of Gyeonggi Province. During the Three Kingdoms of Korea period, the area was a territory of the statelet Mosuguk, part of the Mahan confederacy. It came under the influence of Goguryeo in the late 5th century CE before becoming part of Unified Silla (668-935). Suwon then fell to Goryeo after a military campaign led by King Taejo. In the 13th and 14th centuries, the area was promoted, demoted, merged, and made part of various administrative districts. It then became a part of Joseon upon the dynasty's founding, and in 1395 it was made an administrative centre of Gyeonggi Province.

Paldalmun Gate

Late eighteenth-century builders raised Paldalmun Gate in Suwon as a two-story wooden pavilion on the fortress circuit, mixing Chinese and Western design cues of the period. The gate reads as part of Hwaseong's southern approach rather than a free-standing pavilion. When grounds are open, walls and adjoining towers set the route past the timber upper story.

Suwon Hwaseong Fortress

Suwon Fortress Hwaseong is an 18th-century fortress with ancient city walls and original structures, providing access to the old city. It offers a glimpse into Korean history and culture. Nearby attractions include riding a railway bike in Uiwang for a sunset view near Seoul, exploring Gwangmyeong Cave for unique experiences in wine caves, and visiting Kunchiyan's Hwadam Forest to enjoy nature in summer.

37.2871° N / 127.0119° E

03 AdmissionFree

Hwaseomun Gate

Also called the West Gate of Suwon's Hwaseong Fortress, Hwaseomun sits beside the northwest watchtower Seobuk Gongsimdon. The paired structures mark a defensive corner where wall walks meet the outer gate. Open sections of wall, courtyard paving, and tower stairs form the usual path when the fortress circuit is available.

37.2856° N / 127.0097° E

04 AdmissionFree

Janganmun Gate

Janganmun forms the principal northern gate of Hwaseong Fortress, built in the 1790s under King Jeongjo. A two-story timber pavilion rises above a stone arch and is protected by a semicircular outer wall designed to control entry and expose attackers. Restoration followed wartime damage, while its road passage still demonstrates how the fortress combined royal procession, city access, and defense.

37.2888° N / 127.0142° E

05 AdmissionFree

Hwahongmun Gate (Buksumun)

Hwahongmun carries seven stone arches across the Suwoncheon where the stream passes through the northern wall of Hwaseong Fortress. A wooden pavilion crowns the water gate, combining defensive surveillance with control of the river channel. The repeated arches, flowing water and fortified roofline make the structure one of the clearest examples of how the eighteenth-century walls adapted to Suwon's natural terrain.

37.2875° N / 127.0176° E

Hwaseong Haenggung

Hwaseong Haenggung in Suwon was the temporary royal residence linked to the late-eighteenth-century fortress works begun around 1789. Restored halls now include exhibition rooms and a small theater used for cultural programs inside the palace compound within the broader Hwaseong fortress walls.

37.2820° N / 127.0137° E

07 AdmissionFree

Seojangdae (Western Command Post)

At the summit of Paldalsan, Seojangdae commanded a wide view over Hwaseong Fortress and the planned city below. King Jeongjo observed military exercises from the post, whose present pavilion was reconstructed after fire, and Seojangdae (Western Command Post) is located at the top of Paldal-san and offers a panoramic view of the city.

37.2808° N / 127.0103° E

08 Admission₩2,000

Suwon Hwaseong Museum

Models, construction records, tools, paintings and digital displays at Suwon Hwaseong Museum explain the fortress commissioned by King Jeongjo in the 1790s. Galleries examine its engineering, military system, planned city, royal processions and the Hwaseong Seongyeok Uigwe documentation used in later restoration; the museum adds context that is difficult to read from the walls alone without turning the site into a broad survey of all Suwon history.

37.2826° N / 127.0194° E

09 AdmissionFree

Changnyongmun Gate

Changnyongmun Gate opens one of the principal entries in Suwon's Hwaseong Fortress, built under Joseon planning to guard the eastern side of the city. The masonry and timber work sit against open ground where kite flying is common. Though quieter than some fortress stretches, the gate still shows how the late Joseon castle controlled movement into Suwon.

37.2878° N / 127.0251° E

Yeonmudae (Dongjangdae/Eastern Command Post)

Yeonmudae occupies high ground on Hwaseong's eastern side, where soldiers trained and commanders observed drills. The open pavilion known as Dongjangdae overlooks walls, fields, and the surrounding city, while nearby grounds host modern archery demonstrations and tourist transport. Its original role concerned military instruction and eastern defense, distinguishing it from Seojangdae, the western command post on Paldalsan.

37.2885° N / 127.0230° E
