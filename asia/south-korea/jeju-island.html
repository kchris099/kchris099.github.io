Jeju Island

Jeju rose from volcanic activity that built Hallasan and shaped the island's Gotjawal forests, lava tubes, and coastal cliffs. Centuries of tribute to the wind and stone gods (haenyeo divers, dol hareubang stone statues) layered onto Tamra-era shrines and post-Japanese-occupation resort development. Today the island combines UNESCO-listed geological heritage with working tangerine orchards, beachfront villages, and highland trail networks accessed by visitors year-round.

Hallim Park

Hallim Park occupies a large site on Jeju's northwest coast, with eight themed gardens built around the Hyeopjae and Ssangnyong limestone caves. Paths wind past subtropical plants, folk-village exhibits, and a small zoo, while the dual caves invite short walks through stalactite chambers formed inside Hallasan's volcanic flanks. Travellers pair a visit with the adjacent Hyeopjae and Geumneung beaches, both within walking distance of the gardens and the caves.

Hyeopjae Beach

Hyeopjae is a long crescent of white sand on Jeju's far west, framed by dark volcanic rocks and overlooking Biyangdo Island across a shallow channel. The shallow gradient draws families through summer, and the cool turquoise water supports safe swimming late into September. Nearby cafes serve tangerine cakes and grilled black pork, both island staples associated with the Hallim coastal stretch.

03 Admission₩4,000

Osulloc Tea Museum

In western Seogwangdae tea fields above Hallim, Osulloc documents Jeju green-tea cultivation on volcanic slopes fed by mineral-rich water. The teahouse serves rolled cakes and matcha drinks from the same leaves shown on field tours. Galleries open on posted hours.

04 Admission₩2,500

Cheonjeyeon Waterfalls

Cheonjeyeon Falls descend through three wooded tiers in the Jungmun resort area, ending at a small bridge known as the Seven Nymphs Bridge. The site links to the surrounding Cheonjeyeon pond and Seonim Bridge trail, and connects by path to the nearby Jusangjeolli Cliff columns. Visitors enter through a managed entrance with marked access paths, and the upper overlook gives a quieter vantage than the lower pool.

Jusangjeolli Cliff

Jusangjeolli Cliff is a two-kilometre stretch of hexagonal basalt columns on Jeju's south coast, formed when Hallasan's lava met cold sea water. The angular pillars rise against pounding surf from an elevated walkway maintained by the cultural-heritage administration. Walking the full length takes about forty minutes and connects with the Cheonjeyeon Falls trail to the east.

06 Admission₩2,000

Sanbangsan and Yongmeori Coast

Sanbangsan is a volcanic peak on Jeju's southwest coast with Bomunsa Temple at its base, while the neighbouring Yongmeori Coast stacks of layered sedimentary rock curve into the sea. The two sites share a single access road and a coastal walkway that hugs the cliff base past sea stacks and small caves. Local guides offer short walks along the lower cliffs that require low tide and sturdy footwear.

07 Admission₩2,000

Jeongbang Waterfall

Jeongbang Falls drops roughly 23 metres directly onto Jeju's southern coast near Seogwipo's harbour, which makes it one of the few waterfalls in Asia that meets the sea. A paid lookout points upstream at the basalt column face, and a lower path runs under the cascade at low tide. Visitors typically combine a stop here with the nearby Oedolgae rock and the Seogwipo Olle Market to the east.

Oedolgae Rock

Oedolgae is a 20-metre sea stack on Jeju's south coast near the Jeongbang Falls headland and rises directly from the water. The name translates as "Independence Rock", and a short interpretive trail loops the headland past low coastal walls and a viewing pavilion. Visitors usually combine the rock with the adjacent Jeongbang Waterfall and a walk through Seogwipo harbour.

Seogwipo Maeil Olle Market

Seogwipo Olle Market has served the south-coast city since the 1960s and remains the principal venue for Jeju tangerines, abalone, and bronze-coloured black pork sausages. Indoor lanes host produce stalls, second-generation fishmongers, and grandmother-run noodle stands, while the surrounding street sells brunch benches and tangerine ade. Most stalls keep morning hours but the food lane stays open through evening.

Hallasan National Park

Hallasan National Park protects the shield volcano that forms the center of Jeju Island and rises to South Korea's high summit. Trails cross forests, volcanic slopes, wetlands and seasonal alpine vegetation toward Baengnokdam crater or lower viewpoints. Reservations, turnaround times, weather closures and winter equipment rules apply on major routes, while the park also forms part of Jeju's natural heritage and biosphere landscape.

11 Admission₩4,000

Manjanggul Lava Tube

Manjanggul is a major lava passage within Jeju's Geomunoreum system, preserving flow lines, lava shelves, stalactites and a tall column formed as molten rock drained through the tube. A limited section has traditionally been prepared for public access. Cool temperatures, uneven wet flooring and the scale of the dark passage reveal the physical processes behind the island's volcanic landscape.

33.5282° N / 126.7703° E

12 Admission₩5,000

Seongsan Ilchulbong

Seongsan Ilchulbong rises 182 metres above Jeju's far-eastern coast as a tuff cone formed by hydrovolcanic eruptions roughly 5,000 years ago. Sunrise visitors climb the 460-step rim trail for views across Udo Island and the eastern harbour, and the descent passes agricultural terraces farmed by local haenyeo divers. The cone is part of the same UNESCO Geopark designation that covers Manjanggul Cave and Hallasan.

13 AdmissionFree

Seopjikoji

Seopjikoji is a coastal cape on Jeju's east separating Seongsan from the open ocean, crowned by a white lighthouse and surrounded by rapeseed fields that flower each February. The cape path connects to the coastal walk toward the Gwangchigi Beach and offers public access to the cliffs of Yongduam. Most visitors combine the cape walk with an early drive to Seongsan Ilchulbong for the morning ascent.

Hamdeok Beach

Hamdeok Beach sits on Jeju's northeast coast near Jocheon and links to a small arched bridge and the seafood restaurants of the surrounding village. The shallow bay stays calm through summer because of a concrete breakwater that protects public swimming areas. Local ferries to Udo Island depart from nearby Seongsan, and most travellers combine a swim with a tangerine orchard stop in the same coastal corridor.

15 Admission₩36,000

Aqua Planet Jeju

Aqua Planet Jeju operates as one of the larger indoor aquariums in East Asia, with suites of tanks built around a cylindrical central display and an attached marine research hall. The exhibits cover roughly 500 species native to Korean and Pacific waters, and the facility also runs educational programmes on Jeju's nearby sea-grass meadows. A daytime visit can be combined with the coastal walk toward nearby Seopjikoji cape.

16 Admission₩3,000

Bijarim Forest

Bijarim Forest preserves one of the densest concentrations of aged nutmeg yew (bija) trees in the world, with specimens estimated between 500 and 800 years old and registered as Natural Monument 92 in 1966. The walking path runs beneath interlocking crowns overhead, and the park includes a viewing platform reached by a short uphill staircase. Visitors typically combine the forest with a stop at the nearby Manjanggul Cave and Seongsan Ilchulbong cone.
