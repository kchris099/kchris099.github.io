Vientiane

Established as a capital in the 16th century to evade Burmese invasions, Vientiane was repeatedly sacked and later rebuilt under French colonial administration. Its broad boulevards and gilded stupas reflect a quiet resilience amidst centuries of regional conflict and foreign rule. Visitors today can clearly trace the detailed layers of this unique heritage throughout the preserved streets and monuments.

Pha That Luang Vientiane

Pha That Luang Vientiane is a historic 44-meter gold Buddhist stupa surrounded by a walled enclosure and grassy courtyard. Nearby, visitors can explore an air-conditioned shopping mall offering electronics, sports equipment, and jewelry. The original Morning Market is filled with mass-produced silks, T-shirts, and souvenirs. Along the Mekong River banks, locals and tourists enjoy walks, exercise, dining at restaurants, and shopping for clothes and souvenirs at night.

Patuxai

Concrete reliefs of Buddhist and Hindu figures cover Patuxai, the memorial arch built on Vientiane's Lane Xang Avenue from the late 1950s. Its overall form recalls a European triumphal arch, while rooflines, towers, and decorative motifs use Lao religious imagery.

03 AdmissionFree

Patuxay Park

Formal lawns, fountains, paths, and traffic circles surround Patuxai at the center of Vientiane's ceremonial avenue. The park provides distance for viewing the memorial's towers and reliefs and is an everyday gathering space; grounds around Patuxai hold the war memorial arch built between 1957 and 1968 for those who fought for independence from France.

04 AdmissionFree

Wat Si Muang Temple

Wat Si Muang centers on Vientiane's city-pillar tradition and remains one of the capital's busiest places for blessings and merit-making. The present temple occupies a site associated with the city's sixteenth-century foundation. Popular stories about Si Muang combine legend and historical memory. Colorful halls, offerings, monks and repeated rituals show the shrine's continuing importance in local religious life.

05 AdmissionFree

COPE Visitor Centre

The COPE Visitor Centre explains the lasting toll of unexploded ordnance from the Secret War and the work of the Cooperative Orthotic and Prosthetic Enterprise. Exhibits cover clearance, injury, and rehabilitation with prosthetic workshops nearby. It is one of Vientiane's most direct introductions to that history.

06 AdmissionLAK 30,000

Wat Sisaket

Wat Si Saket was built by the last king of Vientiane and ranks among the city's older surviving temples. A cloister wall holds thousands of small Buddha images, and a museum section displays related artifacts. French-era conflicts spared much of this compound when other temples were destroyed.

Ho Phrakeo Museum

Originally built in the sixteenth century as a royal chapel for the Emerald Buddha, Ho Phra Keo was destroyed and rebuilt more than once after Siamese invasions. The restored structure now displays Lao religious art, carved doors, bronze and stone Buddha images. The Emerald Buddha has remained in Bangkok since the late eighteenth century.

08 AdmissionFree

Chao Anouvong Park

Chao Anouvong Park, named after the last king of Laos, offers a riverside promenade, picnic spots, and a nightly market with souvenir vendors. The park used to have ramshackle riverside bars and restaurants offering sunset views and fresh grilled fish. However, the government transformed the area by building a flood wall for city protection and relocating eateries to create a more communal space.

09 AdmissionFree

Wat Ong Teu Temple

Wat Ong Teu Mahawihan, also known as the 'Temple of the Heavy Buddha,' is a 16th-century Buddhist temple located along Vientiane's riverside. It was built by King Setthathirath and has undergone careful restoration in the 19th and 20th centuries after long running a devastating Siamese invasion.

10 AdmissionFree

Ban Anou Night Market

Evening food stalls gather around Ban Anou in central Vientiane. Grills prepare meat, sausages and fish, while other vendors serve noodles and sticky rice. Sweets, drinks and small Lao dishes round out a compact market focused on ready-to-eat food. The number of stalls and operating nights varies, giving the area a more informal character than a permanent market hall.
