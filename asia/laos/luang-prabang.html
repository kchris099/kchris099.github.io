Luang Prabang

From the fourteenth century Luang Prabang served as royal capital of Lan Xang and a centre of Lao Buddhism. French colonial villas later mixed with monastery compounds along the Mekong–Nam Khan peninsula. Dawn alms-giving processions and gilded wats are the living core of that royal-Buddhist townscape. Royal Palace Museum exhibits explain the monarchy abolished after the 1975 revolution.

Wat Xiengthong

Wat Xieng Thong, built in 1560 by King Setthathirath, is a defining Luang Prabang temple with low sweeping roofs and glass mosaic walls. Shrines, a sim, and riverside gardens fill the peninsula tip where the Mekong and Nam Khan meet. Royal ceremonies long used the compound as a ceremonial stage.

Wat Sensoukharam

Wat Sensoukharam lines Luang Prabang's main peninsula road with a richly gilded facade, red walls, layered roofs, and carved Buddhist imagery. King Kitsarath founded the monastery in the early eighteenth century, and later restoration added much of its visible ornament; monks still live and worship in the compound.

Luang Prabang Night Market

Evening stalls in Luang Prabang fill with woven textiles, carved wood, pottery, silver jewellery, and silk scarves laid out along the main pedestrian stretch. Street food includes local sausage beside other Lao snacks. Vendors pack up after night trade, leaving the same streets for daytime temple and cafe traffic.

Luang Prabang morning market

Luang Prabang's morning market forms in narrow lanes near the royal palace area soon after dawn. Fresh produce and herbs share the route with river fish, meat, rice, fruit, prepared foods and household goods used in local kitchens. Stalls begin closing by late morning, when the compact lanes become noticeably quieter.

05 AdmissionFree

Wat Wisunarat

King Visoun commissioned Wat Wisunarat in the early sixteenth century, making it one of Luang Prabang's historic surviving temple foundations. The present hall reflects rebuilding after nineteenth-century destruction, while the rounded That Makmo stupa gives the compound its distinctive profile; religious objects and Buddha images inside connect the monastery to royal patronage and the city's long Buddhist history.

19.8874° N / 102.1387° E

Phousi Hill

Phousi Hill rises from the center of Luang Prabang between the Mekong and Nam Khan rivers. Stairways pass small shrines, Buddha images, and woodland before reaching That Chomsi at the summit, where views extend across tiled roofs and surrounding mountains. Sunset draws heavy crowds, but the hill is also an active sacred landscape and requires respectful movement around worship areas.

07 AdmissionFree

MandaLao Elephant Conservation

MandaLao Elephant Conservation, a short drive from Luang Prabang, shelters elephants rescued from logging and riding camps. Guests feed fruit and sugarcane under staff guidance, then walk with the animals for about two hours on forest paths. The programme bans riding and keeps group sizes limited. The focus is observation and care rather than performance.

08 AdmissionFree

Phosy Market

Phosy Market is a large everyday trading center on the outer streets of Luang Prabang. Covered halls and open sections sell produce, meat, fish and spices alongside clothing, kitchen goods, tools and household supplies, and its scale and practical merchandise distinguish it from the smaller craft and souvenir markets nearer the historic center.

09 AdmissionFree

Ock Pop Tok Living Craft Centre

Ock Pop Tok's Living Crafts Centre places textile production beside the Mekong River on the edge of Luang Prabang. Weavers work at traditional looms and explain fibers and natural dyes. Patterns associated with different Lao communities connect technique with cultural identity. Workshops, displays and a shop support the social enterprise's training and design work.

19.8786° N / 102.1207° E

The Living Land Company

The Living Land Company is an organic community farm and educational enterprise located near Luang Prabang. It has a hands-on, immersive experience in traditional rice cultivation, allowing them to participate in every step of the process, from plowing with water buffalo to harvesting. The farm promotes sustainable agriculture and supports local community development programs.
