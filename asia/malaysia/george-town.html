George Town

George Town on Penang Island grew after British East India Company settlement in 1786 into a Straits trading port. Chinese clan houses, Indian temples, and colonial shophouses mark Malay, Chinese, and Indian communities that drove maritime commerce and street-food culture in northwest Malaysia. Street-art lanes and clan jetties still extend Penang's merchant quarters along the Malacca Strait.

Kek Lok Si Temple

Terraces climb Air Itam's hillside at Kek Lok Si, a Chinese Buddhist monastery founded in the late nineteenth century. Prayer halls, gardens, statues, a seven-story pagoda combining regional architectural traditions, and a large Guanyin figure developed through successive donations; funiculars and roads assist movement across the steep complex, while worship continues among the heavily visited spaces.

Padang Kota Lama (Esplanade)

Padang Kota Lama, also called the Esplanade, spreads between George Town's colonial civic buildings and the waterfront near Fort Cornwallis. City Hall, Town Hall, sea walls, memorials and food areas frame a broad public landscape used for ceremonies and recreation. The open field helps explain the relationship between the colonial administrative quarter and the harbor. Sea breezes cross the open civic field.

Queen Victoria Memorial Clock Tower

A sixty-foot Moorish-style clock tower marks the junction near Fort Cornwallis, commissioned by Cheah Chen Eok for Queen Victoria's 1897 Diamond Jubilee. Six tiers were intended to represent each decade of the queen's reign, and later road changes left the tower slightly tilted.

04 AdmissionFree

Georgetown UNESCO Historic Site

George Town's protected historic core preserves a dense port-city fabric shaped by Malay, Chinese, Indian, Arab, European and other communities. Shophouses and clan compounds stand near mosques, temples, churches, markets and waterfront jetties. The 2008 joint inscription with Melaka recognizes this living cultural exchange and the urban forms created through centuries of regional trade.

05 AdmissionFree

Kapitan Keling Mosque

Indian Muslim merchants established Kapitan Keling Mosque in the early nineteenth century and later rebuilding produced its present Mughal-influenced domes, minaret, arcades and white walls. The mosque anchors George Town's historic Muslim quarter and remains active for daily prayer.

06 AdmissionFree

Goddess Of Mercy Temple

Incense coils and worshippers fill the courtyard of George Town's historic Goddess of Mercy Temple. Early Chinese settlers established the sanctuary, which expanded over time around devotion to Guanyin. Stone lions, carved roofs and layered altars show the work of different communities, while festivals and daily offerings preserve the temple's active religious role.

Penang Little India

Market Street and surrounding lanes form George Town's Little India, where textile shops, goldsmiths, grocers, restaurants, sweet sellers. Music stores and Hindu temples serve a longstanding Indian Malaysian community. Colonial shophouses provide the physical setting, while festivals and daily commerce shape its identity.

Penang Street Art

Murals and welded caricatures appear across George Town's historic streets. Ernest Zacharevic's 2012 works helped draw international attention, while Malaysian artists and municipal projects added many other pieces depicting local life and memory. Paint fades, buildings change, and new works appear, so any fixed checklist quickly becomes outdated.

09 AdmissionFree

Chew Jetty

Chew Jetty is a UNESCO historic site and a traditional stilt village by the sea, dating back to the 19th century. It's one of the last Chinese settlements on Penang Island, with wooden stilt houses built along a walkway. The location offers a unique and adventurous spot for proposals, with its rustic charm and views of colorful sunsets and boats tied to the docks.

Gurney Plaza anchors the commercial strip along Penang's Gurney Drive with several levels of retail, dining and entertainment. Opened in the early 2000s and expanded later, the mall combines clothing shops and electronics stores with a cinema, restaurants and everyday services. Its scale serves residents across the island as well as visitors staying near the seafront.

Chaiya Mangalaram Thai Buddhist Temple

The Chaiya Mangalaram Thai Buddhist Temple is a traditional Malaysian Siamese temple that is known for its and lengthy Reclining Buddha statue, which measures 108 feet or 33 meters long. The temple also houses various statues of mythical creatures like Yaksha, along with smaller Buddha statues in different poses and Devas. One can witness the mosaic dragons and other sculptures within the shrine.

Wat Buppharam Buddhist Temple Penang

Wat Buppharam serves Penang's Thai Buddhist community through a compound of colorful shrine halls, statues, stupas and residential spaces. The temple developed during the twentieth century and hosts ceremonies tied to the Thai religious calendar. Bright decoration surrounds active worship, linking the site with migrant community life as well as Buddhist practice in George Town. Thai-language religious culture is visible throughout the compound.

13 AdmissionFree

Botanical Gardens Penang

Penang Botanic Gardens began in the nineteenth century on a former quarry and plantation site at the foot of forested hills. Lawns, palms, rainforest edges, streams, plant collections and walking roads support recreation and botanical work. The old nickname Waterfall Gardens refers to a restricted water-catchment cascade beyond the main public grounds, while the accessible garden centers on planted and forested landscapes.

14 AdmissionFree

Tanjung Bunga Floating Mosque

Built on piles beside Tanjung Bungah's shore, this modern mosque appears surrounded by water at high tide. A replacement structure opened after the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami damaged the earlier mosque, using a prominent dome, minaret and Moorish-influenced detailing. Worship remains central to the building, and the minaret is reserved for religious use.

The Habitat Penang Hill

The Habitat occupies rainforest on Penang Hill, where marked trails, elevated walkways, interpretive stations and canopy viewpoints introduce the hill's plants and animals. Commercial facilities are integrated into an older forest landscape reached through the hill transport system. Weather, maintenance and timed access can affect individual structures, so current operating information is necessary.
