Baku

Baku occupies a bay on the Caspian Sea where oil has been extracted since the 19th century, drawing industry and population to the Absheron Peninsula. The walled Old City, or Icherisheher, contains the Maiden Tower and Shirvanshah Palace from medieval times. Soviet-era architecture and later glass towers line the waterfront. The historic core was added to the UNESCO World Heritage list in 2000.

Baku Boulevard

Baku Boulevard follows the Caspian shore through a long sequence of promenades, lawns, gardens, cultural buildings and recreation areas. Its origins date to the early twentieth century, when the city began transforming an industrial waterfront into public space; later extensions added museums, performance venues, shopping centers and landscaped links between the old city, port and newer southern districts.

Azerbaijan Carpet Museum

Carpets from Azerbaijan's regional weaving schools fill the curved waterfront building of the Azerbaijan National Carpet Museum. Displays explain knotting techniques, dyes, motifs, domestic uses, and the social history of pile and flat-woven textiles; founded in 1967, the collection moved to its present rolled-carpet-shaped building on Baku Boulevard in 2014.

Mini Venice

Mini Venice forms a landscaped water attraction within Baku's Seaside Boulevard. First developed in the 1960s and later rebuilt, it uses narrow canals, small islands, arched footbridges and short boat routes to evoke Venetian scenery. Restaurants and pedestrian paths surround the pools, making the complex part of the boulevard's modern recreational landscape.

40.3605° N / 49.8351° E

04 AdmissionFree

Dəniz Mall

A distinctively shaped commercial center located on the Baku Boulevard. Designed to resemble the petals of a lotus flower or the national emblem of a flame, the structure offers extensive retail and dining options along the Caspian Sea coast. The mall opened in 2020 as part of the city's ongoing waterfront redevelopment along Neftchilar Avenue.

40.3582° N / 49.8385° E

05 AdmissionFree

Flame Towers

Three glass towers rise above Baku in a composition shaped to resemble flames, a motif associated with Azerbaijan's natural gas and fire traditions. Completed in the 2010s, the complex contains hotel, office, and residential functions. LED screens across the facades animate the skyline after dark, while the towers remain visible from the old city and waterfront below.

40.3598° N / 49.8262° E

06 AdmissionFree

Nizami St

Nizami Street runs through central Baku as a long commercial corridor lined with shops, cafes, cinemas, and apartment buildings. Nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century facades stand beside later Soviet and contemporary structures, reflecting the city's oil-boom growth; pedestrianized sections near Fountain Square carry the heaviest foot traffic, while outer blocks remain open to vehicles.

40.3722° N / 49.8377° E

07 AdmissionFree

Bibi-Heybat Mosque

Bibiheybat Mosque is a reconstruction of a 13th-century mosque that was destroyed during the Soviet era. Situated near Baku bay, it offers views of the Caspian shipyard and Bibiheybat oilfields. The complex also houses the tomb of Ukeyma Khanum. Travellers can explore its Islamic architecture and interior, but it's to plan visits between 9-11am or 2-4pm to avoid prayer times.

Atashgah Zoroastrian Fire Temple

About 21 km east of Baku, the Atashgah fire temple complex once hosted Zoroastrian and Hindu worship around natural gas flames. Exterior courts and main halls remain viewable outside service times. The Surakhani site preserves a pilgrimage compound on the Absheron Peninsula.

09 AdmissionFree

Highland Park

Highland Park, also called Viewing Park, occupies the ridge above central Baku near the Flame Towers. Terraces and paths give wide views across the city and Caspian shore. Residents use the lawns and lookouts for evening walks; the park functions as a scenic overlook more than a sports ground.

40.3584° N / 49.8299° E

10 AdmissionFree

Heydar Mosque

Opened in 2014 in Baku's Binagadi district, Heydar Mosque is named for former president Heydar Aliyev. The large prayer complex mixes traditional Azerbaijani and Islamic forms with contemporary construction. Outside prayer times, visitors can usually see the exterior courtyards and main prayer hall from public areas.

40.4288° N / 49.8242° E

Baku Stadium

Baku Olympic Stadium opened in 2015 beside Böyükşor Lake with seating for roughly 68,000 spectators; it hosted an early European Games, international football and major ceremonies, functioning as Azerbaijan's principal large-capacity sports venue. The oval, six-storey complex includes a football pitch, athletics track, media areas, hospitality spaces and extensive transport approaches.

40.4300° N / 49.9196° E

Baku Zoological Park

Baku Zoological Park, founded by the government in 1928, keeps roughly 160 species across its grounds in the capital. Paths link animal enclosures and shaded rest areas used by families on weekend visits. As Baku's long-running municipal zoo, it remains a city recreation site rather than a wildlife reserve.

40.3919° N / 49.8491° E

I Love Baku sign

Large letters spelling 'I Love Baku' provide a casual photo point within the landscaped grounds of Baku Zoological Park. The installation belongs to the renovated visitor environment. Paths around it connect enclosures, an artificial lake, education spaces, and play areas developed during the park's reconstruction completed in 2021.
