Explore Nara

A Brief History

Nara became Japan's capital in 710 at Heijō-kyō, modeled on Tang Chinese city plans. Buddhist temples such as Tōdai-ji, with its Great Buddha hall, and Kasuga Taisha shrine date from that Nara period. Deer roam Nara Park near these sites. The city remained a religious center after the capital moved to Kyoto in 794. Eight historic monuments in Nara were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1998.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Nara Deer Park

Nara Park is a public park located in the city of Nara, Japan, at the foot of Mount Wakakusa. Established in 1880, it is one of the oldest parks in Japan. Administratively, the park is under the control of Nara Prefecture. The park is home to hundreds of wild sika deer that roam freely and are considered a natural monument under Japanese law.

¥800

Todai-ji

Tōdai-ji is a Buddhist temple complex that was once one of the powerful Seven Great Temples, located in the city of Nara, Japan. The construction of the temple was an attempt to imitate Chinese temples from the much-admired Tang dynasty. Though it was originally founded in the year 738 CE, Tōdai-ji was not opened until the year 752 CE.

Free

Todaiji Chumon

The Chumon (Middle Gate) is the impressive, two-story wooden gate that serves as the immediate entrance to the inner precinct of Todai-ji Temple in Nara. Painted in vibrant vermilion, the gate frames the approach to the massive Daibutsuden (Great Buddha Hall). It is flanked by fierce Nio guardian statues, protecting the sacred space within.

Free

Nandaimon Gate

The Nandaimon (Great South Gate) is the monumental main entrance to the Todai-ji Temple complex in Nara. Rebuilt in the late 12th century, it is a masterpiece of the Daibutsuyo architectural style, characterized by its massive, exposed wooden pillars. The gate houses two colossal, fiercely expressive wooden Nio guardian statues carved by the master sculptors Unkei and Tankei.

Free

Nigatsu-do

Nigatsu-dō is one of the important structures of Tōdai-ji, a temple in Nara, Japan. Nigatsu-dō is located to the east of the Great Buddha Hall, on the hillside of Mount Wakakusa. It includes several other buildings in addition to the specific hall named Nigatsu-dō, thus comprising its own sub-complex within Tōdai-ji.

Free

Kasugataisha Shrine

Nestled within the lush greenery of Nara Park, Kasugataisha Shrine is a captivating Shinto shrine that dates back to 768 A.D. Its striking vermillion columns create an eye-catching contrast against the natural backdrop, making it a for travellers. According to legend, the shrine was established when a deity from Kashima Shrine arrived on Mikasayama riding a white deer, which has since become symbolic of divine messengers in Japanese culture.

Free

Todai-ji Daibutsuden (Great Buddha Hall)

Todai-ji Daibutsuden, also known as the Great Buddha Hall, is a historic Buddhist temple in Nara. It houses a large Buddha statue and is surrounded by deer roaming the grounds of Nara Park. The colossal statue has been a symbol of Nara for over 1,000 years. The temple's history dates back to the introduction of Buddhism and has undergone major repairs to maintain its original state.

Free

Nara Park

Nara Park is a picturesque public park featuring ancient temples and the Nara National Museum of art, as well as friendly deer roaming freely. The park offers a variety of attractions including Todaiji, Kasuga Grand Shrine, Kohfukuji, Mt. Wakakusa, Nigatsu-do Temple, Toshodaiji Temple, and more.

Free

Yoshikien Garden

Yoshikien Garden is a tranquil and traditional Japanese garden located near the Nara Park attractions. It offers a serene escape with its classical design, wooden teahouse, and beautiful autumn flowers. The garden features various styles including moss gardens, pond gardens, and borrowed scenery landscapes. travellers can enjoy moments of reflection while strolling through the well-maintained grounds before indulging in traditional refreshments at Isuien's tea house.

Free

Heijō Palace Site Historical Park

Heijō Palace Site Historical Park is a reconstruction of the 8th-century imperial palace that served as the residence of the emperor during most of the Nara Period in Heijo-kyo, modern-day Nara. The palace was a large rectangular walled enclosure housing ceremonial and administrative buildings, modeled after Xian in China. Today, travellers can explore the foundations and reconstructions of this once significant site, gaining insight into Japan's ancient history and architectural heritage.

Free

Toshodai-ji

Toshodai-ji is a Buddhist temple complex in Nara, Japan, renowned for its 8th-century Golden Hall, considered a national treasure. Established in 759 by the Chinese priest Ganjin, it was pivotal in the introduction and development of Buddhism in Japan. The main hall underwent extensive renovation and reconstruction over nearly ten years before reopening in late 2009.

Free

Yakushi-ji

Yakushi-ji, a significant Buddhist temple in Nara, was originally constructed in 680 and was once among the Seven Great Temples of the area. The site is known for its two pagodas, each representing different periods of Japanese history. The East Pagoda, built in 1285, is admired for its asymmetrical design and has been likened to 'frozen music' by an early Western specialist in Japanese art.

Free

Koriyama Castle Ruins

Koriyama Castle Ruins, located in a park famous for its cherry blossoms, are the remains of a 16th-century castle. The site is home to Yamato Koriyama Castle, known for its goldfish and historical significance dating back to the Sengoku Period. Despite being destroyed during the Meiji era, the inner moat and stone walls remain well-preserved. The castle grounds are particularly in spring when numerous cherry blossom trees bloom.

Free

Chogosonshi-ji

Chogosonshi-ji, a vast Buddhist temple complex, was founded in 587 and has been a revered site for samurai worship. Perched on the slopes of Mt. Shigi, standing at 437 meters high, this temple is dedicated to Bishamonten, the deity of good fortune. The local community continues to hold strong reverence for this sacred place.

Free

Kohfuku-ji

Nestled in the heart of Nara, Kofuku-ji Temple is a complex that showcases the rich history and architectural beauty of Japan's Buddhist heritage. Originally established as Yamashina-dera in Kyoto back in 669, this temple has undergone several transformations before settling into its current form. Renamed Kofuku-ji after being relocated to Heijo-kyo, it became a significant site during both the Nara and Heian periods.

Free

Five Storied Pagoda - Kofukuji Temple

The Kofuku-ji Gojunoto, or Five-Story Pagoda, stands as a magnificent testament to Japan's rich history and architectural prowess. Originally erected in 730 by Empress Komyoh, this structure reaches an impressive height of 50.1 meters, making it the second tallest wooden pagoda in the country.

Free

Higashimuki Shopping Street

Higashimuki Shopping Street is a covered arcade filled with casual eateries and shops offering souvenirs, clothing, and housewares. travellers can find unique souvenirs featuring deer or monks, as well as local items like calligraphy brushes and Nara Sarashi fabrics. The street is known for its local delicacies such as Narazuke pickles and kaki-no-hazushi sushi.

Free

Gango-ji (Gokurakubo)

Gango-ji is an ancient Buddhist temple located in a serene garden setting. During the Edo period, it was a hub for various businesses and is now recognized as a world heritage site. The surrounding area evolved into a bustling city of commerce and industry. Gango-ji is part of a collection of sites that represent a significant shift in Japanese history during the 8th century, showcasing cultural and political developments during Nara's time as Japan's capital from 710 to 784.
