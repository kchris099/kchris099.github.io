Nara

Nara became Japan's capital in 710 at Heijō-kyō, modeled on Tang Chinese city plans. Buddhist temples such as Tōdai-ji, with its Great Buddha hall, and Kasuga Taisha shrine date from that Nara period. Deer roam Nara Park near these sites. The city remained a religious center after the capital moved to Kyoto in 794. Eight historic monuments in Nara were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1998.

Nara Deer Park

Hundreds of sika deer move freely through Nara Park among lawns, woodland, roads, temples and shrines. Long regarded as messengers of the Kasuga deities, the animals are protected as a natural monument but remain wild. Special crackers are sold for feeding under posted rules; antlers, crowding, food.

Todai-ji

Todai-ji in Nara was founded in the mid-eighth century as one of the Seven Great Temples, with formal opening ceremonies in 752 after work begun around 738. Its Great Buddha Hall houses the massive Vairocana bronze. The complex aimed to match Tang Chinese temple scale and still dominates Nara Park's eastern edge.

Todaiji Chumon

Todai-ji's Chumon stands immediately before the Great Buddha Hall, framing the inner courtyard with a two-story vermilion gate and flanking corridors. The present structure belongs to the temple's later rebuilding after earlier destruction. Guardian figures occupy the gate, but regular visitor circulation usually enters the courtyard from side passages.

Nandaimon Gate

Massive timber columns support Todai-ji's Nandaimon, rebuilt in the late twelfth century in the Daibutsuyo style. Two colossal Nio guardians created by teams led by Unkei and Kaikei stand within the gate, their dynamic carved forms facing approaching worshippers. The open structure marks the main southern approach to the temple while remaining part of Nara Park's deer-filled public route.

Nigatsu-do

Nigatsu-dō is one of the important structures of Tōdai-ji, a temple in Nara, Japan. Nigatsu-dō is located to the east of the Great Buddha Hall, on the hillside of Mount Wakakusa. It includes several other buildings in addition to the specific hall named Nigatsu-dō, thus comprising its own sub-complex within Tōdai-ji.

Kasugataisha Shrine

Kasuga Taisha was established as the tutelary shrine of the Fujiwara family near the ancient capital of Nara, and vermilion buildings stand within forest at the foot of Mount Mikasa, approached past hundreds of stone lanterns and filled with suspended bronze lanterns. Rituals, periodic rebuilding, and the deer associated with its founding deity connect the shrine with the wider sacred landscape of Nara Park.

Todai-ji Daibutsuden (Great Buddha Hall)

Tōdai-ji's Great Buddha Hall shelters the colossal bronze Vairocana Buddha that has stood as Nara's emblem for more than a thousand years. Deer from Nara Park wander the approaches to the hall. Successive rebuilds kept the vast wooden structure in use after Buddhism's early state sponsorship here.

Nara Park

Nara Park is a public park featuring ancient temples and the Nara National Museum of art, as well as friendly deer roaming freely. The park offers a variety of attractions including Todaiji, Kasuga Grand Shrine, Kohfukuji, Mt. Wakakusa, Nigatsu-do Temple, Toshodaiji Temple, and more.

Yoshikien Garden

Yoshikien is a Japanese garden next to Nara Park with moss, pond, and tea-house sections laid out in classical styles. Seasonal plantings and wooden teahouse buildings define the main circuit. The garden is a short walk from Nara Park's main deer lawns and temple approaches.

Heijō Palace Site Historical Park

Heijo Palace Site Historical Park preserves the center of Japan's eighth-century capital across a broad archaeological landscape. Reconstructed gates and audience halls, museums, excavated foundations and open fields explain government, ceremony and urban planning at Heijo-kyo. A railway line still crosses the former imperial compound, emphasizing its scale within modern Nara. Open fields preserve the scale of the former capital.

Toshodai-ji

Chinese priest Ganjin founded the temple in 759, a key step in establishing Buddhist monastic life in Japan. The main hall reopened in late 2009 after nearly a decade of major repair work. Classical Nara-period proportions still define the wide precinct.

Yakushi-ji

Yakushi-ji, a significant Buddhist temple in Nara, was originally constructed in 680 and was once among the Seven Great Temples of the area. The site is known for its two pagodas, each representing different periods of Japanese history. The East Pagoda, built in 1285, is admired for its asymmetrical design and has been likened to 'frozen music' by an early Western specialist in Japanese art.

Koriyama Castle Ruins

Koriyama Castle Ruins, located in a park known for cherry blossoms, are the remains of a 16th-century castle. The site is home to Yamato Koriyama Castle, known for its goldfish and historical significance dating back to the Sengoku Period. Despite being destroyed during the Meiji era, the inner moat and stone walls remain well-preserved. The castle grounds are particularly in spring when numerous cherry blossom trees bloom.

Chogosonshi-ji

Chogosonshi-ji, a vast Buddhist temple complex, was founded in 587 and has been a revered site for samurai worship. Perched on the slopes of Mt. Shigi, standing at 437 meters high, this temple is dedicated to Bishamonten, the deity of good fortune. The local community continues to hold strong reverence for this sacred place.

Kohfuku-ji

Kofuku-ji began as Yamashina-dera in 669 and moved to Heijo-kyo as the Fujiwara clan temple, becoming a major Nara and Heian institution. Pagodas, lecture halls, and museum galleries hold Buddhist sculpture from those eras. The five-story pagoda remains a defining silhouette above Sarusawa Pond.

Five Storied Pagoda - Kofukuji Temple

Kofukuji's five-story pagoda has risen beside Nara's old capital center in several successive versions since the eighth century. The present wooden structure dates from the fifteenth century and reaches roughly fifty meters, making it a defining element of the city's skyline. Conservation work may place the pagoda behind scaffolding or restrict close access for extended periods.

17 AdmissionFree

Higashimuki Shopping Street

Higashimuki Shopping Street runs east from Kintetsu Nara Station beneath a covered roof. Restaurants, confectioners, pharmacies, clothing stores, souvenir shops, and local-food businesses line the arcade on the route toward Nara Park. The name means 'facing east,' a reference to buildings historically oriented toward Kofukuji, whose land once limited entrances on the opposite side.

18 AdmissionFree

Gango-ji (Gokurakubo)

Gango-ji's Gokurakubo precinct descends from one of Japan's earliest Buddhist temples, later woven into Nara's town fabric. In the Edo period the area mixed worship with neighborhood commerce. Today the surviving halls form part of the Historic Monuments of Ancient Nara World Heritage listing.
