Ise

The Ise Shrine (Japanese: 伊勢神宮, Hepburn: Ise Jingū), located in Ise, Mie Prefecture of Japan, is a Shinto shrine dedicated to the solar goddess Amaterasu Ōmikami and the grain goddess Toyouke-hime. Also known simply as Jingū (神宮), Ise Shrine is a shrine complex composed of many Shinto shrines centered on two main shrines, Naikū (内宮) and Gekū (外宮). The Inner Shrine, Naikū (also officially known as "Kōtai Jingū"), is dedicated to the worship of Amaterasu and is located in the town of Uji-tachi, south of central Ise, where she is believed to dwell. The shrine buildings are made of solid cypress wood and use no nails, instead being joined with wood. The Outer Shrine or Gekū (also officially known as the Toyouke Daijingū), is located about six kilometers from Naikū and dedicated to Toyouke-hime, the female deity of agriculture, rice harvest and industry.

Kotai Jingu (Ise Jingu Naiku, Inner Sanctuary)

Kotai Jingu, the Inner Shrine of Ise, is Shinto's most sacred sanctuary dedicated to Amaterasu, rebuilt on a regular cycle in traditional cypress architecture. Gravel approaches lead through forest to fences that limit how close visitors may stand. A sacred horse may appear on the grounds for ritual display.

02 AdmissionFree

Okage Yokocho

Okage Yokocho is a replica Edo- and Meiji-style merchant street beside Ise's Naiku approach, lined with food stalls and craft shops for shrine visitors. Wooden facades and narrow lanes recreate a pilgrimage-town atmosphere. The lane fills with day-trip crowds moving between snacks and the sanctuary road.

34.4624° N / 136.7229° E

03 AdmissionFree

Sarutahiko Shrine

Sarutahiko Shrine near the entrance to Ise's Naiku honors Sarutahiko Okami, the kami of guidance and pathways. The precinct sits close to the pilgrimage route and the Shoha art museum. Worshipers often stop here before or after visiting the Inner Shrine.

34.4670° N / 136.7205° E

04 AdmissionFree

Tsukiyomi-no-miya Shrine

Tsukiyomi-no-miya consists of wooden sanctuaries within the Ise shrine network, established by the ninth century and dedicated to lunar and related solar deities. Quiet forest clearings surround the fenced shrine buildings. The site forms one of the separate miya visited on extended Ise circuits.

34.4733° N / 136.7290° E

05 AdmissionFree

Yamatohime-no-miya Shrine

Yamatohime-no-miya honors Princess Yamatohime-no-mikoto, the figure tradition credits with establishing Amaterasu's sanctuary at Ise. Cypress buildings stand in a wooded precinct along the wider shrine geography. Pilgrims include the site when tracing the founding legends of the Naiku.

34.4859° N / 136.7230° E

06 AdmissionFree

Toyouke Daijingu (Ise Jingu Geku, Outer Sanctuary)

Ise Jingu's Outer Shrine, Toyouke Daijingu, is dedicated to Toyouke Omikami and forms one half of the paired Inner and Outer sanctuaries. Cypress fences and gravel approaches keep the precinct deliberately spare. Pilgrims often visit Geku before or after the Naiku circuit.

34.4871° N / 136.7033° E

07 AdmissionFree

Tsukiyominomiya

Tsukiyominomiya is a compact shrine within the Ise system dedicated to the moon kami Tsukuyomi, quieter than the main Naiku and Geku precincts. Simple fences and gravel enclose the sanctuary buildings among trees. It remains a stop for visitors following the full set of auxiliary shrines.

34.4929° N / 136.7060° E

08 AdmissionFree

Futami Okitama Shrine

Futami Okitama Shrine faces the sea at Futami, where the Meoto Iwa wedded rocks stand offshore bound by shimenawa ropes. The shrine is linked to food offerings and maritime kami worship along Ise Bay. Paths lead from the precinct to viewpoints of the paired stacks at changing tides.

34.5089° N / 136.7878° E

09 Admission¥2,800

Toba Aquarium

Opened in 1955, Toba Aquarium is a large facility on the Shima Peninsula with thousands of animals across about a dozen themed zones, including dugongs and extensive invertebrate displays. Indoor and outdoor tanks overlook the harbor. It ranks among Japan's major aquariums by collection size.

34.4816° N / 136.8457° E

10 AdmissionFree (toll road ¥1,270)

Asama Mountain View Point

The Asama Mountain View Point near Ise refers to the observation area on the Ise-Shima Skyline, commonly associated with Mount Asama and Asama Sancho Park. Terraces look toward Ise Bay, the Shima Peninsula, and surrounding mountains, while a footbath and small visitor facilities may operate seasonally.

34.4597° N / 136.7917° E
