Okayama

Okayama developed as a castle town on the Asahi River in western Honshu. Okayama Castle, rebuilt in concrete after 1945, and the adjacent Kōrakuen garden were completed for the Ikeda lords in 1700. The garden is counted among Japan's three famous landscape gardens. The region is known for Bizen pottery and fruit such as peaches. Rail links on the Sanyo line connect the city to Osaka and Hiroshima.

Okayama Castle

Black weatherboard gives Okayama Castle its nickname, Crow Castle, beside the Asahi River. Ikeda and Ukita lords developed the fortress from the late sixteenth century, but wartime bombing destroyed the original keep in 1945. A concrete reconstruction now houses exhibits, while surviving stone walls, turrets, moats, and the view toward Korakuen retain the historical setting.

Kurashiki Bikan Historical Quarter

White-walled storehouses, black tilework, merchant houses and willow-lined canals define Kurashiki's Bikan Historical Quarter. The district grew as an Edo-period center for collecting and shipping rice, then adapted warehouses as museums, shops, inns and cultural institutions. Stone bridges and narrow lanes preserve the scale of a working merchant town.

Kurashiki Denim Street

Kurashiki Denim Street gathers shops selling Okayama prefecture's noted indigo jeans and related apparel in the historic Bikan area. Storefronts sit among the white-walled storehouses of the old merchant quarter. Browsing here ties the canal town's textile trade to contemporary Japanese denim makers.

Achi Shrine

Achi Shrine rises above Kurashiki's Bikan quarter and is linked in local tradition to Omoikane, a kami of wisdom and counsel. Stone steps climb from the canal streets to a hilltop precinct with views over the tiled roofs. Worshippers seek clarity in decisions as much as ordinary shrine blessings.

34.5968° N / 133.7711° E

Kibitsu Jinja

As the ichinomiya of former Bitchu Province, the shrine holds main festivals on the second Sunday in May and on 15 October each year. The long corridor-like sanctuary is among its architectural distinctions. Approaches lead through a spacious wooded precinct.

34.6685° N / 133.8504° E

Okayama Korakuen

Okayama Korakuen is a large Edo-period stroll garden beside Okayama Castle, commissioned by the Ikeda lords. Lawns, ponds, and borrowed views of the castle keep form a classic daimyo landscape. Paths cross bridges and rice fields maintained as part of the original garden design.

34.6673° N / 133.9362° E

07 AdmissionFree

Kibitsuhiko Shrine

Kibitsuhiko Shrine stands at the foot of Mount Nakayama and honors a prince connected with the Kibitsu region and the Momotaro legend; it is distinct from the larger Kibitsu Shrine nearby, whose covered corridor and unusual main hall are often mistakenly assigned to Kibitsuhiko. The precinct aligns with the mountain and includes worship halls, gates, ponds, and a long approach.

34.6769° N / 133.8641° E

08 AdmissionFree

Saijo Inari Myokyo Temple

Saijo Inari is a Buddhist temple with strong Inari devotion, preserving a syncretic tradition that survived the formal separation of Buddhism and Shinto in the Meiji era. A large torii marks the approach, while temple halls, prayer spaces, and fox imagery spread across the hillside complex.

34.7094° N / 133.8328° E
