Hakodate

Hakodate was one of the first Japanese ports opened to foreign trade after the 1854 Treaty of Kanagawa, drawing consulates and Western-style warehouses onto the waterfront. The Goryokaku star fort marked late-Tokugawa and early-Meiji conflict on Hokkaido. Fire and wartime damage reshaped the centre, while the cable car to Mount Hakodate and morning seafood markets define the modern port city at the island's southern tip.

Goryokaku Park

Goryokaku's five-pointed earthworks and moat were built in the 1860s as a modern defensive and administrative complex. The fort became the final stronghold of the former shogunate during the Boshin War before surrender in 1869; today lawns, cherry trees, bridges, and the reconstructed magistrate's office occupy the star-shaped grounds, whose geometry is clearest from Goryokaku Tower.

Goryōkaku Fort

The star-shaped late-Edo fort was built to guard Hokkaido against Russian pressure and later figured in the 1869 Boshin fighting around Hakodate. Parkland now fills the moats and bastions, famous for cherry blossom season. Paths follow the geometric outline of the former citadel.

41.7969° N / 140.7568° E

03 AdmissionFree (Ropeway ¥1,800 round trip)

Mt. Hakodate Observatory

The Mt Hakodate Observatory crowns the mountain above the harbour city, reached by ropeway or road, with decks looking over the night grid of Hakodate and the coastline. Daytime views show the Tsugaru Strait and linked hills. Crowds thicken at dusk for the lighted city panorama.

41.7595° N / 140.7047° E

04 AdmissionFree

Mount Hakodate

Mount Hakodate rises at the end of the peninsula, with ropeway, road, and hiking routes leading to summit viewpoints. After dark, the city forms a narrow band of lights between Hakodate Bay and the Tsugaru Strait, and weather can obscure the panorama and create long queues, while historical cemeteries and former fortifications occupy lower slopes.

41.7593° N / 140.7039° E

05 AdmissionFree

Kanemori Red Brick Warehouse

Red-brick warehouses line Hakodate's bayfront where merchant Kumashiro Watanabe established the Kanemori business in the nineteenth century. Fire and rebuilding shaped the surviving structures, which now contain shops, restaurants, event halls, and a canal-side promenade; their adaptive reuse recalls the city's early foreign trade without preserving an intact working warehouse district.

41.7665° N / 140.7164° E

06 AdmissionFree

Hachiman-Zaka Slope

Hachiman-zaka descends in a straight line from Hakodate's Motomachi district toward the harbor, framing the former ferry Mashu Maru at the foot of the slope. The street takes its name from a Hachiman shrine once located nearby. Rows of trees, stone paving, and the precise bay view make it distinctive among the city's many hillside streets, though it is an active public road.

41.7639° N / 140.7118° E

07 AdmissionFree

Old Public Hall of Hakodate Ward

Completed in 1910, the Old Public Hall of Hakodate Ward is a Western-style civic building reflecting the port's open-treaty trade era. Blue-and-yellow exterior trim and formal reception rooms survive restoration. The hall sits on the Motomachi slope above the harbor.

41.7650° N / 140.7089° E

08 AdmissionFree

Our Lady of the Angels Trappistine Abbey

Founded around 1898, the French-style abbey still houses a working community of sisters. A small shop sells their work, and gardens remain open while enclosed convent areas stay private. Outside liturgy the exterior and permitted grounds are the usual public circuit.

41.7880° N / 140.8226° E

09 AdmissionFree

Yukura Shrine

Yukura Shrine is approached through a line of red torii and is known for fortunes tied to paper squid charms linked to Hakodate's fishing culture. Worshipers seek blessings for health, business, and household safety. The compact precinct sits among the city's southern shrine and abbey routes.

41.7822° N / 140.7910° E

10 AdmissionFree

Hakodate Hachimangu

Hakodate Hachimangu, with origins traced to around 1445, is a major Hachiman shrine on the city's hillside, busy at New Year and during summer festivals. Paths continue toward Tachimachi Cape and the foreign cemeteries. The shrine grounds overlook parts of the harbor and Motomachi.

41.7539° N / 140.7099° E
