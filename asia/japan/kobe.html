Explore Kobe

A Brief History

Kobe opened to foreign trade in 1868 after Japan ended its isolation policy. Merchants from Europe, China, and elsewhere settled in Kitano and the former foreign concession. The port handled silk and later container traffic on Osaka Bay. The Great Hanshin earthquake of 1995 caused widespread damage in the city. Chinatown, sake breweries in Nada, and mosques reflect its maritime trading history.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Meriken Park

Meriken Park is a visitor site in Kobe. This harborfront park in Kobe's Port Island district was rebuilt after the 1995 Great Hanshin earthquake. Open lawns, art installations, and the memorial to earthquake victims face the bay. The red Port Tower and modern hotels form the backdrop seen in many postcards of the regenerated waterfront.

Free

Ikuta Shrine

Tradition dates this Shinto shrine to the 3rd century, making it among Kobe's oldest religious sites. It sits near Shinkaichi in the city center where streets follow old pilgrimage routes. Rebuilt after wartime air raids, it hosts festivals that mark the calendar for local residents.

¥2,000

Kobe Nunobiki Herb Gardens & Ropeway

On Mount Rokkō above the city, this garden grows seasonal herbs and flowers across terraced plots. The Shin-Kobe Ropeway lifts visitors from near the bullet train station to the upper station in minutes. Paths overlook Kobe Bay and connect to hiking trails on the ridge.

¥2,400

Kobe Animal Kingdom

Kobe Animal Kingdom, located in the Kitano area of Kobe, is a unique and unexpected attraction. This greenhouse-based park is home to a variety of tropical plants, birds, and small mammals. Travellers can enjoy interactive shows and get up close with animals like capybaras, sloths, alpacas, kangaroos, rabbits, and more. The park offers ample space for creatures to roam in environments mimicking their natural habitats.

Free

Kobe Kitano Ijinkan-Gai

Kobe Kitano Ijinkan-Gai is a visitor site in Kobe, Japan. Construction and early use date to around 1868. Kobe Kitano Ijinkan-Gai is a historical district that showcases the elegant Western-style homes built by foreign merchants and diplomats following the opening of Kobe's port in 1868. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Weathercock House (The Former Thomas House)

Weathercock House, also known as the former Thomas House, is a neo-Baroque mansion in Kobe City, Japan. Built in the early 1900s by German architect Georg de Lalande, it was once the private residence of German trader Gottfried Thomas. The house stands out with its brilliant brick hue and stone entryway porch, creating a stately atmosphere unlike other buildings in the area.

Free

Minatogawa Shrine

Minatogawa Shrine is a visitor site in Kobe, Japan. Records associated with the site include 1957, 1872. Minatogawa Jinja is a Shinto shrine that was reconstructed in 1957 after being damaged during the war. Commissioned by the Meiji emperor in 1872 to commemorate the Battle of Minatogawa, this shrine is one of thirteen related to the Kenmu Restoration. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Kobe Harborland

Kobe Harborland is a waterfront area in Kobe, Japan. The site forms part of Kobe Harborland's documented civic and cultural landscape within Japan, with archives and visitor information available from municipal or regional heritage authorities. Quays and embankment paths form the main walking route.

¥2,200

átoa

átoa is a multi-level attraction in Kobe, Japan that offers a mix of art and aquarium experiences. While ticket prices are relatively high at 2400 yen per person, travellers can explore four floors and a rooftop area. The lower levels may seem underwhelming, but as visitors ascend to the third and fourth floors, the experience improves.

¥600

Kobe Ōji Zoo

Kobe Ōji Zoo is a visitor site in Kobe, Japan. Kobe Ōji Zoo, located just a few train stations away from central Kobe-Sannomiya Station, is a well-established zoo with over 850 animals and birds from 150 different species. Travellers can see rare animals such as the giant panda, golden snub-nosed monkey, Amur tiger, polar bear, and snow leopard. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Nishinomiya Shrine (Nishinomiya Ebisu)

Nishinomiya Jinja, also known as Nishinomiya Ebisu, is an ancient and ornate Shinto shrine known for association with the god of wealth, Ebisu. The shrine hosts the Tōka-Ebisu festival every year on January 10th, featuring the 'Lucky Man' race where men compete for the title of Fukuotoko.

Free

Hirota Shrine

Hirota Shrine is a visitor site in Kobe, Japan. The shrine was built following a divine message received by Empress Jingu from the sun goddess Amaterasu after her return from a campaign to Silla. It is dedicated to Amaterasu, the goddess of sports and academics, believed to bestow divine favor upon worshippers. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Suma-dera

Suma-dera, also known as Joya-san Fukusho-ji, is a Buddhist temple complex in Suma Ward. Established in 886, it holds historical structures, cultural relics, statues, and well-maintained gardens. The cherry blossoms and vast grounds have inspired famous historical figures to write poetry. Travellers can admire mechanical art pieces like marionette clocks displayed inside the temple.
