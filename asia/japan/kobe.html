Kobe

Kobe opened to foreign trade in 1868 after Japan ended its isolation policy. Merchants from Europe, China, and elsewhere settled in Kitano and the former foreign concession. The port handled silk and later container traffic on Osaka Bay. The Great Hanshin earthquake of 1995 caused widespread damage in the city. Chinatown, sake breweries in Nada, and mosques reflect its maritime trading history.

Meriken Park

Meriken Park occupies Kobe's harborfront on land rebuilt after the 1995 Great Hanshin earthquake. Lawns, outdoor art, and a memorial to quake victims face the bay, with the red Port Tower rising behind modern hotels. The open waterfront has become a standard postcard view of the city's post-disaster recovery.

Ikuta Shrine

Ikuta Shrine occupies a wooded pocket near central Kobe and is traditionally linked to the city's early name and origins. The sanctuary has been rebuilt after fires, warfare, flooding and the 1995 earthquake, leaving modern structures within an old sacred site; seasonal festivals, marriage ceremonies and the small forest behind the main hall maintain its role in daily urban religious life.

Kobe Nunobiki Herb Gardens & Ropeway

On Mount Rokkō above the city, this garden grows seasonal herbs and flowers across terraced plots. The Shin-Kobe Ropeway lifts visitors from near the bullet train station to the upper station in minutes. Paths overlook Kobe Bay and connect to hiking trails on the ridge.

Kobe Animal Kingdom

Kobe Animal Kingdom combines large greenhouse halls with outdoor zones on Port Island. Birds, capybaras and red pandas occupy planted environments, joined by other small mammals and aquatic species. Demonstrations and controlled contact programs bring visitors close to selected animals, while the enclosed setting allows many exhibits to operate in varied weather.

05 AdmissionFree

Kobe Kitano Ijinkan-Gai

Kitano Ijinkan-Gai grew after Kobe's port opened in 1868, when foreign merchants and diplomats built Western-style houses on the hillside. Timber villas with bay windows and gardens line sloping streets above the business district. Several residences open as house museums, showing how the treaty-port community lived in the late nineteenth century.

34.7007° N / 135.1908° E

06 AdmissionFree

Weathercock House (The Former Thomas House)

A weathercock crowns the red-brick former residence of German merchant Gottfried Thomas in Kobe's Kitano district, and the building is often called Kazamidori no Yakata and belongs to the group of foreign residences reflecting Kobe's treaty-port era. Architect Georg de Lalande designed the early twentieth-century house with German-influenced domestic rooms, decorative woodwork, and views across the port city.

34.7014° N / 135.1895° E

07 AdmissionFree

Minatogawa Shrine

Minatogawa Shrine commemorates the 1336 Battle of Minatogawa and the Kenmu Restoration figures linked to it. The Meiji emperor ordered the shrine in 1872; wartime damage led to reconstruction in 1957. It belongs to a small group of shrines tied to that restoration era, with halls and approach paths set in central Kobe.

34.6818° N / 135.1753° E

Kobe Harborland

Rebuilt waterfront retail and leisure buildings face the harbor along quay and embankment promenades. Evening lights reflect on the water toward the Port Tower and surrounding piers. Walking routes link malls, open decks, and ferry views without leaving the harbor edge.

Kobe Ōji Zoo

Kobe Oji Zoo lies a few stops from Sannomiya and keeps roughly 850 animals across about 150 species. Giant pandas, Amur tigers, polar bears, and snow leopards are among the mammals on view. Tree-lined paths wind between enclosures in a compact city park setting popular with families from the Kansai region.

11 AdmissionFree

Nishinomiya Shrine (Nishinomiya Ebisu)

Nishinomiya Jinja, also known as Nishinomiya Ebisu, is an ancient and ornate Shinto shrine known for association with the god of wealth, Ebisu. The shrine hosts the Tōka-Ebisu festival every year on January 10th, featuring the 'Lucky Man' race where men compete for the title of Fukuotoko.

34.7357° N / 135.3346° E

12 AdmissionFree

Hirota Shrine

Hirota Shrine's founding story credits Empress Jingu with a message from Amaterasu after a campaign toward Silla. The precinct honors the sun goddess in her roles linked to sports and scholarship. Worshippers still climb to the halls seeking favor for exams and athletic contests in a wooded setting above the city streets.

34.7528° N / 135.3401° E

13 AdmissionFree

Suma-dera

Founded in 886, the temple keeps historic halls, statues, relics, and tended gardens that once inspired poetry under cherry blossom. Mechanical pieces such as marionette clocks appear among the displays. Broad grounds give room to walk between buildings at an unhurried pace.
