Destination Guide — Nagasaki

Explore Nagasaki

A Brief History

nagasaki grew as a regional centre beyond an older fortified core, shaped by nineteenth-century commerce and post-war reconstruction that reshaped its public spaces, religious buildings, and surrounding landscape. Restored civic halls, market streets, and natural features now define the contemporary visitor experience for travellers exploring Japan today.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Mt. Inasa Overlook

Mt. Inasa Overlook, located in Nagasaki, offers a panoramic view of the city. travellers can take a cable car to the top of the hill and enjoy vistas during both day and night. The observation deck provides an unobstructed 360-degree view, making it a popular spot for tourists. Additionally, the Inasayama Observatory is renowned for its mesmerizing night views and has been recognized as one of the three night views in the world.

Free

Glover Garden

Glover Garden is a tranquil outdoor museum featuring beautifully landscaped gardens, well-preserved Western-style historic homes, and panoramic views. Located on Minami Yamate Hill, it is one of the many sites in Nagasaki that reflects its rich foreign cultural influences from trade. Other notable attractions in the area include Shinchi Chinatown and Dejima Island, each offering unique glimpses into the city's history.

Free

Megane Bridge

Megane Bridge, also known as Spectacles Bridge, is a historic pedestrian walkway that crosses the Nakashima River in Nagasaki. Built in 1634 by Chinese monks, it holds the distinction of being Japan's oldest stone arch bridge. The bridge's unique design features round arches that create a reflection resembling a pair of spectacles in the water below.

Free

Suwa Shrine

Suwa Shrine is a renowned 17th-century Shinto shrine located in Nagasaki, Japan. It sits atop a forested hill and offers views of the cityscape. The shrine is known for its annual festivals and is a popular destination for people seeking blessings for good health and long life.

Free

Peace Park Nagasaki

Peace Park Nagasaki, established in 1955 to honor the victims of the atomic bombing and advocate for peace, is a tranquil and dignified space. It was created as a response to the devastating events of August 9th, 1945 when an atomic bomb ravaged Nagasaki during World War II. The park spans approximately 18.6 hectares and comprises five distinct zones: Zone of Hopes, Zone of Prayers, Zone of Learning, Sports Zone, and Plaza Zone.

Free

Peace Statue

The Peace Statue, located at the end of the Peace Park, is a significant symbol commemorating the tenth anniversary of the atomic bomb explosion in Nagasaki. Designed by Japanese artists, it features various gestures symbolizing prayers for peace. The hulking bronze statue by Kitamura Seibo stands as a reminder of the devastation caused by the atomic bomb and is surrounded by other statues gifted by different countries in honor of peace.

Free

Hypocenter Park

Hypocenter Park is a solemn site in the heart of the complex, featuring a stark black monolith that designates the epicenter of the atomic explosion. Adjacent to this monument stands a damaged pillar from the former Urakami Cathedral, which was devastated during the blast. Additionally, travellers can peer into a section of soil beneath the park's surface where remnants such as broken roof tiles, bricks, and shards of glass from the explosion are still visible.

Free

Atomic Bomb Hypocenter Monument

The Atomic Bomb Hypocenter Monument in Nagasaki is conveniently located near the Peace Park and can be easily reached by taking a streetcar from Nagasaki Station. The museum offers rich content and is less crowded compared to the one in Hiroshima. Standing at the hypocenter, travellers can feel the weight of the implications and destruction caused by the bomb. The park surrounding the monument is calm, clean, and filled with lush green plants, providing a surreal experience.

Free

Urakami Cathedral

Urakami Cathedral, a large Catholic church in Nagasaki, was reconstructed in 1959 after being obliterated by an atomic bomb during World War II. The parishioners of Urakami, known for the 'Miracle in the East,' defied a 250-year ban on Christianity to build this cathedral with the help of a French priest. Despite being just 500 meters from the bomb's hypocenter, it was completely destroyed.

Free

Sanno Shrine one-legged Torii Gate

The Sanno Shrine one-legged Torii Gate in Nagasaki is a powerful testament to the impact of the atomic bomb that hit the city at the end of World War Two. Located 900 meters from the hypocenter, this shrine features a torii gate with only one leg remaining after the blast, while the remains of its other leg can be seen on the ground. The site serves as a reminder of both the devastation caused by humanity and nature's resilience.

Free

Nagasaki Penguin Aquarium

Nagasaki Penguin Aquarium, located in Nagasaki City, is a must-visit destination for marine life enthusiasts. The aquarium boasts an impressive collection of over 180 penguins and various other aquatic creatures. travellers can enjoy interactive presentations, feeding sessions, and informative displays that offer educational insights into marine ecosystems.
