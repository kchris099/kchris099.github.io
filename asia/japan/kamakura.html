Kamakura

From 1185 to 1333 Kamakura housed Japan's first shogunate and a dense culture of Zen temples. Mountain passes protected the coastal capital of the samurai government. The Great Buddha and surviving monastery precincts are what remain of that medieval warrior regime. Hōkoku-ji's bamboo grove and Engaku-ji's gates illustrate the Zen monastic density of the hills.

Kotoku-in

Kotoku-in is a Buddhist temple whose outdoor bronze Amida Buddha stands about 11.3 meters tall and weighs roughly 121 tonnes, long without a covering hall. The figure is designated a National Treasure and defines Kamakura's coastal temple visits. Courtyard paths circle the statue among smaller halls.

Hasedera

Kamakura Hasedera is a historic Buddhist temple and garden complex dating back to the 8th century. It is known for its colossal wooden statue of the deity Kannon, standing over 9 meters tall, as well as a golden statue of Amida measuring 3 meters. The temple's grounds are adorned with numerous Jizo statues, known as protectors of travelers and unborn children.

35.3125° N / 139.5331° E

Enoshima Sea Candle

Built in 2003, the Enoshima Sea Candle is a lighthouse and observation tower on Enoshima with views across Sagami Bay toward the Miura and Izu coasts. An elevator and stairs reach the lighted crow's nest. The tower rises from the island's botanical garden plateau.

Enoshima Shrine Hetsumiya

Hetsumiya is an early of Enoshima Shrine's three principal sanctuaries encountered after climbing from the island entrance. Rebuilt forms occupy a terrace beside an octagonal hall containing images of Benzaiten, a deity associated with music, eloquence, and water; stone stairs continue to Nakatsumiya and Okutsumiya, making Hetsumiya one stage in a larger sacred route.

35.3004° N / 139.4796° E

05 AdmissionFree

Enoshima Iwaya Cave

Enoshima's Iwaya caves were cut by wave action into two main passages, about 152 and 56 meters long. Walkways lead inland through damp rock chambers once used for ascetic practice. Candles or lamps are provided for the darker stretches toward the inner shrines.

35.2984° N / 139.4756° E

Enoshima Shrine Okutsumiya

Okutsumiya is the inner hilltop shrine of the Enoshima Jinja complex, paired with Hetsumiya and Nakatsumiya along a stair route up the island. Forest paths and coastal overlooks link the three sanctuaries. The upper shrine completes the traditional ascent for worshipers.

07 AdmissionFree

Tsurugaoka Hachimangu

Dedicated to the war deity associated with the samurai, the shrine is closely tied to Minamoto no Yoritomo and the close of the Kamakura period. The long approach and main halls structure the city's historic core. Seasonal festivals still draw large local crowds.

35.3260° N / 139.5563° E

08 AdmissionFree

Kencho-ji

Kenchoji was founded in 1253 under the patronage of the Hojo regents and became the leading temple of Kamakura's Five Mountains Zen system. A monumental sanmon, Buddha hall, teaching hall, garden, and subtemples extend into a narrow valley. Active Rinzai training continues within the complex, while trails rise behind the grounds toward ridge paths and viewpoints.

35.3315° N / 139.5549° E

09 AdmissionFree

Meigetsu-in

Hydrangeas line the approach to Meigetsu-in, drawing large crowds during Kamakura's rainy season. The Rinzai Zen temple developed from medieval memorial and monastic foundations, and its main hall is known for a circular window framing the rear garden. Seasonal iris access, bamboo, rock arrangements, and quiet cemetery areas give the grounds interest beyond the brief peak bloom.

35.3349° N / 139.5515° E

10 AdmissionFree

Engaku-ji

Founded in 1282 as the second of Kamakura's Five Great Zen Temples, the complex was dedicated to warriors fallen after the Mongol invasion attempts. Gates, halls, and garden spaces climb the valley. The precinct remains a major Zen landmark on the Kita-Kamakura side.

35.3377° N / 139.5475° E

11 AdmissionFree

Egara Tenjin Shrine

This twelfth-century shrine is dedicated to Sugawara no Michizane, deity of learning and culture for students and writers. Vermilion buildings and gardens frame the compact precinct. Exam candidates come for prayers and can obtain charms aimed at academic success.

35.3259° N / 139.5643° E

12 AdmissionFree

Ichijo Ekan Sanso

Originally a ruling family's tea house, the building was moved to Kamakura with gardens recreated to match. A roughly fifty-minute guided tour covers sliding doors, tatami rooms, and hanging scrolls. Seasonal garden views change from inside the same rooms.

35.3204° N / 139.5712° E

Zeniarai Benten Shrine

A tunnel through the hillside leads to Zeniarai Benzaiten Ugafuku Shrine in western Kamakura, and worshippers rinse coins and banknotes in spring water as a prayer for prosperity. Tradition links the foundation with Minamoto no Yoritomo and a dream in the late twelfth century.

35.3257° N / 139.5422° E

14 AdmissionFree

Sasuke Inari Shrine

Legend ties Sasuke Inari Shrine to Minamoto no Yoritomo, who is said to have founded it near Kamakura's Hidden Village after guidance in a dream. Fox statues and vermilion accents mark the wooded precinct beside Zeniarai Benten. Worshippers come seeking career luck and personal success, leaving offerings along the quiet paths that climb through the hillside trees.

35.3243° N / 139.5389° E

15 AdmissionFree

Yokohama Hakkeijima Sea Paradise

Hakkeijima Sea Paradise sits on an artificial island in Tokyo Bay, reached from the Kamakura side by train and short ferry or bridge links. Aquariums, rides, and shoreline paths share the same compact island footprint. Marine tanks and outdoor decks face open water, while hotels and shops cluster near the pier for a full day on the bay.
