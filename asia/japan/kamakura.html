Explore Kamakura

A Brief History

Serving as the de facto capital of Japan from 1185 to 1333, Kamakura saw the rise of the first shogunate and the flourishing of Zen Buddhism. Its dense concentration of ancient temples and protective mountain passes remain a striking legacy of an era defined by samurai rule. Museums, markets, and street plans in Kamakura still show layers from those periods.

Top Sights & Activities

¥300

Kotoku-in

Kotoku-in is a visitor site in Kamakura, Japan. The temple's centerpiece is an bronze statue of Amidanyorai, affectionately known as "Buddha sitting in a roofless house." This monumental figure stands at an 11.3 meters tall and weighs around 121 tonnes, making it one of Japan's most significant national treasures. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

¥400

Hasedera

Kamakura Hasedera is a historic Buddhist temple and garden complex dating back to the 8th century. It is known for its colossal wooden statue of the deity Kannon, standing over 9 meters tall, as well as a golden statue of Amida measuring 3 meters. The temple's grounds are adorned with numerous Jizo statues, known as protectors of travelers and unborn children.

Free

Enoshima Sea Candle

Enoshima Sea Candle is a visitor site in Kamakura, Japan. Construction and early use date to around 2003. Enoshima Sea Candle is a modern lighthouse and observatory built in 2003, offering panoramic views of Sagami Bay. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Enoshima Shrine Hetsumiya

Enoshima Shrine is a known mountaintop complex featuring three ancient shrines, grounds, and panoramic views. Dedicated to Benzaiten, the goddess of wisdom and music, this Shinto shrine is among Japan's most famous. Located just a short walk from Katase-Enoshima Station, it includes Hetsunomiya with its octagonal pavilion enshrining Benzaiten.

Free

Enoshima Iwaya Cave

Enoshima Iwaya Cave is a visitor site in Kamakura, Japan. Enoshima Iwaya Cave, consisting of the 152-meter Daiichi Iwaya and the 56-meter Daini Iwaya, was naturally sculpted by ocean waves. Visitors can stroll through with candles provided for an atmospheric experience. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Enoshima Shrine Okutsumiya

Enoshima Shrine Okutsumiya is a visitor site in Kamakura, Japan. Enoshima Shrine Okutsumiya is a hilltop Shinto shrine that forms part of the larger Enoshima Shrine complex, which includes Hetsumiya and Nakatsumiya. Visitors can enjoy a stroll along the stairs connecting the shrines, offering views of nature and the coastline. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Tsurugaoka Hachimangu

Tsurugaoka Hachimangu is a significant Shinto shrine in Kamakura, dating back to the 11th century. It is a attraction dedicated to the god of war and samurai, with historical importance due to its association with Minatomo no Yoritomo and the end of the Kamakura period.

Free

Kencho-ji

Kencho-ji is a visitor site in Kamakura, Japan. Construction and early use date to around 1253. Kencho-ji is the oldest and most important Zen Buddhist temple in Kamakura, founded in 1253. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Meigetsu-in

Meigetsu-in, also known as 'Hydrangea Temple,' is a Rinzai Zen temple established in 1159. The temple has a large round window and hydrangeas on its grounds. It's conveniently located near other popular attractions in Kanagawa, such as Tsurugaoka Hachimangu Shrine and Kotoku-in Temple.

Free

Engaku-ji

Engaku-ji is a Zen Buddhist complex located in the northern part of Kamakura, Japan. Established in 1282, it is the second of Kamakura's Five Great Zen Temples and holds historical significance as it was dedicated to fallen warriors after a failed invasion by the Mongols.

Free

Egara Tenjin Shrine

Egara Tenjin Shrine is a 12th-century Shinto shrine located in Kamakura, Japan. It is dedicated to Michizane Sugawara, the god of learning and culture. The shrine's vermilion red exterior and gardens make it a place to visit. Students often come here to pray for success in their exams, as charms for educational success are available for purchase.

Free

Ichijo Ekan Sanso

Ichijo Ekan Sanso is a historic building originally constructed in the 17th century in Kyoto. It was initially a tea house for the ruling family and has since been relocated to Kamakura, with its gardens faithfully recreated. Visitors can take a detailed 50-minute tour inside, learning about traditional elements like sliding doors, tatami mats, and hanging scrolls. The garden offers views from within the building and is praised for its beauty throughout the seasons.

Free

Zeniarai Benten Shrine

Located western Kamakura, Zeniarai Benten Shrine is a 11th-century Shinto shrine that offers a unique ritual of money-washing. Founded by Minomoto no Yoritomo, the shrine reflects the ancient blend of Buddhism and Shintoism. Visitors partake in the tradition of washing their money in the natural spring, believed to bring financial fortune. This practice has made the shrine a popular destination, despite its otherwise unassuming nature.

Free

Sasuke Inari Shrine

Sasuke Inari Shrine is a visitor site in Kamakura, Japan. According to legend, it was established by Minamoto no Yorimoto next to the Hidden Village of Kamakura after he received guidance in a dream. It is associated with personal success, particularly in one's career. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Yokohama Hakkeijima Sea Paradise

Yokohama Hakkeijima Sea Paradise is a visitor site in Kamakura, Japan. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.
