Hiroshima

Hiroshima became the capital of Hiroshima Prefecture in the Meiji period and developed as a military port on the Seto Inland Sea. On 6 August 1945 an atomic bomb destroyed the city center. Peace Memorial Park, the A-bomb Dome, and museums preserve that history. Reconstruction from the 1950s created broad avenues and trams. Hiroshima Castle, originally built in the 1590s, was rebuilt in concrete after the war.

Atomic Bomb Dome

The skeletal dome of Hiroshima's former Prefectural Industrial Promotion Hall stands close to the hypocenter of the atomic bombing on August 6, 1945, and much of the building was destroyed, but portions of the brick walls and steel roof frame remained. Preserved as the Atomic Bomb Dome and designated a site, the ruin anchors remembrance of the victims and the consequences of nuclear warfare.

Hiroshima Castle

Mori Terumoto built Hiroshima Castle at the end of the sixteenth century on the delta that became the modern city. The atomic bombing destroyed its wooden keep and most buildings in 1945, while a concrete reconstruction opened as a museum in 1958; original stone walls, moats, reconstructed gates, and surviving trees connect the present grounds with both feudal history and wartime destruction.

34.4022° N / 132.4593° E

Peace Memorial Park - Hiroshima

Hiroshima Peace Memorial Park occupies an area near the atomic bomb hypocenter that had been a dense commercial and residential district. Kenzo Tange's postwar plan aligned the museum, cenotaph, and atomic Bomb Dome across open lawns and water; memorials record individual groups and losses, while ceremonies and exhibits connect local testimony with continued debate over nuclear weapons and peace.

34.3927° N / 132.4524° E

Miyajima

Ancient shrines, temple paths, and forest cover Mount Misen above the island shore and tidal flats. Ferries from the mainland deliver pilgrims and day visitors to the vermilion waterfront near the famous gate. Deer and steep trails shape movement once ashore.

34.2974° N / 132.3196° E

05 AdmissionFree

Itsukushima Shrine Otorii Gate

Itsukushima Shrine's great torii stands in the tidal flats off Miyajima, appearing surrounded by water at high tide and approachable across wet sand when the sea recedes. The present vermilion gate was rebuilt in the nineteenth century and rests under its own weight. Tide level changes the view, while the gate remains part of the shrine's wider sacred landscape.

34.2959° N / 132.3188° E

06 AdmissionFree

Itsukushima Jinja

Piers carry the halls and corridors of Itsukushima Shrine over Miyajima's tidal shore, creating a ceremonial relationship among sea, mountain and architecture. The complex developed under Taira no Kiyomori in the twelfth century, though storms and rebuilding shaped the surviving structures. Vermilion galleries, stages, prayer halls and the offshore torii belong to one historic Shinto ensemble.

34.2959° N / 132.3198° E

Daishoin

Daisho-in is a Shingon temple complex on Mount Misen on Miyajima, the fourteenth stop on the Chugoku 33 Kannon pilgrimage. Halls and statues climb the wooded slope, with maples that color strongly in autumn. The temple complements the island's shrine circuit with Buddhist practice sites.

34.2934° N / 132.3168° E

08 AdmissionFree

Daigan-ji

Daigan-ji on Miyajima has long been linked to Benzaiten worship and to the island's shrine-temple history before Meiji-era separations of Shinto and Buddhism. Halls near the shore preserve Buddhist practice beside Itsukushima's shrine precinct. The temple remains part of the island's layered sacred landscape.

Shukkeien Garden

A central pond, bridges, streams, and extended paths structure this compact landscape garden beside tea service rooms. Flowering trees and koi water mark seasonal changes along the circuit. The grounds offer a quieter pause a short distance from downtown streets.

10 AdmissionFree

Mazda Zoom-Zoom Stadium Hiroshima

Mazda Zoom-Zoom Stadium Hiroshima, also known as Hiroshima Municipal Stadium, is the home of the Hiroshima Toyo Carp baseball team. The stadium offers tours and is conveniently located a short walk from Hiroshima Station. It has a spacious and open design with comfortable seating that adheres to Major League standards. The 600m-long concourse behind the seats allows for easy movement and access to concession stands.

34.3919° N / 132.4846° E

原爆の子の像

The Children's Peace Monument (原爆の子の像) in Hiroshima Peace Memorial Park commemorates child victims of the 1945 atomic bombing, including Sadako Sasaki. Paper crane offerings surround the figure. The memorial stands near the park's central paths and is open as part of the free park grounds.

34.3941° N / 132.4528° E

12 AdmissionFree

Okonomimura

Postwar street vendors who gathered around Shintenchi gave rise to this multi-floor food hall. Diners move between counters where cabbage, noodles, and batter cook to order as Hiroshima-style okonomiyaki. Floors stay noisy and practical rather than formal.

34.3914° N / 132.4619° E

13 AdmissionFree

Flame of Peace

A flame has burned in Hiroshima Peace Memorial Park since 1964 beneath a sculpture shaped like two hands opening toward the sky, and the memorial is dedicated to atomic bomb victims and expresses the stated aim that the fire remain until nuclear weapons no longer exist. Positioned on the central axis between the museum and cenotaph, it forms part of a planned commemorative sequence.

34.3934° N / 132.4528° E

14 AdmissionFree

Mitaki Temple

Mitaki-dera climbs a wooded ravine northwest of central Hiroshima, with pagodas, stone Buddhas, and three waterfalls along the approach. Maple and cedar cover the slopes that frame the temple halls. Paths cross short bridges over the stream between worship buildings and viewing spots.

34.4201° N / 132.4384° E
