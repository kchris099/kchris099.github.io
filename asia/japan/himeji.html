Himeji

Himeji grew below Himeji Castle, a hilltop fortress largely completed in 1609 for Tokugawa-era lord Ikeda Terumasa. The white plaster and wooden keep survived wars and earthquakes with careful maintenance. The castle was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1993 as one of Japan's surviving feudal complexes. The city lies on the Sanyo Shinkansen route between Osaka and Okayama in Hyogo Prefecture.

Himeji Castle

Himeji Castle is a visitor site in Himeji. This hilltop castle in Hyōgo Prefecture survives as a rare intact example of feudal Japanese fortification, with 83 buildings linked by walls and gates. White plaster walls earned it the nicknames White Heron or White Egret Castle. The main keep dates to the early 1600s and underwent major restoration before reopening in 2015.

Himeji Park

Himeji Park is a visitor site in Himeji. Parkland rings the castle moats and outer walls where samurai quarters once stood. Cherry trees line paths that lead to viewpoints of the white keep and stone ramparts. The grounds are popular for hanami picnics in spring and serve as a buffer between the monument and the modern city.

Koko-en Garden

Opened in 1992 for Himeji's centennial, this complex contains nine walled gardens in Edo-period styles on the site of former samurai mansions. Ponds, tea houses, and bamboo groves sit a short walk south of the castle. Combined tickets with the castle are available at the garden entrance.

Harima-no-Kuni Sohsha-Gomon shrine (Old Prefectural Shrine)

Harima no Kuni Sōsha Shrine, located southeast of Himeji Castle, has a rich history dating back to the year 787 when the Hyozu god was moved to Onoe. The shrine's Mitsuyama-taisai festival is a significant cultural event held every 20 years since 1533, featuring rituals and traditional clothing. Travellers can admire the red torii gate and explore the large grounds with smaller torii gates.

Ikarugadera

Ikarugadera is a visitor site in Himeji. The Remains of Aboshi Jinya offer a fascinating glimpse into the region's rich history, which dates back to ancient times when it served as a vital hub for water transport, particularly for timber from upstream. During the Edo period, this area was under the jurisdiction of the Himeji domain but later transitioned to the control of the Kyogoku family from Tatsuno domain.
