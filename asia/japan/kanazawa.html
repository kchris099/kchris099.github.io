Kanazawa

Maeda daimyo, among the richest outside the Tokugawa house, built Kanazawa as a castle town with Kenrokuen garden, samurai quarters, and gold-leaf crafts. Escape from wartime bombing left Edo-period street patterns rare elsewhere in Japan. Teahouse lanes in Higashi Chaya are the social counterpart of that domain wealth. Omicho Market supplied the castle town's kitchens and still feeds the contemporary centre.

Nomura-ke Samurai Heritage Residence

The Nomura-ke residence preserves an Edo-period samurai house once owned by a wealthy clan serving Kanazawa's Maeda lords. Restored rooms open onto a carefully tended garden with stone lanterns and a small stream. Tatami chambers, weapon displays, and garden views show how upper-ranking retainers lived inside the old castle town.

Nagamachi Samurai District

Naga-machi District, located on the west side of the city and near Korinbo District, is a village known for its cobblestone streets and well-preserved samurai homes. Travellers can explore a collection of restored samurai houses along two canals, with the option to enter and look around. Walking north along the canal behind Kohrinbo 109 Building and then south along the next canal leads to various restaurants and cafes.

Higashi Chaya District

Higashi Chaya, Kanazawa's Eastern Teahouse District, keeps rows of latticed wooden houses from the city's geisha quarter. Some buildings still host traditional entertainment; others sell gold leaf crafts, sweets, and local dishes. Narrow lanes and timber facades make the neighborhood one of the clearest surviving chaya streetscapes in the city.

Kazuemachi Chaya District

Kazuemachi is one of Kanazawa's three historic chaya entertainment quarters, quieter than Higashi but equally lined with wooden teahouse fronts. Lattice windows and low eaves face lanes once reserved for evening performances. A short walk links it to the Asano River, where the same timber architecture continues along the water.

Kanazawa Station

Kanazawa Station's glass Motenashi dome and timber gate greet arrivals on the Hokuriku Shinkansen and local lines. Concourse shops and the multi-floor Kanazawa Forus complex sit beside the platforms. From the east exit, buses and taxis fan out toward Kenrokuen, the castle, and the chaya districts within a few minutes' ride.

Tsuzumi-mon Gate

The Tsuzumi-mon Gate at Kanazawa Station is a strikingly designed and colored gate with twisting supporting pillars, resembling a traditional drum. The large red structure greets travellers as they exit the west side of the station. Unlike a regular torii gate, the pillars of this gate are modeled after tsuzumi drums used in traditional Japanese Noh theater, giving them their unique shape and name.

Kanazawa Castle Park

Kanazawa Castle Park occupies the former seat of the Maeda family, who ruled the wealthy Kaga domain through the Edo period; broad baileys connect with Kenrokuen, while ongoing archaeological work continues to refine the site's interpretation. Fires destroyed many buildings, but stone walls, moats, gates, storehouses, and carefully researched reconstructions reveal changing defensive styles.

Gyokusen-inmaru Garden

Beside Kanazawa Castle, this reconstructed garden keeps a quieter pace than the main castle routes. A small tea house serves matcha and wagashi within the grounds. Paths and plantings follow a Japanese garden plan with limited tourist overflow from the castle next door.

Oyama Shrine

Maeda Toshiie and his wife Matsu are honored at Oyama Shrine near Kanazawa Castle. Its nineteenth-century Shinmon gate combines Japanese, Chinese and European forms, including colored glass in an upper stage once used as a landmark. A pond garden, statues and quieter inner paths surround the worship buildings, linking Meiji-era design with memory of the Kaga domain founders.

Ishikawa Gate

Ishikawa-mon Gate is a restored gate located east of Kanazawa Castle, facing Kenrokuen Garden. It has historical significance as the residential castle of the Maeda family since 1583. The gate survived a fire in 1881 that destroyed other castle walls and was part of a restoration project from 1998 to rebuild wooden structures on a large scale.

Kenroku-en

Kenroku-en is one of Japan's most celebrated landscaped gardens, known as one of the country's 'Three Famous Gardens.' The name refers to the six classical elements of an ideal garden, and it offers a directly transition through sculpted pine trees, stone lanterns, and ponds. Originally part of a castle's grounds, this garden has developed over several hundred years and is designed to be in every season.

36.5621° N / 136.6627° E

Shirayama Hime Shrine

Shirayama Hime Shrine climbs a wooded slope above the plains near Kanazawa, reached by mossy stone stairs. Lion-head costumes used in local dances appear at festival times, and a gondola serves the hillside approaches. From higher ground the flood plain opens below, tying the mountain shrine to the farming villages it has long watched over.

36.4349° N / 136.6365° E

13 AdmissionFree

Kinken-gu Shrine

Kinkengu stands in Tsurugi in Hakusan City and is associated in popular belief with prosperity and business fortune. A wooded approach leads to a compact shrine complex with worship halls and smaller sacred structures. Recent attention to its financial symbolism has expanded visitation, but the site's stable character comes from local religious history and its place within the surrounding town.

36.4500° N / 136.6299° E

Myouryuji (Ninja Temple)

Built for the Maeda clan in Teramachi, Myoryuji doubled as a defended outpost protecting approaches to Kanazawa Castle, which earned its Ninja Temple nickname. Guided routes show hidden stairs and defensive tricks in the timber fabric. Access follows temple tour rules rather than free wandering.
