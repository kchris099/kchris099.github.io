Yokohama

Yokohama opened its port in 1859 after Japan signed treaties ending isolation, becoming one of the first places where foreigners could trade and reside. The Bluff and Yamate districts retain consulates and homes from the late 19th century. The Great Kanto earthquake of 1923 and air raids in 1945 damaged the city. Red Brick Warehouse and Minato Mirai skyline reflect later harbor redevelopment on Tokyo Bay.

Kishamichi Promenade

Kishamichi Promenade follows a former railway trestle through Yokohama's Minato Mirai waterfront, converting old tracks into a pedestrian deck. Warehouses and new towers line the walk toward the Cosmo Clock Ferris wheel area. The path helps stitch Kannai and the Yokohama Station districts across the redeveloped harbor.

Yokohama Red Brick Warehouse

The Yokohama Red Brick Warehouse is a historical building that is used as a complex that includes a shopping mall, banquet hall, and event venues. The complex, officially known as the Newport Pier Bonded Warehouse, was originally used as customs buildings, and has two sections: Warehouse No.1 and No.2. It is operated by Yokohama Akarenga Co.

03 AdmissionFree

Yamashita Park

Yamashita Park follows Yokohama's waterfront on land created from debris after the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake; the park connects Chinatown and the former foreign settlement with the bay, while seasonal events often occupy sections of the promenade. Lawns, flower beds, monuments, fountains and paths face the harbor, Hikawa Maru and modern port skyline.

35.4457° N / 139.6496° E

04 AdmissionFree

Yokohama Chinatown

Chinese merchants and workers settled near Yokohama's treaty port from the nineteenth century, shaping the district now known as Chinatown, and festivals and tourism have changed the area, but active associations and religious institutions maintain links with local Chinese community history. Ornamental gates, temples, restaurants, grocers, bakeries, and medicine shops fill a dense street network.

Gundam Factory Yokohama

Gundam Factory Yokohama opened in December 2020 with an eighteen-meter moving RX-78 sculpture on the waterfront. Exhibition halls explain the engineering behind the life-size robot. Night illuminations turn the figure into a harbor landmark for franchise fans and casual passersby.

Yokohama Marine Tower

Yokohama Marine Tower, built in 1961 as a lattice lighthouse and observation tower, rises 106 meters above the waterfront. Closure in 2006 led to restoration and reopening in 2009 for the port city's 150th anniversary. Decks look across the harbor toward Yamashita Park and departing ships.

07 AdmissionFree

Yokohama Stadium

Yokohama Stadium opened in 1978 inside Yokohama Park and is the home ground of the Yokohama DeNA BayStars. Renovations and expansions have updated the ballpark while preserving its compact urban setting near Chinatown and government offices. Baseball dominates its identity, though concerts and other large events also use the circular venue. The surrounding park softens the stadium's dense urban setting.

Yokohama Landmark Tower

Yokohama Landmark Tower rises above Minato Mirai with offices, a hotel, shops, restaurants and the Sky Garden observation floor. Completed in the 1990s, the tower was Japan's tallest building for two decades. High-speed elevators reach the deck, where views extend over the port, city and Mount Fuji in clear conditions, though haze and weather often limit distance.

Nogeyama Zoological Gardens

Nogeyama Zoo opened in 1951 on a hill above central Yokohama and keeps more than ninety species, including large cats. Paths climb between outdoor enclosures and indoor houses across the compact hillside site. Nakayoshi Hiroba offers a small-animal contact area for children within the park.

Zoorasia

Zoorasia is a zoo that gives visitors an immersive experience through its expansive open-air habitat zones, showing a diverse array of animals. This unique facility allows visitors to encounter rare species like okapis and Goodfellow's tree kangaroos while exploring exhibits organized by global climate zones.

Kawasaki Daishi

Kawasaki Daishi (Heiken-ji) is a major Shingon temple known for charms against misfortune and New Year pilgrimages from across the Kanto plain. Large halls and busy approach shops fill the precinct in Kawasaki, just north of Yokohama. Incense smoke and goma fire rites structure many visits.

35.5347° N / 139.7295° E

13 AdmissionFree

Soji-ji

Sojiji moved from the Noto Peninsula to Yokohama after a devastating fire in the late nineteenth century. As one of the two head temples of Soto Zen, its broad Tsurumi precinct contains training halls, gates, ceremonial buildings, memorials, and active monastic quarters. Public paths reveal the scale of the institution.

35.5077° N / 139.6707° E

14 AdmissionFree

Nissan Stadium

Nissan Stadium stands in Shin-Yokohama Park as a large multi-use arena built for football and athletics. It hosted the 2002 FIFA World Cup final and remains associated with Yokohama F. Marinos, while concerts and major events also use the venue. Capacity, naming rights, tours, and schedules change, and baseball takes place at the separate Yokohama Stadium.

35.5100° N / 139.6064° E

Yokohama Hakkeijima Sea Paradise

Hakkeijima Sea Paradise occupies an island in southern Yokohama with aquariums, rides, and resort hotels facing Tokyo Bay. Ferry and rail links deliver visitors to a pier gated into the park. Marine shows and shoreline paths fill a full day away from the Minato Mirai skyline.
