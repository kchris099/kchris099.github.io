Hakuba

Historically a quiet agricultural and trading post on the Salt Road through the Japanese Alps, Hakuba remained isolated until the post-war skiing boom. The 1998 Winter Olympics propelled the remote valley onto the global stage, solidifying its status as a winter sports hub. Visitors today can clearly trace the detailed layers of this unique heritage throughout the preserved streets and monuments.

Hakuba Ski Jumping Stadium

Hakuba Ski Jumping Stadium hosted Olympic jumping events in 1998 and still shows the inrun towers above the valley. Between September and October, red buckwheat flowers carpet fields near the jumps before harvest for soba. Outside snow season, visitors walk the base area and look up the competition hills.

Hakuba Happo Onsen (Hot Spring)

Hakuba Happo Onsen draws alkaline hot spring water associated with the geology of the Northern Alps, and despite its size, the onsen offers excellent views of the mountains from the outdoor pool. The facility may feel relatively new and small, with only one indoor and one outdoor bath available.

snow peak Land Station Hakuba

Kengo Kuma designed Snow Peak Land Station Hakuba as a low timber-and-fabric complex facing the Northern Alps; displays focus on field equipment suited to the Northern Alps climate around Hakuba's ski and hiking valleys. The site combines an outdoor-equipment store, restaurant or cafe functions, event space and areas introducing camping culture.

Cherry and other flowering trees bloom in stages across the park, drawing hanami crowds when petals peak. Paths, lawns, and shade structure everyday use outside those blossom weeks. Posted hours govern access to the green space for local families and overnight guests in Hakuba.

Hakuba Happo Onsen Obinata no yu

Obinata no Yu draws slightly alkaline hot-spring water linked to the Himekawa River system at Hakuba Happo. Outdoor baths open toward the mountain valley in a spacious rotenburo layout. Skiers and hikers use the onsen as a recovery stop after days on the slopes or trails.

Happo pond

Happo Ike pond is a popular tourist attraction located on the ridge trail leading to Mt. Karamatsu. The lake offers reflections of the surrounding peaks, making it a sight to behold on clear days. Tourists can reach the lake by taking a gondola and lift to Happo Sanso, followed by traversing through forests and mountain paths. The lake sits at an elevation of 2080m and during winter, it freezes over turning into a walkable terrain.

Kurobishidaira

Above Hakuba, Kurobishidaira is a high-elevation station and alpine terrace on the Happo-one route, reached by lift from the Kurobishi area. Summer trails continue toward Happo-ike and the Northern Alps, while winter access belongs to the ski network. Weather, lift schedules, snow and trail closures determine which parts of the mountain route are reachable. The terrace also provides a transition between lift travel and alpine walking.

36.6988° N / 137.8055° E

Mount Shirouma

Mount Shirouma, standing tall at 2,932 meters (9,620 feet), is a gem located the Hakuba section of the Hida Mountains. Known as one of Japan's top climbing destinations, this majestic peak has steep trails that lead adventurers through lush greenery during summer and year-round snow fields in its deep-snow gorge.

Hakuba Goryu Alpine Botanical Gardens

Alpine plants from Japan and other mountain regions grow across Hakuba Goryu's upper slopes, reached by gondola from the ski resort base. Marked paths pass seasonal flowers, restored habitats, and views toward the Northern Alps. Bloom periods shift with elevation and weather, while lift operation and trail conditions determine access more than the calendar of a lowland botanical garden.
