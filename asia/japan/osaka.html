Osaka

Osaka grew around Ishiyama Hongan-ji and later Osaka Castle, built by Toyotomi Hideyoshi in the 1580s. The city became a merchant hub with the slogan tenka no daini kaiden, or the nation's kitchen, supplying rice and goods to Edo. Canals and warehouses lined the Yodo River delta. Industrialization and expos in 1970 and 2025 shaped modern districts. Dotonbori and Shinsekai retain showa-era commercial streets.

Osaka Castle

Osaka Castle is one of Japan's most famous and historically significant landmarks. Originally built by Toyotomi Hideyoshi in the late 16th century, the massive fortress played a major role in the unification of Japan. The current main keep is a modern reconstruction housing a historical museum, surrounded by stone walls, wide moats, and expansive public parks.

Dotonbori

Dotonbori follows a canal through Osaka's Namba district between Dotonboribashi and Nipponbashi, once a theater quarter and now a dense nightlife strip. Giant illuminated signs, including the Glico runner, face the water. Restaurants and arcades pack both banks from afternoon into late night.

Glico Sign Dotonbori

The illuminated Glico Running Man sign faces Dotonbori Canal from a building near Ebisubashi bridge. Osaka Tenmangu Shrine

Osaka Tenmangu honors Sugawara no Michizane, the scholar-deity Tenjin, within a shrine traditionally founded in the tenth century. Repeated fires destroyed earlier buildings, and the present main structures largely date from nineteenth-century rebuilding; the shrine anchors the Tenjin Matsuri, when land and river processions carry portable shrines through Osaka each July.

Red brick, pale stone bands and a central dome distinguish Osaka City Central Public Hall on Nakanoshima. Completed in 1918 through a major civic donation, the building contains assembly rooms, a large auditorium and restored decorative interiors; lectures, concerts, ceremonies and public tours continue its original civic purpose beside the river and historic library.

Nakanoshima Rose Garden occupies the eastern end of the island between Osaka's Dojima and Tosabori rivers. Formal beds display many rose varieties around paths, lawns, bridges and the riverfront, with the strongest bloom usually occurring in spring and autumn. Mido-Suji

Mido-suji forms Osaka's broad north-south boulevard between Umeda, Shinsaibashi and Namba. Built and widened during the twentieth century, it is lined with ginkgo trees, offices, flagship shops, sculpture and subway stations. Seasonal lighting and events change its appearance, while Shinsaibashi-suji and Amerikamura remain separate commercial areas east and west of the avenue.

Tsutenkaku rises 108 meters over Shinsekai in Osaka's Naniwa Ward, with a main observation deck at 91 meters. Hitachi neon has long marked the tower as a local landmark. Below it, retro shopping streets and kushikatsu restaurants define the Shinsekai neighborhood that grew with the tower's fame.

Shitenno-ji

Shitenno-ji is often counted among Japan's earliest officially established Buddhist temples, traditionally linked to Prince Shotoku. Halls and a central pagoda have been rebuilt many times, most recently in 1963. The layout still follows an ancient axis of gate, pagoda, and golden hall in southern Osaka.

Shinsekai

Shinsekai, a active district in Osaka established in 1912, is a feast for the senses and a true representation of the city's culinary heritage. Dominated by the Tsutenkaku Tower, this area buzzes with life and color. While it may not be the most upscale part of town, Shinsekai has been delighting locals and travellers alike with its beloved soul foods for over a century.

More than a thousand animals from over two hundred species occupy the grounds, including lions, chimpanzees, and koalas. Daisen-ryo Kofun

Daisen Kofun is a vast keyhole-shaped burial mound within Sakai's Mozu tomb group and is traditionally associated with Emperor Nintoku, although the occupant has not been archaeologically confirmed. Three moats and dense vegetation surround the earthwork. Ground-level views are limited to designated edges and nearby observation points, so maps and aerial images are important for understanding its full scale and form.
