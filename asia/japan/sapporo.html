Sapporo

Sapporo was laid out in the Meiji era as a planned grid capital for Hokkaido's colonisation, with American advisors influencing parks and agriculture. The clock tower, beer industry, and later Winter Olympics of 1972 marked its rise. Ainu land and history precede the settler city. Snow festivals, subway lines, and Odori Park's central boulevard now define Japan's largest northern metropolis.

Tanukikoji Shopping Street

Tanukikoji, nicknamed Raccoon Alley, is a covered shopping street running about one kilometer across ten blocks of central Sapporo. Arcades shelter restaurants, boutiques, and drugstores through heavy snow seasons. It has operated as a merchant spine since the city's early growth on the Ishikari plain.

Sapporo TV Tower

Sapporo TV Tower rises from the eastern end of Odori Park, where a clock and observation deck look along the park's long central axis. Built in the 1950s as a broadcasting tower, it now functions mainly as a city landmark and visitor attraction. Enclosed views cover central Sapporo and nearby mountains, while seasonal festivals transform the park below.

Hokkaido Shrine Tongu

Established in 1869, the shrine enshrines four deities and hosts the Sapporo Matsuri from 14 to 16 June with ceremonies and processions through the grounds. The precinct sits among the city's older ritual sites. Grounds open for ordinary worship outside festival peaks.

04 AdmissionFree

JR Tower

JR Tower opened in 2003 as a 173-meter complex directly above Sapporo Station. Observation floors, offices, and department-store levels connect to rail, subway, and bus terminals. The tower functions as the main indoor gateway between arriving trains and the downtown grid.

Suwa Shrine

Suwa Shrine in central Sapporo is a compact Shinto precinct amid the downtown grid near Tanukikoji. Local festivals and ordinary neighborhood rites continue here despite the surrounding office blocks. Torii and a small hall offer a brief pause between the station area and the covered shopping streets.

Sapporo Beer Museum

Galleries trace how beer brewing took root in Hokkaido, ending for many visitors in a tasting flight of house styles in the brick complex. Rooms open on posted hours along the museum route. The stop pairs industrial history with a short guided sample.

07 AdmissionFree

Hōhei Kan

Hoheikan began as a Western-style government guest house in 1880 and later moved to Nakajima Park, and imperial visitors and civic events shaped its history, while restoration preserves the building as a cultural property and event venue. Its white timber facade, blue trim, chandeliers, staircases, and reception rooms combine American-influenced design with Japanese craftsmanship.

Hokkaido Jingu

Hokkaido Jingu, founded in 1869 in Maruyama, enshrines deities including Emperor Meiji and spirits tied to the island's pioneer settlement. Torii, gravel courts, and forested approaches fill a large shrine precinct. Locals still bring offerings through the year, especially at New Year.

Hokkaido Shrine Mikado

Hokkaido Shrine's mikado approach in Sapporo's Chuo Ward leads into grounds founded in 1869 under Emperor Meiji for the pioneer deities known as Kaitaku Sanshin. Cedar-lined paths open to large halls used for civic and seasonal rites. The shrine remains a spiritual center for Hokkaido's settlement history.

Sapporo City Maruyama Zoo

Maruyama Zoo opened in 1951 beside Sapporo's Maruyama Park and has gradually replaced older enclosures with larger, climate-conscious habitats. Polar bears, elephants, primates, native species and other animals appear across themed zones. Species numbers, breeding programs and exhibit access change, so current zoo information is more reliable than promotional rankings by size.

Shiroi Koibito Park

Ishiya operates Shiroi Koibito Park as a confectionery-themed visitor complex in western Sapporo; factory-viewing areas, exhibits, gardens, shops, cafes and optional workshops center on the company's sandwich cookies and chocolate products. European-inspired facades create a theatrical setting, while the site's main purpose remains brand interpretation and food production.

Mt. Moiwa Summit Observation Deck

Mount Moiwa rises southwest of central Sapporo, with a ropeway and short cable car leading toward the summit observation deck. Clear conditions reveal the city grid, Ishikari Plain, surrounding mountains and a distant section of coastline. Snow, cloud and strong wind can interrupt transport, while the elevated view helps explain how Sapporo spreads across a broad basin bordered by wooded hills.

Mt. Moiwa

Mount Moiwa rises above southwestern Sapporo with night views across the city grid and Ishikari Bay. A ropeway and the Morris Car incline finish the climb to the summit observatory. Winter illuminations and clear summer evenings both draw residents seeking the wide urban panorama.
