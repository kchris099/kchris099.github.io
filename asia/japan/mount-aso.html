Mount Aso

Mount Aso is an active volcano in central Kyushu with one of the world's largest calderas, formed in eruptions roughly 90,000 years ago. Grasslands called Kusasenri lie inside the rim, used for grazing and tourism. The Nakadake crater releases steam and is monitored for safety. Nearby towns such as Aso and Takamori grew around shrines and routes crossing the caldera. Volcanic soil supports agriculture in surrounding Kumamoto Prefecture.

Kusasenrigahama

Kusasenrigahama spreads as a grassy basin near Mount Aso's active central cones, and Kusasenrigahama is a broad grassy crater meadow inside the Aso caldera in Aso Kuju National Park. Ponds, grazing horses and cattle, walking areas, and broad volcanic views change with season, weather, and ash conditions.

Kamishikimi Kumanoimasu Shrine

A long mossy stone stair lined with nearly a hundred lanterns climbs to the shrine through deep cedar shade. Popular culture has used the approach as a visual reference, and locals treat the precinct as a power spot. The walk up is as memorable as the halls above.

32.8228° N / 131.1492° E

04 AdmissionFree

Aso Shrine

Aso Shrine ranks among Japan's older Shinto centers, long tied to agricultural rites around the Aso volcano. Fire festivals and farming prayers still mark the calendar for surrounding villages. Large wooden halls and stone lanterns sit at the caldera floor, linking shrine life to the active volcanic landscape above.

32.9482° N / 131.1147° E

Takachiho Gorge

Sheer columnar cliffs rise from emerald water in this narrow volcanic gorge cut by the Gokase River. Rental rowboats pass close to Minainotaki waterfall along the channel below. Walkways above the water give another angle on the same basalt walls and spray.

32.7013° N / 131.3005° E

06 AdmissionFree

Ama-no-Iwato Shrine

Ama-no-Iwato Shrine stands near the cave where myth places Amaterasu's hiding, said to have darkened the world until she was lured out. The Shinto precinct treats the gorge and cave mouth as sacred ground. River paths and shrine buildings frame a landscape that local tradition reads as the stage of that solar myth.

32.7337° N / 131.3524° E
