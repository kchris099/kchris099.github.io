Kyoto

Kyoto served as Japan's imperial capital from 794 to 1868, when the court moved to Tokyo. Temples, shrines, and gardens from the Heian, medieval, and Edo periods line its eastern hills and river valleys. The city was largely spared from air raids in 1945. Seventeen historic monuments in Kyoto were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1994. Traditional crafts and festivals continue in preserved districts.

Kyoto Sento Imperial Palace

In Japan, the Sentō Imperial Palace traditionally does not refer to a single location, but to any residence of retired emperors. Before Emperor Akihito abdicated in 2019, the last Emperor to retire did so in 1817, so the designation commonly refers to the Kyoto Sentō Imperial Palace.

Ninenzaka

Restored machiya along this stone-paved slope hold craft shops, tea houses, and sweet stalls between Kodai-ji and Kiyomizu-dera. Walkers often pair it with parallel Sannenzaka. Together the lanes form one of Kyoto's most photographed historic streetscapes.

Sannenzaka

Chionin Temple

Chion-in is the head temple of the Jodo school founded on the teachings of Honen. Its vast Higashiyama precinct includes the Sanmon, Miedo, Amidado, gardens, subtemples, and steep routes rising above Kyoto. Fires and rebuilding shaped the present halls, while daily chanting and memorial rites continue to connect the monumental architecture with active Pure Land Buddhist practice.

Chionin Sanmon Gate

Chion-in's two-story Sanmon dominates the lower entrance to the temple precinct. Tokugawa patronage produced the early seventeenth-century wooden gate, whose broad roof and massive columns frame the climb toward the main halls. Upper interiors contain Buddhist images and painted decoration but are normally closed except for special openings, so the public experience is usually from the plaza below.

Maruyama Park

Kyoto's main hanami ground sits at the base of the eastern hills near Yasaka Shrine and the Gion district. A weeping cherry, lit at night in season, draws the densest evening crowds. Outside blossom weeks the lawns and paths stay quieter for ordinary walks.

At the east end of Shijo-dori, gates, main hall, and stage form the Gion-faith precinct dedicated chiefly to Susanoo, with Kushinadahime and eight offspring deities beside him. Festival traffic spills from here into surrounding streets. Lanterns and vermilion paint mark the approach.

Nene-no-michi

Nene-no-michi follows a stone-paved lane through Kyoto's Higashiyama district and is named for Kita no Mandokoro, commonly called Nene, the widow of Toyotomi Hideyoshi. The route passes Kodaiji, which she founded in his memory. Smaller temples, gardens, shops and preserved streets continue toward Maruyama Park. The historical association is carried by the surrounding route and religious sites.

Hokan-ji Temple

Hokan-ji is remembered mainly for the five-story Yasaka Pagoda, the last major structure of a much older temple complex in Higashiyama. Tradition links the foundation to settlers from Goguryeo and places the site among Kyoto's earliest Buddhist grounds. The pagoda rises above Sannen-zaka and Ninen-zaka, framing many views of eastern Kyoto.

Kiyomizu-dera

Founded in 778, Otowa-san Kiyomizu-dera belongs to the Kita-Hosso lineage, with a hibutsu Juichimen Kannon as honzon. The present main hall of 1633 projects from the hillside on a wooden terrace built without nails. Crowds gather on that balcony for the city and valley view.

Kyoto Shinkyogoku Shopping Street

Shinkyogoku is a covered shopping street running roughly five hundred meters between Sanjo and Shijo in central Kyoto. Created in the Meiji period through a district containing temples and entertainment, it now holds souvenir shops, food counters, fashion stores, game arcades, and religious entrances. The parallel Teramachi arcade broadens the commercial network but remains a distinct street.

Fushimi Inari Taisha

Thousands of vermilion torii form paths up Mount Inari behind Fushimi Inari Taisha, the head shrine of the Inari faith; worship centers on deities associated with rice, agriculture, business and prosperity, represented throughout the precinct by fox messengers. Senbon Torii

A torii is a traditional Japanese gate most commonly found at the entrance of or within a Shinto shrine, where it symbolically marks the transition from the mundane to the sacred, and a spot where kami are welcomed and thought to travel through. The presence of a torii at the entrance is usually the simplest way to identify Shinto shrines, and a small torii icon represents them on Japanese road maps and on Google Maps.

Arashiyama Bamboo Forest

Tall moso bamboo borders a short public path in Arashiyama between Tenryuji's northern area and the Sagano neighborhood. The grove is neither a vast wilderness nor a closed botanical garden; it forms part of a heavily used local route shared by pedestrians and service traffic. Light, wind, and the sound of moving stalks shape the experience, while early hours reduce congestion.

Nijō Castle

Tokugawa Ieyasu ordered Nijo Castle in 1603 as the shogun's Kyoto residence. Ninomaru Palace preserves painted rooms, carved transoms, audience chambers and floors designed to sound underfoot, while walls, moats, gates and gardens structure the larger site. The restoration of imperial rule was formally announced here in 1867, giving the complex importance beyond its architecture.

Kinkaku-ji

Kinkaku-ji began in the late fourteenth century as a retirement villa for shogun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu before becoming a Zen temple. Its gold-leaf upper stories reflect in a mirror pond framed by pines. Paths circle the water so the pavilion can be seen from several angles without entering the building itself.

Nishiki Market

Nishiki Market, a historic gem in Kyoto, has been delighting food lovers for over 400 years. This active covered market stretches five blocks between Shijo and Sanjo streets and is often referred to as 'Kyoto's Kitchen.' With more than a hundred stalls, each specializing in unique Japanese delicacies, from fresh produce to artisanal snacks, travellers can explore an array of local ingredients.

Sanjūsangendō Temple

Sanjusangendo takes its name from the thirty-three spaces between columns along its exceptionally long wooden hall. Inside, a central thousand-armed Kannon is flanked by one thousand standing Kannon figures and guardian deities, creating a dense sculptural assembly from the twelfth and thirteenth centuries. The present hall dates from reconstruction after a fire and is an active temple.

Philosopher's Path (Tetsugaku no Michi), North End

The Philosopher's Path, also known as Tetsugaku-no-michi, is a walkway along a narrow river adorned with an abundance of cherry blossom trees. The path leads to the Silver Pavilion, which may not be as extravagant as the Golden Pavilion but still holds its own charm. Visiting during the Sakura peak bloom offers a magical experience with petals blanketing the canals and occasional gusts of wind causing more to fall.

Shimogamo Shrine

Shimogamo Shrine stands within Tadasu-no-Mori at the meeting of the Kamo and Takano rivers. Tenryu-ji

Tenryuji was founded in the fourteenth century under shogun Ashikaga Takauji and became the leading Zen temple of Kyoto's Five Mountains system. Repeated fires destroyed many early buildings, but the garden attributed to Muso Soseki preserves a pond composition using Arashiyama as borrowed scenery. Halls, paintings, and the garden lie beside the bamboo-grove route in western Kyoto.

Kitano Tenmangu Shrine

Located Kyoto, Kitano Tenmangu Shrine has a rich history dating back to the 10th century. This revered site is dedicated to Sugawara Michizane, celebrated as the God of Learning in Shinto belief. A highlight of visiting Kitano Tenmangu is its collection of 1,500 plum trees that burst into bloom from early February through late March.

Katsura Imperial Villa

Prince Toshihito and his heirs developed Katsura Imperial Villa during the seventeenth century as a retreat shaped by court culture and tea practice. A pond organizes the garden route, linking residences, bridges and tea houses through carefully framed views. The restrained architecture, stepping-stone paths and changing relationship between buildings and water became influential in later studies of Japanese design.

Eisai founded the temple in the early thirteenth century, and later rebuilding produced halls, dry gardens, painted screens, and a ceiling dragon created for the temple's anniversary. Enryaku-ji Temple

Enryaku-ji, founded by Saicho in 788, is the head temple of Tendai Buddhism on Mount Hiei above Kyoto and Lake Biwa. It once supported thousands of warrior-monks across Todo, Saito, and Yokawa. Cable cars and buses reach the summit; clear days open wide views over the Kyoto basin from the ridge trails.

Kuramadera Temple

Located on a mountainside, Kuramadera Temple is a historic Buddhist temple accessible by cable car or through a challenging climb up numerous steps. It holds significance as the place where Minamoto no Yoshitsune, an early 12th-century military commander, was raised after his family's tragic demise. Legend has it that he encountered a mythical creature during his training in the wilderness and learned swordsmanship there.
