Destination Guide — Kansai

Explore Kyoto

A Brief History

Kyoto served as Japan's imperial capital from 794 to 1868, when the court moved to Tokyo. Temples, shrines, and gardens from the Heian, medieval, and Edo periods line its eastern hills and river valleys. The city was largely spared from air raids in 1945. Seventeen historic monuments in Kyoto were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1994. Traditional crafts and festivals continue in preserved districts.

Kyoto Sento Imperial Palace

In Japan, the Sentō Imperial Palace traditionally does not refer to a single location, but to any residence of retired emperors. Before Emperor Akihito abdicated in 2019, the last Emperor to retire did so in 1817, so the designation commonly refers to the Kyoto Sentō Imperial Palace.

Free

Ninenzaka

Ninenzaka, or Ninen-zaka is an ancient stone-paved pedestrian road in Higashiyama-ku, Kyoto, Japan. The road is lined with restored traditional machiya buildings housing craft shops, tea houses, and confectionery stalls. It connects Kōdai-ji Temple with Kiyomizu-dera and is often walked in sequence with the parallel Sannenzaka lane, together forming one of Kyoto's most photographed historic streetscapes.

Free

Sannenzaka

Sannenzaka, or Sannen-zaka, is a stone-paved pedestrian road and tourist attraction in Higashiyama-ku, Kyoto, Japan. The road is lined with traditional buildings and shops, and is often paired with the similar road, Ninenzaka. The two roads lead to Kiyomizu-dera Temple, Kodaiji Temple and Yasaka-jinjia Shrine, which are a few famous sights in Kyoto.

Free (inner garden ¥500)

Chionin Temple

Chion-in in Higashiyama-ku, Kyoto, Japan is the headquarters of the Jōdo-shū founded by Hōnen, who proclaimed that sentient beings are reborn in Amida Buddha's Western Paradise by reciting the nembutsu, Amida Buddha's name. The vast compounds of Chion-in include the site where Hōnen settled to disseminate his teachings and the site where he died.

Free

Chionin Sanmon Gate

The Sanmon Gate of Chion-in Temple in Kyoto is the largest wooden temple gate in Japan, standing 24 meters tall. Built in the early 17th century, the monumental two-story structure serves as the grand entrance to the head temple of the Jodo sect of Buddhism. Its massive scale and intricate woodwork symbolize the transition from the secular world to the sacred.

Free

Maruyama Park

Maruyama Park is a park in Kyoto, Japan. It is noted as the main center for cherry blossom viewing in Kyoto, and can get extremely crowded at that time of year. The park's star attraction is a weeping cherry tree which becomes lit up at night. It sits at the base of the eastern hills near Yasaka Shrine and the Gion district.

Free

Yasaka Shrine

Yasaka Shrine, once called Gion Shrine, is a Shinto shrine in the Gion District of Kyoto, Japan. Situated at the east end of Shijō-dōri, the shrine includes several buildings, including gates, a main hall and a stage. The Yasaka shrine is dedicated to Susanoo in the tradition of the Gion faith as its chief kami, with his consort Kushinadahime on the east, and eight offspring deities on the west.

Free

Nene-no-michi

Kōdai-in, formerly known as Nene, One, Nei, was an aristocrat and Buddhist nun, founder of the temple Kōdai-ji in Kyoto, Japan. She was formerly the principal samurai wife of Toyotomi Hideyoshi under the name of Toyotomi Yoshiko. When she rose in higher political status, she took the title of "Kita no mandokoro".

¥400

Hokan-ji Temple

The Yasaka Pagoda, also known as the Tower of Yasaka, is a Buddhist pagoda located in Higashiyama-ku, Kyoto, Japan. The 5-story tall pagoda is the last remaining structure of a 6th-century temple complex known as Hōkan-ji. It was founded by the Goguryeo clan that established in Japan, and is the oldest temple in Kyoto.

¥500

Kiyomizu-dera

Kiyomizu-dera is a Buddhist temple located in eastern Kyoto, Japan. It belongs to the Kita-Hosso sect of Japanese Buddhism and its honzon is a hibutsu statue of Jūichimen Kannon. The temple's full name is Otowa-san Kiyomizu-dera. Founded in 778, the current main hall dates to 1633 and was built without nails, projecting from the hillside on a wooden terrace.

Free

Kyoto Shinkyogoku Shopping Street

Shinkyōgoku Street is a shopping street that runs from north to south in the center of the city of Kyoto. The street extends for approximately 500 m from Sanjō Street on its northern end to Shijō Street on its southern end and it is located between Ura Teramachi Street and Teramachi Street.

Free

Nishiki Tenmangu Shrine

A small Shinto shrine situated within the Nishiki Market shopping arcade in central Kyoto. It enshrines the deity of learning and scholarship, Sugawara no Michizane, and has been part of the market district since the Edo period. The shrine is known for its ema votive tablets and annual festival in late October.

Free

Nishi Honganji Temple

Nishi Hongan-ji is a Buddhist temple in Shimogyō-ku, Kyoto, Japan. It serves as the head temple of the Jodo Shinshu Hongwanji-ha subsect. It is one of two Jōdo Shinshū temple complexes in Kyoto, the other being Higashi Hongan-ji, which is the head temple of the Ōtani-ha subsect.

Free

Fushimi Inari Taisha

Fushimi Inari-taisha is the head shrine of the kami Inari, located in Fushimi-ku, Kyoto, Japan. The shrine sits at the base of a mountain, also named Inari, which is 233 metres above sea level, and includes trails up the mountain to many smaller shrines which span 4 kilometres and take approximately 2 hours to walk up. It is unclear whether the mountain's name, Inariyama, or the shrine's name came first.

Free

Senbon Torii

A torii is a traditional Japanese gate most commonly found at the entrance of or within a Shinto shrine, where it symbolically marks the transition from the mundane to the sacred, and a spot where kami are welcomed and thought to travel through. The presence of a torii at the entrance is usually the simplest way to identify Shinto shrines, and a small torii icon represents them on Japanese road maps and on Google Maps.

Free

Arashiyama Bamboo Forest

The Bamboo Forest, Arashiyama Bamboo Grove, or Sagano Bamboo Forest is a natural bamboo forest in Arashiyama, Kyoto, Japan. It consists mostly of mōsō bamboo and has several pathways for tourists and visitors. The Ministry of the Environment considers it a part of the soundscape of Japan.

¥1300

Nijō Castle

Nijō Castle, constructed in 1603, is a example of cypress-wood architecture set amidst expansive gardens that have captivated travellers for centuries. Once the residence of shogun Ieyasu, this UNESCO World Heritage site offers a glimpse into Japan's feudal past. The castle grounds are particularly famous for their cherry blossoms, drawing crowds each spring to admire the fleeting beauty of these flowers.

¥500

Kinkaku-ji

Kinkaku-ji, also known as the Golden Pavilion, is a historic and serene temple nestled in the heart of Kyoto. Originally constructed in the 14th century as a retirement villa for Shogun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu, it has since transformed into a Zen Buddhist temple. The pavilion's striking gold-leaf facade glimmers beautifully against its tranquil surroundings, which include meticulously landscaped gardens and a reflective pond that enhances its picturesque charm.

Free

Nishiki Market

Nishiki Market, a historic gem in Kyoto, has been delighting food lovers for over 400 years. This vibrant covered market stretches five blocks between Shijo and Sanjo streets and is often referred to as 'Kyoto's Kitchen.' With more than a hundred stalls, each specializing in unique Japanese delicacies—from fresh produce to artisanal snacks—travellers can explore an impressive array of local ingredients.

Free

Sanjūsangendō Temple

Sanjūsangendō Temple, a remarkable Buddhist site in Kyoto, is celebrated for its collection of 1,001 life-sized wooden statues of the goddess Kannon. The temple's main hall stretches an impressive 120 meters and houses a magnificent seated figure of Senju-Kannon, crafted by the renowned sculptor Tankei. This towering statue stands at 4.8 meters tall and symbolizes infinite mercy with her thousand arms and eyes.

Free

Philosopher's Path (Tetsugaku no Michi), North End

The Philosopher's Path, also known as Tetsugaku-no-michi, is a picturesque walkway along a narrow river adorned with an abundance of cherry blossom trees. The path leads to the Silver Pavilion, which may not be as extravagant as the Golden Pavilion but still holds its own charm. Visiting during the Sakura peak bloom offers a magical experience with petals blanketing the canals and occasional gusts of wind causing more to fall.

Free

To-ji Temple

Tōji Temple is a historic Buddhist temple in Kyoto, Japan, known for its 5-story wooden pagoda and ancient sculptures of deities dating back to the 8th century. Despite being spared during World War II bombings, it holds great cultural significance. The temple is famous for its weeping cherry blossom called the fujizakura which blooms early and can be seen upon entering the grounds along with numerous other impressive cherry trees.

¥1000

Ginkaku-ji

Nestled in the serene landscapes of Kyoto, Higashiyama Jisho-ji, also known as Ginkaku-ji or the Silver Pavilion, is a 15th-century Zen temple that embodies Japan's rich cultural heritage. Originally constructed as a retirement villa for Shogun Yoshimasa Ashikaga during the tumultuous Muromachi period, this exquisite site was later transformed into a Buddhist temple after his passing.

Free

Shimogamo Shrine

One of Kyoto's oldest Shinto shrines, founded before the capital moved here in 794. The precinct sits where the Kamo and Takano rivers meet, shaded by ancient trees in Tadasu-no-Mori. Seasonal rituals include the Aoi Matsuri procession in May. The main halls and vermillion torii are approachable on foot or by bicycle from downtown.

Free

Tenryu-ji

Nestled at the base of majestic mountains, Tenryu-ji is a must-visit Zen temple in Arashiyama, just west of Kyoto. As one of Japan's five most significant Zen temples and a UNESCO World Heritage site, it offers travellers an enchanting experience with its gardens that beautifully blend with the surrounding natural landscape—a concept known as shakkei or 'borrowed scenery.'.

Free

Nidec Kyoto Tower

Kyoto Tower is a modern attraction in the historically rich city of Kyoto. Standing at 430 feet, it offers an incredible panoramic view from its observation deck, equipped with binoculars and touch-screen guides. The tower's towering spire is hard to miss in the city center and serves as a landmark. It also houses a food court and a five-star hotel.

Free

Kitano Tenmangu Shrine

Nestled in the heart of Kyoto, Kitano Tenmangu Shrine boasts a rich history dating back to the 10th century. This revered site is dedicated to Sugawara Michizane, celebrated as the God of Learning in Shinto belief. As the principal shrine among thousands across Japan, it attracts students seeking blessings for academic success. A highlight of visiting Kitano Tenmangu is its collection of 1,500 plum trees that burst into bloom from early February through late March.

Free

Katsura Imperial Villa

Katsura Imperial Villa, dating back to the 17th century, is a renowned historic site in Japan. The villa boasts splendid traditional architecture and is famous for its classical garden designed by Kobori Enshu. The garden's layout ensures that travellers always have a picturesque view, with smaller gardens surrounding a large pool against the backdrop of Mounts Arashiyama and Kameyama.

Free

Eikandō Zenrinji

Eikan-dō Temple is a major Buddhist temple in Kyoto, known for its ancient art and the famed statue of Buddha looking over his shoulder. The temple grounds are adorned with beautiful autumn leaves, creating a harmonious scene with the architecture, pond, and garden. Established in 853 by Shinjo, a student of the monk Kukai, Eikan-dō offers travellers an opportunity to refresh their souls amidst the serene beauty of nature and historical significance.

Free

Kifune Shrine

Nestled at the base of Mt. Kibune and alongside the serene Kibune River, Kifune Shrine is a hidden gem in Kyoto that beckons travellers with its lush, mountainous backdrop. This intimate Shinto shrine is renowned as the head shrine among approximately 2,000 water god shrines across Japan, dedicated to Taka-okamino-kami, the deity overseeing water.

Free

Kennin-ji Temple

Kenninji Temple, founded in 1202 by the priest Yosai, is a historic and ornate Buddhist temple located in central Gion, Kyoto's famed geisha district. It boasts traditional Zen gardens, tea ceremonies, and colorful murals. The site is catalogued as a buddhist temple.

Free

Kamigamo-jinja Shrine

The upper shrine of the Kamo faith, paired with Shimogamo downstream. Its sand cones (tatezuna) in the main court are renewed for ceremonies. The wooded approach along the Myōjin River feels quieter than central Kyoto. Aoi Matsuri rites begin here before the procession crosses the city.

Free

Nanzen-ji

Nanzen-ji Temple, located just outside Kyoto's city center, is a significant Zen temple complex with a rich history dating back to 1291. The temple features a striking entrance gate commemorating a historic battle and the Hojo main hall, which houses a renowned rock garden designed by landscape artist Kobori Enshu. The expansive grounds encompass artfully landscaped gardens, walking paths, and numerous sub-temples.

¥700

Sanzen-in Temple

A Tendai temple in rural Ohara, northeast of the city. Moss gardens, painted fusuma, and the famous smiling Amida triad in Ojo Gokuraku-in draw steady day-trip traffic. Maple color peaks in late autumn. The walk from Ohara bus stop passes shops selling local pickles and yuzu products.

¥1000

Enryaku-ji Temple

Head temple of Tendai Buddhism on Mount Hiei, overlooking Kyoto and Lake Biwa. Founded by Saicho in 788, it once housed thousands of warrior-monks. Cable car and bus routes reach the summit; walking trails link Todo, Saito, and Yokawa areas. Clear days offer wide views over the basin.

Free

Kimono Tea Ceremony MAIKOYA Nishiki

Experience the KIMONO TEA CEREMONY KYOTO MAIKOYA at NISHIKI for an authentic Japanese cultural immersion. Located in downtown Kyoto, it offers travellers the chance to don a kimono and delve into the history and significance of the tea ceremony through informative exhibitions. Led by knowledgeable hosts, guests can participate in making their own tea, gaining insight into this revered tradition.

Free

Kuramadera Temple

Nestled on a mountainside, Kuramadera Temple is a historic Buddhist temple accessible by cable car or through a challenging climb up numerous steps. It holds significance as the place where Minamoto no Yoshitsune, an early 12th-century military commander, was raised after his family's tragic demise. Legend has it that he encountered a mythical creature during his training in the wilderness and learned swordsmanship there.

Free

Ninna-ji Temple

Ninnaji Temple, a historic Buddhist temple founded in 886, is known for its picturesque gardens and a 5-story pagoda. While the UNESCO World Heritage Listed Goten Palace requires an admission fee, the temple grounds are usually free to enter except during the spring cherry blossom season. travellers can explore impressive gates, a pagoda, and other Edo Period buildings within the temple precincts.

Free

Shugakuin Imperial Villa

An Edo-period retreat built for Emperor Go-Mizunoo, with three connected gardens using borrowed scenery from the Higashiyama hills. Reservations are required through the Imperial Household Agency; guided tours follow fixed paths. The upper pond garden is especially noted for its artificial cascade and tea house.
