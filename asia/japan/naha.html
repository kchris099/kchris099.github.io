Naha

Naha is the capital of Okinawa Prefecture on the main island of the Ryukyu chain. Shuri Castle served as the royal seat of the Ryukyu Kingdom from the 15th century until annexation by Japan in 1879. The city was heavily damaged in the 1945 Battle of Okinawa. Postwar U.S. Administration lasted until 1972. Kokusai Street and public markets reflect rebuilding and trade with mainland Japan and East Asia.

kokusai-dori

Kokusai-dori is Naha's main commercial avenue, lined for many blocks with souvenir shops, restaurants, and hotels. Neon signs and covered arcades keep foot traffic moving day and night. Side streets lead toward the public market and toward Shuri, so the boulevard works as the city's primary visitor spine.

Makishi Public Market

Stalls of produce, meat, and prepared Okinawan foods fill the covered market halls during standard trading hours. Browsing the aisles costs nothing; purchases stay between seller and customer. Nearby craft counters and eateries extend a visit beyond the main food floors.

Shuri Castle

For centuries, Shuri Castle functioned as the political and ceremonial center of the Ryukyu Kingdom for centuries before Okinawa's annexation by Japan. War destroyed the complex in 1945, and reconstructions later restored gates, walls, courtyards, and the main hall. A major 2019 fire again destroyed central buildings, so the current visit includes active reconstruction and interpretation of both royal history and preservation work.

Cape Manzamo

Cape Manzamo juts from Okinawa's Onna coast in Kunigami District, where limestone cliffs drop to the East China Sea. A broad rock ledge and lookout paths are the usual stopping points. Buses from Naha reach the cape in under two hours, making it a standard northern day trip from the capital.

06 AdmissionFree

Bise Beach

Bise Beach lies beside the village of Bise on Okinawa's Motobu Peninsula, close to lanes sheltered by dense fukugi trees. Coral shallows, exposed rock and small patches of sand shape a shoreline that changes noticeably with the tide. Views extend toward nearby islands, while the adjacent residential village and traditional windbreak trees give the beach a quieter setting than the larger resort areas farther along the peninsula.

Okinawa Churaumi Aquarium

Okinawa Churaumi Aquarium anchors Ocean Expo Park on the Motobu Peninsula in northern Okinawa. Its Kuroshio Sea tank holds whale sharks, manta rays and other open-water fish. Separate galleries cover coral reefs, deep-sea life and local marine ecosystems. Outdoor attractions across the wider park form additional facilities beyond the aquarium building.

Former Japanese Navy Underground Headquarters

About four kilometres south of central Naha, a 450-metre tunnel network housed Japanese Navy command during the Battle of Okinawa. Preserved passages document staff life and the battle's human cost underground. The tunnel route is the core of the visit.

Himeyuri Cenotaph

The Himeyuri Cenotaph and adjoining museum commemorate female students and teachers mobilized as nursing personnel during the Battle of Okinawa, and many died in caves, field hospitals or the final collapse of organized resistance in 1945. Testimony, photographs, artifacts and the preserved cave entrance document their experience without romanticizing sacrifice or directing the visitor's emotional response.

Okinawa Peace Memorial Park at Mabuni commemorates the 1945 Battle of Okinawa with the Cornerstone of Peace name walls, monuments, and open clifftop lawns. Cornerstone of Peace

The Cornerstone of Peace lists names of those who died in the Battle of Okinawa, set within the Prefectural Peace Memorial Park at Mabuni. Stone walls arranged by nationality face the southern cliffs. The adjacent museum and library support research on the battle and postwar peace education.

Shikina-en Royal Gardens

Shikina-en was a secondary residence of the Ryukyu royal family, used for retreat and for receiving envoys. A pond, arched stone bridges, pavilion, paths, and Chinese-influenced design sit within subtropical planting south of Shuri; wartime destruction required extensive reconstruction, and the restored garden now forms part of the historic Gusuku Sites and Related Properties.

Tamaudun

King Sho Shin built Tamaudun near Shuri Castle in the early sixteenth century as the royal mausoleum of the Second Sho Dynasty, and war caused heavy damage before restoration, and the site now belongs to Okinawa's historic Ryukyu heritage group. Three stone chambers held remains according to rank and stage of burial, while a walled forecourt and guardian figures define the solemn precinct.

Okinawa World

Okinawa World is a unique theme park that offers a full experience of Okinawa's nature, history, and culture. Travellers can explore a replica castle townscape, stroll through a tropical fruit orchard with over 450 fruit trees, and witness the active eisa dance performances during special festivities. The highlight of the park is the massive natural cave called Gyokusendo, known for its formations and well-designed lighting.

Nakagusuku Castle Ruins

Curving limestone walls enclose several baileys at Nakagusuku Castle, one of Okinawa's well-preserved gusuku ruins. Regional lords expanded the hilltop stronghold before the Ryukyu Kingdom consolidated power, and the commander Gosamaru is closely associated with its fifteenth-century history. Terraces overlook both coasts, while uneven stone paths and exposed slopes require practical care.

Naminoue Shrine

Naminoue Shrine stands on a limestone bluff above Naha's coast, historically facing the sea route toward the sacred world of Nirai Kanai. The present buildings reflect postwar reconstruction after destruction in 1945. Worship halls, seasonal festivals, and the cliff setting preserve its role as Okinawa's principal shrine, while the beach below and nearby parks are separate public spaces.

Tsuboya Yachimun Street concentrates Okinawa's traditional pottery workshops in central Naha, a craft line going back to the Ryukyu Kingdom. Kilns, galleries, and small shops display yachimun bowls, plates, and sake ware. Walking the lane shows working studios rather than a single museum display.
