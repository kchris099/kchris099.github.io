Explore Tokyo

A Brief History

Tokyo began as the castle town of Edo under the Tokugawa shogunate from 1603, replacing Kyoto as the de facto capital. Meiji restoration in 1868 renamed the city and opened it to rapid modernization. Earthquake and fire in 1923 and bombing in 1945 destroyed large areas, followed by reconstruction for the 1964 Olympics. Today the metropolis spans former wards, harbors, and suburbs linked by rail.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Imperial Palace East National Gardens

The Imperial Palace is the main residence of the emperor of Japan. It is a large park-like area located in the Chiyoda district of the Chiyoda ward of Tokyo and contains several buildings, including the Fukiage Palace, where the emperor has his living quarters; the main palace, where various ceremonies and receptions take place; some residences of the Imperial Family; an archive; and museums and administrative offices.

Free

Wadakura Fountain Park

Wadakura Fountain Park sits within the Tokyo area of Tokyo, documented in local heritage registers for its civic or cultural role. Visitors approach from the main access road and combine the visit with neighbouring heritage buildings and public spaces in the district.

¥1500

Tokyo Tower

Tokyo Tower, also known by its official name Japan Radio Tower is a communications and observation tower in the district of Shiba-koen in Minato, Tokyo, Japan, completed in 1958. At 332.9 meters, it was the tallest tower in Japan until the construction of Tokyo Skytree in 2012. It is a lattice tower inspired by the Eiffel Tower, and is painted white and international orange to comply with air safety regulations.

Free (treasure gallery ¥700)

Zojo-ji

Zojo-ji is the grand head temple of the Jodo sect of Buddhism in the Kanto region, located adjacent to Tokyo Tower. Founded in 1393, it served as the primary family temple of the Tokugawa shogunate. The expansive grounds feature the monumental Sangedatsumon gate, the mausoleum of six Tokugawa shoguns, and a striking contrast between traditional temple architecture and the modern Tokyo skyline.

Free

Shibuya Crossing

Shibuya Scramble Crossing, commonly known as Shibuya Crossing, is a scramble crossing in Shibuya, Tokyo, Japan. It is located near Shibuya Station in Shibuya, a major commercial and entertainment district in Tokyo. It has been described as the world's busiest pedestrian crossing, with as many as 3,000 people crossing during a single green light cycle.

Free (inner garden ¥500)

Meiji Jingu

Meiji Jingu is Tokyo's most prominent Shinto shrine, dedicated to the deified spirits of Emperor Meiji and Empress Shoken. Completed in 1920, the shrine is situated within a vast, 170-acre artificial forest that provides a profound, tranquil sanctuary in the center of the bustling metropolis. It is a major center for traditional Shinto weddings and New Year's prayers.

Free

Meiji Jingu Ichino Torii

The Ichino Torii (First Gate) is the massive wooden gateway marking the primary entrance to the sacred grounds of Meiji Jingu in Tokyo. Constructed from ancient Japanese cypress, the towering structure signifies the transition from the secular, modern city into the serene, spiritual environment of the shrine's expansive forested precinct.

Free

North Observation Deck, Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building

The Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building, also referred to as the Tochō for short, is the seat of the Tokyo Metropolitan Government, which governs the special wards, cities, towns, and villages that constitute the Tokyo Metropolis. Located in Shinjuku ward, the building was designed by architect Kenzo Tange. It consists of a complex of three structures, each taking up a city block.

Free

Senso-ji

Senso-ji is Tokyo's oldest and most significant Buddhist temple, located in the historic Asakusa district. Founded in 628 AD, the temple is dedicated to Kannon, the bodhisattva of compassion. The vibrant complex features the iconic Kaminarimon (Thunder Gate) with its massive red lantern, a five-story pagoda, and a constant stream of worshippers and tourists experiencing traditional Japanese culture.

Free

Nakamise Shopping Street

Nakamise Shopping Street is one of the oldest shopping streets in Japan, forming the bustling approach to Senso-ji Temple in Asakusa. The 250-meter-long pedestrian avenue is lined with nearly 90 traditional stalls selling a wide variety of Japanese souvenirs, traditional crafts, and local street food snacks, such as freshly baked ningyo-yaki and rice crackers.

Free

Kaminarimon Gate

The Kaminarimon is the outer of two large entrance gates that ultimately leads to the Sensō-ji in Asakusa, Tokyo, Japan. The gate, with its lantern and statues, is popular with tourists. It stands at 11.7 m tall, 11.4 m wide and covers an area of 69.3 m2.

Free

Sumida River Walk

Adachi is a special ward in the Tokyo Metropolis in Japan. It is located to the north of the heart of Tokyo. The ward consists of two separate areas: a small strip of land between the Sumida River and Arakawa River and a larger area north of the Arakawa River.

¥2100

Tokyo Skytree

Tokyo Skytree, a.k.a Tokyo Sky Tree, is a broadcasting and observation tower, located in Sumida, Tokyo, Japan. It has been the tallest tower in Japan since opening in 2012, and reached its full height of 634 metres in early 2011, making it the tallest tower in the world, displacing the Canton Tower, and the third tallest structure in the world behind Merdeka 118 and Burj Khalifa.

Free

Unicorn Gundam

In the heart of Tokyo's Odaiba district stands the Unicorn Gundam Statue, a towering 20-meter tall tribute to the beloved Japanese manga and anime series Mobile Suit Gundam. This larger-than-life white statue comes alive at night with captivating lights and music, making it a for fans and travellers alike. Situated outside the Divercity Tokyo Plaza Mall, this landmark has become an integral part of Odaiba's vibrant anime and manga culture.

Free

Tokyo Dome

Tokyo Dome is a remarkable venue nestled in the heart of Tokyo, renowned for its versatility as it hosts an array of events from thrilling baseball games to electrifying concerts and various sporting competitions. This domed stadium serves as the home ground for the Yomiuri Giants and has welcomed legendary artists like Mick Jagger, Michael Jackson, and Madonna over the years.

Free

Hachiko Statue

The Hachikō Memorial Statue in Shibuya is a popular meeting spot featuring a bronze statue honoring Hachiko, the famously loyal Akita dog. The story of Hachiko and his unwavering loyalty has captured hearts worldwide, inspiring movies and gaining recognition as a symbol of canine devotion. This legendary dog would wait at Shibuya Station every day for his owner to return from work, continuing to do so for nine years after his owner's passing.

Free

Loft

Loft is a vibrant shopping haven that caters to all your needs, whether for home, kitchen, office, or even travel. With a strong emphasis on design, Loft stands out by offering an eclectic mix of products ranging from quirky cartoon character merchandise like phone straps and stationery to modern interpretations of traditional Japanese items such as fans and ceramics.

Free

Konno Hachiman-gu Shrine

Konno Hachimangu Shrine, In Shibuya amidst modern skyscrapers, is a serene place of worship that pays homage to a 15th-century emperor. Despite not being as grand as other shrines like Meiji, its resilience through WWII bombings and its historical significance make it a compelling attraction. Just a short walk from Shibuya Station, this Shinto shrine offers travellers an oasis of history amid the bustling city.

Free

Kiddy Land

Kiddy Land, located in Harajuku, is a vibrant retail store spread across four levels and offers an extensive collection of Japanese and American toys. The shop is a paradise for fans of famous Japanese characters, with a five-story building dedicated to various character goods such as Snoopy, Rilakkuma, Disney, Star Wars, and Hello Kitty. It's not just for kids; adults and even celebrities frequent the store.

Free

Togo Shrine

Togo Shrine, located near Harajuku Station in Tokyo, is a serene Shinto shrine dedicated to Admiral Togo, known for his victory over the Russian fleet in 1905. While smaller than the nearby Meiji Shrine, it offers a peaceful escape from the bustling city. The shrine was once home to the Togo Antique Market and now features Hello Kitty-themed offerings. travellers can explore its tranquil grounds and may even witness traditional ceremonies or weddings taking place.

Free

Kiyomasa's Well

Located within Meiji Jingu, Kiyomasa's Well is a renowned "power spot" in Tokyo. It is believed to have been created by Kato Kiyomasa, a prominent feudal lord from the 16th and 17th centuries. The well's pristine water attracts travellers who seek its positive energies. Although touching the water in the well is prohibited, travellers can touch the water around the small pond.

Free

Hatonomori Hachiman Shrine

Hatonomori Hachiman Shrine, located in Shibuya Ward near Sendagaya station, is a centuries-old Shinto place of worship known for its beautiful hydrangeas in the summer. The shrine features a mound resembling Mount Fuji and a charming bamboo forest. It is also depicted in the anime series 'March Comes in Like a Lion,' where the protagonist often visits before his shogi matches.

¥500

Shinjuku Gyoen National Garden

Shinjuku Gyoen National Garden, a former estate turned 144-acre park, was originally created as a recreational garden for the Imperial family. It is an Important Cultural Property and features three types of gardens: English, French, and Japanese. Throughout the year, the park hosts various events such as photography lessons, chrysanthemum flower exhibitions, and traditional Japanese art performances.

Free

Isetan Shinjuku

Isetan Shinjuku is a renowned department store in Japan, tracing its origins back to 1886 when it started as a kimono shop. Today, it is celebrated for its diverse range of products, from luxury brands to casual wear. The flagship store in Shinjuku is considered the birthplace of fashion trends and offers an extensive selection of fashion items from top global brands, beauty products, and gourmet food.

Free

Hanazono Shrine

Hanazono Shrine, In Shinjuku, is a compact Edo-period Shinto shrine dedicated to Inari Okami, the god of trade and business success. The grounds feature smaller shrines devoted to various deities, including a kitsune fox shrine with a tunnel of torii gates and the Geino Asama Shrine for performers.

Free

Shinjuku Golden-Gai

Shinjuku Golden Gai is a lively and popular district in Tokyo, known for its narrow and winding alleys filled with cozy taverns. This hidden gem offers a nostalgic atmosphere, reminiscent of Japan's post-war era, where small bars that can only accommodate a few patrons at a time provide an intimate setting for drinks with friends. While the area has gained popularity among international travellers, it still retains its local charm and may have some establishments with 'Locals Only' signs.

Free

Omoide Yokocho Memory Lane

Omoide Yokocho, also known as Memory Lane Alley or Yakitori Alley, is a renowned landmark in Tokyo located just north of Shinjuku station. Once a black market area post-World War II, it has transformed into a bustling alleyway filled with tightly compressed restaurants offering cheap izakaya food and alcoholic drinks. The vibrant atmosphere attracts both locals and tourists seeking to savor its culinary offerings.

Free

Meguro Sky Garden

Meguro Sky Garden is a modernized urban oasis located in Meguro City, Tokyo. Built over an expressway junction, this unique outdoor green space features seasonal flowers, a bamboo grove, and pine trees. The park rises up to 35 meters high and offers superb views of the surrounding buildings and the city of Tokyo. Despite its location above a highway road, it provides a peaceful nature break with no cafes or restaurants on site.

Free

Hibiya Park

Hibiya Park, Japan's oldest Western-style park, is located in Tokyo's Chiyoda Ward. Spanning over 161,636.66 square meters, it was originally a military parade ground before transforming into a picturesque urban oasis. Situated near the Imperial Palace and surrounded by bustling districts, the park offers lush gardens, seasonal blooms, and a Japanese garden. travellers can enjoy flower beds, outdoor music venues, public halls as well as beautifully maintained lawns and ponds.

Free

Tokyo Takarazuka Theater

Tokyo Takarazuka Theatre is a grand performing arts space that introduces travellers to the world of Takarazuka Creative Arts. It was formerly known as the Ernie Pyle Theater and hosts performances by the all-female musical troupe, Takarazuka Revue. The theater features Western-inspired musicals and Japanese folklore with women playing all roles. Performances are held at various times throughout the year, and English story synopses are available for non-Japanese speakers.

Free

Imperial Theatre

Imperial Theatre, a historic venue established in 1911, is a significant part of Tokyo's cultural scene. It hosts diverse performances annually, including Western musicals like Les Miserables and Elisabeth, as well as captivating shows by Japanese entertainers such as Endless SHOCK. Despite its smaller size, the theater offers comfortable seating with good views of the stage from various angles.

Free

Kokyo Gaien National Garden

Kokyo Gaien National Garden, located in central Tokyo, is a sprawling green space featuring 2000 black pines and family-friendly areas like Kitanomaru Park. It offers an understated experience with scenic moats and the East Gardens. travellers can view the Nijubashi from the large plaza in front of the Imperial Palace, which provides a backdrop against the city skyline.

Free

Sakurada-mon Gate

Sakurada-mon Gate, located near the Imperial Palace in Tokyo, Japan, is a significant historical landmark that dates back to the Edo period. Originally built in 1457 and reconstructed in 1636, it has survived through centuries of history. The gate's steel storehouse structure stands as a testament to its resilience after being destroyed in the Great Kanto Earthquake of 1923.

Free

Marunouchi Tokyo Station Square

Marunouchi Square is a spacious, white granite-paved area flanked by lawns and adjacent to the striking red-brick Tokyo Station. It serves as a harmonious blend of tradition and modernity, nature and architecture, functionality, and beauty. This popular spot is frequented by locals and tourists alike due to its convenient access to Tokyo's landmarks such as the Imperial Palace, Ginza, and Nihonbashi.

Free

Tokyo Character Street

A shopping area in Tokyo Station filled with stores selling merchandise of popular Japanese characters and anime. The site forms part of Tokyo Character Street's documented civic and cultural landscape within Japan, with archives and visitor information available from municipal or regional heritage authorities.

Free

Statues of Kirin

Kachidoki is a bustling city market in Japan that takes place once a month, attracting around 20,000 travellers over the weekend. The market features approximately 100 stalls from farms and restaurants across the country, offering fresh seasonal vegetables and processed foods for purchase. It's a great place for families to enjoy various activities such as eating, shopping, learning, and experiencing together.

Free

Fukutoku Shrine

Fukutoku Shrine, also known as Mebuki or Sprouting Shrine, is an urban shrine located in Nihonbashi with a history dating back to the mid-9th century. It has been patronized by historical figures such as Minamoto no Yoshiie and Tokugawa Ieyasu. The shrine's main gate, made of sawtooth oak, is said to have sprouted buds according to local legend.

Free

Koami Shrine

Koami Shrine, located in Nihonbashi, Chuo-ku, Tokyo, is a historic shrine with a rich cultural heritage. Believed to be at least 1,000 years old, it houses wooden carvings of two dragons that survived the firebombing of Tokyo during World War II. The shrine is famous for Sennarai-benten, the goddess of luck and protection from bad luck.

Free

Suitengu Shrine

Suitengu Shrine, a small Shinto shrine in Tokyo, is dedicated to fertility and childbirth. It is a branch shrine of Kurume Suitengu in Fukuoka Prefecture and is known for its deity for safe childbirth. The shrine is popular among worshippers praying for easy childbirth and the birth of their children.

Free

Okuno Building

Nestled amidst the modern structures of Ginza, Tokyo's upscale district, the Okuno Building is a captivating blend of art, history, and antiquity. Constructed in the 1930s as a lavish apartment complex, it endured the ravages of World War II and subsequent urban development. Today, this architectural gem houses an array of art galleries and studios within its meticulously preserved walls.
