Tokyo

Tokyo began as the castle town of Edo under the Tokugawa shogunate from 1603, replacing Kyoto as the de facto capital. Meiji restoration in 1868 renamed the city and opened it to rapid modernization. Earthquake and fire in 1923 and bombing in 1945 destroyed large areas, followed by reconstruction for the 1964 Olympics. Today the metropolis spans former wards, harbors, and suburbs linked by rail.

Imperial Palace East National Gardens

The East Gardens occupy the former inner enclosures of Edo Castle beside today's Imperial Palace. Stone walls, moats, gatehouses, gardens, guardhouses, and the foundation of the lost main keep remain within a public national garden. The Imperial Palace is the main residence of the emperor of Japan.

Tokyo Tower

Tokyo Tower was completed in 1958 as a broadcasting and observation structure above Shiba Park. Its orange-and-white steel lattice reaches 333 meters, with public decks offering views across central Tokyo and toward Mount Fuji in clear weather. Antenna functions, exhibitions, shops, and lighting have changed over time, while the tower remains a postwar symbol despite being surpassed in height by Skytree.

Zojo-ji

Zojoji is the principal Jodo Buddhist temple of eastern Japan and a former family temple of the Tokugawa shoguns. Its surviving Sangedatsumon gate dates from the early seventeenth century, while wartime destruction led to reconstruction of major halls; tokugawa tombs, rows of child-protecting Jizo figures, and the modern backdrop of Tokyo Tower create a layered Shiba precinct.
