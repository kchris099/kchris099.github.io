Ankara

Galatian and Roman Ancyra was a modest Anatolian town until Mustafa Kemal made it the nationalist headquarters in 1920 and capital of the republic. Planned boulevards and mausoleum architecture express that deliberate reinvention as administrative centre, away from Ottoman Istanbul. The Museum of Anatolian Civilizations presents Hittite finds that long predate the republic.

Haci Bayram Mosque

Beside the Roman Temple of Augustus in Ankara's Ulus district, Haci Bayram Mosque developed around the tomb of the Sufi teacher Haci Bayram Veli. The present structure reflects Ottoman construction and later repairs, with painted wood, tile, calligraphy and a compact prayer hall. Pilgrims, worshippers and neighborhood activity give the complex a continuing religious role beyond its architectural interest.

Ataturk Statue

Raised in 1927 for the War of Independence, the Atatürk Statue in Ankara shows bronze figures with the leader on horseback in an open plaza. The memorial honors those who fought for the republic. Roadside and square approaches keep the sculpture publicly visible.

Suluhan Bazaar

Also called Hasan Paşa Han, sixteenth-century Suluhan once traded coffee and yarn; cafés and souvenir shops now fill the caravanserai while Ulus stalls sell produce, meat, fish, and household goods. Market hours keep the han commercial. Ottoman trade architecture still frames the aisles.

39.9397° N / 32.8585° E

04 AdmissionFree

Anıtkabir

Anitkabir occupies a commanding hill in Ankara as the mausoleum of Mustafa Kemal Ataturk, founder of the Turkish Republic. A long approach lined with lion sculptures leads to the ceremonial plaza, Hall of Honor, and museum galleries covering the War of Independence and early republic. The burial place of President Ismet Inonu also lies within the formal landscape and state memorial complex.

39.9249° N / 32.8371° E

05 AdmissionFree

Ankara Castle

Ankara Castle, an ancient fortress perched on a rocky hill, provides panoramic views of the city. With its strong fortifications and detailed architecture, this historic site has a glimpse into Turkey's past. The castle has been used by various civilizations for defense and settlement over its 4,000-year history and has undergone numerous repairs.

06 AdmissionFree

Aslanhane Mosque

Historic fabric and carefully matched carpets define Aslanhane Mosque in Ankara, with exterior and main interiors usually viewable outside prayer times. The wooden and masonry hall rewards a quiet look at early Anatolian mosque craft. Active worship continues in the old structure.

Kuğulu Park

Also called Swan Park, Kuğulu Park sits on busy Tunalı Hilmi Street with benches, paths, lawns, and shade for a pause before shops and cafés. The small urban green tracks daily street life around it. Locals use it as a short rest stop in the shopping corridor.

Seğmenler Parkı

In Çankaya, Seğmenler Park spreads lawns, paths, and shade used by young people, families, and dog walkers looking for open ground in the capital. The large public park keeps a relaxed pace away from denser shopping streets. Facilities stay simple: walking routes and grass rather than ticketed garden attractions.

09 AdmissionFree

Kocatepe Mosque

Four slender minarets and a broad central dome make Kocatepe Mosque a major element of Ankara's modern skyline, and built from the late 1960s to the 1980s, the complex follows monumental Ottoman mosque forms. Marble courts, calligraphy, stained glass, and a large prayer hall support active worship and national religious ceremonies.

10 AdmissionFree

Ahmet Hamdi Akseki Mosque

Completed in the early 2010s beside Turkey's religious-affairs headquarters, Ahmet Hamdi Akseki Mosque interprets classical Ottoman forms through a large contemporary complex; the mosque is an active place of worship, so public access varies around services and events. Marble surfaces, patterned domes, calligraphy, courtyards, and four minarets frame a prayer hall designed for major gatherings.

39.9066° N / 32.7631° E

11 AdmissionFree

Şehit Cuma Dağ Tabiat Tarihi Müzesi

Ankara's Sehit Cuma Dag Natural History Museum is named for a museum employee killed during the 2016 coup attempt. Fossils, minerals and rocks introduce Turkey's geological development. Taxidermy specimens and displays on the history of life cover biological diversity, placing the collections within a scientific institution near the Ahmet Hamdi Akseki Mosque.

39.9024° N / 32.7986° E

Altınköy Açık Hava Müzesi

Altınköy Open Air Museum reconstructs a traditional Anatolian village on Ankara's outskirts, with houses, streets, and craft displays interpreting rural life in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Costumed interpretation and farm animals appear on busy days. Families use it as an educational park that contrasts with the capital's modern districts.

Panora Alışveriş ve Yaşam Merkezi

Panora is a large Ankara shopping and leisure mall with extensive retail floors, restaurants, an aquarium, and a games area. Escalator atriums organize fashion and household brands for local shoppers and out-of-town visitors. The complex functions as a full indoor leisure stop beyond simple clothing stores.

14 AdmissionFree

Lake Eymir

Lake Eymir southwest of Ankara sits among pine slopes used for walking, jogging, and cycling on shoreline paths. Picnic clearings and birdwatching spots draw weekend visitors from the city. Access rules and university management shape how crowded the trails feel, but the water and woodland remain a nearby nature outlet for the capital.

39.8262° N / 32.8324° E

15 AdmissionFree

METU Forest

Middle East Technical University Forest spreads across the university's Ankara campus, created through decades of planting on formerly eroded steppe. Pine stands, open slopes, reservoir edges, and unpaved tracks support wildlife and outdoor recreation while buffering the city from development; access conditions depend on campus security and conservation rules, and the area remains a managed university landscape.

39.8340° N / 32.8058° E

16 AdmissionFree

Aqua Vega Aquarium

Aqua Vega Aquarium occupies part of the Nata Vega complex in eastern Ankara. A long underwater tunnel and a series of marine and freshwater tanks display sharks, rays, reef fish and species from rivers and lakes. Additional themed rooms broaden the indoor circuit beyond the main aquarium, while interpretive panels introduce habitats and basic biology within a commercial family attraction.

Atatürk Bahçesi

Lawns, paths, and shade at Atatürk Bahçesi give midday and evening rest among Ankara's government and commercial blocks. Benches and open grass support informal gatherings near downtown routes. Locals treat it as green relief rather than a ticketed destination garden.

18 AdmissionFree

Korean War Memorial

Turkey's Korean War Memorial in Ankara commemorates soldiers who served and died with the Turkish Brigade during the 1950-1953 conflict. Inscribed stonework, flags, military symbols, and a formal plaza create a setting for official remembrance and anniversary ceremonies. The monument explains a continuing diplomatic connection between Turkey and South Korea.
