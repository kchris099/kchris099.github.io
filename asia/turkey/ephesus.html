Ephesus

Ephesus was a major Ionian Greek city whose Artemis temple and later Roman harbour made it capital of Asia province, drawing colonnaded avenues, the Library of Celsus, and an amphitheatre that could host political assembly. Early Christian councils met here, and tradition places the Virgin Mary's house nearby on Bulbuldag. Silting of the Cayster River shifted settlement toward Selçuk; extensive ruins still record classical urban life on the Aegean coast.

House of Virgin Mary

The House of the Virgin Mary on Nightingale Mountain near Ephesus is a small stone shrine venerated by Christian and Muslim pilgrims as a possible final home of Mary. A chapel interior, spring, and votive wall structure the visit. Forest roads climb from Selçuk to the sanctuary overlooking the Aegean hinterland.

Daytime visits and ticketed events open the vast Greek theatre at Ephesus, where seating and stage remains face the ancient city's street grid. Guides often pair the theatre with the Terrace Houses exhibit on the same archaeological ticket. Stone cavea and orchestra still read clearly.

Ephesus Ancient City

Ephesus near Selçuk preserves marble streets, the Library of Celsus facade, theatre seating, and houses from Greek and Roman phases of a major Aegean city. Biblical and early Christian associations add another layer to the civic ruins. Travellers walk a mainly downhill route through public monuments that once anchored provincial Asia.

Library of Celsus

Celsus Library in Ephesus, a 2nd-century Roman library ruins, is an symbol of the ancient city. The columned facade with its two-story structure is a evidence of its rich history. Travellers are suggested to get an audio or walking tour guide for better understanding of the place and spend about 1.5hrs here. One can also find friendly cats around the area and photographers who can take picture for a fee in front of the library.

Odeon

The Odeon at Ephesus is a small roofed theatre used for council meetings and performances, with seating rising above a compact stage building near the upper city. Excavated marble seats and vaulted substructures show its civic role beside larger public spaces. Visits usually combine it with the nearby state agora and temple platforms on the same terrace.

Virgin Mary Statue

The Virgin Mary Statue (Meryem Ana Evi) is a significant religious and historical site located near Ephesus, outside Izmir. According to Catholic tradition, it marks the location of the stone house where the Virgin Mary spent her final years. The small, modest shrine is a major pilgrimage destination for both Christians and Muslims, surrounded by a peaceful, wooded environment.

Basilica of Saint John

On Ayasuluk Hill above Selcuk, the Basilica of Saint John was built in the sixth century over a tomb traditionally identified with John the Apostle. Massive piers, marble paving, columns, baptistery remains, and a reconstructed section outline the former cross-shaped church. Byzantine fortifications and views toward Ephesus place the ruin within a wider sacred and defensive landscape.

İsa Bey Mosque

Isa Bey Mosque was built in the fourteenth century below Ayasuluk Hill, using stone and columns that reflect both Seljuk traditions and material from nearby ancient sites. An asymmetrical courtyard, monumental portal, domed prayer space, and single surviving minaret distinguish the building. It remains a functioning mosque beside the Basilica of Saint John and the approach to Ephesus.

The Temple of Artemis

The Temple of Artemis, once celebrated as one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World, is a must-visit historic site located just a few kilometers from Ephesus and within walking distance from Selcuk. Although time has taken its toll on this structure, travellers can still marvel at a solitary pillar that is a evidence of its grandeur dating back to 550 BC. The remnants evoke curiosity about what this temple might have looked like in its prime.

Ayasuluk Citadel

Ayasuluk Citadel, situated in the historic region of Selcuk, is a significant site that includes the Temple of Artemis. Perched on a hill overlooking the Selcuk district and located near the ancient Greek city of Ephesus, this castle was constructed using stones from abandoned Greek and Roman ruins. While it has undergone partial restoration, there is still much work to be done to preserve its rich heritage.

Kuşadası Castle

Kusadasi Castle occupies Pigeon Island at the end of a causeway from the town waterfront. Fortifications were developed and repaired under Ottoman rule to protect the harbor, with walls enclosing a compact inner tower and paths above the sea. Popular stories connecting every phase to pirates or Admiral Barbarossa simplify a structure altered repeatedly for coastal defense and modern recreation.

Shopping streets around Kervansaray in Kuşadası invite bargaining across varied goods, while day trips reach Ephesus, the House of the Virgin Mary, and the Temple of Artemis site. The caravanserai area mixes lodging and retail. Coastal town trade and nearby ruins share visitor time.

El Heykeli

Kusadasi's Hand Statue stands on the waterfront as a modern public sculpture, its open palm supporting white doves associated with the town's name and coastal identity. The surrounding promenade links the cruise harbor, marina, cafes, and sea views. It is primarily a meeting point and photographic landmark.

Ladies Beach

Also called Kadınlar Denizi, Ladies Beach once reserved for women in the Ottoman period now welcomes everyone along a palm-lined promenade of shops, bars, cafés, and hotels. Foot access from roads and beach paths is typical. The resort strip faces the Aegean sand.
