Mardin

Assyrian, Arab, Seljuk, Kurdish, and Armenian communities shared the hillside above the Mesopotamian plain. Zinciriye Medrese, the Grand Mosque, and stone terrace houses embody that plural frontier city looking toward the plains of Syria. Syriac monasteries such as Deyrulzafaran stand in the same limestone hills outside town. Honey-colored mansions step down the slope in terraces toward the plains.

Kasımiye Madrasah

Perched atop a hill in Mardin, Kasımiye Madrasah is a historic gem with local Artuklu architecture. The 1469 Islamic college has a weathered sandstone facade adorned with carvings, offering visitors an immersive journey through its arched doorways and courtyard. The site's two large domes house the tombs of Kasim Pasa and his sister, while its vantage point provides panoramic views of the surrounding plains of Mesopotamia.

Mardin Old Town

Mardin's old town climbs a limestone ridge above the Mesopotamian plain, its terraces, lanes, courtyards, mosques, churches and houses built largely from warm-colored local stone. Arab, Kurdish, Turkish, Armenian and Syriac histories overlap across the urban fabric. Shops and guesthouses have expanded with tourism, but steep residential streets and active religious communities remain central to the district.

Mardin Ulu Camii

Mardin Grand Mosque developed through Artuqid and later rebuilding, with a ribbed dome and single minaret rising above the dense old town, and exact foundation dates remain debated because the complex was altered repeatedly, while the mosque continues to serve local worshippers. A stone courtyard, fountain, arcades, inscriptions, and prayer hall show the craftsmanship associated with medieval Mardin.

37.3128° N / 40.7396° E

04 Admission₺50

Zinciriye Madrasa

Melik Necmettin Isa, the last Artuqid sultan, completed Zinciriye Madrasa in 1385 as an educational and spiritual complex. Domes rise above a carefully carved portal that faces an open plaza along the old-town street. Courtyards and teaching rooms sit behind the entrance screen, still readable as a late medieval foundation.

37.3142° N / 40.7400° E

Mardin Castle

Mardin Castle occupies the ridge above the old city, a strategic position used by several powers and now within a restricted military zone. Walls and installations can be seen from below, but ordinary public access is generally unavailable and advertised viewpoints from the summit may be misleading. The castle's importance lies in its control of routes across the Mesopotamian plain.

37.3154° N / 40.7390° E

06 AdmissionFree

Mardin Evleri

Mardin's traditional houses step down the ridge in terraces of pale limestone, often organized around courtyards that protect privacy and respond to the steep climate. Carved portals, vaulted rooms, rooftop views, narrow passages and water channels vary by neighborhood and period. Many remain private homes, while some have become museums, hotels or cafes.

37.3150° N / 40.7495° E

Deyrulzafaran Monastery

Deyrulzafaran Monastery stands on a rocky slope southeast of Mardin and has long been a major center of the Syriac Orthodox Church. Honey-colored stone buildings enclose churches, courtyards, monastic rooms and tombs of patriarchs. Centuries of worship, education and church administration shaped the complex, whose living religious community continues a tradition rooted in the region's Syriac Christian heritage.

37.2993° N / 40.7926° E

08 Admission₺150

Deyrulzafaran Syriac Monastery

Deyrulzafaran Monastery stands on a rocky slope outside Mardin, its honey-colored masonry looking across the Mesopotamian plain. Chapels and courtyards organize the complex. Former patriarchal rooms and burial spaces recall centuries of Syriac Orthodox administration. Older foundations beneath the buildings and continuing religious use connect the monastery with the Christian history of Tur Abdin.

37.2990° N / 40.7913° E

09 AdmissionFree

Cumhuriyet Meydanı

Cumhuriyet Meydanı is Mardin's central square, ringed by civic and commercial buildings where residents gather through the day. Open pavement and surrounding stone facades frame views toward the old city's terraced streets. Cafes and shops on the edges make the square a practical starting point for walking the historic core.

37.3131° N / 40.7353° E

10 Admission₺70

Forty Martyrs Church

Forty Martyrs Church belongs to Mardin's long Christian tradition in southeastern Turkey, where Syriac and Armenian communities shaped the old city's skyline. Stone walls and a compact nave sit among the terraced streets of the historic center. Outside liturgy, the exterior and main interior spaces are commonly open for respectful visits.

37.3136° N / 40.7326° E
