Bursa

Bursa became the first major capital of the Ottoman state after Orhan Ghazi captured the city in 1326, with early mosques, markets, and the Green Tomb of Mehmed I displaying the dynasty's emerging style. Silk routes from Iran and China passed through its bazaars and thermal springs at Çekirge. Later Istanbul's rise shifted political power, but Bursa remained a textile centre on Mount Uludağ's slopes, where Ottoman foundations and republican factories share a valley setting.

Tophane Clock Tower

Tophane Clock Tower rises above Bursa near the tombs of Osman and Orhan Gazi. The present six-story masonry structure dates from the early twentieth century and also served as a fire lookout, reflecting the importance of high observation points in the dense Ottoman city. Terraces around the tower open toward modern Bursa, while the surrounding hill retains memorial and civic significance.

Osman Gazi Türbesi

The tomb attributed to Osman Gazi, founder of the Ottoman dynasty, stands in Bursa's Tophane district beside the burial place of his son Orhan. The current mausoleum was rebuilt after a nineteenth-century earthquake on a site associated with an earlier Byzantine chapel. A draped cenotaph, calligraphic decoration, and ceremonial guards frame a place of national remembrance.

Zafer Plaza

From the street Zafer Plaza looks compact, yet three underground floors hold shops and cafés in one building plus a food court, cinema, and more shops in another. Rainy days often push shoppers indoors here. Open pavement above links to the buried retail layers.

Emir Sultan Mosque

Emir Sultan Mosque and its adjoining tomb occupy a hillside district long associated with the scholar and mystic Emir Sultan; although the foundation reaches back to the early Ottoman period, earthquakes led to substantial rebuilding, including the present nineteenth-century form. A domed prayer hall, arcaded courtyard, cemetery, and pilgrimage activity connect architectural history with continuing devotion.

Muradiye Complex

Commissioned by Sultan Murad II in the fifteenth century, the Muradiye Complex combines a mosque, school, bath, charitable buildings and a garden of Ottoman royal tombs. Tile, carved stone, painted decoration and varied domes record several generations of court patronage. The complex stands apart from Bursa's busiest center, giving its burial precinct a quieter role within the former imperial capital.

40.1906° N / 29.0459° E

İnkaya Historical Plane Tree

The İnkaya Historical Plane Tree, a symbol of the city, is a towering tree that has stood for centuries. It reaches an height of 35 meters and has branches that extend up to 18 meters in diameter. This ancient tree is estimated to be around 610 years old and is a popular tourist attraction. Travellers can park their cars at the nearby Otopark for a fee of 10TL and take a short walk of approximately 150 meters to reach the site.

1. Murat Hudavendigar Mosque

Hudavendigar Mosque forms the center of a fourteenth-century complex commissioned by Sultan Murad I in Bursa's Cekirge district. Its unusual two-level arrangement places former school rooms above the prayer hall, combining mosque and madrasa functions within one monumental building; stone and brick walls, arches, and later repairs reflect early Ottoman experimentation as well as continuing religious use.

Bursa Grand Mosque

Also called Ulu Cami, Bursa Grand Mosque was built in 1399 under Sultan Bayezid with ornate interior calligraphy across a multi-dome plan. Exterior and main halls can usually be seen outside prayer times. Early Ottoman scale still dominates the bazaar district.

Emirhan

Emir Han was built in the fourteenth century as part of Orhan Gazi's commercial and charitable foundation in central Bursa. Two stories of rooms surround an open courtyard where merchants once stored goods, lodged, and managed caravan trade near the covered market; shops continue to occupy the building, so the han remains part of the city's commercial life.

Silk clothing, jewelry, and local foods fill Bursa Grand Bazaar stalls during regular market hours as crowds move through covered lanes near the Grand Mosque. The bazaar focuses on trade and daily shopping. Mountain outings such as Uludağ National Park sit as separate day trips beyond the market halls.

Koza Han

Koza Han was commissioned by Sultan Bayezid II near the end of the fifteenth century to support Bursa's silk trade and imperial foundations, and tea tables and retail activity continue the building's trading function, though the goods and tourist focus have changed. Arcaded rooms surround a stone courtyard with a small prayer pavilion at its center, while upper galleries now hold textile and souvenir shops.

Orhan Gazi Mosque in Bursa is an early Ottoman foundation near the Grand Mosque district, with a multi-domed prayer hall and a quiet courtyard. The mosque remains in active use; visitors may view the exterior and main hall outside prayer times when the doors stay open to the street.

Ahmet Vefik Paşa Bursa Devlet Tiyatrosu

Named for the Ottoman statesman and dramatist Ahmet Vefik Pasa, Bursa State Theatre occupies a central performance venue used for repertory drama, touring productions and public cultural events. The institution matters more as an active theater company than as a preserved monument. Auditorium access normally depends on a scheduled performance and programs, casts and seasonal operations change throughout the year.

14 AdmissionFree

Cumalikizik Village (UNESCO)

Cumalikizik preserves an early Ottoman village pattern on the lower slopes of Uludag, with narrow stone lanes, timber-framed houses, courtyards, fountains, and small farms. Its inclusion with Bursa on the World Heritage List reflects links between rural production and the emerging Ottoman capital. Tourism has brought cafes and souvenir stalls, but the settlement remains a residential community.

40.1761° N / 29.1722° E

Podyum Park

In Nilüfer, Podyum Park mixes open-air shopping and entertainment with paths, lawns, and shade for mixed ages spending an afternoon on the campus. Modern retail and leisure share the same grounds. The layout reads as a contemporary leisure hub rather than a historic garden or fortress court.

17 AdmissionFree

Irgandi Bridge

Irgandı Bridge, built in 1442, carried an Ottoman bazaar on its deck with storage below the roadway. Craft shops still line the upper level above the Gökdere. The yellow-painted market rooms on the old stone span remain a distinctive historic Bursa crossing.

Setbasi Bridge

Stone Setbaşı Bridge, renovated since the fifteenth century, links the Yeşil district to Bursa's center, with streamside cafés for tea and a city library nearby. Pedestrian crossing remains the main use. Historic fabric and everyday errands share the span.

40.1809° N / 29.0698° E

Bursa Kalesi

Bursa Castle traces fortifications from Bithynian origins through Roman, Byzantine, and Ottoman rebuilds above the old city. Walls, gates, and terraces overlook tiled roofs and the plain beyond. Walkers follow surviving rampart sections to understand how successive powers reused the same defensive spur.

40.1835° N / 29.0496° E
