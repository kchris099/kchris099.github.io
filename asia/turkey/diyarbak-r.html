Diyarbakır

Diyarbakır commands the upper Tigris where Amida's black basalt walls, largely Roman with later repairs, enclose one of Mesopotamia's oldest continuously inhabited cities. Armenian, Syriac, Kurdish, and Turkish communities built churches, mosques, and caravanserais along streets that linked Anatolia to the Jazira. The Hevsel Gardens and citadel record centuries of frontier rule under Arab, Seljuk, and Ottoman empires before the city became a cultural centre of southeastern Turkey.

Diyarbakir Castle

Diyarbakır Castle anchors the black basalt walls that enclose the old city above the Tigris. Towers, gates, and inner courtyards show layered Roman-to-Ottoman fortification; walkers follow rampart stretches when sections are open along the historic circuit of the Sur district.

02 AdmissionFree

Diyarbakır Walls

Dark basalt walls and towers encircle Diyarbakir's historic Sur district, following a defensive line strengthened under Roman, Byzantine, Islamic and Ottoman rule. Gates, inscriptions, reliefs and viewpoints overlook the Tigris basin and the Hevsel Gardens. Some sections have suffered conflict damage and reconstruction, so access and preservation conditions vary around the long historic circuit.

37.9164° N / 40.2370° E

03 AdmissionFree

Hasan Pasha Inn

Hasan Pasha Han, built between 1572 and 1575 near Diyarbakır's Great Mosque, is a two-story Ottoman courtyard caravanserai with domed fountains and stout columns. Cafes now occupy parts of the court, so breakfast and tea share space with the historic fabric. The inn sits on the main tourist walking route through the walled city.

37.9128° N / 40.2371° E

04 AdmissionFree

Diyarbakır Grand Mosque

Diyarbakir Grand Mosque occupies a large basalt courtyard in the old city, developed after the Muslim conquest and substantially rebuilt during the Seljuk period. Arcades, inscriptions, reused classical columns, a sundial, and separate prayer areas reveal many phases of construction. Traditions linking the site to an earlier church are widely repeated, but its present significance rests on centuries of Islamic worship and scholarship.

37.9124° N / 40.2359° E

05 AdmissionFree

Sülüklü Han

Suluklu Han is an Ottoman caravanserai in Diyarbakir's old city, named for a courtyard well traditionally associated with medicinal leeches. Stone arches and rooms once served merchants and animals, while restoration introduced cafes and social space. Its current hospitality use keeps the courtyard active.

37.9116° N / 40.2374° E

06 AdmissionFree

Dört Ayaklı Minare

The Four-legged Minaret belongs to Sheikh Matar Mosque in Diyarbakır, completed around 1500 under Aq Qoyunlu patronage by Hajji Hüseyin. A single-dome prayer hall pairs with a minaret raised on four columns, an unusual structural silhouette in the old city. The corner landmark is easy to spot while walking between Sur's mosques and bazaars.

37.9108° N / 40.2378° E

07 AdmissionFree

Ongözlü Bridge

Also called the Dicle or Silvan Bridge, Ongözlü was built under Marwanid Nizamuddin Nasr between 1065 and 1067, spanning about eighteen meters long and six meters wide. Pedestrian decks and adjacent streets provide viewing points. Medieval masonry still crosses the river approach.

37.8872° N / 40.2280° E

08 AdmissionFree

Gazi Köşkü Şark Evi

Gazi Kosku stands in landscaped grounds outside Diyarbakir's walled core and is associated with Mustafa Kemal's command during World War I. The traditional house opens toward terraces, fountains and pools within a garden setting. Memorial interpretation and cultural uses share the property with dining facilities, connecting military history with a later public recreation area.

37.8956° N / 40.2292° E

09 AdmissionFree

Hevsel Gardens

Hevsel Gardens, also known as Hevsel Bahceleri, is a historic landmark located between the Diyarbakir City Walls and the Tigris River. This UNESCO World local history site spans approximately 4,000 decares and has fertile alluvial soils ideal for growing various fruits, vegetables, and popular trees.

37.8989° N / 40.2415° E

10 AdmissionFree

Syriac Orthodox Church of the Virgin Mary

Its complex contains a stone church, courtyards, carved details, liturgical furnishings, and additions from several periods. The Syriac Orthodox Church of the Virgin Mary is one of the surviving Christian institutions within Diyarbakir's old city; the small congregation and continuing worship connect the site to the region's long Syriac Christian history.

37.9089° N / 40.2306° E
