Istanbul

Istanbul, formerly Byzantium and Constantinople, served as capital of the Roman Byzantine and Ottoman empires. The Hagia Sophia, Topkapı Palace, and Theodosian walls record those periods. The city controlled trade between the Black Sea and Mediterranean. It ceased to be Ottoman capital in 1923 when Ankara became the seat of the Turkish Republic. Historic areas on both sides of the Bosphorus hold mosques, bazaars, and synagogues.

The Blue Mosque

Commissioned by Sultan Ahmed I in the early seventeenth century, the Sultan Ahmed Mosque faces Hagia Sophia across Istanbul's historic peninsula, and it is an active imperial mosque, so prayer schedules and religious conduct shape public entry. Six minarets, a cascade of domes, a broad courtyard, stained glass, calligraphy, and blue-toned tiles define the complex.

Hagia Sophia

Completed in 537 under Emperor Justinian, Hagia Sophia served successively as a Byzantine cathedral, Ottoman mosque, secular museum and mosque again. Its vast pendentive dome, marble revetment, mosaics, calligraphy and later minarets record those changes in use; current worship arrangements affect access to galleries and imagery, making the building both a living religious site and a contested monument.

Gulhane Park

Once part of the outer gardens of Topkapi Palace, Gulhane Park now provides long shaded walks between the historic peninsula and the Bosphorus edge, and seasonal planting and crowds change its appearance, but the former palace setting remains visible in walls and gates. Lawns, mature trees, flower beds, monuments, and museum buildings occupy a landscape opened to the public in the early twentieth century.

Topkapi Palace Museum

Topkapı Palace served Ottoman sultans from the 1460s until Dolmabahçe took over in 1853, beginning with Mehmed II's construction after 1459. Courtyards, kitchens, the Harem, and treasury chambers now form a museum of imperial life on the Seraglio Point headland. Visitors move through successive courts that once controlled access to the sultan's household.

Grand Bazaar

Istanbul's Grand Bazaar developed from an Ottoman commercial foundation after the conquest of Constantinople, expanding into a dense network of covered lanes, hans, workshops and shops. Jewelry, carpets, leather, textiles, ceramics and tourist goods now mix with long-established trades. Repeated fires, earthquakes, repairs and changing retail patterns mean the market is historic without being frozen in one era.

The Theodosian land walls stretch six kilometers from the Sea of Marmara to the Golden Horn, built in the fifth century. Sections near Edirnekapı and Yedikule can be walked along outer paths. Restoration work continues on towers and gates that once held off sieges for a millennium.
