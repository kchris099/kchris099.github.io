Tel Aviv

Ahuzat Bayit settlers founded Tel Aviv on dunes north of Jaffa in 1909. 1930s immigrants trained in Bauhaus design produced the White City apartment fabric. The result is a deliberately modern Hebrew city beside the older Arab-Ottoman port. Independence Hall on Rothschild Boulevard marks where the state was declared in 1948. Neve Tzedek lanes show an earlier neighbourhood phase before Bauhaus expansion.

The Clock Tower

Built in 1903 under Sultan Abdul Hamid II to honor the Ottoman ruler, Jaffa's limestone clock tower faces historic buildings, beach approaches, and surrounding shops and galleries. Later renovations added clocks and stained glass depicting Jaffa's history. The tower remains the square's vertical marker in Old Jaffa.

Park HaTachana

Also called the Old Train Station, the 1882 Jaffa-Jerusalem rail terminus now hosts weekend craft markets among restored station buildings. Paths and plazas replace active tracks. The complex sits as a leisure and shopping adaptive reuse of early railway fabric rather than a working freight yard.

Tel Aviv Port

Tel Aviv Port opened in 1936 and handled cargo until commercial operations ended in the 1960s. A later redevelopment retained the low warehouses while adding timber decks and public space along the Mediterranean shoreline. The former harbor now links shops, dining areas and cultural events with a long waterfront promenade, preserving the outline of the docks within a contemporary recreation district.

Azrieli Ayalon Mall

Azrieli Ayalon Mall opened in Ramat Gan during the 1980s and became an influential example of Israel's suburban shopping-center development. Retail floors, food outlets and services are supported by extensive parking and links with nearby stadium and transport infrastructure. The complex shows how metropolitan consumer space expanded beyond older city-center streets.

ANU Museum of the Jewish People

ANU, the Museum of the Jewish People, occupies the Tel Aviv University campus. Rebuilt galleries follow Jewish communities across many regions through artifacts, media and personal stories. Models and music help explain belief, language and cultural exchange, while the institution's broad chronological scope connects ancient history with modern identities and debates.

Old Jaffa

Old Jaffa grew around an ancient Mediterranean port with archaeological layers, Ottoman buildings, churches, mosques and markets. Stone alleys climb from the harbor toward squares, artists' lanes and viewpoints now integrated into Tel Aviv-Yafo. Traditions associated with Jonah form one strand of a much longer urban history shaped by trade, conquest, migration and modern restoration. The harbor remains the clearest link to Jaffa's origins.

St. Peter's Church

St. Peter's Church overlooks Jaffa's harbor from a Franciscan compound completed in its present form in the late nineteenth century; Catholic worship continues in several languages, while service schedules and group access determine when the interior can be viewed. Red brick, a high bell tower, vaulted nave, stained glass, and paintings refer to episodes involving the apostle Peter.

Jaffa Light

Jaffa Light marks the edge of the historic harbor from a stone tower incorporated into the old coastal defenses. Built during the Ottoman period and altered in later years, it guided vessels approaching a port known for reefs, surf and a difficult entrance; the compact structure now forms part of a layered shoreline of fortifications, religious buildings and working harbor spaces.

Habima Theatre

Located Tel Aviv, Habima Theatre is a landmark of cultural expression and artistic exploration. Established in 1912 in Bialystok, Russia, this venue has evolved into Israel's national theater since 1958, showing a rich history of Hebrew performances that look at Jewish traditions and contemporary issues. The theater is equipped with translating and subtitling technology to ensure accessibility for all audiences.

Ramat Gan Safari covers more than 250 acres east of central Tel Aviv, combining a drive-through savanna with a conventional zoo. Cars and safari buses pass open paddocks where antelope, giraffe, and other herd animals range at a distance. Pedestrian sections add denser animal houses and educational exhibits beside the open-range loop.

Carmel Market

Shuk HaCarmel extends along a narrow street in central Tel Aviv. Produce stands and butchers occupy the traditional food-market core, joined by fishmongers, spice sellers and bakeries. Prepared-food counters and clothing stalls have expanded the mix, while side lanes connect the market with the Yemenite Quarter. Deliveries and crowds are heaviest early in the day.

Tel Aviv Promenade

The Tel Aviv Promenade runs more than seven kilometers along the Mediterranean, linking city beaches with a continuous walkway for pedestrians, cyclists, and scooters. Seafront hotels and Bauhaus blocks rise inland while the path itself stays open to the water. Benches and ramps mark the main beach access points along the route.
