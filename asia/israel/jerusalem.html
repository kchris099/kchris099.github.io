Jerusalem

Jerusalem's Old City layers Canaanite, Jewish, Roman, Byzantine, Islamic, and Ottoman sacred landscapes within Ottoman walls. The Western Wall, Church of the Holy Sepulchre, and Dome of the Rock anchor competing yet intertwined pilgrimage traditions that shaped Levantine history for three millennia. Mount of Olives viewpoints and city-wall gates connect sacred quarters to surrounding Judaean hills.

Church of the Holy Sepulchre

The Church of the Holy Sepulchre is a historic basilica dating back to the 4th century and is believed by some Christians to be the site of Jesus's tomb. The church holds historical significance, with its roots tracing back through thousands of years of Jewish history, from Byzantine Rule to the British Mandate. Visitors can experience a whirlwind tour that includes stops at sites like the Western Wall, Via Dolorosa, and Bethlehem.

New Gate

New Gate opens the northwest wall of Jerusalem's Old City toward the Christian Quarter, added in the late Ottoman period to ease access. Stone towers and a vehicle-and-pedestrian passage face the modern Mamilla side. Pilgrims and residents use it as a practical entrance rather than a Crusader relic.

Museum of The Underground Prisoners

The Museum of Underground Prisoners occupies a former Russian Compound hostel converted by the British Mandate authorities into Jerusalem's central prison, and the museum emphasizes Zionist resistance. Cells, workshops, a synagogue, gallows, photographs, and personal records interpret Jewish underground members and other detainees.

Located Jerusalem, the Friends of Zion Museum is a evidence of the long running friendship between Jewish and Christian communities over time. St. Anne's Church, Jerusalem

St. Anne's Church is a twelfth-century Crusader building near the Pools of Bethesda in Jerusalem's Old City; excavated reservoirs and later religious structures beside the church belong to a separate archaeological complex with many chronological layers. Its austere Romanesque interior is known for resonant acoustics used during sung prayer, while Christian tradition associates the site with Mary's birthplace.

Dome of the Rock

The Dome of the Rock is an Islamic shrine with a gold-topped structure built around 690AD. It holds great significance for Christians and Jews as well. Situated on the Temple Mount, it was completed under the rule of Umayyad Caliph Abd al-Malik and has been covered in gold twice in the 20th century. Despite common misconceptions, it is not a mosque but rather a shrine to the Foundation Stone, important in both Torah and Islamic beliefs.

Western Wall

The Western Wall in Jerusalem's Jewish Quarter is a surviving retaining face of the Temple Mount platform, built about two thousand years ago. After the Second Temple's destruction it became the chief place of Jewish prayer and lament beside the mount. Worshippers recite psalms and press written notes into the joints between the Herodian stones.

Al-Aqsa Mosque

Rebuilt between the eighth and eleventh centuries, the domed mosque on the Temple Mount ranks among Islam's holiest sites and shares the plateau sacred in Judaism for the ancient Temple. Saint Peter in Gallicantu, built in 1931 on the eastern slope of Mount Zion, commemorates Peter's denial of Jesus and later repentance. Chapels, mosaics, and excavated spaces occupy the hillside site. Views toward the Old City accompany the liturgical focus of the church.

Zion Gate

Suleiman the Magnificent's builders completed Zion Gate in the sixteenth century as an entrance between the Armenian and Jewish quarters and Mount Zion. Bullet scars around the angled opening record fighting during the 1948 war. Vehicles and pedestrians pass through a gate whose defensive form, modern damage, and proximity to religious sites reveal several periods.

The Menachem Begin Heritage Center examines Begin's life from Polish Jewish youth and underground leadership through opposition politics, premiership and the peace treaty with Egypt. Jerusalem's First Station adapts the city's nineteenth-century railway terminus and platforms as a privately managed dining, retail and event compound. Restored station buildings, tracks, sheds, outdoor seating, markets and cultural programs preserve parts of the Jaffa-Jerusalem line; tenant turnover and weekend schedules change, while the complex no longer carries intercity trains.

Montefiore Windmill

The Montefiore Windmill, built in 1857 at Yemin Moshe's edge, was meant to grind flour for a new neighborhood outside the Old City walls promoted by Moses Montefiore. The stone tower overlooks the Hinnom Valley. Restored mechanism displays explain the philanthropic housing project around it.

Damascus Gate

Damascus Gate, a historic stone structure dating back to 1537, is one of the primary entrances to Jerusalem's Old City. It is located near significant religious sites such as the believed crucifixion and resurrection site of Jesus. Annually, an evening of Christmas carols in English, Hebrew, and Arabic takes place here, nearby attractions include Ein Kerem village with its ancient holy sites like the Church of the Visitation and the Church of John the Baptist.

Mt of Olives

The Mount of Olives forms a long ridge east of Jerusalem's Old City with Jewish cemeteries, Christian churches, mosques, villages, roads and viewpoints. Biblical and later traditions associate it with King David, prophets, Jesus's teaching, arrest and ascension. The large landscape contains many separate sites, while steep roads, funerary space and current neighborhood conditions shape movement across it.

Shrine of the Book

The Shrine of the Book, located within the Israel Museum, is a unique dome-shaped structure that houses the Dead Sea Scrolls, significant archaeological discoveries of the 20th century. These ancient biblical manuscripts were accidentally found in remote caves near Qumran and are considered a literary treasure of importance. The building's design is inspired by the covers of the pots in which the scrolls were originally discovered.

Church of the Nativity

A sixth-century basilica stands over a grotto traditionally identified as the birthplace of Jesus. The Church of the Nativity preserves an active Greek Orthodox nave, surviving Byzantine floor mosaics, later Crusader decoration and adjoining Armenian and Catholic chapels. Narrow entrances and layered religious spaces reflect centuries of rebuilding, shared custody and continuous pilgrimage within a site of major Christian significance.

Yad Vashem

Yad Vashem, established in 1953, is a memorial complex dedicated to honoring the six million Jewish victims of the Holocaust. It also pays tribute to the bravery of Jewish resistance fighters and non-Jews who risked their lives to save Jews during that time. Located on Har Hazikaron in Jerusalem, it includes monuments, a historical museum, an archive and research center for documenting the Holocaust, and an educational facility.

Herzl Museum

The Herzl Museum on Mount Herzl uses staged rooms, audiovisual presentation, documents and a reconstruction of Theodor Herzl's study to examine the founder of political Zionism. Its narrative follows his journalism, writings, diplomatic efforts and legacy in the creation of Israel. The adjacent national cemetery and Herzl's grave are separate memorial spaces within the larger hill.

Jerusalem's Biblical Zoo occupies landscaped valleys and hills with animal habitats, paths, a lake, education areas and conservation-breeding programs. Its original mission emphasized species mentioned in the Hebrew Bible, but the current collection includes wildlife from many regions; animal holdings, exhibits and ethical standards change, while the large grounds require more than a simple label as a biblical display.

Ein Lavan

In the Judean Hills west of Jerusalem, Ein Lavan is a natural spring with two refurbished pools fed by cool hill water. One basin stays shallow for wading; the other is deep enough for a full swim. Late summer and early autumn bring the steadiest flow and the busiest picnic crowds among the pines.

Sataf

Sataf preserves reconstructed agricultural terraces, springs, pools, irrigation channels, orchards and village remains in the Jerusalem hills. Walking routes explain how water supported olives, vines, almonds and gardens across generations, while parts of the Arab village were depopulated in 1948. That recent history belongs beside ancient farming interpretation.

Ein Hemed, also known as Aqua Bella, protects a stream-fed valley west of Jerusalem with Crusader-period ruins beside picnic lawns. Stone arches from a medieval farm estate sit above pools and shaded paths used for day walks. The park links woodland trails with the spring channel that gave the site its Arabic and Hebrew names.

Nebi Samuel crowns a hill northwest of Jerusalem around a mosque and synagogue complex traditionally linked to the prophet Samuel.
