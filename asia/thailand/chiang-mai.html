Chiang Mai

Founded in 1296 as the capital of the independent Lanna Kingdom, Chiang Mai was protected by massive walls and a defensive moat. Its profound Buddhist heritage and distinct northern cultural identity survived centuries of Burmese occupation before eventual integration with modern Thailand. Visitors today can clearly trace the detailed layers of this unique heritage throughout the preserved streets and monuments.

Tha Phae Gate

Tha Phae Gate marks the reconstructed eastern entrance of Chiang Mai's old city wall. Much of the original defensive gate and adjoining masonry was lost or altered before twentieth-century work created the present red-brick form. A broad public square outside the gate hosts festivals, markets and gatherings, while the surviving moat and wall line help explain the former boundaries of the fortified city.

Wat Phra That Doi Suthep

Wat Phra That Doi Suthep occupies a mountain terrace high above Chiang Mai. A long naga stairway leads to cloisters surrounding a gilded chedi, bells, murals and smaller shrines used by an active Buddhist community. Temple tradition links its foundation to a sacred relic carried by a white elephant in the fourteenth century, while the elevated grounds open across the city and northern plain.

Wiang Kum Kam

Wiang Kum Kam is an ancient city and temple complex located just 5 kilometers southeast of the Old City in Chiang Mai, Thailand. This historic settlement was the capital of the Lanna Kingdom, built in the 13th century under King Mangrai the Great. Today, Wiang Kum Kam is an open-space museum showing Lanna culture from that period. Visitors can explore brick ruins, Buddhist sculptures, and everyday items from this era.
