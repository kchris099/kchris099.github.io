Sukhothai

= Borihan Thepthani claimed the city of Sukhothai was founded in 494 CE, followed by its twin city of Chaliang (เชลียง). In contrast, the Legend of the Arhat (Tamnan Nithan Phra Arahant; ตํานานนิทานพระอรหันต์) says Sukhothai was founded in 679 CE by Indrajayadhirāja (อินทราไชยธิราช), who was from the city of Nakhon Luang (นครหลวง; lit. 'capital city'; potentially Lavapura); however, he, in 687, was deposed by Balidhiraja, the elder son of Kalavarnadisharaja of Lavo. The succession of rulers following Balidhiraja remains obscure in the extant historical record.

Sukhothai Historical Park

UNESCO listing covers Sukhothai Historical Park, which preserves kingdom ruins from the twelfth to fourteenth centuries among paths, lawns, and shade. Brick temples and reservoirs mark the former capital. Walking routes stitch the monuments across open park ground.

17.0188° N / 99.7065° E

02 AdmissionFree

King Ramkhamhaeng Monument

Inside Sukhothai Historical Park, the King Ramkhamhaeng Monument honors the third Sukhothai ruler credited with creating Thai script. The memorial sits in an open plaza setting among the UNESCO ruins. The statue focuses memory on literacy and kingship rather than a temple hall.

17.0200° N / 99.7047° E

03 Admission฿200

Wat Sa Si

A late fourteenth-century main chedi at Wat Sa Si survives in clear Singhalese-influenced form inside Sukhothai Historical Park. Exterior remains and open interiors can usually be seen outside ritual times. The island-like setting among ponds marks the ruin on park maps.

17.0197° N / 99.7014° E

04 Admission฿200

Wat Mahathat

Wat Mahathat forms the ceremonial center of Sukhothai Historical Park, with a large lotus-bud chedi, assembly halls, subsidiary stupas, Buddha images, and moats. Royal patronage expanded the temple during the Sukhothai kingdom, while later abandonment and restoration shaped the ruins.

17.0169° N / 99.7037° E

05 Admission฿200

Wat Si Sawai

Laterite prangs with mythical figures show Angkor-period style at Wat Si Sawai, once a Hindu temple dedicated to Vishnu and later turned to Buddhist use. The sacred towers sit among Sukhothai's open ruins on park ground. Hindu origins and Buddhist reuse share the same laterite group without a modern rebuild.

17.0138° N / 99.7023° E

06 Admission฿200

Wat Si Chum

Wat Si Chum (Wat Srijun) in Sukhothai is known for a massive seated Buddha enclosed in a mondop with a narrow viewing slit. Nearby stands a small ordination hall with multi-tiered roofs and carved eaves showing Burmese stylistic influence on the woodwork around the compound.

17.0268° N / 99.6932° E

07 Admission฿200

Wat Phra Phai Luang

Wat Phra Phai Luang in Sukhothai Historical Park once held three towers; one still stands largely intact while foundations and fragments of the others remain nearby. The brick Khmer-influenced ruin sits among trees outside the densest central monument zone near Wat Si Chum.

17.0292° N / 99.6995° E

08 Admission฿200

Wat Chang Lom

Thirty-two elephant statues surround the base of Wat Chang Lom's stupa-shaped pagoda, whose name means temple surrounded by elephants. A vihara, ordination hall, lesser chedis, and a boundary wall complete the enclosure. The elephant ring is the temple's clearest motif.

17.0200° N / 99.7197° E

09 Admission฿100 (match ticket)

Thalay Luang Stadium

Thalay Luang Stadium, located Sukhothai, is a venue that captures the essence of local sports culture. This well-kept stadium has clean facilities and a spacious layout, making it an inviting spot for both avid football fans and curious visitors. The atmosphere here is electric, especially during matches featuring the local team, Sukhothai FC.

17.0619° N / 99.7934° E

10 AdmissionFree

San Phra Mae Ya

San Phra Mae Ya in Sukhothai houses a stone image of Phra Mae Ya, regarded as an ancient queen figure. Locals come to pray for good fortune; the shrine compound is modest, with the idol hall as the main focus rather than large historical-park ruins near Thalay Luang Stadium.

17.0052° N / 99.8256° E
