Ayutthaya

For four centuries Ayutthaya was Siam's capital and a cosmopolitan river port until the Burmese sack of 1767. Brick prang and monastery ruins across the island city are the burnt shell of that pre-Bangkok kingdom. Foreign trading quarters once lined the rivers; Japanese and European villages are marked in ruins and museums. Wat Phra Mahathat's tree-wrapped Buddha head is the ruin visitors photograph most.

Wat Mahathat

A Buddha head wrapped in tree roots draws attention at Wat Mahathat in Ayutthaya, whose colorful shrine remains survive Burmese invasion damage that ended the capital's temple life. Grounds still show brick towers and broken halls. The ruin remains central to the historic park circuit.

Wat Ratchaburana

Built to remember King Intharacha and two sons who died in an elephant duel, Wat Ratchaburana in Ayutthaya keeps elaborate carvings and a restored tower and tomb. Crypt frescoes depict scenes from the Buddha's life. The memorial temple sits among the city's major ruins.

Wat Phanan Choeng Worawihan

Wat Phanan Choeng Worawihan, also known as the temple of the seated Buddha, is a historic and ornate temple complex located by the river. Dating back to before the founding of the Ayutthaya Kingdom, it has survived fires and been renovated over time. The main hall houses an 19-meter tall sitting Buddha statue that attracts both locals and visitors.

Wat Yai Chai Mongkol Worawihan

Wat Yai Chai Mongkhon developed from a monastery founded in Ayutthaya's early period and later became associated with King Naresuan. A tall restored chedi, rows of seated Buddha images, cloisters, and an active modern temple occupy the grounds outside the historic island; much visible masonry has been repaired.

Chedi Wat Sam Pluem

Chedi Wat Sam Pluem stands in a traffic circle east of Ayutthaya's historic island, its brick stupa isolated from the larger monastery that once surrounded it. The surviving monument is visible from passing roads and marks a former religious site now absorbed into the modern street network. Its exact date remains uncertain, but the form reflects Ayutthaya's long tradition of brick Buddhist architecture.

Wat Lokaya Sutha

Wat Lokayasutharam holds a reclining Buddha about 42 metres long within Ayutthaya Historical Park, roughly a kilometre behind the old palace grounds. Access roads pass Wat Worachet and related ruins before the open brick terrace. A small canal separates the image from denser clusters of the park.

Wat Phra Si Sanphet

Inside Ayutthaya's former royal palace grounds, Wat Phra Si Sanphet is known for three aligned Sri Lankan-style chedis that once held royal ashes. A vihara on the site displayed a tall gilded Buddha image, later lost, remembered in period accounts for its size and gold covering. Brick platforms and chedi silhouettes now define the open ruin that travellers walk among within the historic park.

Wihan Phra Mongkhon Bophit is a busy and contemporary temple located just 4 km from the city center of Ayutthaya. Wat Phra Ram

Fourteenth-century brick at Wat Phra Ram shows Buddha images on outer walls and several ornate stupas beside Bueng Phra Ram lake. Sunset reflections in the water make the ruin a frequent photo stop. Crumbling masonry still reads as a working religious site in outline.

Wat Kasattrathirat Worawihan

Wat Kasattrathirat Worawihan remains an active Buddhist monastery on Ayutthaya's west bank, with living monks, tended gardens, and a river pier where fish gather. Ordination halls and chedis stand among mature trees rather than as abandoned brick fields. Wat Tha Ka Rong

Wat Tha Ka Rong sits beside the Chao Phraya in Ayutthaya with brightly painted halls, folk statues, and animatronic figures that give the compound a playful character. Wat Niwet Thammaprawat stands on an island beside Bang Pa-In Palace, reached by a small cable crossing. King Chulalongkorn commissioned the nineteenth-century Buddhist temple in a Gothic Revival style resembling a European church, with stained glass, pointed arches, and a spire.

Wat Kudi Dao

Wat Kudi Dao is a ruined Ayutthaya monastery whose major restoration phase ran from 1711 to 1715, leaving brick chedis, prang fragments, and monastery walls in a quieter corner of the historic landscape. Pathways thread through the remains without the densest tourist crowds of the central island. Nearby Wat Maheyong extends the same outing for those continuing on foot or by bicycle.

Wat Maheyong

King Borommarachathirat II sponsored major work in the fifteenth century, drawing on Sri Lankan Buddhist forms; modern monastic facilities stand nearby, while the ruins' open terrain and partial restoration reveal part of the original complex. Wat Maheyong preserves brick foundations, a large chedi, boundary walls, and rows of elephant sculptures from a royal monastery outside Ayutthaya's island.

Wat Na Phra Meru Rachikaram

Wat Na Phra Meru Rachikaram survived Ayutthaya's wars as a living temple and is known for unusual Buddha images, including a figure in royal attire and a green-stone Buddha linked to Dvaravati workmanship. The ubosot and supporting halls still host ceremonies across from the former palace area. Travellers come for the intact interiors as much as for the exterior brick and stucco.

Wat Thammikarat

Wat Thammikarat contains ruins from several Ayutthaya periods alongside an active monastery. A bell-shaped chedi ringed by lion figures, a roofless ordination hall, Buddha images, and a reclining Buddha reflect repeated patronage and restoration. Legends connect the foundation with rulers before Ayutthaya's rise, but visible remains and inscriptions provide firmer context than an exact mythical date.

Wat Phutthaisawan

Wat Phutthaisawan occupies the south bank of the Chao Phraya opposite Ayutthaya's island. Tradition links its foundation to King U Thong, while later rulers rebuilt the white central prang, cloisters, Buddha images, and monastic halls. The active temple combines restored architecture with ruins and riverside worship, making it more than a single surviving monument from 1353.

King Naresuan Monument

A mounted statue of King Naresuan stands within a ceremonial complex near Ayutthaya, commemorating the ruler associated with restoring Siamese independence from Burma in the sixteenth century. Surrounding offerings often include rooster figures linked to popular legend. The monument reflects modern national memory and devotion.

วัดภูเขาทอง

Wat Phu Khao Thong, often called the Golden Mount of Ayutthaya, rises as a large chedi on a man-made mound northwest of the historic island. The whitewashed tower commemorates conflicts of the Ayutthaya period and remains a climbable monument with surrounding monastery buildings. From the upper terraces, fields and distant prangs of the old capital come into view across the flat central plain.

Queen Sri Suriyothai Memorial

The Queen Sri Suriyothai Memorial in Ayutthaya commemorates the queen remembered for dying in battle while defending her husband, King Maha Chakkraphat. A chedi and statues stand in a landscaped memorial ground that travellers reach alongside visits to the wider historic park. Interpretive plaques retell the sixteenth-century story that made Suriyothai a national symbol of sacrifice.

Chao Phrom Market

Dating from Ayutthaya's capital era, Chao Phrom Market trades local foods, fresh produce, fish, meats, electrical goods, and household items around the clock. Crowds keep the aisles active day and night. The market remains a practical shopping floor rather than a staged craft fair.
