Tbilisi

Tbilisi was founded in the 5th century under King Vakhtang Gorgasali around sulphur springs in a Mtkvari river gorge. Arab, Persian, Mongol, and Russian periods layered mosques, churches, baths, and boulevard planning onto the ridge-and-valley site. It became capital of a unified Georgia and later of the Soviet Georgian republic. Narikala fortress, Abanotubani baths, and Rustaveli Avenue still map those successive capitals.

Holy Trinity Cathedral of Tbilisi

Sameba, the Holy Trinity Cathedral, rises on Elia Hill above the Kura, designed by Archil Mindiashvili and built between 1995 and 2004. The complex includes a bell tower, monastery buildings, and a seminary beside the main Georgian Orthodox church. At nearly 105 metres it dominates the skyline east of the old town.

Mother of Georgia

A twenty-metre aluminum figure known as Kartlis Deda stands on Sololaki Hill above Tbilisi. Sculptor Elguja Amashukeli created the monument in 1958 for the city's 1,500th anniversary, giving the woman a bowl of wine for friends and a sword for enemies. Paths and cable-car routes approach the statue, whose scale and symbolism become clearer against views of the old town and river valley.

Bridge of Peace

Italian architects and French lighting designers shaped this steel-and-glass pedestrian span of about 150 meters across the Mtkvari. It links the old and new districts with a curved, illuminated deck. Walkways at either end open toward Rike Park and the historic lanes across the river.

Zion Cathedral of the Dormition of Tbilisi

Sioni Cathedral has served as a central church of Tbilisi through repeated destruction, rebuilding and political change. Most of the present structure reflects medieval and later reconstruction, with stone walls, domes, icons, frescoes and chapels surrounding a relic traditionally identified as Saint Nino's cross. The cathedral remains active, so liturgy and devotional use take precedence over sightseeing.

Public Service Hall

The Public Service Hall, also known as the House of Justice, is a striking glass building with 11 white "petals" located by the riverside. Designed by Italian architect Massimiliano Fuksas, it is part of a series of modern government buildings. Travellers praise its efficient service and multilingual staff. Despite some maintenance issues, the building's unique architecture and busy location near the Bridge of Peace make it a notable landmark in Tbilisi.

Dry Bridge Market

The Dry Bridge Market in Tbilisi is a active al fresco flea market that offers a diverse array of goods, from art and housewares to Soviet memorabilia. Located near Rustaveli Avenue, this daily market (with more vendors on weekends) is a cultural hub where find 19th century European porcelain, modern Dagestani jewelry, Georgian enameling, antique musical instruments, and contemporary artworks.

Narikala Fortress

Walls of Narikala Fortress follow a ridge above Tbilisi's old town and the Mtkvari River. Defensive structures occupied the site from late antiquity, then changed under Persian, Arab, Georgian, Mongol, Ottoman and Russian rule before an 1827 explosion damaged much of the complex. Surviving ramparts and the rebuilt Church of Saint Nicholas can be approached by path or cable car, although closures may restrict unstable sections.

National Botanical Garden

The National Botanical Garden of Georgia, also known as the Tbilisi Botanical Garden, is a historic hilltop garden located a valley between the Sololaki and Teleti Ridges in Tbilisi. Originally established in the mid-19th century on the grounds of a former royal park, it was designated as the national botanical garden in 2012. The garden has manicured grounds with seasonal flowers, a waterfall, and panoramic views of the city.

Tbilisi Juma Mosque *

Tbilisi's Juma Mosque stands on the slope below Narikala near the sulfur-bath district. Rebuilt in the nineteenth century after earlier mosques were destroyed, the brick structure has long been associated with shared worship by Sunni and Shia Muslims in the city. A simple prayer hall, mihrab, calligraphy, carpets, and balcony reflect an active religious space.

Also called the Colorful Bathhouse, it sits in Abanotubani, the sulfur-bath quarter tied to the legend of King Vakhtang Gorgasali discovering the springs while hunting. Domed bathhouses cluster around the mineral waters used for soaking and social bathing. Tiled facades mark Chreli Abano among the neighboring houses.

Chronicles of Georgia

Massive dark columns rise above Tbilisi Sea at the monument commonly called the Chronicle of Georgia. Zurab Tsereteli began the ensemble in the 1980s, covering its bronze-clad forms with scenes from Georgian rulers, literature, Christianity and the life of Christ. The project remains incomplete, yet its scale, reliefs, exposed hilltop setting and distant city views make its intended national narrative legible.

Funicular

The Tbilisi Funicular climbs Mount Mtatsminda from the city to the hilltop park, television tower, and terrace restaurants. Cars rise above the rooftops toward viewpoints over the old town and the Kura valley. The line remains a practical link as well as a scenic ride for residents and guests.

Mtatsminda Park

Mtatsminda Park spreads across the ridge above Tbilisi at the upper end of the historic funicular. Amusement rides, a Ferris wheel, restaurants, play areas, walking paths, and broad viewpoints occupy a site developed for public recreation during the twentieth century. Ride schedules and seasonal operations vary, while the hillside panorama and funicular journey remain available independently of many attractions.

Mtatsminda Pantheon

The Mtatsminda Pantheon, situated on the slopes of Mount Mtatsminda in Tbilisi, Georgia, is a known necropolis and final resting place for legendary authors, dancers, national heroes, and scholars. Established in 1929 within the St. Davids Church Complex or Mamadaviti churchyard, it holds the graves of some of Georgia's most prominent writers, artists, and public figures.

Opera and Ballet Theater of Tbilisi

Moorish Revival arches and patterned ornament distinguish the Tbilisi Opera and Ballet State Theatre on Rustaveli Avenue, and Georgia's opera institution began in the nineteenth century, while the present building opened in 1896 and underwent major restoration before reopening in 2016. Its auditorium, foyers, stage, orchestra, and resident ballet and opera companies are normally experienced through performances or arranged tours.

Kashueti St. George Church

Kashveti Church of St George on Rustaveli Avenue was finished in 1910 as a stone replica of eleventh-century Georgian cathedral forms on the site of an earlier damaged church. Notable Georgians, including David Sarajishvili, are buried in the grounds. The facade faces the busy central boulevard.

Mimino Monument

Mimino, a known comedy from 1977, holds a significant place in the cinematic history of the Soviet Union. The film's protagonist, Mimino, aspires to become an international airline pilot and continues to hold a special spot in people's hearts. This is evident from the monument at Avlabari square where bronze statues of the characters stand prominently. While some may tire of statues, these particular ones pique curiosity due to their connection to a beloved movie.

Gabriadze Theater

Rezo Gabriadze founded his small puppet theater in Tbilisi's old town in 1981, combining handcrafted figures, literary storytelling, and adult themes. The adjoining leaning clock tower was added later from mismatched architectural fragments and stages a brief mechanical scene at set times; performances occur in an intimate auditorium, while the tower and cafe can be seen without attending a show.

Anchiskhati Basilica

A compact three-nave stone basilica, later additions, icons, fresco fragments, and a freestanding bell tower stand close to the modern government quarter. Anchiskhati Basilica is generally regarded as Tbilisi's historic surviving church, with origins in the sixth century. The name recalls an icon once brought from Ancha, while current Orthodox worship gives the small interior a living role beyond its age.
