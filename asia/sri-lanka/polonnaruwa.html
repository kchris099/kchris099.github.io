Polonnaruwa

Polonnaruwa succeeded Anuradhapura as Sri Lanka's medieval capital after Chola invasions, flourishing under Parakramabahu I in the twelfth century. Gal Vihara rock Buddhas, royal palace foundations, and reservoir works record Sinhalese revival and hydraulic engineering. Parakrama Samudra embankments and quadrangle ruins extend medieval Sinhalese capitals across the dry zone.

Gal Viharaya

Four large Buddha images cut from a granite outcrop form Gal Vihara within ancient Polonnaruwa. Seated, standing and reclining figures display refined twelfth-century carving associated with the reign of Parakramabahu I. Sockets and foundations show that protective buildings once enclosed parts of the shrine, while the standing figure's intended identity remains a subject of scholarly debate.

Lankatilaka Temple

Towering brick and stone walls at Lankatilaka Temple in Polonnaruwa still suggest the original height of the image house. The ruin blends massive masonry with careful brickwork along the sacred city circuit. Exterior remains and open interiors can usually be viewed outside ritual times.

03 Admission$30

Rankoth Vehera

Rankoth Vehera is Polonnaruwa's large stupa, built in the twelfth century under King Nissanka Malla in a form recalling Anuradhapura's great dagobas. A broad circular terrace, brick dome, image houses, and cardinal approaches define the sacred precinct. Restoration has altered surfaces and stabilized masonry, while the monument remains a place of Buddhist reverence.

7.9582° N / 81.0033° E

Royal Palace of King Parakramabahu

Foundations and brick walls outline the royal palace traditionally associated with Parakramabahu I at Polonnaruwa; fire and later abandonment shaped the ruins. Chronicles describe a multi-story complex, but lower levels, thick walls, stairways, and adjacent audience and bathing structures survive.

7.9425° N / 81.0008° E

05 Admission$30

Vatadage

The Polonnaruwa Vatadage is a circular relic house with concentric stone terraces, guard stones, and Buddha images facing the cardinal points. Fine moonstones and carved steps mark entrances into the raised platform. Nearby palace and council remains of Parakramabahu and Nissankamalla sit within the same sacred quadrangle.

7.9473° N / 81.0015° E

06 Admission$30

Atadage

The Atadage in Polonnaruwa was a Temple of the Tooth relic house from the city's time as capital, with stone stairs, carved pillars, and a Buddha image among the ruins. It stands near the Hetadage and Vatadage in the inner sacred area. Pilgrims and visitors move between these related shrine platforms on one ticketed archaeological circuit.

7.9478° N / 81.0013° E

Polonnaruwa Ancient City

Polonnaruwa's ancient city spreads across royal, monastic, administrative and hydraulic remains from Sri Lanka's medieval capital. Palaces, audience halls, stupas, image houses, carved statues, reservoirs and gardens occupy several separated clusters. Heat, distance and sacred areas affect movement through the broad cultural landscape. Reservoirs and canals reveal the capital's dependence on managed water.

7.9520° N / 81.0048° E

08 Admission$30

Potgul Viharaya (Potgul Temple)

King Parakramabahu the Great is credited with Potgul Viharaya in Polonnaruwa, once home to a major library of ancient Sri Lanka. The twelfth-century complex sits among the sacred city's monastic ruins. Exterior walls and main chambers can usually be seen outside services.

7.9245° N / 80.9946° E

09 Admission$30

Thivanka Image House

Thivanka Image House in Polonnaruwa holds large Buddha figures and exterior brick decoration with fine, lace-like molded patterns. Interior paintings and sculptures survive better than in many neighboring shrines. The building sits in the northern monastery area of the ancient city for visitors extending beyond the central quadrangle.

Parakrama Samudra

Parakrama Samudra is the great reservoir attributed to King Parakramabahu I, an engineered lake that watered fields around Polonnaruwa and still defines the city's western edge. Bund roads and shore temples look across open water toward forested banks. The tank embodies the hydraulic foundation of the medieval capital's agriculture.

7.8996° N / 80.9705° E
