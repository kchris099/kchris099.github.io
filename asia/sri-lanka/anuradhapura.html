Anuradhapura

From the fourth century BCE Anuradhapura was the first major Sinhalese capital, organised around monasteries and irrigation tanks. Dagobas and the sacred Bodhi tree precinct embody early Theravada kingship on the northern plain. Pilgrimage circuits still follow the ancient tank-and-stupa geography. Mihintale nearby commemorates the arrival of Buddhism to the island under royal patronage.

Ruwanweli Maha Seya

Ruwanweli Maha Seya, an ancient stupa dating back to 140 BCE, is a evidence of Sri Lanka's rich Buddhist heritage. Towering at 103 meters originally, it now reaches a height of 55 meters due to historical damages. This white dagoba is encircled by a wall adorned with a frieze of 344 elephants, most of which are modern replicas.

Thuparamaya

Thuparamaya in Anuradhapura is traditionally counted among Sri Lanka's earliest Buddhist stupas, with origins traced to the third century BCE after Buddhism's arrival. Stone pillars once supported a vatadage-style roof around the relic mound. The site sits in the sacred city among tanks and other dagobas still used for offerings.

8.3553° N / 80.3965° E

Jethawanaramaya

Jetavanaramaya rises as a vast brick stupa within the ancient monastic city of Anuradhapura. King Mahasena sponsored its construction in the fourth century, and the mound once formed the center of a major Buddhist community with image houses, refectories, baths, and residential structures. Restoration has stabilized the monument, while active devotion requires respectful behavior around the sacred enclosure.

8.3515° N / 80.4037° E

Jaya Sri Maha Bodhi

Jaya Sri Maha Bodhi is revered pilgrimage sites in Anuradhapura, known as Atamasthana. It is a sacred fig tree that grew from a sapling taken from the original tree in India where Buddha achieved enlightenment. This significant symbol of Sri Lankan culture and spirituality holds great religious importance for Buddhists, making it an destination for pilgrims.

8.3446° N / 80.3972° E

Isurumuniya Temple

Isurumuniya Temple is a Buddhist temple with a rich history dating back thousands of years. It is known for its detailed stone carvings, including The Lovers and The Royal Family. This cloistral center, carved out of solid rock, once housed 500 Buddhist monks or children from noble families dedicated to the pursuit of enlightenment. Surrounding bright murals depict the life of Lord Buddha and stories from ancient Anuradhapura.

Ranmasu Uyana (Royal Park)

Ranmasu Uyana, the Royal Park beside Isurumuniya and Tissa Wewa, preserves ancient ponds, baths, and carved stonework from Anuradhapura's royal pleasure gardens across about forty acres. Paths follow the old hydraulic layout. The park shows early Sinhalese garden planning next to the monastery rock.

8.3384° N / 80.3898° E

Mirisawetiya stupa

Mirisawetiya Vihara, located in Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka, is a significant Buddhist temple with a rich history dating back to the 2nd century BC. The temple is known for its grand stupa, the Mirisawetiya Stupa or Mirisavetiya Dagaba, built by King Dutugamunu to honor the relics of Buddha.

08 Admission$30

Abhayagiriya

Abhayagiri developed into one of Anuradhapura's large monastic institutions after its foundation around an early century BCE; different Buddhist traditions and international links shaped the community, so the site extends far beyond the single restored dagoba commonly photographed. The tall brick stupa anchors a broad archaeological landscape of ponds, image houses, carved guardstones, moonstones, meditation buildings, and monastic residences.

8.3709° N / 80.3953° E

09 Admission$30

Anuradhapura Moonstone

Anuradhapura's carved moonstones are semicircular doorstep slabs with bands of animals, foliage, and flame motifs traditionally read as protective and symbolic thresholds into sacred precincts. Fine examples lie at monastery entrances across the ancient city. Visitors photograph the low reliefs at ground level before entering image houses.

8.3715° N / 80.3920° E

10 Admission$30

Kuttam Pokuna

Kuttam Pokuna, the Twin Ponds of Anuradhapura, are elaborately cut stone bathing tanks once serving monastic communities with stepped sides and filtered inlets. Precise masonry shows how the sacred city managed water beyond the great reservoirs. The paired pools remain a quiet archaeological stop among trees.

8.3710° N / 80.4017° E
