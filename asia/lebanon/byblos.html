Byblos

Continuously inhabited since the Neolithic period, Byblos became a major Phoenician port city known for trading cedar wood and papyrus. It was the center of the cult of Adonis and played a key role in the development of the Phoenician alphabet. Successive layers of Egyptian, Assyrian, Persian, Greek, Roman, and Crusader occupation reshaped its urban layout over millennia. Today, visitors can trace this extensive historical continuity through the ruins of the Temple of the Obelisks, the Crusader Castle, and the medieval harbor.

Byblos Citadel

Local limestone walls at Byblos Citadel reuse Roman masonry and carry later Phoenician-to-Ottoman layers inside the castle footprint. Courtyards and towers organize movement when the grounds open. The fortress sits above the ancient harbour city, tying medieval defense to much older coastal settlement.

The Old Souq

Stone-paved lanes of Byblos Old Souq connect the modern town with the archaeological site, harbor, churches and coastal streets; the district preserves the scale of a Levantine market quarter, though much of its present character comes from tourism-oriented reuse. Restored buildings now contain shops, cafes, restaurants, bars and souvenir sellers, especially active in the evening.

Byblos Dock And Fishing Port

Working boats and quay paths at Byblos Dock sit beside visible ruins of the ancient city, so harbour traffic and archaeology share one waterfront. Short boat rides leave from the dock for sea views of the old town. Embankment walks link the fishing berths to nearby shoreline streets.

34.1217° N / 35.6435° E

Jbeil Public Park

Jbeil Public Park is a modest municipal green space in modern Byblos. Paths, lawns and mature trees provide shade near residential streets and local services. Benches and play areas support everyday family use, while the simple grounds offer a neighborhood pause within a town whose ancient harbor and archaeological monuments occupy other nearby districts.
