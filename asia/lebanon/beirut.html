Beirut

Beirut is an ancient city with a history spanning over 5,000 years, originally settled by the Canaanites and Phoenicians. It flourished as a major Roman colony, known for its prestigious law school. Following periods of Arab, Crusader, and Ottoman rule, Beirut emerged as a cosmopolitan cultural and intellectual center in the twentieth century. Despite the devastation of the Lebanese Civil War, the city has continually rebuilt itself, maintaining its status as a dynamic Mediterranean capital.

Beirut Souks Lebanon

Beirut Souks occupies a rebuilt downtown commercial district over the footprint of older market streets damaged during Lebanon's civil war. Rafael Moneo's master plan retained portions of the historic street pattern while introducing modern retail blocks, cinemas, restaurants, and public passages; archaeological traces and reconstructed facades complicate any description of the area as either an intact traditional bazaar or an ordinary mall.

02 AdmissionFree

Martyrs' Square

A bronze memorial in central Beirut honors Lebanese and Arab nationalists executed by Ottoman authorities in 1916. The surrounding Martyrs' Square later became a stage for independence demonstrations, civil-war front lines, reconstruction and large political gatherings; open paving, the Mohammad Al-Amin Mosque and nearby archaeological and commercial districts show how the square remains contested civic ground.

33.8964° N / 35.5072° E

03 AdmissionFree

Mohammad Al Amin Mosque

Mohammad Al Amin Mosque, an Ottoman-influenced Muslim mosque, has a striking 48-meter blue dome and was reopened in 2008 after extensive renovations. Situated in downtown Beirut on the corner of Martyrs Square, this Sunni mosque stands out with its vast and towering presence compared to the surrounding architecture.

33.8952° N / 35.5063° E

04 AdmissionFree

Beit Beirut - Museum and Urban Cultural Center

Bullet-scarred walls remain visible inside Beit Beirut, the restored Barakat Building on the former civil-war dividing line. Architect Youssef Aftimus designed the apartment house in the 1920s and its open corner later gave snipers control over nearby streets; exhibitions and cultural programs use preserved damage, domestic rooms and archival material to examine Beirut before, during and after the conflict.

33.8870° N / 35.5084° E

05 AdmissionFree

Al Raouche Rocks

Two limestone formations rise just offshore from Beirut's Raouche cliffs, commonly called Pigeon Rocks. Waves and erosion created arches and steep faces that can be viewed from the corniche, cliffside paths, or boats operating in suitable conditions. Their geological form is clear.

33.8904° N / 35.4717° E

06 AdmissionFree

Beirut Lighthouse

A cylindrical tower on Beirut's Manara coast carries the city's active lighthouse above dense residential streets. It replaced an earlier light that became obscured by later construction, illustrating how the shoreline changed through urban growth. The structure is generally viewed from surrounding roads and the corniche.

33.9000° N / 35.4698° E

07 AdmissionFree

Corniche El Manara

Beirut's western corniche follows the Mediterranean from the city center toward Raouche and Ramlet al-Bayda. Wide pavements support walking, jogging, fishing, cycling, and evening gathering beside sea walls and rocky shore. Cafes and traffic line parts of the route, but the promenade's public role and coastal views matter more than stereotypes about luxury cars or nightlife.

33.9028° N / 35.4799° E

08 AdmissionFree

Sanayeh Park

Sanayeh Park, officially Rene Mouawad Garden, has provided central Beirut with shaded paths and public greenery since the late Ottoman period; renovation and wartime use have altered the grounds, yet the park remains an everyday neighborhood space. Mature trees, benches, lawns, fountains, and children's areas serve nearby residents within a walled city block.

33.8937° N / 35.4902° E

09 AdmissionFree

Horsh Beirut

Pine woodland covers Horsh Beirut, the capital's large major public green space, on land long associated with the city's historic pine forest. War, reconstruction, restricted access and repeated reopening campaigns shaped its recent history. The campaign aims to open it securely and permanently for the public to enjoy fresh air and nature.

33.8712° N / 35.5104° E

10 AdmissionFree

Souk El Tayeb Farmers' Market

Located on Armenia Street in Mar Mikhael, Beirut, Souk El Tayeb is a busy farmers' market that opens every Saturday. The market offers a wide variety of seasonal produce, dairy items, sweets, and crafts from around 100 small producers across Lebanon. Despite the damage caused by the Beirut Port Explosion, the market continues to attract the freshest produce from countryside farms.
