Guatemala City

Guatemala City became the national capital in 1773 after earthquakes devastated Antigua, relocating government to a highland valley surrounded by volcanoes. Metropolitan cathedral, National Palace, and Kaminaljuyú Maya mounds record how Spanish colonial administration, nineteenth-century coffee exports, and twentieth-century civil conflict shaped Central America's largest urban population centre.

Zoológico La Aurora

Located Guatemala City, Zoológico La Aurora has been visitors since its establishment in 1924. This enchanting zoo spans 14 acres of lush gardens and is home to over 100 species of both native and exotic animals, making it a popular with wildlife enthusiasts and families alike. Guests can explore diverse habitats that include the African savanna, Asian subcontinent, Mesoamerican tropics, and even a farm area.

Plaza Obelisco

Plaza del Obelisco in Guatemala City centers on a 19-meter stone obelisk erected in 1935 to commemorate the centenary of Central American independence. The traffic circle and surrounding Reforma Avenue district contain government offices, hotels, and mid-century commercial buildings. The monument is a common orientation point in the capital's Zona 9 civic corridor.

Torre del Reformador

Torre del Reformador is a 71.85-meter steel monument raised in Guatemala City's Zone 9 in 1935 for the centenary of president Justo Rufino Barrios. Its open lattice recalls contemporary European engineering, and a suspended bell originally hung within the frame before a signal light replaced it. Heavy traffic surrounds the tower, so it is viewed mainly from nearby streets and plazas.

Mercado Central

Beneath the blocks beside Guatemala City's cathedral, Mercado Central concentrates produce stalls, prepared food counters, textiles, crafts, household goods and religious items in a dense working market. Rebuilt after the 1976 earthquake, it mixes everyday shopping with products aimed at travelers. Narrow aisles, deliveries and busy meal periods matter more to its character than polished souvenir displays.

Catedral Metropolitana de Santiago de Guatemala

Facing Constitution Square, the Metropolitan Cathedral records Guatemala City's establishment after the capital moved from Antigua in the late eighteenth century. Construction extended into the early 1800s, and later earthquakes required repeated repair. A broad neoclassical facade, side chapels, artworks, memorials, and an active liturgical schedule connect national ceremonies with ordinary parish life.

La Sexta

Sixth Avenue, commonly called La Sexta, forms a pedestrian spine through Guatemala City's historic center. Restored facades, shops, street vendors, government buildings, churches, plazas and frequent public events line the route between major civic spaces. Its appearance changes block by block, making it useful for understanding both recent urban renewal and the commercial pressures shaping the old center.

Iglesia San Francisco

Franciscan worship has occupied this part of Guatemala City since the capital's relocation, although earthquakes and rebuilding changed the church repeatedly. The present Iglesia San Francisco shows a restrained neoclassical frontage on a busy central street. Altars, devotional images and regular services continue its religious role within architecture formed by several periods of reconstruction. The rebuilding history is visible in its mixed architectural details.

Parroquia Nuestra Señora de La Merced

Parroquia Nuestra Señora de La Merced is a yellow church with a neoclassical style, dating back to 1813. Inside, visitors can admire gold-plated Baroque altars that were originally from the La Merced church in Antigua. The church also is a museum, showing elaborate paintings, religious statues, and detailed sculptures from the 17th and 19th centuries.

Iglesia Cerrito del Carmen

A wooded rise above Guatemala City's center holds Cerrito del Carmen, an early sanctuary around which the new capital began to expand, and rebuilding has altered the structure over time, while processions and parish use preserve its importance beyond the hilltop views. The small church, terraces, paths, steps, and surrounding park create a quieter setting than the traffic below.

14.6469° N / 90.5057° W

Centro Cultural Miguel Ángel Asturias

Efraín Recinos conceived the Miguel Ángel Asturias Cultural Centre as sculpture, landscape and performance architecture on the former site of Fort San José. Curving concrete forms, murals, plazas, theaters and outdoor spaces support drama, music, dance and national ceremonies. The complex reflects twentieth-century Guatemalan modernism, although entry to interior halls depends on scheduled events.
