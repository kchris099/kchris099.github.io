Tikal

From the fourth to the ninth century AD, Tikal ranked among the foremost Classic Maya capitals of the Petén, allied at times with Teotihuacan and rivalled by other lowland dynasties. Kings raised Temple I (the Great Jaguar), the Great Plaza, and the Lost World complex before the city's abandonment by the tenth century. Jungle reclamation followed; excavated pyramids now form Guatemala's principal Maya archaeological park.

Templo IV, Tikal

Temple IV rises above Tikal's forest canopy as the city's tallest surviving pyramid, built in the eighth century during the reign of Yik'in Chan K'awiil. A steep modern stair system reaches a platform below the temple crest, revealing distant roof combs across the rainforest.

Templo del Gran Jaguar

Known as Temple I, the Temple of the Great Jaguar anchors the eastern side of Tikal's Great Plaza. The funerary pyramid was built for the eighth-century ruler Jasaw Chan K'awiil I, whose burial and offerings were found within it. Nine stepped levels, a high roof comb, carved lintels, and the relationship with Temple II express royal authority; climbing the original stair is generally restricted.

17.2220° N / 89.6230° W

Tikal National Park

Tikal National Park protects a major Maya city within lowland rainforest, where temples, palaces, reservoirs, causeways, residential groups and carved monuments spread far beyond the central plazas. Its mixed designation recognizes both archaeology and biodiversity. Long walking distances, heat, rain, wildlife and uneven paths shape exploration of a landscape occupied and modified for many centuries.

17.2220° N / 89.6237° W

The Lost World

Mundo Perdido, or the Lost World, forms one of Tikal's major ceremonial groups. A broad pyramid faces platforms and temples that were rebuilt repeatedly from the Preclassic period onward. Archaeologists have studied alignments between several structures and the movement of the sun, while elevated points reveal the surrounding forest and neighboring temple crests.
