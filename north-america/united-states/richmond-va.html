Richmond, VA

Richmond served as the capital of the Commonwealth of Virginia and later became the capital of the Confederate States of America during the Civil War, making it a primary target for Union forces. The city was strongly shaped by the domestic slave trade before the war and became a major center of African American commerce and culture in the Jackson Ward neighborhood afterward. Industrial tobacco and iron manufacturing fueled its later expansion. Visitors explore this complex, layered history at the Virginia State Capitol, the American Civil War Museum, and the historic Tredegar Iron Works.

Altria Theater

Altria Theater, situated on the Virginia Commonwealth University campus in Richmond, is a historic landmark that has been a prominent venue for various events since its opening in 1927. Originally known as The Mosque Theater, it has become popular stages for theatrical and musical performances in the city. The theater hosts a wide range of events including Broadway productions, concerts, comedy shows, children's theater, lectures, school commencements and fashion shows.

Virginia War Memorial

The Virginia War Memorial, dedicated in 1956 on a bluff above the James River in Richmond, honors Virginians who served in the U.S. armed forces. An education center and outdoor shrine list names in glass and stone. The open memorial terrace looks toward the downtown river bend.

Richmond Raceway

North of central Richmond, the raceway centers on a short oval used for major stock-car competitions and other events. Grandstands, garages, infield facilities and broad parking areas surround the track, whose calendar produces periods of intense activity and long quiet intervals. Entry, camping, tours, traffic plans and seating depend on scheduled races.

Virginia State Capitol

Virginia's State Capitol was designed by Thomas Jefferson with influence from a Roman temple and completed in the late eighteenth century on Richmond's Capitol Square. Later wings, legislative chambers, monuments and landscaped grounds reflect the continuing work of state government. Tours and public access operate around sessions, security, demonstrations, ceremonies and official business.

White House of the Confederacy

The White House of the Confederacy served as the Richmond residence of Confederate president Jefferson Davis during much of the Civil War. Restored rooms and interpretation examine the household, government, slavery, war, and the people who lived or worked there.

Maymont

Maymont, located in Richmond, Virginia, is a 19th-century estate that offers tours of its Gilded Age mansion, gardens, and wildlife center. Originally a private estate, it was generously donated to the city of Richmond and now spans over 100 acres. Visitors can start their tour at the Robins Nature & Visitor Center to see various fish, reptiles, and mammals from the James River and Chesapeake Bay.

The Robins Nature Center at Maymont

The Robins Nature Center at Maymont introduces Virginia's James River ecology through aquariums, exhibits and outdoor connections within a historic estate park. Fish, turtles, otters and other species may appear in managed habitats, while displays explain watersheds and local environments; animal care, renovations, school programs and estate events affect access and the center is distinct from Maymont's mansion and gardens.

The Byrd Theatre

The Byrd Theatre, a historic movie palace established in 1928 and located in Richmond's Carytown district, is a beloved institution offering a nostalgic cinematic experience. With its opulent interior featuring crystal chandeliers and a Wurlitzer organ, the theatre screens second-run films, classic movies, and special events at affordable prices.

Historic St. John's Church

Historic St. John's Church, a 1741 Episcopal church in Richmond, Virginia, holds great significance in American history. It was here on March 23, 1775, that Patrick Henry delivered his famous "Give me liberty or give me death!" speech during the Second Virginia Convention, persuading them to arm a Virginia militia.

Libby Hill Park

Libby Hill Park occupies an elevated site above Richmond's riverfront, with lawns, mature trees and monuments overlooking the James River and former industrial districts. The bend in the river is often linked to the city's naming tradition because it resembles a Thames view from Richmond, England; paths and steep surrounding streets connect the small neighborhood park with the broader history of the city below.
