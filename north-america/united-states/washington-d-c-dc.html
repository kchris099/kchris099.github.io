Destination Guide — District of Columbia

Explore Washington D.C., DC

A Brief History

Washington was established in 1790 as the permanent seat of the United States government on the Potomac River, with Pierre Charles L'Enfant's plan organizing radiating avenues around the Capitol and White House. Federal monuments, museums, and civic buildings expanded through the 19th and 20th centuries as the capital grew with wars, civil rights struggles, and national commemoration. Reclaimed waterfront, Metro lines, and embassy quarters reshaped movement through the city.

Top Sights & Activities

Lincoln Memorial

The Lincoln Memorial was dedicated in 1922 on the west end of the National Mall. Henry Bacon's Greek temple shelters Daniel Chester French's seated Lincoln and inscriptions of the Gettysburg Address and Second Inaugural. Steps overlook the Reflecting Pool toward the Washington Monument.

Free

Washington Monument

The Washington Monument is a massive, 555-foot marble obelisk located on the National Mall in Washington, D.C. Completed in 1884, it honors George Washington, the first U.S. President. Visitors can take an elevator to the observation deck at the top, which offers unparalleled, panoramic views of the capital city and its surrounding monuments.

Free

World War II Memorial

The National World War II Memorial opened in 2004 between the Washington Monument and Lincoln Memorial. Granite pillars represent states and territories, while fountains and bronze panels commemorate the armed forces and home front of the 1941–1945 war.

Free

Thomas Jefferson Memorial

The Thomas Jefferson Memorial was completed in 1943 in West Potomac Park. John Russell Pope's circular colonnade frames a bronze statue of Jefferson and excerpts from the Declaration of Independence and other writings. Cherry trees surround the Tidal Basin setting.

Free

Smithsonian National Museum of Natural History

The National Museum of Natural History opened on the National Mall in 1910 as part of the Smithsonian Institution. Its halls present fossils, minerals, cultural collections, and the Hope Diamond within one of the most visited museums in the United States.

Free

Smithsonian National Museum of American History

The National Museum of American History collects objects that record political, social, and technological change in the United States. Exhibits include the Star-Spangled Banner, presidential artifacts, and displays on industry, entertainment, and daily life.

Free

National Gallery of Art

The National Gallery of Art comprises the 1941 West Building and 1978 East Building on the Mall. Federal patronage created a collection spanning European old masters to modern American painting and sculpture, with a sculpture garden along Pennsylvania Avenue.

Free

National Archives Museum

The National Archives Museum in Washington, D.C., houses the original, founding documents of the United States: the Declaration of Independence, the Constitution, and the Bill of Rights. Displayed in the grand, dimly lit Rotunda for the Charters of Freedom, the museum provides a profound, direct connection to the origins of American democracy.

Free

United States Botanic Garden

The United States Botanic Garden is a living plant museum located on the grounds of the U.S. Capitol. Established in 1820, it features a massive, historic glass conservatory housing diverse global environments, including a tropical jungle, a desert, and rare orchids. The adjacent outdoor gardens focus on native North American plants and sustainable landscaping.

Free

Smithsonian Castle

The Smithsonian Institution Building, commonly known as the Castle, is the historic headquarters and visitor center for the Smithsonian museum complex. Completed in 1855, the distinctive red sandstone building features Norman-style architecture. It houses the crypt of James Smithson, the institution's founding benefactor, and provides orientation for the numerous museums on the National Mall.

Free

United States Capitol

The United States Capitol is the iconic, domed building that serves as the seat of the legislative branch of the U.S. federal government. Located at the eastern end of the National Mall, the massive neoclassical structure houses the Senate and the House of Representatives. The Capitol Visitor Center offers guided tours exploring its history, art, and legislative functions.

Free

The White House

The White House is the official residence and workplace of the President of the United States. Located at 1600 Pennsylvania Avenue, the historic neoclassical mansion has housed every U.S. president since John Adams. While interior tours require advance booking through a member of Congress, the exterior is a very photographed and globally recognized symbol of the American presidency.

$17

Washington National Cathedral

The Washington National Cathedral is a massive, dramatic Neo-Gothic Episcopal cathedral located in Northwest Washington, D.C. It is the sixth-largest cathedral in the world. The building features intricate stone carving, magnificent stained glass windows (including the Space Window containing a moon rock), and serves as a venue for significant national memorial services.

Free

Smithsonian National Zoological Park

The Smithsonian National Zoological Park, commonly known as the National Zoo, is a large, free-admission wildlife park located in Rock Creek Park, Washington, D.C. It is renowned for its comprehensive conservation programs and houses over 2,000 animals across diverse habitats. The zoo is particularly famous for its successful giant panda breeding program.

Free

National Mall

The National Mall, a vast green space in the heart of Washington, DC, is a major attraction for travellers. It features American landmarks such as the Lincoln Memorial, Capitol, Washington Monument, White House, FDR Memorial, Jefferson Memorial, WWII Memorial and Vietnam Veterans Memorial. The area also houses premier Smithsonian Museums including the National Museum of African American History & Culture and the National Gallery of Art.

Free

Martin Luther King, Jr. Memorial

The Martin Luther King, Jr. Memorial is a significant tribute to the renowned civil rights leader, Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr., located near the spot where he delivered his "I Have a Dream" speech. This 30-foot granite memorial was dedicated in 2011 and features a design that symbolizes Dr. King emerging from a "mountain of despair," referencing his famous speech.

Free

Korean War Veterans Memorial

The Korean War Veterans Memorial, situated on the National Mall, is a remarkable tribute to the brave individuals who served and sacrificed during the Korean War. The memorial features 19 striking stainless steel statues of soldiers from various branches of the armed forces, symbolizing the diverse group of Americans who fought in the war. These lifelike statues are set against a granite wall adorned with images of the war and inscribed with the powerful words 'Freedom Is Not Free.'.

Free

Vietnam Veterans Memorial

The Vietnam Veterans Memorial is a solemn tribute to the men and women who served in the Vietnam War. It consists of three parts: the Three Soldiers statue, the Vietnam Women's Memorial, and the Vietnam Veterans Memorial Wall. The wall, made of black granite, bears over 58,000 names of soldiers who either died or went missing during the war. Despite initial controversy over its design, it has become a place for healing and remembrance.

Free

Basilica of the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception

This enormous Catholic church is in the world and has been visited by many high-profile figures including popes. It was only finished in December 2017, when the Trinity Dome mosaic was realised, and can hold up to 10,000 worshippers. It features a vast art collection as well as air-conditioning and radiant heating in order to keep worshippers cool during hot summers.

Free

The memorial features a 60-foot bronze flagpole with figures depicting the moment of raising the American flag on Iwo Jima during World War II. Free

Georgetown Waterfront Park

Georgetown Waterfront Park is a serene green space with a walkway for strolling and biking, offering picturesque views and observation points. Situated between the National Mall and the Washington Channel, this area was once an old neighborhood but has since transformed into a lively district known for its seafood restaurants and entertainment venues.

Free

The Pentagon

The Pentagon, located south of Arlington Cemetery, is the world's largest office building and serves as the headquarters for the United States Department of Defense, Army, Navy, and Air Force. The Pentagon Memorial outside honors the victims of the September 11, 2001 terrorist attack. travellers can book free guided tours online with proper ID and reservations must be made well in advance.

Free

Old Town Alexandria Waterfront

Old Town Alexandria Waterfront is a charming riverside promenade with an array of bars, restaurants, and ice cream parlors. The historic Old Town Theatre, though no longer hosting performances, remains a beloved landmark in the city. Strolling along the waterfront at sunset offers views of National Harbor across the water. The bustling streets feature shopping, dining options, cobblestone streets, and 18th and 19th century architecture.

Free

Alfred Street Baptist Church

The Alfred Street Baptist Church is one of the oldest congregations in Alexandria and one of the oldest in the Washington area. The church was originally built in 1855, and has since offered both religious and secular learning opportunities for African Americans. In addition to its traditional services, the church also operates an educational branch that provides education to both children and adults.

$18

Free

9:30 Club

Located in the U Street Corridor, 9:30 Club is a legendary rock concert venue that has been a vital part of the District's music scene for over three decades. Originally founded in 1980 as Nightclub 9:30, it has hosted acts like the Red Hot Chili Peppers and Dolly Parton. The club's intimate atmosphere, pub food menu, and beer selection make it a popular spot for music lovers.

Free

Library of Congress

The Library of Congress is a federal cultural institution and the largest library in the world. It offers tours, talks, and concerts for travellers to explore its vast collection. The Library Store provides various items for commemorating the visit or continuing knowledge acquisition, including Thomas Jefferson-themed merchandise, wearables, mugs, bookends, greeting cards, and calendars.

Free

Supreme Court of the United States

The Supreme Court of the United States, housed in an Corinthian-style white building with 16 marble columns, is the highest tribunal for cases and controversies under the Constitution. travellers can explore this grand seat of justice on self-guided tours and learn from courtroom lectures, films, and exhibitions. The court's ultimate responsibility is to ensure equal justice under law as it interprets and guards the Constitution.

Free

Eastern Market

Eastern Market, established in 1873, is a lively marketplace situated in the heart of Capitol Hill. It offers a diverse range of products including fresh produce, handmade crafts, and prepared foods. The market is divided into two sections: the South Hall Market showcases an array of farm-fresh products and deli delights while the North Hall Events Space hosts various community events throughout the year.

$25

Museum of the Bible

Located just south of the National Mall in Washington, DC, the Museum of the Bible is a massive 430,000-square-foot facility that opened in 2017. Despite some controversy surrounding its origins, it offers an immersive and richly textured exploration of the world's -selling book. The museum features state-of-the-art exhibits, including a recreation of Jesus's Nazareth and ancient scrolls and rare Bibles.

Free

White House Visitor Center

The White House travellers Center offers an immersive experience showcasing the history and daily life of the presidential residence. With approximately 100 historical artifacts, interpretive panels, videos, and interactive elements for all ages, travellers can take a touchscreen tour of the White House and view over 90 artifacts from its collection.

Free

Ford's Theatre

Ford's Theatre is a historic site in downtown Washington, D.C., known for being the location of President Lincoln's assassination. The theater offers nightly performances and tours, allowing travellers to explore the events leading up to Lincoln's assassination and its impact on U.S. history. The museum inside the theater exhibits artifacts related to the tragic event, while admission includes a visit to Petersen House across the street, where Lincoln spent his final hours.

Free

Renwick Gallery of the Smithsonian American Art Museum

The Renwick Gallery of the Smithsonian American Art Museum is a branch that celebrates contemporary national artists' handicrafts, textiles, and decorative arts. Located near the White House, it houses a remarkable collection of over 2,000 objects and hosts exhibits that push boundaries and promote activism. The museum's current exhibit 'The Present Moment: Crafting a Better World' sheds light on often-overlooked stories while offering free admission.

Free

U.S. National Arboretum

The United States National Arboretum, located in Washington DC, is a free outdoor space showcasing a diverse collection of trees, shrubs, and plants from around the world. Established in 1927 as the national collection of botanical specimens, it serves as a hub for botanical research and preservation.

Free

Saint Camillus Church

Saint Camillus Church is a dynamic and diverse Catholic community that promotes an inclusive atmosphere. It boasts a strong sense of faith, with dedicated individuals leading and inspiring others through the teachings of Saint Francis. The liturgies held at this church are characterized by their beauty, while various ministries contribute to the overall positive impact they make. Notably, improvements have been made in maintaining restroom cleanliness.
