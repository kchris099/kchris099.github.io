Charleston, SC

Charleston was founded in 1670 as Charles Town, a Carolina colonial port built on rice, indigo, and enslaved labour. Its peninsula grid holds Georgian and Federal houses, churches, and markets that survived wars and fires. The Civil War began in its harbour at Fort Sumter. Preservation rules from the 20th century kept the historic core intact. Cobbles, piazzas, and battery views still organise the old port city.

Charleston City Market

Charleston City Market is a historic 4-block market that has been around for over 200 years. It's a hub for local artisans selling sweetgrass baskets, leather goods, art, and more. The market also offers a variety of food options and fresh farm-to-table produce. The architectural significance of the buildings adds to the charm of this cultural hotspot. Visitors can explore the area and indulge in local cuisine at nearby eateries like Butcher and Bee or Home Team BBQ.

Rainbow Row

Thirteen pastel Georgian rowhouses from the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries line Rainbow Row in Charleston's Historic District. Guided LGBTQ+ history walks support the College of Charleston's SC LGBTQ Archive, and a self-guided online map is also available. Sidewalk viewing covers the colorful facades.

McLeod Plantation Historic Site

Located on Johns Island, the McLeod Plantation Historic Site is a well-preserved historical plantation featuring a Georgian-style mansion, slave cabins, and interpretive tours. Established in 1851 with funds from sea island cotton and the labor of enslaved individuals, it now is a 37-acre Gullah/Geechee heritage site. Visitors can explore the McLeod family home, slave dwellings, and learn about the plantation's significance during the Civil War.

Fort Sumter and Fort Moultrie National Historical Park

Fort Sumter and Fort Moultrie National Historical Park is a pair of historic military sites with origins dating back to 1776. The park offers views of Charleston Harbor. At Fort Moultrie, visitors can see a model of the original palmetto log and sand fort that defended Charleston from British invasion in 1776.

Sullivan's Island Lighthouse

Sullivan's Island Lighthouse, located in the Fort Moultrie unit of Fort Sumter National Monument, is a historic landmark that has been guiding mariners into Charleston Harbor since its construction in 1862. This 85-foot tower, with a unique triangular shape resembling a tapered cone, is an assurance and symbol of guidance for generations. Although it does not offer indoor tours or visits, the lighthouse's distinctive architectural design makes it resistant to gales and hurricanes.

Fort Sumter National Monument

Fort Sumter occupies an island at the entrance to Charleston Harbor, where an early shots of the American Civil War were fired in 1861; boat access leads to brick ruins, gun positions, exhibits and memorials altered by bombardment and later military use. Weather, tides, ferry schedules and limited shade shape the visit, while interpretation includes slavery, secession and national memory.

Arthur Ravenel Jr. Bridge

The Arthur Ravenel Jr. Bridge carries road traffic, pedestrians and cyclists across the Cooper River between Charleston and Mount Pleasant, and completed in the early twenty-first century, its cable-stayed towers and long shared path open views toward the harbor, city and ships. Wind, heat, traffic noise, distance and temporary event closures make the crossing a substantial urban route.

Boone Hall Plantation in Mount Pleasant dates its working estate to 1681, with a half-mile oak alley planted from the 1740s. Nine brick antebellum slave cabins line the approach to the 1930s Colonial Revival house, cotton barn, and formal garden. The site documents Lowcountry plantation agriculture and enslaved labour.

Charles Pinckney National Historic Site

Located in Mount Pleasant, South Carolina, the Charles Pinckney National Historic Site is a 28-acre remnant of Charles Pinckney's original 18th-century plantation known as Snee Farm. The site offers indoor and outdoor exhibits that look at the life and times of Charles Pinckney, who was a signer of the U.S. Constitution. Visitors can explore archaeological findings that tell the story of both Pinckney and the enslaved people who lived on the plantation.

The Unitarian Church in Charleston was rebuilt in a Gothic Revival form during the nineteenth century on a congregation site with earlier colonial roots. A fan-vaulted interior, stained glass, memorials, and an overgrown churchyard distinguish the complex. Worship and community events continue, while guided access and cemetery conditions vary within the active religious property.

Cathedral of Saint John the Baptist

The Cathedral of Saint John the Baptist was completed in the early twentieth century after fire destroyed Charleston's earlier Catholic cathedral. Brownstone walls, pointed arches, stained glass, carved wood, religious art and a later-added spire define the Gothic Revival building. It is an active diocesan church, so services, ceremonies and parish life guide access.

The Battery

The Battery follows Charleston's southern waterfront where historic houses, defensive seawalls, White Point Garden, monuments and harbor views meet. The promenade reflects military history, elite residential development, slavery, storms and preservation. Flooding, heat, traffic and private property shape movement through the district.
