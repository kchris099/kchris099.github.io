Cincinnati, OH

Cincinnati prospered after Ohio River steamboats linked Appalachian coal and grain to New Orleans markets in the early nineteenth century. Roebling suspension bridge, Over-the-Rhine brewery brick, and Music Hall halls record how German immigration, pork-packing wealth, and abolitionist publishing earned the hillside city a Queen of the West reputation before Midwest industrial competition intensified.

Cincinnati Museum Center

The 1933 Art Deco Union Terminal, listed on the National Register, now holds the Cincinnati History Museum, Duke Energy Children's Museum, and Museum of Natural History & Science. Galleries open on posted hours inside the former station. The rotunda remains the shared civic arrival hall.

Great American Ball Park

Opened in 2003 as home of the Cincinnati Reds, Great American Ball Park pairs baseball with a sports bar and non-game events on the Ohio riverfront. Concourses overlook the water and downtown skyline. Game and event entry follow posted gates for each schedule.

Newport Aquarium

Newport Aquarium stands across the Ohio River from Cincinnati in Newport, Kentucky. Tunnel exhibits, sharks, rays, penguins, reptiles, fish and changing interactive programs form a commercial indoor attraction. Species collections, breeding claims and animal welfare practices evolve.

Cincinnati Music Hall

Cincinnati Music Hall opened in the nineteenth century near Washington Park as a venue for concerts, exhibitions and civic gatherings. High Victorian Gothic facades, a large auditorium, rehearsal areas and renovated public spaces support the orchestra, opera, ballet and other events; access is tied mainly to performances or tours, while the building's earlier uses include complex social history.

Findlay Market

Findlay Market has operated in Cincinnati's Over-the-Rhine neighborhood since the nineteenth century. A historic market house and surrounding sheds contain produce, meat, baked goods, prepared food and small businesses, with outdoor vendors added on selected days. The market remains commercial and community-focused, while tenant mix, hours and crowd levels change.

Duke Energy Convention Center

Downtown Cincinnati's convention center spans two city blocks and dates to a 1968 original hall later expanded and renamed. Exhibit floors and meeting rooms handle trade shows and banquets steps from Fountain Square. Skywalk links help attendees move between hotels and the center without crossing winter streets.

Cathedral Basilica of the Assumption

The Cathedral Basilica of the Assumption stands in Covington, Kentucky, across the river from Cincinnati. Built in a Gothic Revival style, it contains stained glass, carved stone, mosaics, sculpture, and a facade modeled partly on French medieval churches. The unfinished tower plan and continuing Catholic worship are part of its story.

Krohn Conservatory

Krohn Conservatory fills a 1930s glasshouse in Eden Park with tropical rooms, desert plants, and seasonal butterfly shows. Palm Court and orchid displays stay green through Cincinnati winters. City gardeners rotate holiday and spring exhibits that draw local families as much as out-of-town walkers.

Eden Park

Eden Park covers about 186 acres in Cincinnati's Mount Adams area above the Ohio River. Overlooks, Twin Lakes, and tree-lined drives share the ridge with Krohn Conservatory and other civic buildings. Lawns and paths make the park a large upland green space for the city.

Ault Park

Ault Park is a 224-acre Cincinnati Parks signature greenspace on the city's east side, spanning the western face of Cincinnati's Mt. Lookout ridge. Visitors use the Ault Park pavilion, formal gardens, a four-mile perimeter trail, and observation decks with views of the Little Miami River valley. The park hosts an annual summer concert series, an orchard programme maintained by the Friends of Ault Park, and the Cincinnati Observatory (adjacent, separately ticketed) for public astronomy nights.
