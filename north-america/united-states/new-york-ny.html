New York, NY

New York City grew from the Dutch settlement of New Amsterdam at the Hudson estuary into a major port after British takeover in 1664 and rapid expansion in the 19th century through immigration, finance, and industry. Manhattan's grid, bridges, parks, tenements, and skyscrapers record waves of arrival from Europe, the Americas, and Asia, along with labor movements and cultural institutions. The 20th century cemented its role as a media and financial capital.

Statue of Liberty

The Statue of Liberty stands on Liberty Island as a copper monument presented by France and dedicated in 1886. Frédéric Auguste Bartholdi designed the figure, while Gustave Eiffel's structural system supports the skin above a pedestal containing museum spaces.

Central Park

Frederick Law Olmsted and Calvert Vaux designed Central Park, opened in stages from 1858 across 843 acres of meadows, lakes, and carriage paths cut into Manhattan's expanding grid. Paths and lawns remain the primary public walking surfaces. The nineteenth-century plan still structures how the park meets surrounding avenues.
