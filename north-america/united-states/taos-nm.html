Taos, NM

Taos Pueblo has been continuously inhabited for centuries and remains a living Tiwa community beside the later Spanish colonial plaza town. Artists colonies from the early 20th century marketed adobe light and mountain views. The Taos Revolt of 1847 marked conflict under U.S. takeover. Pueblo multi-storey houses, ranchos churches, and plaza galleries show overlapping Indigenous, Hispanic, and Anglo layers in northern New Mexico.

Taos Pueblo

Taos Pueblo is an Indigenous community and ceremonial landscape continuously inhabited for centuries at the base of the Sangre de Cristo Mountains. Multi-story adobe buildings, plazas, homes and sacred spaces remain part of a living sovereign settlement. Tribal rules, ceremonies, photography restrictions, closures and resident privacy guide all tourism activity.

Red Willow Park

Red Willow Park provides open green space in Taos with paths and lawns for casual walking and neighborhood recreation. Shade and grass support everyday outings near local streets. The park functions as community open space rather than a fenced athletic complex alone.

Taos Art Museum at Fechin House

Russian artist Nicolai Fechin's 1933 adobe residence now houses the Taos Art Museum, showing works associated with the Taos Society of Artists, including landscapes and portraits from the colony's history. Galleries open on posted hours. Carved wood details from Fechin's era remain in the house.

San Francisco de Asís Catholic Mission Church

Light and shadow on the sculptural adobe masses of San Francisco de Asis Catholic Mission Church drew painters and photographers including Georgia O'Keeffe and Ansel Adams. Exterior and main interior spaces can be viewed outside service times when open. The twin towers and buttressed walls define the Ranchos de Taos skyline.

Taos Plaza

Taos Plaza is a public square and community focal point known for its craft shops, cafes, and laid-back vibe. It holds historical significance as the earliest residents of the Taos-Tiwa tribe have inhabited the valley for over a millennium. The Spanish settlers also left their mark in the area, particularly in the village of Ranchos de Taos with its San Francisco de Asis Church.

Downtown Taos Historic District

The Downtown Taos Historic District include the central plaza and surrounding streets of Taos, New Mexico. The area is characterized by its well-preserved, traditional Pueblo Revival adobe architecture. It is the cultural and commercial the town, featuring numerous art galleries, boutique shops, historic inns, and museums.

08 AdmissionFree

Kit Carson House & Museum

The Kit Carson Home and Museum is a historic adobe house located near the Taos Plaza. Built in 1825, it was the residence of the famous American frontiersman, trapper, and scout, Kit Carson, and his family. The museum preserves the home's 19th-century appearance and exhibits artifacts related to Carson's complex life and the history of the American West.

Fred Baca Park's green space in Taos supports morning walks with bird and butterfly watching, plus a shaded play area and walking loop for families. Paths and lawns cover the modest acreage. Clean restrooms and play equipment keep the park in regular local use.

Millicent Rogers Museum

Millicent Rogers Museum in Taos holds a major collection of Native American and Hispanic art from the Southwest across permanent galleries open on posted hours. Jewelry, textiles, pottery, and related works form core strengths of the holdings gathered from Rogers' own collecting. Rooms guide visitors through regional craft and fine-art traditions.
