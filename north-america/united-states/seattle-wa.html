Seattle, WA

Seattle rose as a Puget Sound timber and shipping entrepôt after the Great Northern Railway reached Elliott Bay in 1893, then hosted the 1962 World's Fair that left the Space Needle. Pioneer Square brick, Pike Place Market stalls, and Boeing-era suburbs record how Pacific Northwest resource extraction, Japanese immigration, and tech industry expansion reshaped a rain-soaked harbour city.

Space Needle

Built for Seattle's 1962 international exposition, the Space Needle rises above Seattle Center with a narrow tower, observation levels and a saucer-shaped crown. Later renovation added floor-to-ceiling glass and a rotating glass floor to the viewing experience. The structure remains a symbol of the fair's futuristic design language and of Seattle's mid-twentieth-century transformation.

Gas Works Park

At the north end of Lake Union, Gas Works Park reuses about 20 acres of a former gasification plant closed for pollution, cleaned, and reopened with picnic lawns and city views. Preserved industrial structures remain on the grass. Paths climb the hill for skyline overlooks.

Fremont Troll

The Fremont Troll crouches beneath the Aurora Bridge as a large concrete and mixed-media sculpture created in 1990 by a team of local artists. One hand grips a real automobile, while the rough surface and low bridge setting turn unused infrastructure into public art. Climbing, graffiti, traffic, gatherings, and maintenance affect the work, and the surrounding roadway requires attention.

The Gum Wall

Layers of used chewing gum cover sections of Post Alley near Pike Place Market, forming the informal attraction known as the Gum Wall. The accumulation began around a theater entrance and has been periodically removed for cleaning before returning. It is neither commissioned art nor protected heritage, and sanitation, crowding, business access, and maintenance remain practical concerns in the narrow alley.
