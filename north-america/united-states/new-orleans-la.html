New Orleans, LA

New Orleans was founded by the French in 1718 on a Mississippi River crescent above delta swamps, then shaped by Spanish rule, Haitian immigration, and American purchase in 1803. French Quarter balconies, St. Louis Cathedral, and Garden District mansions record how Caribbean trade, Creole culture, and port commerce built a jazz and festival city on filled alluvial land.

Jackson Square

Laid out in 1721, Jackson Square is where the Louisiana Purchase was finalized and where St. Louis Cathedral, the Cabildo, and the Presbytere face the plaza. Artists, musicians, and fortune tellers line the fence and sidewalks with original work. The open square still anchors the French Quarter's civic front toward the river.

St. Louis Cathedral

St. Louis Cathedral is a grand church dating back to the 1700s, featuring stained glass and a painted ceiling. In the mid-1800s, Baroness Micaela Almonester de Pontalba oversaw the rebuilding of the cathedral and initiated the construction of Pontalba Buildings in an effort to counteract development across Canal Street. The cathedral's white facade and sleek steeples dominate the square, flanked by historic structures like Cabildo and Presbytere.

Marie Laveau's House Of Voodoo

On Bourbon Street in the French Quarter, Marie Laveau's House of Voodoo occupies a former home linked to Marie Laveau, New Orleans' known Voodoo Queen, combining museum displays with a shop. The business opened in 1988 and draws both locals and travellers. Galleries and retail share the same compact Quarter address.

Napoleon House

Napoleon House occupies an eighteenth-century French Quarter building as a long-running Creole restaurant and bar. The courtyard and dim rooms serve classics such as muffulettas in an unhurried setting. Local lore about a possible refuge for Napoleon still colors the house's name and decor.

Steamboat NATCHEZ - Official Site

Steamboat Natchez has operated sightseeing and music cruises on the Mississippi from New Orleans, using a sternwheel vessel modeled on earlier riverboats. Routes, narration, meals, and jazz programs vary by departure, and substitute vessels may operate during maintenance.

House of Blues New Orleans

House of Blues New Orleans pairs Southern plates with a music hall in the French Quarter. Standing-room crowds face a stage laid over Mississippi mud, a detail the venue emphasizes. Rock and blues bookings fill most nights beside the restaurant side.

Bourbon St

Bourbon Street cuts through the French Quarter with bars, music clubs, and balconies that open over the pavement. Writers including Truman Capote and Tennessee Williams spent time along these blocks. Daylight reveals Creole townhouses; nights shift toward louder club traffic.

Musical Legends Park

Musical Legends Park occupies a small courtyard on Bourbon Street with statues honoring New Orleans performers and a stage used for live music. Seating, food service and commercial activity make it closer to an entertainment venue than a conventional public park. Performers, operating hours and access change, while the sculptures provide a compact introduction.

Saenger Theatre

Saenger Theatre is a historic landmark known for hosting live musical acts and touring theatrical productions. The theater, which opened in 1927, has a grand Italian courtyard-inspired interior with decorative moldings and an overhead lighting design resembling a starry constellation. Despite its capacity being reduced from 4,000 to 2,600 after restoration following Hurricane Katrina in 2005, it remains theaters in town.

Congo Square

Congo Square in present-day Louis Armstrong Park preserves the memory of a gathering ground where enslaved and free people of African descent traded, socialized, danced, and made music, especially on Sundays. These practices helped sustain cultural traditions that influenced New Orleans music and community life.

Central Grocery and Deli

Italian specialty foods and the muffuletta sandwich define Central Grocery and Deli in the French Quarter, where founder Lupo Salvatore created the sandwich in 1906. After Hurricane Ida in 2021, muffulettas associated with the market continued at nearby locations while the legacy name persisted. The deli remains tied to that Sicilian sandwich origin story.

The Shops of the Colonnade form part of the French Market complex near the Mississippi River, placing small retailers and food businesses beneath an arcaded structure. The wider market evolved through Indigenous trade, colonial commerce, municipal rebuilding, and modern tourism.

New Orleans Jazz Museum

Housed in the former U.S. Mint, the New Orleans Jazz Museum combines music history with a building once used for federal coin production. Instruments, recordings, photographs, sheet music, and changing exhibitions examine jazz's development and the people who shaped it.

Audubon Aquarium

Audubon Aquarium occupies the Mississippi riverfront near Canal Street and displays marine and freshwater habitats connected with Louisiana, the Gulf of Mexico, the Caribbean and other regions. Large tanks, a walk-through passage, penguins, fish and interactive areas form part of a redeveloped complex with the insectarium. Animal care, renovations and changing programs affect which exhibits are available.

Lafayette Square

Downtown New Orleans' Lafayette Square hosts concert series, inaugurations, weddings, and Mardi Gras parade watching on its open green. The Crescent City Blues and BBQ Festival, organized by the New Orleans Jazz and Heritage Festival Foundation, returns annually to the square. Surrounding office blocks frame the lawn for civic gatherings.

New Orleans City Park

New Orleans City Park offers swan-boat rentals on Big Lake, Storyland for children, and Carousel Gardens Amusement Park among its programmed areas. Paths, lawns, and shade fill the broader parkland between those attractions. Families often combine lake, playground, and carousel stops in one visit.

Chalmette Battlefield

Chalmette Battlefield preserves the ground where American forces defeated a British army in the Battle of New Orleans in January 1815, and heat, storms, ceremonies and restoration affect access, while interpretation also addresses plantation history and enslaved labor. Earthworks, interpretive signs, the Chalmette Monument, visitor facilities and the adjoining national cemetery place the battle within a longer riverfront landscape.

Audubon Zoo

Audubon Zoo occupies landscaped grounds in New Orleans's Uptown area, with animal habitats, gardens, education spaces and a Louisiana-themed wetland exhibit. The institution developed from earlier park displays into a modern zoo with conservation and breeding programs. Heat, storms, construction, animal welfare and daily behavior change visibility.

Tree of Life

A massive live oak known as the Tree of Life spreads across part of Audubon Park near the Mississippi River. Broad limbs extend close to the ground, creating a shaded gathering and photography spot within the park's open landscape. Its precise age is uncertain, but the tree's scale and long association with the site make it a recognizable example of the old live oaks that shape New Orleans's public spaces.
