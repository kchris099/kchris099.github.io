Philadelphia, PA

Philadelphia was planned by William Penn in 1682 on the Delaware River and grew into the largest city in British North America before serving as a revolutionary capital where the Declaration of Independence and Constitution were debated. Independence Hall, broad streets, banks, and 19th-century institutions record colonial trade, Quaker governance, and industrial expansion. Immigration and railroads fed manufacturing districts beyond the historic core.

Independence Hall

Independence Hall is a Georgian statehouse where the Second Continental Congress adopted the Declaration of Independence in 1776 and the Constitutional Convention met in 1787. Guided tours cover the Assembly Room and adjacent chambers in Independence National Historical Park.

The Liberty Bell hung in the Pennsylvania State House and later became a symbol associated with American independence. It is displayed in a dedicated center on Independence Mall with exhibits on its casting, cracking, and public use in the 19th century.

Second Bank of the United States Portrait Gallery

The Second Bank of the United States occupies a Greek Revival building completed in the 1820s near Independence Hall. After its controversial financial role ended, the structure became a federal portrait gallery displaying paintings of political, military and civic figures; the collection provides a visual record of early national leadership, while security, conservation and park staffing affect interior hours.

Philadelphia City Hall

Philadelphia City Hall reached completion in 1901 after decades of French Second Empire construction. William Penn's statue crowns the tower above the intersection of Broad and Market. Municipal offices still fill the building at the center of William Penn's original grid.

Love Park, officially John F. Kennedy Plaza, sits beside Philadelphia City Hall with Robert Indiana's LOVE sculpture in place since the 1970s. The Wanamaker Building

Built as a department store, Philadelphia's Wanamaker Building remains known for its vast Grand Court, bronze eagle and monumental pipe organ. The early twentieth-century interior expressed the scale and ceremony of urban retail at its height. Although the building now contains offices and changing commercial uses, the court and organ continue to connect it with the history of John Wanamaker's flagship store.

Carpenters' Hall was built in the 1770s for the Carpenters' Company of Philadelphia and hosted an early Continental Congress in 1774.
