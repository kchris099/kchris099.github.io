Yosemite, CA

Yosemite Valley was home to the Ahwahneechee before 19th-century displacement and the arrival of tourists drawn to granite cliffs and waterfalls. Federal protection began with the 1864 Yosemite Grant and national park status in 1890, shaped by figures such as John Muir. Roads, tunnels, and lodges opened the high country. Half Dome, El Capitan, and sequoia groves still organise travel through this Sierra Nevada park.

Yosemite National Park

Yosemite National Park protects glaciated valleys, granite cliffs, waterfalls, giant sequoia groves, high-country meadows, rivers and extensive wilderness in California's Sierra Nevada. Roads and developed areas occupy only a small part of the park. Snow, wildfire, floods, rockfall and seasonal closures can sharply change access across different elevations, while heavy traffic concentrates around a few famous valleys and viewpoints.

Olmsted Point

Olmsted Point lies along Yosemite's Tioga Road on a granite slope overlooking Tenaya Canyon, Clouds Rest and the northern side of Half Dome. Glacial polish, scattered boulders and sparse trees reveal the geology of the high-elevation Sierra Nevada. Short paths extend across exposed rock, where the open terrain gives a clear view of landforms shaped by ancient ice.

Glacier Point

Glacier Point stands high above Yosemite Valley with views toward Half Dome, Yosemite Falls, Vernal and Nevada falls and the Sierra crest in clear conditions. A seasonal road reaches the developed viewpoint, while trails connect it with the valley and high country. Snow, wildfire smoke, road construction, crowds, lightning and steep unprotected edges influence access and visibility.

04 Admission$20

Washburn Point

Washburn Point provides a roadside view shortly before Glacier Point, looking toward Half Dome, the Merced River canyon and the upper falls of the Mist Trail corridor. Its angle differs from the broader valley view farther ahead, making waterfalls more prominent in suitable seasons. Road closure, snow, smoke, crowds and limited parking affect the short stop.

Yosemite Valley Welcome Center

It replaces or reorganizes earlier visitor services as circulation in the valley changes. The Yosemite Valley Welcome Center is the primary orientation and information hub for visitors arriving in the park's main valley. It provides full maps, trail conditions, ranger advice, and educational exhibits detailing the geology, history, and wildlife of Yosemite.

Lower Yosemite Fall Vista Point

Lower Yosemite Fall Vista Point stands along the accessible loop near the base of the waterfall's final drop. Granite walls, forest, boulders, spray and seasonal water volume shape the view, which can be powerful in spring and nearly dry later in the year. Ice, wet pavement, crowds, rockfall zones and trail closures affect access beyond the brief paved approach.

Lower Yosemite Falls Trailhead

The Lower Yosemite Falls Trailhead marks the beginning of a short, paved, and very accessible loop trail that leads directly to the base of the lower falls. The easy walk winds through a pleasant pine forest and offers numerous vantage points of both the upper and lower sections of the massive waterfall.

Yosemite Falls Trailhead

The Yosemite Falls Trail begins near Camp 4 and climbs steeply from the valley floor toward Columbia Rock and the top of the falls. Long switchbacks, exposed slopes, forest and granite viewpoints produce substantial elevation gain with limited water. Heat, ice, snow, rockfall, lightning and seasonal waterfall flow make the route demanding, while the trailhead itself is a starting point.

09 Admission$20

Four Mile Trailhead

The Four Mile Trailhead, located near the base of Sentinel Rock, begins a strenuous, continuous ascent from the Yosemite Valley floor up to Glacier Point. The trail offers dramatic, constantly changing views of Yosemite Falls, El Capitan, and Half Dome as it climbs over 3,200 feet in elevation through dense forest and exposed granite switchbacks.

Yosemite Falls

Yosemite Falls descends from the north rim of Yosemite Valley in three main sections: the upper fall, middle cascades and lower fall, and snowmelt drives the strongest flow in spring, while drought or late summer can reduce the waterfall greatly. Viewpoints range from accessible valley paths to strenuous high trails, with rockfall, spray, ice and seasonal closures affecting nearby areas.
