Explore Yosemite, CA

A Brief History

Protected by Abraham Lincoln in 1864, the dramatic granite valleys of Yosemite sparked the very genesis of the global national park idea. Its monumental wilderness became the crucial battleground for early environmentalists, fundamentally shaping modern American conservation policy. This continuous historical development established a lasting framework that strongly informs the area's modern cultural evolution.

Yosemite National Park

Yosemite National Park sits within the Yosemite National Park area of Yosemite, CA, documented in local heritage registers for its civic or cultural role. Visitors approach from the main access road and combine the visit with neighbouring heritage buildings and public spaces in the district.

$20

Olmsted Point

Olmsted Point is a prominent, rocky overlook located along the Tioga Road in Yosemite National Park. It offers a unique, reverse perspective of Half Dome, viewing its sheer northern face, as well as expansive views of Tenaya Canyon and Clouds Rest. The landscape is characterized by massive, glacier-polished granite expanses scattered with erratic boulders.

$20

Glacier Point

Glacier Point is an iconic, high-elevation viewpoint in Yosemite National Park, situated 3,200 feet directly above Yosemite Valley. It provides expansive, panoramic vistas of the valley floor, Half Dome, Yosemite Falls, and the High Sierra. Accessible by road in the summer and cross-country skis in the winter, it is one of the park's most dramatic destinations.

$20

Washburn Point

Washburn Point is a scenic overlook located just south of Glacier Point in Yosemite National Park. It offers a dramatic, slightly different angle on the park's major landmarks, providing particularly excellent views of the distinct profile of Half Dome and the profile of both Vernal and Nevada Falls cascading down the Merced River canyon.

$20

Yosemite Valley Welcome Center

The Yosemite Valley Welcome Center is the primary orientation and information hub for visitors arriving in the park's main valley. It provides comprehensive maps, trail conditions, ranger advice, and educational exhibits detailing the geology, history, and wildlife of Yosemite. It serves as the starting point for exploring the valley's numerous iconic landmarks.

$20

Lower Yosemite Fall Vista Point

The Lower Yosemite Fall Vista Point offers a dramatic, easily accessible view of the final 320-foot drop of North America's tallest waterfall. Located near the base of the falls, the viewpoint allows visitors to feel the powerful mist and hear the thunderous roar of the water, particularly during the peak spring runoff.

$20

Lower Yosemite Falls Trailhead

The Lower Yosemite Falls Trailhead marks the beginning of a short, paved, and very accessible loop trail that leads directly to the base of the lower falls. The easy walk winds through a pleasant pine forest and offers numerous vantage points of both the upper and lower sections of the massive waterfall.

$20

Yosemite Falls Trailhead

The Yosemite Falls Trailhead, located near Camp 4, is the starting point for one of the park's oldest and most strenuous historic hikes. The steep, switchbacking trail climbs relentlessly to the top of the 2,425-foot waterfall. It offers hikers dramatic, close-up views of the upper falls and expansive panoramas of Yosemite Valley from the summit.

$20

Four Mile Trailhead

The Four Mile Trailhead, located near the base of Sentinel Rock, begins a strenuous, continuous ascent from the Yosemite Valley floor up to Glacier Point. The trail offers dramatic, constantly changing views of Yosemite Falls, El Capitan, and Half Dome as it climbs over 3,200 feet in elevation through dense forest and exposed granite switchbacks.

$20

Yosemite Falls

Yosemite Falls is the highest waterfall in North America, dropping a total of 2,425 feet from the valley rim to the floor. It consists of three distinct sections: Upper Yosemite Fall, the middle cascades, and Lower Yosemite Fall. The falls are a major, dramatic landmark, flowing most powerfully in the spring and often drying up completely by late summer.
