Albuquerque, NM

Albuquerque developed around a Spanish villa founded in 1706 along the Rio Grande near Pueblo trading routes. Old Town adobe around the San Felipe de Neri church, Route 66 motels, and Sandia Peak tramways document how Mexican land grants, Atchison Topeka and Santa Fe Railway promotion, and federal research laboratories shaped New Mexico's largest city.

Petroglyph National Monument

Petroglyph National Monument protects volcanic escarpments west of Albuquerque containing thousands of images pecked into dark basalt by Indigenous peoples and later settlers. Several trail areas reveal different concentrations of carvings across exposed desert terrain. Meanings cannot be reduced to decoration, while heat, limited shade, loose rock, and respect for sacred cultural material shape the visit.

ABQ BioPark Zoo

Part of Albuquerque's BioPark with aquarium, botanic garden, and Tingley Beach, the zoo sits along the Rio Grande near Old Town. Animal exhibits and outdoor activity areas share the riverside campus. Paths and shade organize family movement between habitats.

03 AdmissionFree

Old Town Plaza

Old Town Plaza marks the Spanish founding of Albuquerque by Governor Francisco Cuervo y Valdés in 1706. Adobe church and shop fronts face a central square that once sat on El Camino Real de Tierra Adentro between Mexico City and New Mexico's early capitals. Paved walks and surrounding porches still frame the plaza.

04 AdmissionFree

San Felipe de Neri Catholic Church

San Felipe de Neri Church has served Albuquerque's Old Town since the Spanish colonial period, though the present adobe building reflects eighteenth-century reconstruction and later alterations. Twin towers, a simple nave, religious art, a courtyard, and surrounding parish buildings support active Catholic worship. The church anchors the plaza while remaining a living congregation.

ABQ BioPark Aquarium

ABQ BioPark Aquarium shows marine and freshwater ecosystems in an indoor facility beside the Rio Grande Botanic Garden. Sharks, rays, reef fish, river species and changing exhibits connect New Mexico visitors with distant waters; the aquarium forms one part of the larger municipal BioPark, while species holdings, welfare standards and timed programs change.

Isleta Amphitheater

Summer concert bookings fill Isleta Amphitheater's roughly 15,000 seats on Albuquerque's south side. Food and drink vendors operate on show nights across the outdoor bowl. Arrival follows published event gates rather than a daily museum-style schedule.

Albuquerque New Mexico Temple

The Albuquerque New Mexico Temple belongs to The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints and stands on landscaped grounds in the city's northeast. Its exterior uses light stone, a single spire and formal gardens, while interior ceremonies are reserved for eligible church members. Public appreciation normally centers on the architecture and grounds outside periods of special access.

Expo New Mexico

Expo New Mexico contains the New Mexico State Fairgrounds in Albuquerque, with exhibition halls, arenas, racetrack facilities, agricultural spaces and event venues. Activity changes sharply with the state fair, concerts, markets, racing and private bookings. Access, parking and security depend on the scheduled event across this large multi-use complex.

Popejoy Hall

Popejoy Hall is a performing-arts auditorium on the University of New Mexico campus in Albuquerque. Its large stage and seating host touring Broadway productions, concerts, dance, speakers and community events. Smaller university venues operate elsewhere on campus. The hall's role centers on scheduled live performance and regional arts programming.
