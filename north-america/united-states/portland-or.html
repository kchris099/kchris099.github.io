Portland, OR

Portland was platted in 1845 where the Willamette River meets Oregon Trail routes, then grew as a timber-export and shipbuilding centre. Pioneer Courthouse Square, Powell's bookstore blocks, and Columbia River Gorge access document how Pacific Northwest settlement, Progressive-era planning, and postwar suburban expansion produced a riverfront city known for parks and streetcar suburbs.

Lan Su Chinese Garden

Built with Portland's sister city Suzhou, Lan Su Chinese Garden recreates the courtyards and ornate pavilions of a wealthy sixteenth-century Chinese household in a compact downtown block. Covered walkways and planted courts organize the circuit. Paths stay inside the walled garden rather than a large open park.

The Grotto

The Grotto occupies a wooded Catholic sanctuary in northeast Portland, centered on a rock cave and shrine dedicated to Mary. Gardens, chapels, paths, religious art and an upper-level area create spaces for prayer and reflection above the city; it is an active ministry.

Built in 1893, Providence Park has hosted the Portland Timbers, Thorns, and Vikings plus cricket, ski jumping, and a 1957 Elvis concert across its long calendar. Timbers match days draw loud supporter sections around the pitch. The historic bowl still frames soccer under modern stands.

Washington Park gathers the Oregon Zoo, Hoyt Arboretum, World Forestry Center, and Children's Museum on forested west-hills ground adjoining Portland's larger Forest Park belt. Paths and lawns link those attractions. Day visitors often combine zoo and arboretum stops on one outing.

International Rose Test Garden

The International Rose Test Garden, located in Washington Park between the historic Portland neighborhoods of Arlington Heights and Goose Hollow, is a city-maintained garden that was established in 1917. It has over 10,000 rose plants displayed in careful manicured gardens. The garden offers views of Downtown Portland and Mt. Hood and is a popular destination for both locals and tourists.

Oregon Zoo

The Oregon Zoo, located in the lush Washington Park, spans 64 acres and is home to nearly 3,000 animals from 90 different species, including 19 endangered ones. The zoo's exhibits are divided into five themed areas: the Great Northwest, Pacific Shores, Discovery Zone, Africa, and Elephant Lands. Visitors can observe a variety of wildlife such as elephants, polar bears, river otters, penguins and lions.

Pioneer Courthouse Square

Pioneer Courthouse Square fills a downtown Portland block with brick terraces, steps, public art, transit stops and event infrastructure. Opened in the 1980s after a civic campaign, it replaced a parking structure and became a gathering place for markets, performances, protests and seasonal installations. Programming changes constantly, while the square's heavy transit use and private events can alter circulation.

Fort Vancouver National Historic Site

Fort Vancouver National Historic Site preserves parts of a nineteenth-century Hudson's Bay Company trading headquarters, later U.S. Army post, airfield and surrounding cultural landscape on the Columbia River. Reconstructed stockade buildings, archaeology, historic homes and exhibits interpret trade, colonial expansion, Indigenous displacement, military history and aviation. Individual areas use different hours and entrances.

Angela Danadjieva designed Ira Keller Fountain Park so more than 13,000 gallons of water move through cascades each minute across a one-acre downtown block of pools, fountains, and small falls. Children play in the water on warm days. Paths and seating rim the concrete channels.

Witch's Castle

The structure called Witch's Castle is a moss-covered stone ruin beside Balch Creek in Portland's Forest Park. Built in the 1930s as a public restroom, it later fell into disuse and acquired a nickname shaped by local folklore. Forest trails reach the site over muddy and uneven ground, while vandalism, erosion, weather and stream conditions influence the appearance of the remaining walls.

Hollywood Theatre

A Prohibition-era movie house restored for modern use, Portland's Hollywood Theatre mixes new and classic films with specialty nights such as Kung Fu Theater, B-Movie Bingo, Mondo Trasho, and 35mm or 70mm screenings. Ticketed shows fill the auditorium. The neighborhood marquee still marks the entrance on Sandy Boulevard.

Tilikum Crossing Bridge

Tilikum Crossing carries light rail, streetcars, buses, bicycles and pedestrians across the Willamette River without ordinary private automobile traffic. Opened in 2015, its cable-stayed form links Portland's South Waterfront with the central eastside. Wind, rain, transit operations, events and construction affect the crossing, while river and skyline views unfold from a functional transportation bridge.
