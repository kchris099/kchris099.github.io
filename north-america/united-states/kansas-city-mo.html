Kansas City, MO

The Hannibal Bridge of 1869 locked Kansas City into rail and stockyard primacy at the Missouri bend. Jazz around 18th and Vine, the Liberty Memorial, and Country Club Plaza later layered music, war memory, and planned shopping onto that livestock-and-freight base. Negro Leagues Baseball Museum anchors the same 18th and Vine cultural corridor. American Jazz Museum shares that Vine Street block with the baseball museum.

Kauffman Stadium

Opened in 1973 and nicknamed The K, Kauffman Stadium hosts Kansas City Royals baseball with sightlines toward the city's fountain district and open Midwest sky. Concessions include Boulevard Brewing Company beer, Belfonte's ice cream, and AZ Canteen from chef Andrew Zimmern. Game days fill the stands and concourses with hometown crowds for regular-season and special-event baseball.

Loose Park

Jacob L. Loose Park covers about 75 acres of urban green space in Kansas City, with lawns, tree cover, and walking paths spread across the grounds. Within Swope Park, the Kansas City Zoo & Aquarium shows animals from several regions alongside a newer marine complex. Paths connect large outdoor habitats, indoor exhibits, conservation interpretation and family facilities across a hilly site. The Midland Theatre

Built around 1927 and seating about 3,200, the Midland Theatre (also billed as the Arvest Bank Theatre at the Midland) anchors downtown Kansas City as a historic concert house. Ticketed performances fill the auditorium for music and touring shows; daytime visits may open selected public areas when schedules allow. The 1920s shell still frames the lobby and stage for modern bookings.

Kansas City's National WWI Museum and Memorial combines the Liberty Memorial tower with galleries devoted to World War I and its global consequences. A glass bridge over symbolic poppies leads into collections of weapons, uniforms, documents and vehicles. Personal objects and propaganda connect combat with civilian life, while outdoor memorial areas extend interpretation beyond the museum galleries.

Historic Truman Courthouse

Harry S. Truman worked as a Jackson County judge inside the courthouse on Independence Square before entering national politics. The building's present Colonial Revival appearance reflects a 1930s reconstruction, while restored offices and exhibits interpret local government and Truman's career. Some county functions have continued in the structure, making it both a civic landmark and a historic interior.

Clinton's Soda Fountain

Clinton's Soda Fountain occupies the Independence Square building where a young Harry Truman briefly worked at an earlier drugstore. A marble counter, period-style fittings, ice cream, drinks and memorabilia evoke a neighborhood soda shop, although the present business opened much later. Its value comes from the documented local association and commercial revival of the space.

Memorial Hall

Memorial Hall in Kansas City, Kansas, was built as a civic auditorium and memorial to local military service after World War I. Its arena-like interior has hosted ceremonies, sports, concerts and community meetings. Commemorative elements remain integrated with a flexible event building, linking public entertainment with the city's remembrance of residents who served.
