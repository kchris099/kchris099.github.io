Sedona, AZ

Sedona sat as a small ranching and orchard community among red-rock canyons until mid-20th-century Western films and later New Age tourism expanded lodging and galleries. Indigenous peoples including the Yavapai and Apache have deeper ties to the Verde Valley. Oak Creek and surrounding national forest land limit sprawl. Chapel architecture, trailheads, and vortex lore now organise a desert town built on scenery and seasonal visitors.

Chapel of the Holy Cross

Located atop the red rock buttes of Sedona, the Chapel of the Holy Cross is a modern notable building that dates back to 1956. Perched about 250 feet high on a twin pinnacled spur, this chapel offers panoramic views of the surrounding landscape. While it is an important site for the Catholic Church Diocese, it warmly welcomes visitors daily (except on major holidays) to explore its unique design and artwork.

02 AdmissionFree

Sedona Airport Scenic Lookout

Sedona Airport Scenic Lookout sits beside the road near the top of Airport Mesa, providing a broad view across red-rock formations, neighborhoods and forested uplands. The paved overlook differs from the longer mesa trails and nearby vortex claims promoted by tourism businesses. Parking limits, heat, sunset crowds, smoke, clouds and roadside traffic affect the brief stop.

03 AdmissionFree

Airport Mesa

Airport Mesa rises above central Sedona with trails and overlooks across red sandstone formations and the Verde Valley, and paths cross exposed rock and connect several viewpoints near the airport, while spiritual vortex interpretations come from modern tourism. Heat, lightning, loose surfaces, limited parking, and heavy sunset use require preparation beyond the short distance from town.

04 AdmissionFree

Amitabha Stupa and Peace Park

A 36-foot Tibetan Buddhist stupa hung with prayer flags anchors Amitabha Stupa and Peace Park in Sedona, where pilgrims leave offerings at the base. Tara Stupa and a mahogany Shakyamuni Buddha statue share the grounds open from dawn to dusk. Paths and desert plantings fill the quiet park around the shrines.

34.8755° N / 111.8084° W

05 AdmissionFree

Soldier Pass Trailhead

Soldier Pass Trailhead provides access to a popular Sedona route leading toward red-rock walls, sinkholes and seasonal pools. Parking and entry systems have changed to control crowding, with shuttle or permit arrangements sometimes replacing direct vehicle access; heat, flash flooding, route confusion, fragile soils and limited water affect the hike, while nearby caves require careful stewardship and may face restrictions.

06 AdmissionFree

Sedona.org Vacation Rentals

Sedona.org Vacation Rentals is a commercial lodging service with homes dispersed across the Sedona area. Property inventory, management, amenities, prices and availability change as rentals enter or leave the service. Each accommodation has its own neighborhood setting and access rules, so the name refers to a booking portfolio. The properties occupy separate neighborhoods and share no common visitor setting.

07 AdmissionFree

Red Rock Ranger District Visitor Center

The Red Rock Ranger District Visitor Center orients travelers to Coconino National Forest lands around Sedona. Staff provide trail maps, condition updates, and geology exhibits. Outdoor displays introduce local plants and rock layers before people drive to trailheads.

08 AdmissionFree

Boynton Canyon Trail

Boynton Canyon Trail follows a broad red-rock canyon northwest of Sedona through open desert, woodland and increasingly enclosed cliffs. Side routes and spiritual-tourism claims can distract from the main path, while nearby resort land and wilderness boundaries complicate access; heat, snow, lightning, crowds, limited parking and fragile cryptobiotic soil require care along the long out-and-back route.

34.9074° N / 111.8485° W

09 AdmissionFree

Devil's Bridge Trailhead

Devil's Bridge Trailhead refers to access points for the route leading to Sedona's large natural sandstone arch. Vehicle restrictions, rough roads, shuttle systems and alternate parking areas have changed how hikers reach the trail, so the named trailhead may not be available to ordinary cars.

34.9030° N / 111.8141° W

10 AdmissionFree

Devil's Bridge

Devil's Bridge spans a sandstone gap in the red-rock country north of Sedona, reached by a heavily used trail from several possible access points. The narrow upper surface and steep drop attract queues for photographs but have no protective railings. Heat, ice, lightning, crowd pressure, road restrictions, and fragile terrain make the hike more serious than its social-media image suggests.
