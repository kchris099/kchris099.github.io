Miami, FL

Miami was incorporated in 1896 after Henry Flagler's Florida East Coast Railway reached Biscayne Bay, opening a resort and citrus-shipping port. Art Deco Ocean Drive hotels, Little Havana calle storefronts, and cruise-ship terminals record how Caribbean migration, real-estate booms, and Latin American banking turned a mangrove shoreline into a bilingual Atlantic gateway.

Holocaust Memorial Miami Beach

The Holocaust Memorial on Miami Beach is a public sculpture garden centered on a bronze hand reaching skyward, surrounded by inscribed stone and reflecting water. Paths lead through the memorial plaza a short walk from the beachfront hotels. The site commemorates victims of the Holocaust rather than functioning as a bathing beach.

Española Way cuts a pedestrian lane through Miami Beach with Spanish Colonial and Mediterranean Revival fronts. Restaurants and cafes line the open street between Washington and Collins avenues. Lummus Park

Along Miami Beach, Lummus Park mixes sand access with outdoor gym gear, bike and skate paths, and family lawns. Swimming, cycling, roller skating, and open-air workouts share the same oceanfront strip. Paths and shaded patches fill gaps between the beach and Ocean Drive blocks.

Art Deco Welcome Center

Miami Beach's Art Deco Welcome Center provides orientation to the protected architectural district along Ocean Drive and nearby streets. Exhibits, maps, talks and guided walks explain Art Deco, Mediterranean Revival and Miami Modern buildings, as well as preservation campaigns that shaped the area. The center is an educational starting point within the district.

Ocean Drive fronts South Beach with more than 900 Art Deco and mid-century buildings, including the Colony Hotel. Hotels, restaurants, and nightlife pack the blocks facing Lummus Park and the Atlantic. The street remains the clearest outdoor museum of Miami Beach's 1930s hotel boom.

Gesù Church

Gesu Church traces its parish roots to Miami's nineteenth-century Catholic community, while the present downtown building opened in the 1920s. Stained glass, a decorated sanctuary, towers and a dense urban setting reflect its role as one of the city's longstanding religious institutions. The church remains active, so Masses, pastoral work, ceremonies and preservation needs govern public access.

Completed in 1987, the 47-story Miami Tower carries a tiered crown and programmable LED lighting visible across downtown along Biscayne Boulevard. Observation access stays limited, yet the illuminated facade appears in civic light displays and evening skyline views. The office shaft remains a recognizable downtown silhouette after dark.

Historic Virginia Key Beach Park

Historic Virginia Key Beach Park preserves one of Miami's early segregated public beaches, reopened in 2008 after community restoration efforts. The 82-acre Atlantic shoreline includes picnic pavilions, nature trails, and interpretive markers on the civil-rights history of beach access in Dade County. Swimming, kayaking, and cultural festivals are offered seasonally.

Curtiss Mansion preserves the 1920s Pueblo Revival-style home associated with aviation pioneer Glenn Curtiss in Miami Springs. Interior rooms and tropical grounds reflect his residential life as well as the planned development of the surrounding community, and restoration returned the building to public use for interpretation, tours and private events.

Venetian Pool

Coral Gables' Venetian Pool began as a 1923 quarry that supplied oolitic limestone for early local buildings, then Denman Fink and Phineas Paist reshaped the pit into a spring-fed swimming basin. The rock walls and water still show the quarry origin. Seasonal swimming use keeps the former mine pit in active civic recreation.
