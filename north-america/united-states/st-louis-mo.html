St. Louis, MO

St. Louis began as a French fur-trade village founded in 1764 near the Mississippi and Missouri confluence. Gateway Arch stainless curve, Soulard market brick, and Forest Park exposition grounds record how Lewis and Clark departures, steamboat-era commerce, and 1904 World's Fair planning made the Mississippi Valley's western gateway before railway rivals shifted trade east.

Saint Louis Zoo

Saint Louis Zoo occupies part of Forest Park with animal habitats, conservation programs, gardens, education facilities and historic structures spread across a large site. Its public collection has evolved from early exhibition practices toward modern welfare and breeding standards. Weather, construction, veterinary care and animal behavior affect visibility, while individual experiences and paid activities operate separately.

Forest Park

Forest Park covers a broad landscape west of central St. Louis, created for public recreation and expanded for the 1904 international exposition. Lakes, woods and lawns share the grounds with trails, monuments, sports areas and major cultural institutions. Museums, the zoo and public landscapes occupy distinct sections, making the park both an everyday green space and a record of large-scale civic planning.

03 AdmissionFree

The Cathedral Basilica of St. Louis

The Cathedral Basilica of Saint Louis was completed in the early twentieth century as the seat of the Roman Catholic archdiocese. A massive dome and Romanesque exterior enclose extensive mosaics, chapels, marble and a formal sanctuary assembled over many decades; daily worship, funerals, tours and preservation work overlap, so the building is an active church.

04 AdmissionFree

Tower Grove Park

Tower Grove Park was laid out in the nineteenth century by merchant and philanthropist Henry Shaw as a formal public landscape south of central St. Louis. Curving roads, pavilions, statues, lawns, trees, sports areas and gardens reflect Victorian park planning. Markets, festivals, ecological work, weather and private events change use, while its decorative structures are spread across a large area.

05 AdmissionFree (tram ride $15)

The Gateway Arch

The Gateway Arch rises 630 feet above the Mississippi riverfront as a stainless-steel monument completed in the 1960s, and Eero Saarinen's weighted catenary form commemorates westward expansion, a theme now interpreted alongside Indigenous displacement, slavery and federal policy. A tram reaches the enclosed top, while weather, security, maintenance and timed capacity affect access.

Gateway Arch National Park

Gateway Arch National Park combines the Arch, museum, landscaped riverfront and Old Courthouse within a compact historic area in downtown St. Louis. Exhibits address exploration, migration, federal power, slavery and western expansion, while the courthouse connects with the Dred Scott case.

Kiener Plaza Park

Kiener Plaza occupies a civic block between downtown St. Louis and the Gateway Arch grounds. Lawns, paths, a fountain, seating, playground equipment and event space form part of a redesigned urban corridor. Festivals, protests, sports celebrations, maintenance and private programming change the layout.

Grant's Farm

Grant's Farm developed from land associated with Ulysses S. Grant and later the Busch family into a commercial animal and heritage attraction. Historic buildings and landscaped grounds frame transport rides, livestock areas and managed wildlife displays. Seasonal programs and private events influence operations, while interpretation connects the property with both families who shaped its later history.

St. Louis Aquarium at Union Station

St. Louis Aquarium occupies part of the redeveloped Union Station complex. Tanks and themed galleries present river species, freshwater habitats and marine animals inside the former rail property. Feeding programs and special encounters add scheduled activities, while maintenance and animal care affect individual exhibits. Hotels and other attractions occupy separate sections of the larger development.

St. Louis Union Station

After long-distance service ended, the Romanesque headhouse and surrounding complex were adapted for a hotel, restaurants, events, and entertainment attractions. St. Louis Union Station opened in 1894 as a major passenger rail terminal beneath a vast train shed; retail and visitor uses change frequently, while restored public rooms preserve part of the site's original transportation function.

Westport Plaza

In Maryland Heights near St. Louis, Westport Plaza mixes shopping, dining, and entertainment with an outdoor amphitheater for live music and a comedy club for evening shows. Open pavement and surrounding commercial facades define the plaza. Guests move between restaurants and performance venues on foot.
