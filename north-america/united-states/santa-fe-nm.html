Santa Fe, NM

Santa Fe was founded in 1610 as a Spanish colonial capital on Pueblo lands, making it among the oldest continuous seats of government in the United States. The Palace of the Governors and adobe churches mark that era. Mexican and then U.S. rule followed the Santa Fe Trail. Artists and preservation rules in the 20th century reinforced Pueblo Revival style. Plaza markets and canyon light still organise the high-desert capital.

Santa Fe Plaza

Santa Fe Plaza has been the city's central square since the Spanish founded Santa Fe in 1610. Adobe portales and shops face a tree-shaded rectangle used for markets, festivals, and daily gathering. The plaza reflects the grid of a Spanish colonial capital still active as a civic heart.

The Cathedral Basilica of St. Francis of Assisi

The Cathedral Basilica of Saint Francis of Assisi was built in the late nineteenth century under Archbishop Jean-Baptiste Lamy, replacing an older adobe church while retaining a small historic chapel nearby. Romanesque stonework, twin towers, stained glass and a formal nave contrast with Santa Fe's adobe tradition. Worship, funerals, diocesan events and restoration guide entry to the active cathedral.

Lensic Performing Arts Center

The Lensic Performing Arts Center occupies a Spanish Renaissance-style theatre in downtown Santa Fe for live music, dance, film, and lectures. Programming ranges from classical concerts and flamenco to jazz and spoken-word nights. The restored house anchors the city's indoor performance calendar beside the Opera's summer season.

Cross of the Martyrs

The Cross of the Martyrs is a large, white cross situated on a prominent hill overlooking downtown Santa Fe. It commemorates the Franciscan friars and Spanish colonists who died during the 1680 Pueblo Revolt. A short, paved walkway leads to the monument, providing historical plaques along the route and offering panoramic views of the city and the Jemez Mountains.

San Miguel Chapel

Adobe walls from the seventeenth century define San Miguel Chapel in Santa Fe, tied to a congregation whose history reaches into early colonial settlement. Exterior and main interior spaces can be viewed outside service times when open. The chapel remains an active small worship building on its historic block.

Kakawa Chocolate House

Kakawa Chocolate House in Santa Fe is a unique specialty chocolate shop that offers authentic and historic drinking chocolates. Their hot chocolate drinks are small but intensely rich, focusing on traditional Pre-Columbian Mesoamerican Mayan Aztec and 1600's European drinking chocolates. The Marie Antoinette hot chocolate, made with chocolate, almond milk, orange blossom, cinnamon, and vanilla, is recommended.

Meow Wolf Santa Fe's House of Eternal Return is a 22,000 square foot immersive art experience filled with multimedia elements and a mysterious narrative. Housed in a former bowling alley owned by George R.R. Martin, this fantasy world created by 150 artists offers layers of creative chaos and interactivity without the gore of Game of Thrones.

Santa Fe Farmers Market

Santa Fe Farmers Market gathers regional growers and producers in the Railyard on scheduled market days. Seasonal vegetables, fruit and chile reflect agriculture across northern New Mexico. Meat, cheese, bread, flowers and prepared foods add to the selection. Indoor and outdoor sections shift with weather and harvest cycles, while the direct presence of producers distinguishes the market from nearby festivals, restaurants and permanent shops.

The Santa Fe Opera

On a hilltop outside Santa Fe, The Santa Fe Opera stages classic and contemporary works in an open-sided festival house where many guests arrive early for tailgate dinners. Ticketed performances fill summer evenings. Mountain air and night views frame the stage house.

Fort Marcy Park

Fort Marcy Park occupies a hill above downtown Santa Fe where the U.S. Army established a fort after taking control of New Mexico in 1846. Little of the military installation survives, but earthwork traces, paths, recreation facilities and overlooks mark the area. The park's history connects expansion and occupation with the wider landscape, while steep trails, weather, events and fire restrictions affect use.
