Dallas, TX

Dallas grew from a Trinity River trading post chartered in 1856 into a cotton and banking centre after regional railways converged in the 1870s. Dealey Plaza, Arts District museums, and Fair Park art-deco exposition halls record how East Texas oil wealth, suburban expansion, and late-twentieth-century corporate headquarters reshaped a prairie commercial capital.

Dealey Plaza

Dealey Plaza preserves a civic landscape at the western edge of downtown Dallas, internationally associated with the assassination of President Kennedy in 1963. Roads, grassy slopes, monuments, the former Texas School Book Depository and surrounding buildings support historical interpretation of the event. Continuing street traffic makes the plaza both an active urban space and a memorial setting.

John F. Kennedy Memorial Plaza

Dedicated in 1970 near Dealey Plaza, the John F. Kennedy Memorial Plaza is an open cenotaph designed by Philip Johnson. Thirty-foot concrete walls form a roofless square, with inscribed passages from Kennedy's speeches on the approaches. The empty chamber invites a quiet pause a short walk from the assassination site.

The Dallas World Aquarium

The Dallas World Aquarium occupies converted warehouses in the city's West End district. Indoor paths move through planted rainforest habitats, aquariums and mixed exhibits containing fish, birds, reptiles and mammals from several regions. Waterfalls, elevated walkways and large viewing windows create a continuous route, while interpretation addresses ecosystems, breeding programs and threats facing selected species.

The Dallas Arboretum is a blend of nature and art, offering visitors a chance to explore 66 acres of gardens, trails, and unique exhibits. Fair Park

Fair Park in Dallas is a large civic complex of museums, sports venues, an amusement area, and memorials on the State Fair of Texas grounds. Art Deco buildings from the 1936 Centennial Exposition still define the campus. The Children's Aquarium, modernized in 2009, is one of several year-round attractions among the lawns and plazas.

Giant Eyeball

Located in Dallas, the Giant Eyeball is a 30-ft. Tall fiberglass sculpture of a human eyeball situated in a fenced garden near the Dallas Arts District. Created by multimedia artist Tony Tasset, this realistic rendering has become an emblem for the city. Originally installed in Chicago, it found its permanent home in Dallas and is now owned by the Joule Hotel. While usually fenced off from public access, visitors can still admire this unique piece of art from afar.

The Majestic Theater, located in Downtown Dallas, is a historic Spanish baroque performance venue that originally opened as a vaudeville theater nearly 100 years ago. Managed by the City of Dallas Office of Cultural Affairs, it has become a multi-purpose megaplex hosting Broadway musicals, comedy shows, concerts by known artists like Lyle Lovett and Mary Chapin Carpenter, dance performances including those by the Moscow Ballet, and popular podcasts.

Texas Theatre

The Texas Theatre, a historic site in Dallas, gained notoriety as the place where Lee Harvey Oswald was captured. Beyond its historical significance, the theater has an elegant art deco interior and a stylish bar offering boutique drinks and affordable Texan and Mexican beers. It hosts a diverse range of events including classic movie screenings, live performances by bands and comedians, as well as special celebrations like the 50th anniversary of Jesus Christ Superstar.
