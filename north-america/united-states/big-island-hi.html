Big Island, HI

Hawaiʻi Island, often called the Big Island, is the largest and geologically youngest of the Hawaiian chain, formed by active volcanoes Mauna Loa and Kīlauea. Polynesian navigators settled the coasts centuries before Kamehameha I unified the islands from his base near Kailua-Kona in the late 18th century. Ranching, sugar plantations, and later astronomy on Mauna Kea reshaped upland and coastal communities.

Kaimu Black Sand Beach

Kaimu Black Sand Beach is a beach in Big Island, HI, United States. Records associated with the site include 1990. Kaimū Black Sand Beach formed after lava flows from Kīlauea buried the former Kalapana shoreline in 1990. New black sand has accumulated where the flow met the ocean, and a short trail leads travellers to the present coast east of Pāhoa. Access is typically on foot from nearby roads or beachfront paths.

Nāhuku Lava Tube

Nāhuku, also known as the Thurston Lava Tube, is a massive, accessible subterranean cave located within Hawai'i Volcanoes National Park. Formed hundreds of years ago by a river of molten lava, the 600-foot-long illuminated tunnel allows visitors to walk through the geological formation, surrounded by a lush, tropical rainforest environment at the entrance.

Kaūmana Caves

The Kaūmana Caves are a network of lava tubes located near Hilo, Hawaii, formed by an 1881 eruption of Mauna Loa. Unlike the illuminated Nāhuku tube, these caves are largely undeveloped, requiring visitors to bring their own flashlights to explore the dark, rugged interior. The site offers a more adventurous, self-guided geological experience.
