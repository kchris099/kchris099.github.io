Great Smoky Mountains, NC

The Great Smoky Mountains were home to the Cherokee for millennia before European-American settlers arrived in the late eighteenth century, establishing isolated Appalachian farming and logging communities. Intensive commercial logging in the early twentieth century devastated the forests, sparking a grassroots conservation movement that culminated in the establishment of the national park in 1934. The park preserved both the recovering biodiversity and the region's pioneer heritage. Visitors experience this Appalachian history at the historic cabins, gristmills, and churches in Cades Cove and Cataloochee.

Great Smoky Mountains National Park

Great Smoky Mountains National Park protects forested ridges, streams, wildlife, historic buildings and diverse plant communities across Tennessee and North Carolina. Roads and hundreds of miles of trails reach valleys and high country, while fog, rain, crowds, wildlife and changing weather affect access. The park also preserves displacement, logging, settlement.

Ripley's Aquarium of the Smokies

In downtown Gatlinburg, Ripley's Aquarium of the Smokies keeps more than 10,000 marine animals year-round. Shark tunnels, sea turtles, stingrays, and penguins fill the main galleries. A systems-control overlook explains the saltwater plant, and a glass-bottom boat ride crosses a large exhibit pool.

Ben Morton Overlook

Ben Morton Overlook, located in the Great Smoky Mountains National Park, is a popular roadside stop along Route 441 that offers sunset views. The westward-facing vantage point provides panoramas of the forested canyon and skyline ridges. This spot is particularly known for its sunset views during late spring through high summer. It's an ideal location for capturing sunset photography and offers a less congested alternative to other nearby pullouts.

Roaring Fork Motor Nature Trail

The Roaring Fork Motor Nature Trail is a must-visit scenic drive located the Great Smoky Mountains National Park. This enchanting 5.5-mile loop takes visitors through lush old-growth forests, where towering trees and active wildlife abound. As visitors navigate this route, encounter log cabins that whisper tales of the past, alongside the soothing sounds of the Roaring Fork stream cascading nearby.

Grotto Falls

A moderate three-mile round trip on Trillium Gap Trail reaches Grotto Falls through forest on the Roaring Fork Motor Nature Trail corridor. Hikers can walk behind the curtain of water at the fall. Marked track and viewing edges structure the arrival.

Great Smoky Mountains

The Great Smoky Mountains form a broad Appalachian range spanning Tennessee and North Carolina. Forested slopes, valleys and communities extend far beyond any single viewpoint or trail. A large national park protects the central highlands, while gateway towns, private land and developed tourism districts occupy the surrounding region. Elevation and rainfall create marked ecological differences.

Gatlinburg Scenic Overlook lies along the Gatlinburg Bypass with a roadside view toward the town and surrounding Smoky Mountain ridges. Kuwohi Observation Deck

Kuwohi Observation Deck stands near the summit of the mountain formerly known as Clingmans Dome in Great Smoky Mountains National Park, and exhibits address the mountain's Cherokee name and cultural importance, adding human history to the high-elevation forest and landscape. A steep paved path leads from the parking area to the concrete tower, whose spiral ramp opens views across layered Appalachian ridges.

Gatlinburg Space Needle

Ascend to the top of the Gatlinburg Space Needle, a 407-ft. Observation tower that has been an part of the city since 1969. Cataract Falls

About three-quarters of a mile round trip, Cataract Falls Trail sits just under two miles from downtown Gatlinburg for an easy family waterfall walk. Marked path leads to the viewing area beside the fall. The short distance suits limited hiking time in the Smokies.
