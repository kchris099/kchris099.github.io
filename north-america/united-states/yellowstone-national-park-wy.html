Yellowstone National Park, WY

Yellowstone became the world's first national park in 1872 on a volcanic plateau spanning northwestern Wyoming and parts of Montana and Idaho. Its geysers, hot springs, canyons, and wildlife reflect ongoing geothermal activity beneath the Rocky Mountains, studied by expeditions long before park designation. Army administration and later National Park Service management balanced conservation with rising visitation. Indigenous peoples including Shoshone and Crow hunted and traveled these highlands for centuries.

Roosevelt Arch

The Roosevelt Arch marks the north entrance at Gardiner and was dedicated in 1903 by President Theodore Roosevelt. Its inscribed gateway frames the road into the park and recalls early federal efforts to protect Yellowstone's geothermal and wildlife resources for public use.

Old Faithful

Old Faithful, a cone geyser in Yellowstone's Upper Geyser Basin, erupts on a relatively predictable schedule watched from boardwalks set back from the sinter cone and runoff channels. Predicted windows posted nearby organize waiting times between eruptions. Crowds gather on the circled boardwalk before each spout.

Old Faithful Inn

Old Faithful Inn opened in 1904 beside Yellowstone's well-known geyser as a large rustic lodge built with logs and stone. Its towering lobby, balconies, massive fireplace, guest rooms and dining spaces reflect the early development of national-park tourism. The building remains an operating seasonal hotel, so access to rooms and services depends on lodging operations, weather and conservation work within the historic structure.

44.4596° N / 110.8313° W

04 Admission$20

Grand Prismatic Spring

Grand Prismatic Spring fills a broad thermal basin with deep blue water surrounded by bands of orange, yellow and green microbial mats. Boardwalks in Midway Geyser Basin provide close but low views, while a separate trail reaches an elevated overlook.

44.5251° N / 110.8382° W

05 Admission$35

Mammoth Hot Springs

Mammoth Hot Springs builds terraced travertine on a hillside near Yellowstone's north entrance. Boardwalks cross white and orange mineral shelves that change as water flow shifts. The Albright Visitor Center nearby interprets the thermal area and fort-era buildings.

44.9624° N / 110.7144° W

06 Admission$20

Norris Geyser Basin

Norris is Yellowstone's hottest and most changeable geyser basin, with Porcelain and Back Basin trail loops. Geysers, fumaroles, and mineral pools line the boardwalks. Steam and shifting vents make the ground feel less stable than smoother basins farther south.

44.7266° N / 110.7028° W

07 Admission$20

Sheepeater Cliffs

Sheepeater Cliffs are columnar basalt outcrops along the Gardner River near Mammoth. The formation results from cooling lava and is named for the Sheepeater Shoshone who used the area. A roadside pullout and short walk provide views of the cliffs and river canyon within the park.

44.9330° N / 110.6766° W

08 Admission$20

Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone

The Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone cuts through volcanic rock between Canyon Village and Tower-Roosevelt. Artist Point and other overlooks frame the Lower Falls dropping into a yellow-stained gorge shaped by erosion and hydrothermal alteration. Trails along the rim connect multiple viewpoints.

44.7203° N / 110.4795° W
