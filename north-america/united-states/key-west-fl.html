Key West, FL

Key West grew rich in the nineteenth century first on wrecking (salvaging ships on Florida reefs), then on sponges, cigars, and a naval station. Isolation fostered a local culture later tied to writers such as Ernest Hemingway. Hurricanes repeatedly forced timber rebuilds. The Overseas Highway linked the island to the mainland in 1938. Conch-style houses, Mallory Square, and the southernmost point marker frame the city today.

Fort Zachary Taylor Historic State Park

A Civil War-era coastal fortress sits at the center of Fort Zachary Taylor Historic State Park in Key West, operated as a Florida State Park. The grounds hold a large collection of Civil War armament, with walls, courtyards, and towers forming the usual walking circuit when open. Harbor approaches and park paths bring walkers onto the fort's masonry perimeter.

Southernmost Point of the Continental U.S.A.

At South and Whitehead streets, Key West's painted concrete buoy marks a frequently photographed corner near the island's southern shore. Installed as a tourism marker, it labels the area as the southernmost point of the continental United States, although restricted and privately held land extends farther south. Queues, traffic, heat and storms shape the brief roadside stop around this symbolic object.

The Key West Butterfly and Nature Conservatory

A glasshouse on Duval Street holds free-flying butterflies and tropical birds among planted paths. Climate control keeps the conservatory humid and warm through Key West's milder winters. Visitors walk a single loop past nectar plants and quiet observation benches.

Key West Lighthouse

Key West Lighthouse began service in 1848 after an earlier coastal light was destroyed by a hurricane. An iron stairway rises through the tower to an exterior gallery, while the keeper's quarters interprets the work and family life associated with the station.

Key West Aquarium

Opened in 1934 beside Mallory Square, Key West Aquarium was designed as an early public display of marine life from the Florida Keys. Compact tanks and interpretation focus on reef fish, sharks, sea turtles and local coastal habitats. The building's partly open-air design reflects its tropical setting, while later alterations adapted the small historic facility to modern animal care.

Sunset Celebration at Mallory Square

Before sunset, Mallory Square fills with performers, artists, vendors and spectators facing the Gulf of Mexico. The gathering developed into a daily Key West tradition tied to the waterfront's long use as a commercial landing place, and weather, cruise traffic, seasonal crowds and performer participation change the atmosphere each evening and the celebration is a recurring public event.

Smathers Beach

Smathers Beach extends along Key West's Atlantic shoreline as a city-managed strip of imported sand. A promenade and roadside parking make it one of the island's more accessible recreational beaches. Picnic areas and seasonal rental vendors line parts of the shore, while seagrass, currents and storms alter water conditions. Heat and water quality also affect the experience throughout the year.

Edward B. Knight Pier

Formerly White Street Pier, the Edward B. Knight Pier pushes a wide concrete walk far into the Atlantic off Key West's south shore. Anglers and walkers share the deck with open water on both sides. Sunrise light and unbroken horizon views make the pier a daily stop for residents as much as travelers.
