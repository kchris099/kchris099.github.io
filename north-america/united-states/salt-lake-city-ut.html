Salt Lake City, UT

Salt Lake City was laid out in 1847 by Mormon pioneers led by Brigham Young at the southeastern edge of the Great Salt Lake. Temple Square granite, grid-pattern blocks, and Union Pacific depot warehouses record how Latter-day Saint settlement, territorial status debates, and twentieth-century mining and ski-industry growth built Utah's commercial and spiritual centre.

Wheeler Historic Farm

Wheeler Historic Farm preserves a working public farm landscape in the Salt Lake Valley, with barns, historic buildings, fields, livestock, paths and seasonal programs. The property reflects rural life and later suburban growth. Animal care, agricultural work, school visits, events, weather and trail conditions change what is accessible across the county-operated site.

Conference Center

The Conference Center in Salt Lake City was built for large gatherings of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, including its semiannual general conference. A vast auditorium, religious art, meeting areas and a landscaped roof occupy the complex north of Temple Square. Tours and public events operate around worship, broadcasts, rehearsals, security and church administration.

Salt Lake Utah Temple

The Salt Lake Temple of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, completed in 1893, dominates Temple Square in downtown Salt Lake City. Granite spires and the Angel Moroni statue define the skyline of the block. Public access focuses on the surrounding Temple Square grounds and visitor centers rather than the temple interior.

Temple Square

Temple Square covers about ten acres in downtown Salt Lake City as the administrative and ceremonial headquarters campus of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Exterior grounds and many public interior spaces can be viewed outside worship times when open. Gardens and visitor pathways organize movement around the Temple block.

Salt Lake City Tabernacle

On Temple Square, the Salt Lake Tabernacle is known for its broad dome and unusually clear acoustics. The Mormon Tabernacle Choir and other ensembles rehearse and perform under the wooden roof. Visitors often test the whisper effect from across the hall during open hours.

Assembly Hall

Assembly Hall stands on Temple Square as a Gothic Revival meeting building completed in the nineteenth century from granite left over from temple construction. Pointed windows, a steep roof and a compact auditorium distinguish it from the larger Tabernacle nearby. It remains a religious and performance space, so concerts, worship, renovation and square access rules determine when the interior opens.

Cathedral of the Madeleine

The Cathedral of the Madeleine was completed around the start of the twentieth century as the mother church of Utah's Roman Catholic diocese. A Romanesque exterior encloses a richly decorated Gothic-inspired interior with murals, stained glass, carved wood and an organ. Daily worship, choir programs, funerals and preservation needs shape access to this active religious building.

Utah State Capitol crowns a hill above Salt Lake City in a domed Classical Revival building completed in the early twentieth century. Legislative chambers, ceremonial rooms, murals, marble, exhibits, monuments and surrounding grounds reflect state government and public memory. Liberty Park

Liberty Park spreads across a large block south of downtown Salt Lake City with lawns, mature trees, a pond, paths, sports areas, playgrounds and civic institutions. Developed in the nineteenth century, it remains one of the city's central recreation grounds. Events, weather, water conditions, construction and seasonal facilities change use, while Tracy Aviary is a separate attraction within the park.

Utah's Hogle Zoo, situated on a 42-acre site at the base of Emigration Canyon, is a family-friendly destination that has been visitors since 1931. The zoo is home to over 1000 animals from more than 250 regions worldwide, including rare species like the white rhino and snow leopard.

Red Butte Garden and Arboretum

Red Butte Garden and Arboretum, located on the University of Utah campus, is a large 100-acre botanical garden located the scenic Wasatch Mountains foothills. It has over 21 acres of careful maintained gardens, five miles of nature trails for hiking enthusiasts, and an arboretum housing diverse horticultural collections. The garden is known for its display of daffodils and springtime blooming bulbs.
