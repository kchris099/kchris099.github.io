Anchorage, AK

The Alaska Engineering Commission laid out Anchorage in 1914 as a railroad camp on Cook Inlet. Statehood in 1959, the 1964 earthquake rebuild, and pipeline headquarters made it the state's commercial hub. Dena'ina place names and Chugach backdrop remind visitors the city is recent on an older Indigenous landscape. The 1964 quake memorial and coastal trail interpret the disaster that reshaped downtown.

Earthquake Park

Earthquake Park covers about 134 acres on Anchorage's coast where the 1964 Good Friday earthquake tore and dropped large tracts of ground. Trails cross the crumpled terrain, and interpretive panels explain the quake's force. Paths and open ground make the park both a memorial landscape and a daily walking area.

02 AdmissionFree

Point Woronzof Park

Point Woronzof Park occupies a coastal bluff west of Anchorage near the airport, with views across Cook Inlet toward mountains in clear weather. Trails and shoreline access pass through forest and exposed tidal terrain, while aircraft frequently cross overhead. Extreme tides, mudflats, wildlife, wind, and erosion make the setting more complex than a simple sunset viewpoint.

Alaska Zoo

The Alaska Zoo keeps northern and cold-climate animals on a wooded hillside in Anchorage. Its collection has been shaped by rescue, rehabilitation, breeding and transfers, with native Alaskan species shown alongside animals from other regions. Forested paths and seasonal conditions give the grounds a distinctly northern character, while education and animal care remain central to the institution's role.

61.1238° N / 149.7907° W

04 AdmissionFree

Delaney Park

Named for a former mayor, the block-wide Delaney Park Strip began as a 1910s firebreak, later a golf course and airstrip, before becoming post-war public parkland. Sports fields, tennis courts, and horseshoe pits now occupy the long green. Downtown Anchorage uses it as a continuous recreation band.

61.2131° N / 149.8962° W

Alaska Center for the Performing Arts

The Alaska Center for the Performing Arts occupies a downtown Anchorage complex with several theaters used for music, drama, dance, opera, comedy, and civic events. Resident organizations and touring productions share the stages, while public foyers connect to the surrounding arts district.

Town Square Park

Town Square Park provides a small public green in downtown Anchorage beside the performing-arts center and civic buildings. Lawns, paths, seating, winter lights, markets, protests and festivals give the space different roles across the year. Snow, maintenance, events and social conditions can affect use.

Visit Anchorage Log Cabin Visitor Information Center

The Log Cabin Visitor Information Center stands in downtown Anchorage as a staffed orientation point with maps, brochures, local advice and seasonal displays. Its rustic appearance recalls a frontier cabin, although the building is a modern tourism facility. The center helps travelers understand transport, neighborhoods and regional activities before exploring the city and surrounding areas.

Alaska Wild Berry Products

Alaska Wild Berry Products operates a commercial shop and confectionery attraction in Anchorage, selling chocolates, candies, gifts and products themed around local berries and wildlife. Displays and a large chocolate feature are designed for retail appeal.

09 AdmissionFree

Kincaid Park

Kincaid Park, located near the southern end of the Tony Knowles Coastal Trail in Anchorage, is a natural retreat named after former Alaska Governor Bob Kincaid. Spanning over 1,500 acres along the rugged coastline, this park offers a mix of recreational facilities and untamed wilderness. The extensive trail network covers over 40 miles and provides opportunities for activities such as hiking, trail running, mountain biking, cross-country skiing, and dog mushing during winter.

Alaska Botanical Garden

Alaska Botanical Garden occupies a wooded site in Anchorage where trails connect perennial beds, herb gardens, native plants and experimental northern horticulture. The short growing season shapes what is visible at different times of year, while forest habitat remains part of the grounds. Programs and collections focus on plants that can survive Alaska's climate.
