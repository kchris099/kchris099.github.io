Phoenix, AZ

Phoenix grew from Hohokam canal agriculture revived by nineteenth-century irrigation companies in the Salt River Valley. Territorial capitol buildings, Papago Park red buttes, and postwar subdivision grids document how reclamation law, air-conditioning adoption, and Sun Belt migration transformed a desert farming town into Arizona's largest metropolitan area.

Hole in the Rock

Within Phoenix's Papago Park, erosion opened a broad cavity through the sandstone formation called Hole in the Rock. A short, uneven ascent reaches the opening and frames views across desert terrain, lagoons, roads and the city. Heat, exposed rock and fragile soils shape conditions around the formation, whose compact urban-park trail differs from similarly named routes elsewhere in the American Southwest.

Phoenix Zoo

The Phoenix Zoo covers 125 acres and offers a variety of animal exhibits, including a children's area and petting zoo. Visitors can also enjoy a narrated tram ride around the zoo. Anticipated events at the Phoenix Zoo is Zoo Lights, where millions of lights are strung on trees and displayed as animal shapes along pathways.

ASU Gammage

ASU Gammage, a historic theater located on the Arizona State University campus, is known for hosting various arts performances. Designed by the famous architect Frank Lloyd Wright in the 1950s, it was originally intended to be an opera house for the King of Iraq but was repurposed as an auditorium for ASU after political events led to the cancellation of the original project.

Symphony Hall

Phoenix's main downtown concert hall forms part of the convention and performing-arts complex. Its auditorium and stage accommodate orchestral programs, touring theater, dance, opera, graduations and civic events throughout the year. More than 2,300 seats face a broad proscenium, and the surrounding plaza links the venue with other cultural buildings in the central city.

St. Mary's Roman Catholic Basilica

Saint Mary's Basilica grew from Phoenix's early Catholic parish and occupies a Mission Revival church completed in the early twentieth century. Twin towers, stained glass, arches and a decorated sanctuary reflect its role in the city's religious history, while later designation recognized it as a minor basilica. Worship, funerals, parish programs and restoration guide entry to the active church.

Orpheum Theatre Phoenix

The Orpheum Theatre Phoenix opened in 1929 as a downtown Beaux-Arts movie and vaudeville house later restored for live performance. Gilded plaster, murals, and a full-stage house support concerts, theatre, and dance. Public entry is usually for ticketed shows in the restored auditorium.

The Van Buren

The Van Buren, located in downtown Phoenix, has a rich history as it was originally an automotive showroom for the Phoenix Motor Company back in 1939. However, today it is a music venue that can accommodate around 1,800 concertgoers. The interior is adorned with chandeliers and desert murals on the walls, creating a unique and steady trade for attendees.

At 2,330 feet atop South Mountain Park and Preserve, Dobbins Lookout opens panoramic views of Phoenix's skyline, metro basin, and surrounding ranges. Drivers and hikers both reach the overlook via mountain roads or trail approaches. Railings and parking define the summit viewing area.
