Bryce Canyon, UT

Bryce Canyon's amphitheatres of hoodoos formed through frost and stream erosion on the Paunsaugunt Plateau. Paiute people told stories of the rock figures; Mormon settler Ebenezer Bryce ranching nearby lent the site its English name. It became a national monument in 1923 and a national park in 1928. Rim walks and high-elevation viewpoints now show layered Claron Formation colour above southern Utah's plateaus.

Bryce Canyon National Park Sunset Point

Sunset Point overlooks the amphitheaters of Bryce Canyon National Park, where erosion has carved hoodoos, fins and cliffs from colorful sedimentary rock. A paved rim path connects nearby viewpoints, while steep trails descend below. Despite the name, light conditions vary throughout the day and snow, ice, lightning, altitude, crowds and cliff edges require caution.

Bryce Canyon National Park Sunrise Point

Sunrise Point stands on the rim of Bryce Canyon with views across hoodoos and the Paunsaugunt Plateau, the Queen's Garden Trail begins nearby, dropping quickly into the formations, while the rim route remains easier. The viewpoint's name does not guarantee clear sunrise conditions, weather, seasonal road access, snow, altitude, and exposed edges all affect the experience.

Bryce Canyon National Park Visitor Center

Bryce Canyon National Park Visitor Center provides maps, exhibits, films and ranger information near the main entrance. Displays introduce the park's geology, ecology, Indigenous history and high-elevation environment before roads continue toward the rim viewpoints, making the center a practical planning stop where seasonal transport and trail conditions are explained.

04 Admission$20

Bryce Point

Bryce Point overlooks the amphitheater hoodoos for sunrise and sunset light, reached by road or the Rim Trail. The 22.9-mile Under-the-Rim Trail continues into quieter forested backcountry for longer trips. Railings and short paths serve the primary vista.

05 Admission$20

Lower Inspiration Point

Lower Inspiration Point is a vista located atop steep cliffs, providing visitors with, unobstructed views of Bryce Canyon's main amphitheater. While this viewpoint offers a fantastic perspective of the canyon, many travelers recommend taking a short hike to Upper Inspiration Point for an even more view. The area is rich in wildlife; during my visit, I spotted chipmunks and deer roaming about.

37.6158° N / 112.1702° W

06 Admission$20

Fairyland Point

Fairyland Point overlooks the northern end of Bryce Canyon's eroded landscape from a quieter rim area near the park entrance; hoodoos, ridges and distant plateaus spread below, while the strenuous Fairyland Loop descends from the viewpoint. Seasonal road gates, snow, limited services, altitude, lightning and long trail distances require planning beyond the brief roadside stop.

37.6491° N / 112.1474° W

07 AdmissionFree (requires park admission $20)

Natural Bridge

Bryce Amphitheater is a natural formation with rock formations and cliff trails. The Bryce Amphitheater Traverse Trail offers a 3-4 hour hike starting at Bryce Point and ending at Sunrise Point, providing a unique perspective of the area. Visitors can stop by the Bryce Canyon Visitor Center to learn about the park's geography and pick up brochures before exploring the amphitheater.

08 Admission$50

Dark Ranger Telescope Tours

If visitors are looking for an stargazing experience, consider joining the Dark Ranger Telescope Tours in Bryce Canyon. Located just a short drive from town, this family-owned business offers an entertaining and educational night of cosmic exploration. With modern equipment and passionate guides like Sawyer, Liam, and Thomas, have the chance to view celestial wonders such as Venus, Mars, Vega, Mizar, and the Milky Way through their massive telescopes.

Bryce Wildlife Adventure

Bryce Wildlife Adventure Museum stands on Scenic Byway 12 near the entrance to Bryce Canyon National Park. Founded in the 1990s, the private natural-history collection uses taxidermy dioramas to present animals from Utah and other regions, supported by mounted butterflies, skulls and Western artifacts. The displays emphasize species identification and habitat interpretation inside a compact roadside museum.
