Death Valley, CA

Death Valley is the hottest and driest region in North America, shaped by intense tectonic activity that created a deep basin below sea level. The Timbisha Shoshone people have inhabited the valley for centuries, adapting to its extreme conditions. In the late nineteenth century, the area gained notoriety during the California Gold Rush and subsequent borax mining operations. Designated a national monument in 1933 and a national park in 1994, Death Valley preserves a stark landscape of salt flats, sand dunes, and rugged mountains that illustrate the extremes of the natural world.

Zabriskie Point

Badland hills at Zabriskie Point catch sunrise and sunset color above Death Valley floors once tied to Gold Rush-era borax mining. Trails continue toward Gower Gulch and Golden Canyon from the main overlook. Short paths from parking reach the primary rail.

02 Admission$15

Golden Canyon Trailhead

Golden Canyon Trailhead begins a route through layered badlands near Furnace Creek in Death Valley National Park. Washes and side paths lead toward colorful formations, Red Cathedral and longer connections, with little shade and rapidly rising heat; flash floods, loose rock, route choices and extreme temperatures make water, timing and current park conditions essential.

03 Admission$15

Furnace Creek Visitor Center

Furnace Creek Visitor Center is the main hub at Death Valley National Park, offering information about park attractions, wildlife, plants, and history. Visitors can also learn about NPCA's conservation efforts and the return of American Pronghorn to the park. The center provides an introduction to Death Valley from park staff and is a starting point for exploring nearby trails like Salt Creek Interpretive Trail and Mosaic Canyon Trail.

36.4619° N / 116.8668° W

04 Admission$15

Harmony Borax Works

Harmony Borax Works preserves adobe ruins, equipment and interpretive displays from a short-lived nineteenth-century borax operation in Death Valley, and the industrial remains sit in exposed desert, where heat and fragile structures limit the visit. A small loop explains mineral processing, labor, transportation and the later marketing legend of twenty-mule teams.
