Grand Canyon, AZ

The Grand Canyon was carved by the Colorado River through nearly two billion years of rock, long known to Native nations including the Havasupai, Hopi, and Navajo. John Wesley Powell's 1869 river trip mapped much of the gorge for science. National park status came in 1919 after earlier monument protection. Rim villages, mule trails, and river corridors organise travel along one of North America's deepest exposed geologic sections.

Mather Point

Mather Point is a popular scenic viewpoint located along the South Rim of the Grand Canyon. It offers canyon vistas and is conveniently close to the visitor center. Yavapai Point, situated west of Mather Point, provides an equally view of the Grand Canyon Village area. From Yavapai Point, visitors can admire various landmarks such as the Bright Angel Trail, Indian Gardens, Phantom Ranch, and even catch a glimpse of the Colorado River and its suspension bridge.

Grand Canyon Visitor Center

Near Mather Point on the South Rim, the Grand Canyon Visitor Center gathers maps, exhibits, and a short film orientation. Bike rentals, a cafe, and a bookstore sit beside shuttle stops that fan out along the rim. Most day visitors begin here before walking to overlooks or boarding village-bound buses.

Grandview Point

Located along the South Rim of the Grand Canyon, Grandview Point is a must-visit destination that offers views of active canyons and the winding Colorado River. Hermit's Rest

Hermit's Rest, a historic structure designed in the National Park rustic style by architect Mary Colter in 1914, is a rest stop along Hermit Road. This road offers some of the most viewpoints in Grand Canyon National Park and is closed to private vehicles from March through November. Visitors can enjoy canyon viewing and easy hiking without traffic or parking issues, thanks to the free shuttle buses that stop at eight overlooks along the route.

Hopi Point

Hopi Point juts from the South Rim with wide, unobstructed canyon views along Hermit Road. Sunrise and sunset crowds gather on the stone walls when shuttle schedules allow. Layered cliffs drop toward the Colorado River corridor far below the railing.

Powell Point

Powell Point is a scenic overlook at the Grand Canyon with sweeping views and a commemorative memorial to exploratory crews. It's recommended for photography enthusiasts, but caution is advised during monsoon season due to lightning risks. Accessible by shuttle buses, Powell Point offers geological and historical insights from drivers en route to Hermits Rest.

Mohave Point

Mohave Point is a popular South Rim overlook along the Hermit Road, offering vistas of terraced cliffs, sandstone mesas, and the curving Colorado River. It's part of the Trail of Time, where each step represents a million years of geological history. The area is known for its famous sunrise and sunset viewpoints, including The Abyss, a one-mile vertical indentation into the canyon wall. Artists often find inspiration in the unique way sunlight interacts with the rock formations here.

Grand Canyon

The Grand Canyon exposes layers of rock carved by the Colorado River across northern Arizona, and rims, side canyons, plateaus, desert ecosystems, Indigenous homelands and difficult trails form a landscape far larger than any single viewpoint. Heat, altitude, winter ice, water scarcity and deceptive distances create serious risks, while tribal histories and living communities are essential to interpretation.

Yaki Point projects from the Grand Canyon's South Rim with broad views into the gorge and toward the South Kaibab Trail. Grand Canyon Depot

Experience the magic of a bygone era with a journey on the Grand Canyon Railway, a historic train that takes passengers on a scenic ride to the South Rim of the Grand Canyon. This heritage railroad, which dates back to 1901, lets visitors witness Arizona's landscapes while reliving the romance of classic rail travel. Departing from Williams, Arizona, the train travels 64 miles through terrain before reaching its destination at Grand Canyon National Park.

Bright Angel Trailhead

Bright Angel Trailhead is the starting point for a challenging 7.9-mile hike, known as the park's 'superhighway,' which connects the Colorado River and South Rim. To began on this one-way journey, it is recommended to park at the Backcountry Information Center at the South Rim and take the Trans Canyon Shuttle to the North Rim. The shuttle arrives at a convenient time for beginning the hike in shade.

Trail View Overlook is a must-visit spot for anyone exploring the Grand Canyon. This observation point provides bird's-eye views of the expansive 18-mile-wide red rock national park, showing the active Grand Canyon Village and the winding Bright Angel Trail below. South Rim Trail

Paved and dirt segments of the South Rim Trail link overlooks between Grand Canyon Village and farther west viewpoints. Shuttle stops make out-and-back walks easy to shorten. Outdoor panels along one stretch compress about 2.1 billion years of rock history into a little more than a mile of path.
