Los Angeles, CA

Los Angeles expanded from a Spanish pueblo at the Porciúncula River into a global metropolis after Southern Pacific and Santa Fe railways arrived in the 1870s. Mission San Gabriel influence, Hollywood studio lots, and Pacific port infrastructure document how Mexican rancho land grants, citrus irrigation, and twentieth-century film and aerospace industries reshaped a semi-arid basin.

Angels Flight Railway

Two funicular cars climb a 298-foot incline on Angels Flight, built in 1901 beside Bunker Hill. Often called the world's shortest railway, the line reopened after restorations and still links Hill and Olive streets. Film crews have used the orange cars, including scenes in 500 Days of Summer.

Bradbury Building

An iron-and-glass atrium gives the 1893 Bradbury Building one of downtown Los Angeles's most recognizable interiors. Open staircases, cage elevators, brick walls, and skylit galleries contrast with the restrained exterior, reflecting an early commercial office design. The building remains in active use, so public viewing is usually limited to designated ground-floor areas and may be restricted by filming or security.
