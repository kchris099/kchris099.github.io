San Antonio, TX

San Antonio grew from a Spanish mission and presidio on the San Antonio River in 1718. The Alamo, River Walk, and Spanish colonial missions record Tejano, Mexican, and Texan identities on the South Texas frontier. Mission trail bike routes and River Walk barges extend Spanish colonial landmarks along the San Antonio River. Walking routes through the historic centre help visitors relate these monuments to wider patterns of settlement and civic life in United States.

Hemisfair

Hemisfair's 92 acres in downtown San Antonio held the 1968 World's Fair and still host the Tower of the Americas and Institute of Texan Cultures. Lawn events and playgrounds fill the former fairgrounds. Paths link the park to the nearby River Walk without leaving the central district.

La Villita National Historic Village

La Villita preserves a pocket of early San Antonio dwellings as galleries and craft shops. Cobblestone lanes and adobe-scale buildings sit above the River Walk. Artists sell work that references local materials and motifs rather than chain souvenirs alone.

San Antonio River Walk

The San Antonio River Walk is a network of pathways, bridges, and lush greenery that winds through the city. It offers a chance to enjoy natural scenery and city views while providing access to top attractions, restaurants, hotels, and entertainment options. The pathway runs along the San Antonio River and includes The Museum Reach section with art installations and pedestrian access to cultural sites.

Sisters Grimm Ghost Tours & Oddities Parlor

Sisters Grimm Ghost Tours and Oddities Parlor operates themed walking tours in central San Antonio. Guides combine documented local history with folklore, reported hauntings and theatrical storytelling along changing routes through older streets. A small shop extends the subject through books and curiosities. The business offers a commercial interpretation of the city's legends and built heritage.

The Alamo

The 1836 Battle of the Alamo made the Spanish mission-fort compound central to the Texas Revolution narrative; the preserved chapel and Long Barrack now hold shrine and museum spaces for the defenders and regional history. Stone walls enclose the plaza. Walkers enter from surrounding Alamo Plaza streets.

29.4260° N / 98.4861° W

06 AdmissionFree

Majestic Theatre

The Majestic Theatre, a national landmark in downtown San Antonio, is known for its ornate Mediterranean-style architecture and local history. This historic gem, opened in 1929, has a lavish interior resembling a grand Spanish castle with detailed detailing and a ceiling reminiscent of a starry night. With the capacity to accommodate over 2,000 guests, it has become a popular venue for touring concerts, Broadway shows, live entertainment, comedy acts, and classic films.

San Fernando Cathedral

San Fernando Cathedral, a historic church dating back to 1738, holds significant cultural and historical importance in San Antonio. The cathedral's architecture reflects the city's diverse heritage, influenced by Spanish colonists, indigenous communities, and European immigrants. Visiting this landmark provides a chance to connect with San Antonio's rich history and make visitor trip more meaningful.

Historic Market Square

Historic Market Square, known as El Mercado, spreads across three downtown San Antonio blocks as a Mexican-market shopping district. More than one hundred shops sell apparel, crafts, and food in a plaza tradition dating to about 1890. Stalls and restaurants keep regular daytime hours for locals and travellers.

Pearl

The former Pearl Brewery complex now holds restaurants, shops, apartments, and a weekend farmers market. Brick industrial buildings frame a central lawn used for outdoor dining. Culinary schools and food halls keep the district busy beyond retail hours.

Tower of the Americas

The Tower of the Americas rises from Hemisfair as the signature observation structure built for San Antonio's 1968 international exposition. Elevators reach a high viewing level and revolving dining space above a slender concrete shaft. The tower remains a commercial attraction within the surrounding public park, with its design recalling the optimism and vertical spectacle of the fair era.

Mission Concepción

Mission Concepcion was established in the eighteenth century as one of the Spanish colonial missions along the San Antonio River. Its stone church retains original architectural fabric and portions of painted decoration, while surrounding grounds reveal the mission community's religious, agricultural, and defensive organization. The church is an active Catholic parish within the national historical park.

Mission Espada- San Antonio Missions National Historical Park

Mission Espada forms the southernmost of San Antonio's Spanish colonial mission sites, with an eighteenth-century church, compound remains and connections to a historic irrigation system. The mission reflects Indigenous labor, conversion, agriculture and colonial control as well as continuing Catholic worship. Park grounds and parish interiors follow separate schedules, while nearby irrigation structures extend beyond the main enclosure.

San Antonio Missions National Historical Park

San Antonio Missions National Historical Park links the Spanish colonial missions south of downtown, including Espada, San Juan, San José, and Concepción. Adobe churches, acequias, and compound walls remain along the river corridor. Trails and roads connect the mission sites for a single National Park Service visit.

Mission San Juan

Mission San Juan Capistrano along San Antonio's Mission Trail includes an unfinished 1780s church and ruined outbuildings. Farm fields and acequia traces recall the mission's agricultural role. Quiet grounds contrast with busier Mission San Jose farther along the route.

15 AdmissionFree

Espada Aqueduct

The Espada Aqueduct carried irrigation water to Mission Espada fields as part of San Antonio's Spanish colonial acequia system. Stone arches still span a creek bed south of downtown. The structure is among the rare surviving above-ground aqueducts from that irrigation network.

San Antonio Zoo

On about 56 acres in an old limestone quarry on St. Mary's Street, San Antonio Zoo holds more than 9,000 animals from 779 species, including alligators and zebras. Early cageless design choices shaped how exhibits open toward guests. Quarry walls still appear behind some habitats.

17 AdmissionFree

Brackenridge Park

Near downtown San Antonio, Brackenridge Park follows the river with grounds used since prehistoric times and a park bridge built in 1890, later relocated after flood damage. Paths and lawns fill the riverside acreage. The bridge remains a named historic crossing inside the park.

Japanese Tea Garden

The Japanese Tea Garden sits inside San Antonio's Brackenridge Park, with landscaped grounds that date back to 1901. Ponds, stone bridges, and a pavilion occupy a former rock quarry above the park's lower lawns. Paths climb the rock walls and loop the water gardens for a short enclosed walk.

19 AdmissionFree

San Antonio Botanical Garden

San Antonio Botanical Garden is a 38-acre scenic area featuring various collections and exhibit areas, including formal and seasonal display gardens such as the rose garden, sensory garden, water-saving garden, and Kumamoto En Japanese Garden. The Lucile Halsell Conservatory offers a futuristic experience with an underground tunnel leading to the Palm House. Visitors can also explore the 11-acre Texas Native Trail and enjoy guided tours, bird-watching walks, lectures, and other events.

San Antonio Aquarium

San Antonio Aquarium is a privately operated commercial facility on the city's northwest side. Compact galleries display fish and reptiles in tanks and enclosures, joined by birds and other animals. Some interaction programs operate under site rules. Educational labels accompany an experience designed primarily around close viewing and family entertainment.
