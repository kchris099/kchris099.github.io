A Brief History

Costa Rica emerged as a distinct polity after 1821 when the Captaincy General of Guatemala gained independence from Spain, briefly joining the First Mexican Empire before becoming part of the Federal Republic of Central America. Dismembered in 1838, Costa Rica abolished its military in 1948 under President José Figueres Ferrer, redirecting that expenditure toward education, healthcare, and ecological preservation. The country has continued this peaceful tradition for the last three quarters of a century, between Pacific and Caribbean coastlines, supported by agriculture, ecotourism, and a stable democratic tradition that has welcomed millions of international visitors to its national parks and coffee fincas.

National Theater of Costa Rica

National Theater of Costa Rica is a theatre in San Jose, Costa Rica. Construction and early use date to around 1897. The National Theater of Costa Rica is a grand and ornate landmark that offers music and dance performances as well as guided tours in both Spanish and English. Located on the southern side of the Plaza de la Cultura, this revered building was constructed in 1897 and has a neoclassical facade adorned with statues of Beethoven and Calderon de la Barca.

National Stadium Of Costa Rica

In Costa Rica's Central Valley lies the active capital city of San Jose, where visitors can immerse themselves in the country's rich culture. One of the attractions is the newly constructed National Stadium of Costa Rica, a 40,000-seat venue that hosts international concerts and sporting events. The stadium replaced its predecessor in 2011 with support from the Chinese government and has since become a prominent landmark in San Jose.

Parque Central de San José

Parque Central de San José is a busy square located between Avenida 2 and 4 on Calle Central. It is a popular meeting spot for city dwellers, offering park benches and hosting occasional fairs, concerts, and other events. The park is surrounded by significant buildings and shops, including the prominent Melico Salazar Theater to the north and the neoclassical Metropolitan Cathedral to the east.

Costa Rican Center Of Science and Culture

The Costa Rican Center of Science and Culture is a varied complex housed in a former penitentiary. It include a children's museum, an auditorium, art galleries, and various cultural buildings. Originally a prison for nearly six decades, the site was repurposed in 1994 to promote scientific and technological advancement for all ages.
