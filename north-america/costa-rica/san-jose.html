A Brief History

Costa Rica emerged as a distinct polity after 1821 when the Captaincy General of Guatemala gained independence from Spain, briefly joining the First Mexican Empire before becoming part of the Federal Republic of Central America. Dismembered in 1838, Costa Rica abolished its military in 1948 under President José Figueres Ferrer, redirecting that expenditure toward education, healthcare, and ecological preservation. The country has continued this peaceful tradition for the last three quarters of a century, between Pacific and Caribbean coastlines, supported by agriculture, ecotourism, and a stable democratic tradition that has welcomed millions of international visitors to its national parks and coffee fincas.

National Theater of Costa Rica

The National Theater of Costa Rica is a grand and ornate landmark that offers music and dance performances as well as guided tours in both Spanish and English. Located on the southern side of the Plaza de la Cultura, this revered building was constructed in 1897 and features a neoclassical facade adorned with statues of Beethoven and Calderon de la Barca. The lavish marble lobby and auditorium are adorned with paintings depicting various aspects of 19th-century life.

Free

Plaza de la Cultura

Plaza de la Cultura is a bustling public square located in downtown San Jose, Costa Rica. It offers a glimpse into the city's traditional community life, with vendors selling handicrafts and locals strolling through the area. The plaza features a fountain and greenery, creating a vibrant atmosphere for visitors to enjoy. Additionally, it is home to the Museum of Pre-Columbian Gold and the renowned Teatro Nacional, which showcases neoclassical architecture and hosts various performances.

Free

National Stadium Of Costa Rica

In the heart of Costa Rica's Central Valley lies the vibrant capital city of San Jose, where visitors can immerse themselves in the country's rich culture. One of the attractions is the newly constructed National Stadium of Costa Rica, a 40,000-seat venue that hosts international concerts and sporting events. The stadium replaced its predecessor in 2011 with support from the Chinese government and has since become a prominent landmark in San Jose.

Free

Parque Central de San José

Parque Central de San José is a bustling square located between Avenida 2 and 4 on Calle Central. It serves as a popular meeting spot for city dwellers, offering park benches and hosting occasional fairs, concerts, and other events. The park is surrounded by significant buildings and shops, including the prominent Melico Salazar Theater to the north and the neoclassical Metropolitan Cathedral to the east.

$15

Teatro Popular Melico Salazar

Teatro Popular Melico Salazar, also known as the Melico Salazar Theater, is a significant cultural venue in Costa Rica. It offers a diverse range of performances including music, theater, ballet, and modern dance. The theater pays tribute to the country's renowned opera and classical tenor by hosting a variety of concerts, operas, and musical events.

Free

Metropolitan Cathedral of St. Joseph of Costa Rica

The Metropolitan Cathedral of San José, constructed in 1871, showcases a blend of Greek orthodox, neoclassical and baroque architectural styles. It stands as significant religious buildings in the city. Visitors can explore nearby attractions such as The Costa Rica University, The Old Train Station, and The National Liquor Factory. Additionally, they can enjoy local fruit samples at Borbon Market and artisanal ice cream at Central Market.

$30

Costa Rican Center Of Science and Culture

The Costa Rican Center of Science and Culture is a multifaceted complex housed in a former penitentiary. It encompasses a children's museum, an auditorium, art galleries, and various cultural buildings. Originally a prison for nearly six decades, the site was repurposed in 1994 to promote scientific and technological advancement for all ages.

$25

Parque Diversiones

Parque Diversiones is the only traditional amusement park in Costa Rica, established in 1981 to support the local Children's Hospital system. It offers a wide range of rides for both adults and kids, including loop-the-loops, water slides, and vertical drops. The park also features Tio Conejo, a beloved character from Costa Rican folklore. Located just outside of San Jose, it provides various attractions and activities for families to enjoy together.

Free

Our Lady of Solitude Catholic Church

The Iglesia de la Soledad is a church located in the heart of San Jose. This beautiful place of worship offers mass exclusively in Spanish, but they provide programs so that visitors can follow along. The church itself boasts remarkable architecture and holds a significant statue of the Virgin of Solitude atop an airplane propeller, symbolizing the divine protection believed to have been bestowed upon President Kennedy during his visit to Costa Rica in 1963.

Free

Municipal Crafts Market

The Municipal Crafts Market in San Jose is a vibrant indoor market offering a wide array of souvenirs and artisanal crafts. With around 88 stalls, visitors can explore colorful masks, folk art, wood carvings, handmade jewelry, and artwork at reasonable prices. Located near the National Museum, this market is known for its beautiful wood crafts and honesty about product origins. It's a great place to find unique gifts for friends and family while experiencing local artisan culture.

Free

Parque Nacional

Parque Nacional, also known as the National Park, is a historic and park in San Jose, Costa Rica. Established in 1895, it offers a peaceful retreat from the bustling city streets and showcases significant statues such as the National Monument depicting the battles against North American invader William Walker. The park is lined with important landmarks and historical buildings and features a statue of national hero Juan Santamaria.
