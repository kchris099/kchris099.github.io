A Brief History

Costa Rica emerged as a distinct polity after 1821 when the Captaincy General of Guatemala gained independence from Spain, briefly joining the First Mexican Empire before becoming part of the Federal Republic of Central America. Dismembered in 1838, Costa Rica abolished its military in 1948 under President José Figueres Ferrer, redirecting that expenditure toward education, healthcare, and ecological preservation. The country has continued this peaceful tradition for the last three quarters of a century, between Pacific and Caribbean coastlines, supported by agriculture, ecotourism, and a stable democratic tradition that has welcomed millions of international visitors to its national parks and coffee fincas.

Top Sights & Activities

$32

Puentes Colgantes Arenal, Mistico Park

Místico Arenal Hanging Bridges is a nature preserve offering various adventure tours amidst lush surroundings and a scenic lake. The main attraction is the 16 hanging bridges of different lengths, ranging from eight to 98 meters, which take visitors high up into the rainforest canopy for views and wildlife spotting opportunities. Visitors can expect to see colorful birds, spider monkeys, and snakes while crossing these exciting but slightly shaky bridges.

Free

Mirador el Silencio

Mirador el Silencio offers an hiking experience, even during the rainy season when downpours are common. The lush rainforest canopy provides a shield from the rain, allowing you to stay relatively dry as you navigate the trails. While some sections may get slippery after passing the lava falls, overall, it's manageable and enjoyable. consider pack insect repellent for your adventure!.

Free

Free Natural Hot Springs River

The Free Natural Hot Springs River is a publicly accessible stretch of the Tabacon River on the southern flank of Volcan Arenal, heated by the volcano's geothermal activity. The river runs between boulder banks where visitors wade into iron-stained mineral water that reaches bathing temperatures for year-round swimming. Local signage at parking lay-bys marks the path down to the riverbank; bathing here is free while developed hot-spring resorts along the same valley charge for day passes.

$15

Parque Nacional Volcán Arenal

Parque Nacional Volcan Arenal protects Volcan Arenal, a 1,670-metre stratovolcano that returned to active eruption in 1968 after centuries of dormancy. SINAC administers the park, covering lava fields now overtaken by tropical forest, the active cone, and Lake Arenal, the country's largest artificial lake formed in 1979 behind a hydroelectric dam. Travellers pay USD15 at the park entrance, then walk paved trails through secondary-growth forest to viewpoints beneath the summit.

$20

Fortuna Waterfall

Located in the La Fortuna area, Fortuna Waterfall is a 70-meter natural wonder on the Fortuna River. The surrounding tropical forest and crystal-clear waters create an enchanting atmosphere, perfect for swimming and picnicking. Visitors can expect a challenging hike with 500 steps but are rewarded with motivational quotes along the way. The waterfall's rocky surroundings and swimming hole offer opportunities to observe fish, while an orchid path provides a scenic walk.

$5

El Salto (Rope Swing)

El Salto is a swimming and rope-swing spot on the Rio Fortuna downstream of the town of La Fortuna, fed by cool headwaters draining the Arenal volcano flank. The site operates as a private, locally run river park with a lifeguard, wading platforms, a basic changing area, and a small cafe. Travellers reach it by a short drive south of the town centre along the paved road to the Catarata La Fortuna trailhead, paying a USD5 entry fee per person.

$35

Rainforest Chocolate Tour

The Rainforest Chocolate Tour is a working cacao farm and visitor centre on the outskirts of La Fortuna, growing Trinitario cacao hybrids under shade canopy alongside turmeric, banana, and vanilla. The on-site tour covers the bean-to-bar process from fermented pod through roasted nibs, and includes hands-on grinding of cocoa on a traditional metate stone. The site charges USD35 per adult for the 2-hour small-group experience.

$95

Sky Adventures

Sky Adventures is a combined aerial-tram, zipline, and suspension-bridge operator on the eastern flank of the Arenal volcano at El Castillo, established in the early 2000s. The property includes 1.5 kilometres of forested zip lines descending the slopes between the Sky Tram base and the Sky River viewpoint. Travellers pay USD95 per adult for the full Arenal Combo of tram, walk, and seven-stage zipline.

$35

Arenal Observatory Lodge

Arenal Observatory Lodge & Trails is a secluded rainforest retreat with volcano views. The lodge offers a restaurant, bar, spa, and outdoor pool. Guests can enjoy the abundance of wildlife and birds in the area along with great hiking trails. The rooms provide amazing views but are basic in amenities. The friendly staff and cleanliness of the resort are praised by visitors. It's recommended to have an SUV for access and to be prepared for limited dining options nearby.

$30

Don Olivo Chocolate Tour

Don Olivo Chocolate Tour is a family-run cacao farm on the road between La Fortuna and San Carlos, founded by the Salazar-Vargas family and offering guided tours of their wet-mill and drying patios. The visit focuses on the full bean-to-bar process including hand-roasting on a comal, winnowing with traditional baskets, and grinding on a metate. The 90-minute tour charges USD30 per adult.
