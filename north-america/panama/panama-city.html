Panama City

Panama City was founded by the Spanish in 1519 as the first European settlement on the Pacific coast of the Americas. It served as an important transit point for gold and silver from the Inca Empire before being destroyed by pirate Henry Morgan in 1671 and rebuilt at its current location. The completion of the Panama Canal in 1914 transformed the city into a global maritime and financial hub. Today, Panama City contrasts its historic Casco Viejo with a modern skyline of towering skyscrapers.

Albrook Mall

Albrook Mall extends beside Panama City's main intercity bus terminal through long retail corridors marked by animal-themed entrances. Department stores and supermarkets anchor the complex. Restaurants, cinemas and service businesses support residents as well as travelers changing transport nearby. The terminal connection illustrates the Albrook district's growth beyond its former canal-zone functions.

Causeway Amador

A causeway built with rock excavated during Panama Canal construction links the mainland to Naos, Perico, Culebra and Flamenco islands; traffic, construction and private development vary along its length, which is more than one viewpoint or plaza. The Amador route now supports walking, cycling, marinas, restaurants, museums and views toward the canal entrance and city skyline.

Miraflores Visitor Center

Miraflores Visitor Center interprets Panama Canal history and engineering beside the Pacific-side lock complex. Observation areas, film, exhibits, and timed viewing may allow ships to be seen passing through chambers, but transit schedules vary. The center sits near routes to Casco Viejo and the Amador Causeway.

Miraflores Locks

Two-step chambers at Miraflores raise and lower vessels between the Pacific approach and the canal's higher water level; public observation occurs from designated visitor areas, with ship timing dependent on canal traffic. Gates, locomotives or tug assistance, control systems, and constant water management make the locks an operating transport facility.

Pacific Lookout

Mirador del Pacifico forms part of Panama City's Cinta Costera, the public waterfront curving around the bay near Casco Antiguo. Paths and lawns lead to seating facing the Pacific, with the old quarter, modern skyline and marine viaduct visible in one broad view. The lookout provides a resting point along a longer walking and cycling corridor on the reclaimed shoreline. Sea breezes and traffic remain part of the experience.

Large PANAMA letters stand along the Cinta Costera as a municipal photo installation against changing views of the bay and skyline. Their design or position may shift during waterfront works and events. The letters have little independent historical content, while the surrounding promenade supports walking, cycling, recreation, and access between modern districts and Casco Viejo.

Metropolitan Cathedral Basilica of Santa Maria the Ancient

Facing Plaza de la Independencia, Panama City's Metropolitan Cathedral Basilica was built over more than a century and consecrated in 1796. A broad stone facade is flanked by white bell towers decorated with mother-of-pearl, while the interior contains a long nave and side chapels. The cathedral remains the principal church of the archdiocese. Its towers are visible across much of Casco Viejo.

In Casco Viejo, Independence Square faces the cathedral and the National Theatre, where theatrical events continue. Open pavement and colonial facades frame the historic gathering ground. Daytime visitors use the plaza as a hub between church and theatre doors.

Plaza Simón Bolívar

Plaza Simón Bolívar, a historic square in Panama City's Casco Viejo, is a spot filled with cafes and restaurants. Renamed in 1883 to honor the independence hero Simon Bolivar, it offers a bohemian nightlife with live music and dancing at venues like La Casona and Relic Bar. The plaza is surrounded by historic buildings and outdoor restaurants, making it an ideal place for people-watching.

Arco Chato

Spanning roughly fifteen metres without a keystone support, Arco Chato is a flat colonial arch that once belonged to a church later ruined by fire and quakes. The shallow curve still appears in local engineering lore. Stone fabric alone remains of the wider complex.

Plaza de Francia

Plaza de Francia commemorates French efforts on the Panama Canal at the tip of Casco Viejo, with open pavement and surrounding facades. Daylight visits are brief, while nearby rooftop bars and restaurants densify evenings. Obelisks and plaques carry the canal memorial story.

Church of the Mercy

Iglesia de la Merced retains a carved stone facade transferred or rebuilt after Panama City moved to Casco Viejo following the 1671 destruction of Panama Viejo. The church developed through later colonial phases around a nave, chapels, tower, and religious artworks. Active worship continues, while exact fabric dates require caution because relocation and reconstruction histories are complex.

Casco Viejo

Casco Viejo occupies the peninsula where Spanish authorities rebuilt Panama City from 1673 after abandoning Panama Viejo. Churches, plazas, government buildings, houses, defensive traces and later French. Caribbean and republican architecture form the historic district, restoration, tourism, displacement and residential life coexist, making it more than a picturesque collection of cafes and facades.

Built in Casco Viejo for the 500th anniversary of Vasco Núñez de Balboa's Pacific sighting, Plaza V Centenario is a modern paved square among historic streets. Surrounding facades close the space. The commemorative program is clearer than any soft landscaping.

Panamá Viejo

Founded in 1519, Panama Viejo became an early long-standing Spanish city on the Pacific coast of the Americas before attack and fire led to abandonment in 1671. Cathedral tower, convents, bridges, houses, streets, and archaeological remains spread across the original site. Henry Morgan's forces contributed to its fall, but local destruction and defensive decisions complicate a simple pirate-burning narrative.

Parque Omar

Parque Omar converted a former golf course into a large Panama City park with lawns, paths, and the national library on the grounds. Juice bars and small restaurants serve park users, and summer programs include free concerts and art events. The green space offers shade in a dense urban district.

Cerro Ancon Reserve

Ancon Hill rises above Panama City with forest, former Canal Zone roads, communication structures and viewpoints toward the canal, Casco Viejo and modern skyline. Sloths, birds and other wildlife may appear but are never guaranteed. Military and sovereignty history gives the hill national meaning, while access, security, heat and visibility vary along the climb.

Metropolitan Natural Park

Metropolitan Natural Park protects dry and moist tropical forest within Panama City's urban area. Established in 1985, it supports research, environmental education and recreation close to the city center. Marked trails climb through secondary woodland toward overlooks, where birds, monkeys, sloths and dense plant life may appear amid views of the surrounding metropolitan landscape.
