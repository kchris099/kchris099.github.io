Merida

Spaniards founded Mérida in 1542 atop the Maya city of T'hó. The late-nineteenth-century henequen boom lined Paseo de Montejo with Parisian-style mansions. Cathedral and plaza sit on dismantled temple platforms, an explicit colonial overlay on Maya urban ground. Weekly dance and craft nights on the plaza reuse the colonial square for living Yucatecan culture.

Monumento a La Patria

The Monumento a la Patria (Monument to the Fatherland) is a massive, intricately carved stone monument located on the Paseo de Montejo in Mérida. Created by Colombian sculptor Rómulo Rozo in the mid-20th century, the monument depicts key figures and events from Mexican history, from the pre-Columbian era to the modern republic.

Plaza Grande

Plaza Grande, also known as Plaza de la Independencia or Plaza Mayor, is a busy park in Merida. It's a popular gathering spot for locals and tourists alike, offering shaded paths, benches, and regular music and dance events. The plaza is surrounded by important buildings such as the 16th-century Cathedral of San Ildefonso and the Governors Palace, which houses giant paintings depicting Yucatan's history.

03 AdmissionFree

Catedral de San Ildefonso

The Catedral de San Ildefonso, also known as the Merida Cathedral, is a 16th-century cathedral with Renaissance architecture. It was partially constructed using stones from Mayan ruins. The cathedral is located in the main zocalo, surrounded by well-preserved 16th-century mansions and is one of the oldest churches in Latin America.

Dzibilchaltún sits within the Dzibilchaltún area of Merida, documented in local heritage registers for its civic or cultural role. Visitors approach from the main access road and combine the visit with neighbouring heritage buildings and public spaces in the district.

10 AdmissionFree

Cenote Xlacah

Cenote Xlacah is a natural limestone pool located near the ancient Maya archaeological site of Dzibilchaltun, making it a perfect combination of history and natural beauty. This cenote is and deepest in the Yucatan, measuring 320 feet wide and 147 feet deep. It's often referred to as the cenote in Merida due to its proximity, being less than 45 minutes away from the city.

Parque de las Américas

Parque de las Américas is a active urban oasis in Merida, celebrated for its Mayan-inspired outdoor concert space that comes alive with evening dancing and an array of food trucks. This park is a cultural hub, fostering connections among locals and visitors alike. With its recently restored monumental fountain and dedicated children's area, it has become a beloved gathering spot for the community, benefiting over 11,500 residents.
