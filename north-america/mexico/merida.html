Destination Guide — South Mexico

Explore Merida

A Brief History

Built in 1542 directly over the ancient Maya city of T'ho, Merida accumulated extensive wealth during the late 19th-century henequen boom. The resulting Parisian-style mansions along the Paseo de Montejo reflect a golden era that significantly shaped the cultural fabric of the Yucatan. Museums, markets, and street plans in Merida still show layers from those periods.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Monumento a La Patria

The Monumento a la Patria (Monument to the Fatherland) is a massive, intricately carved stone monument located on the Paseo de Montejo in Mérida. Created by Colombian sculptor Rómulo Rozo in the mid-20th century, the monument depicts key figures and events from Mexican history, from the pre-Columbian era to the modern republic.

Free

Plaza Grande

Plaza Grande, also known as Plaza de la Independencia or Plaza Mayor, is a busy park in Merida. It's a popular gathering spot for locals and tourists alike, offering shaded paths, benches, and regular music and dance events. The plaza is surrounded by important buildings such as the 16th-century Cathedral of San Ildefonso and the Governors Palace, which houses giant paintings depicting Yucatan's history.

Free

Catedral de San Ildefonso

The Catedral de San Ildefonso, also known as the Merida Cathedral, is a 16th-century cathedral with Renaissance architecture. It was partially constructed using stones from Mayan ruins. The cathedral is located in the main zocalo, surrounded by well-preserved 16th-century mansions and is one of the oldest churches in Latin America.

Free

Jose Peon Contreras Theater

In the active city of Merida, visitors can immerse themselves in a history that includes not only natural wonders like cenotes and beaches but also an array of artistic experiences. The historic center is brimming with significant landmarks such as the Peon Contreras Theater, which hosts compelling plays and performances by the known Yucatan combination.

Free

Parque de Santa Ana

Parque de Santa Ana is a visitor site in Merida, Mexico. Parque de Santa Ana is a city square that is a hub in Merida, perfect for travelers seeking both relaxation and local culture. Surrounding the park, find the active Santa Ana Mercado, where indulge in authentic Yucatecan street food like tacos and cochinita pibil. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

MX$210

Museo Regional de Antropología, Palacio Cantón

Museo Regional de Antropología, Palacio Cantón is a visitor site in Merida, Mexico. The Regional Museum of Anthropology is housed in the Palacio Cantón, an opulent, early 20th-century mansion located on the Paseo de Montejo in Mérida. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Free

Parque Zoológico del Centenario

Parque Zoológico del Centenario is a destination for families seeking an engaging day out in Mérida. This zoo showcases over 1,000 animals, including both local wildlife and exotic species like tigers and lions. The park is thoughtfully designed with pleasant wooded paths that invite leisurely strolls, alongside playgrounds and picnic areas perfect for relaxation.

Free

Parque Zoológico Animaya

Parque Zoológico Animaya is a unique zoo experience that transports visitors to an environment reminiscent of a safari, featuring spacious habitats designed for the animals to feel at home. This enchanting park is divided into three distinct prairies representing American, Euro-Asian, and African wildlife, all set against the backdrop of an ancient Mayan city reclaimed by nature.

MX$105

Dzibilchaltún

Dzibilchaltún sits within the Dzibilchaltún area of Merida, documented in local heritage registers for its civic or cultural role. Visitors approach from the main access road and combine the visit with neighbouring heritage buildings and public spaces in the district.

Free

Cenote Xlacah

Cenote Xlacah is a natural limestone pool located near the ancient Maya archaeological site of Dzibilchaltun, making it a perfect combination of history and natural beauty. This cenote is and deepest in the Yucatan, measuring 320 feet wide and 147 feet deep. It's often referred to as the cenote in Merida due to its proximity, being less than 45 minutes away from the city.

Free

Parque de las Américas

Parque de las Américas is a active urban oasis in Merida, celebrated for its Mayan-inspired outdoor concert space that comes alive with evening dancing and an array of food trucks. This park is a cultural hub, fostering connections among locals and visitors alike. With its recently restored monumental fountain and dedicated children's area, it has become a beloved gathering spot for the community, benefiting over 11,500 residents.
