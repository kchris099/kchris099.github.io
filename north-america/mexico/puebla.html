Puebla

Puebla was founded in 1531 as a Spanish grid city east of volcan Popocatépetl. Talavera tile facades, cathedral towers, and culinary traditions record viceregal trade between Veracruz port and Mexico City on the central plateau. Cholula pyramid views and Talavera workshops extend viceregal tile craft across the central volcanic belt. Walking routes through the historic centre help visitors relate these monuments to wider patterns of settlement and civic life in Mexico.

Zócalo de Puebla

Puebla's Zocalo is the formal central square framed by the cathedral, portales, and trees where graduates, families, and office workers gather. Historic facades enclose the paved and planted expanse. Most old-town walks begin or end on this plaza, which remains the city's main civic stage for festivals and daily life.

Catedral de Puebla

Sixteenth-century work produced a Herreresque-Renaissance and early Baroque cathedral with multiple chapels and an octagonal altar. Twin towers rise higher than any other church spires in Mexico. Ornate interiors reward slow viewing after the plaza approach.

Africam Safari

Carlos Camacho Espiritu founded Africam Safari in the 1970s across about 500 acres holding more than 450 species. Drivers pass open enclosures of elephants, giraffes, rhinos, and tigers, or join guided tours. Educational stops include animal tracking and giraffe picnic programs.

Estrella de Puebla

Built in the Angelopolis district, Estrella de Puebla operated as a modern observation wheel in the Angelopolis district. Enclosed cabins once provided views across newer urban development toward distant volcanoes in clear weather. An observation wheel in a linear park lifts riders above Puebla's colonial grid, itself a UNESCO World Heritage centre of cobbled streets and preserved facades.

Chapel of the Rosary, St. Dominic’s Temple

Puebla's Capilla del Rosario inside Santo Domingo is a gilded Baroque chapel often called the Golden House, with Talavera tile, onyx, and dense gold leaf over every surface. It opens from the Dominican church nave. Outside Mass visitors file through to see the ornamented dome and altar.

Museo Amparo

Museo Amparo opened in 1991 inside linked colonial buildings to display a private collection of pre-Columbian and colonial art. Galleries move from sculpture and ceramics into later religious works. The restored houses sit in Puebla's historic center within walking distance of the zocalo.

Callejon de los Sapos, the Alley of the Frogs, concentrates antique and craft shops on a sloping street near Puebla's center. Furniture, folk art, and collectibles spill onto sidewalks, especially on weekend market days. Evening bars add another layer of traffic after shops close.

Zona Arqueológica de Cholula

Cholula's archaeological zone is dominated by the Tepanapa pyramid, a vast layered structure that reads as a hill until tunnels reveal its core. Excavated passages allow walks inside the adobe mass. A church on the summit and wide plazas below show the site's long reuse.

Santuario de la Virgen de los Remedios

A Catholic sanctuary crowns the platform of Cholula's Great Pyramid, visually linking colonial devotion with a much older sacred landscape. The present church reflects rebuilding after earthquakes, with domes, towers, tiles and a hilltop atrium. Its position above the archaeological mound shows how Spanish religious construction occupied a place already prominent in Indigenous ritual geography. The summit opens wide views across Cholula and Puebla.

Parian market

El Parian fills a small market court beside Puebla's Barrio del Artista, continuing a commercial tradition linked to the former San Roque marketplace. Rows of permanent stalls concentrate regional crafts, especially Talavera ceramics, embroidered textiles and leatherwork. Arcaded colonial buildings and narrow passages give the market a clear relationship to the historic center's long-standing exchange of goods and artisan production.

Teatro Principal is a gem located Puebla, showing the city's local history. This 18th-century theater is a evidence of antique architecture, making it one of Latin America's most venues. Visitors can immerse themselves in a variety of performances, from popular Mexican plays to concerts that matter to history and artistry.

Paseo San Francisco

Paseo San Francisco runs as a leafy historic walkway through central Puebla, lined with gardens and long-established restaurants. Local food history ties the corridor to chalupas, the small fried tortillas topped with salsa and meat that many Puebla kitchens still treat as a house specialty. Evening crowds fill the benches and terraces along the paseo.

Templo de Santa María Tonantzintla

Indigenous artisans transformed the interior of Santa Maria Tonantzintla into a dense field of stucco saints, angels, fruits, flowers, feathers, and local faces. Successive seventeenth- and eighteenth-century work combined Catholic iconography with Nahua visual language. The comparatively restrained exterior heightens the contrast, while active parish worship continues beneath the elaborate vaults.

Zoo Parque Loro Puebla

Parque Loro is a privately managed zoological park near Puebla with compact paths linking enclosures for birds, mammals and reptiles. The collection began with a strong emphasis on parrots and later expanded to other species. Shaded grounds, interpretive signs and close-set exhibits create a relatively short circuit, while the park's educational programs focus on animal care and public awareness.

Teleférico Puebla

A short cable-car line crosses part of Puebla's Forts district between stations near the Zaragoza exhibition area and the historic hill. Gondolas provide elevated views over the city, forts, stadiums and volcanoes when weather is clear. Historic Zone of the Forts

The hill northeast of Puebla's center contains Fort Loreto, Fort Guadalupe, museums, monuments, lawns, paths and civic venues associated with the Battle of Puebla on 5 May 1862. Later redevelopment created a broad cultural and recreation district around the military sites. The forts themselves require historical attention.

San Pedro Cholula's center joins the main plaza, parish church, former Franciscan convent, municipal buildings, portals, markets and streets leading toward the Great Pyramid. Convento de San Gabriel Arcángel stands in Cholula as a large Franciscan foundation with a fortress-like atrium and multiple chapels. Cholula Parrish of Saint Andrew

San Andres Cholula's parish church anchors the town center with a broad atrium, towers, religious art, and spaces used for Mass and feast-day processions. Construction and later alterations reflect the colonial evangelization of a much older Indigenous settlement. It is distinct from the sanctuary atop the Great Pyramid and from neighboring San Pedro Cholula's separate parish institutions.
