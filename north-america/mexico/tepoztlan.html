Explore Tepoztlan

A Brief History

Tepoztlán was founded as a Nahua altepetl on the northern flank of the Sierra de Tepozteco at least 1,500 years ago, with the Tepozteco pyramid at the cliff summit recorded in Codex Xolotl as a 13th-century Mexica cult shrine to Ometochtli. Spanish Dominicans founded the Ex-Convento de la Natividad on the central plaza in 1555, and the village passed through 17th-century hacienda consolidation and 19th-century liberal reform with the convent's 1862 reinstallation as a parish church. Twentieth-century Bohemian and muralist artist communities settled in the village from the 1920s, leaving behind the cooperative market and the cable car to the Tepozteco cliff.

Top Sights & Activities

MX$105

Tepozteco Pyramid

The Tepozteco Pyramid is an Aztec-period ceremonial platform built in the 15th century at 2,100 metres elevation on the cliff ridge of Cerro del Tepozteco above Tepoztlan, dedicated to Ometochtli Tepoztecatl, the Nahua god of pulque. The site consists of a single step pyramid, an enclosed platform, and an open-air altar, reached via a 2-kilometre uphill footpath from the end of Calle del Tepozteco. Travellers pay MXN210 at the INAH ticket booth.

Free

Tepoztlan Town Market

The Tepoztlan Town Market operates on weekends and select weekdays in the central plaza adjoining the Ex-Convento de la Natividad, where village artisans sell itacates (herb-flecked corn masa cakes), native amaranth candy, and dyed textiles. The market expanded in the 1970s alongside the growth of Mexico City weekend tourism into the Morelos highlands. The market is freely accessible, with food tastings sold by individual vendors on the north side of the plaza.

Free

Ex-Convento de la Natividad

The Ex-Convento de la Natividad is a 16th-century Dominican convent on the central plaza of Tepoztlan, founded in 1555 on the foundations of the Mexica-Tlatelolca ceremonial precinct. The complex retains the original painted plateresque entryway, the double-arcaded two-storey cloister, and the adjacent open chapel built into the convent wall. Travellers pay MXN70 at the INAH ticket booth and walk through the cloister and refectory.
