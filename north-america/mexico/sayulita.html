Sayulita

Sayulita began as a small fishing village on Nayarit’s Pacific coast within the Riviera Nayarit stretch north of Puerto Vallarta. Surf breaks drawn to international attention from the late 20th century brought guesthouses and galleries, while fishing pangas still work the bay. Cobblestone streets and a beach plaza organise the compact town. Growth remains tied to coastal tourism rather than heavy industry or colonial monuments.

Sayulita Plaza

Sayulita's central plaza is a compact civic space between the parish church and streets leading toward the beach. Shade trees, benches and a small bandstand provide a meeting place for residents throughout the day, while public celebrations periodically extend into the surrounding blocks. Shops and food businesses face the square, but its main role remains that of an everyday social center for the town.

Playa De Los Muertos

A cemetery separates Playa de los Muertos from central Sayulita, giving the small cove its name. A short path leads past decorated graves to sand enclosed by rocky headlands. Playa De Los Muertos, also known as the Beach of the Dead, is located just south of Sayulita's main beach.

03 AdmissionFree

Mercado del Pueblo

Mercado del Pueblo, also known as Sayulita's Farmer's Market, has been showing the wonders of the region since 2009. This outdoor market operates from November through May and is a hub for local farmers and families offering high-quality natural products. Travellers can explore a variety of stalls featuring organic produce, artisan goods, and even enjoy live music while sampling delicious treats.

20.8690° N / 105.4378° W

04 AdmissionFree

Sayulita Beach

Sayulita's main beach fronts the town with surf schools, fishing boats, vendors, restaurants and dense seasonal crowds. Waves attract beginning and experienced surfers under changing conditions, while river runoff and water quality require attention after rain.

20.8698° N / 105.4408° W

05 AdmissionFree

Playa Carrecitos

Playa Carricitos lies west of Sayulita beyond a hilly approach through tropical vegetation. The small Pacific cove has limited built facilities, dark sand and an exposed shoreline where waves can be stronger than at the town's main beach. Its relative separation from roads and businesses creates a quieter coastal setting, while the surrounding headlands frame open views across the ocean.

20.8688° N / 105.4541° W
