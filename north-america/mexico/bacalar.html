Bacalar

Maya settlement preceded the Spanish fort built in the eighteenth century to resist pirates on the lagoon known as the Lake of Seven Colours. Fuerte San Felipe and the bacalar shoreline record a military outpost on the Yucatán's eastern edge rather than a henequen boom town. Pirate lore and cenote swimming now share the lagoon the fort was built to watch.

Canal de los Piratas

The Canal de los Piratas (Pirates' Channel) is a narrow, shallow waterway connecting the Bacalar Lagoon to the Hondo River. Historically used by pirates to access the town, the channel is now a popular tourist destination known for its striking, multi-toned blue waters and mineral-rich mud, which visitors often apply to their skin for its purported exfoliating properties.

Balneario Ejidal Mágico Bacalar

Balneario Ejidal Magico Bacalar occupies a community-managed stretch of the lagoon's western shore. Wooden docks and palapa-covered areas provide direct contact with the shallow, clear water, while simple recreation structures and food facilities support family outings. The site reflects the ejido's role in managing public leisure beside a lagoon increasingly surrounded by private tourism development.

03 AdmissionMX$75

Fuerte de San Felipe de Bacalar

Fuerte de San Felipe de Bacalar, built in 1729 from volcanic rock, marine stone, and limestone, guarded the town against Caribbean pirates and European raiders. Thick walls and corner bastions enclose a compact courtyard museum. The fort anchors Bacalar's colonial shoreline above the lagoon.

18.6770° N / 88.3885° W

04 AdmissionFree

Letras Bacalar

The 'Letras Bacalar' is a large, colorful, freestanding sign spelling out the name of the town, located prominently in the central plaza. Decorated with active motifs representing the local culture and natural environment, the sign is a typical photo opportunity for tourists visiting the Pueblo Mágico, symbolizing their arrival at the famous lagoon destination.

18.6768° N / 88.3891° W

05 AdmissionFree

Zócalo

Bacalar's Zocalo, or Plaza Principal, is the town's central square for markets, civic events, and evening gatherings. Benches and shade trees face municipal buildings and church fronts. Visitors use it as an orientation point between lagoon piers and inland streets.

18.6771° N / 88.3897° W

06 AdmissionFree

Laguna de los 7 colores

Laguna de los Siete Colores shifts through blues and turquoise where depth changes over a white limestone bed. Swimmers, kayakers, and boat tours use public access points along Bacalar's shore. The long lagoon defines the town's identity more than any single building.

18.6745° N / 88.3890° W

07 AdmissionFree

Balneario Municipal El Aserradero

Balneario Municipal El Aserradero provides public lagoon access near central Bacalar through wooden platforms, steps, shaded structures and simple seating beside the water. The municipal site supports swimming and short visits without the extensive services of a private beach club. Water conditions, maintenance and local rules affect how the access area operates.

18.6724° N / 88.3902° W

DayTour Bacalar

Guided boat and pontoon trips with DayTour Bacalar cross Bacalar Lagoon toward Cenote Negro, the Pirates' Channel, and Bird Island. Commentary stresses lagoon ecology and regional history rather than speed thrills alone. Groups leave from shore bases for a structured circuit of the lagoon's named spots.

09 AdmissionFree

Cenote Esmeralda

Boat routes often pass the opening, but depth figures and swimming conditions require reliable local guidance. A dark circular patch within Bacalar Lagoon marks Cenote Esmeralda, where the limestone floor drops below surrounding shallow water. The cenote is part of the connected lagoon system.

18.6555° N / 88.4051° W

10 AdmissionMX$25

Cenote Azul

Cenote Azul near Bacalar is a deep sinkhole lake on the Yucatan limestone shelf, with clear water reported around ninety meters in places. Platforms and swimming areas rim the open cenote. It sits a short trip from the seven-color lagoon and draws day visitors between both waters.
