Bacalar

Maya settlement preceded the Spanish fort built in the eighteenth century to resist pirates on the lagoon known as the Lake of Seven Colours. Fuerte San Felipe and the bacalar shoreline record a military outpost on the Yucatán's eastern edge rather than a henequen boom town. Pirate lore and cenote swimming now share the lagoon the fort was built to watch.

Canal de los Piratas

The Canal de los Piratas (Pirates' Channel) is a narrow, shallow waterway connecting the Bacalar Lagoon to the Hondo River. Historically used by pirates to access the town, the channel is now a popular tourist destination known for its striking, multi-toned blue waters and mineral-rich mud, which visitors often apply to their skin for its purported exfoliating properties.

Balneario Ejidal Mágico Bacalar is a popular, community-run public beach club situated on the shores of the Bacalar Lagoon. It offers accessible facilities, including wooden docks, water slides, and palapas for shade. The site provides an affordable and family-friendly environment for swimming and enjoying the dramatic, clear waters of the 'Lagoon of Seven Colors.'.

Fuerte de San Felipe de Bacalar

Fuerte de San Felipe de Bacalar is a visitor site in Bacalar, Mexico. Construction and early use date to around 1729. Fuerte de San Felipe de Bacalar is a sturdy stone fort built in 1729 to protect the town from Caribbean pirates. Constructed with volcanic and marine rocks as well as limestone, it is a evidence of the town's history and resilience against Dutch, French, and English invaders. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Cenote Esmeralda

Cenote Esmeralda is one of the deepest cenotes located within the Bacalar Lagoon. Known for its intense, dark blue waters, it creates a striking contrast with the surrounding shallow, turquoise lagoon. The cenote is a popular stop for boat tours, offering visitors a unique chance to swim and observe the dramatic underwater drop-off.
