Palenque

Palenque was a major Maya city-state that flourished in the seventh century under the rule of K'inich Janaab' Pakal. It is known for its sophisticated architectural design and extensive hieroglyphic inscriptions, which have provided important insights into Maya history and mythology. The city was abandoned in the eighth century and reclaimed by the jungle until its excavation in the modern era. Today, Palenque is a UNESCO World Heritage site, celebrated for its elegant temples and detailed stucco reliefs.

Zona Arqueológica Palenque

Palenque's excavated core reveals temples, palaces, tombs, aqueducts, plazas, and inscriptions from a major Classic Maya city set against Chiapas rainforest. The Temple of the Inscriptions and palace tower anchor the public route, while most of the ancient settlement remains unexcavated beneath vegetation.

Palenque Site Museum "Alberto Ruz Lhuillier"

Named for the archaeologist who identified Pakal's tomb, the Alberto Ruz Lhuillier Site Museum interprets Palenque through sculpture, ceramics, inscriptions, funerary objects, architectural fragments and reconstructions. Galleries explain the ancient city's urban development, royal court, ritual life and household areas. The museum stands near the archaeological entrance and complements the exposed temples with objects removed for conservation.

Parque Central Palenque

Parque Central in Palenque town is the main plaza for evening social life, with food stalls and benches facing civic buildings. Travelers based here for the Maya ruins use it as a meeting point after day trips. Music and street vendors pick up after sunset heat fades.

04 AdmissionFree

Cabeza Maya

The Cabeza Maya is a prominent, large-scale sculpture of a Mayan head located at a major traffic roundabout in the modern town of Palenque. It is a welcoming landmark for visitors arriving to explore the nearby archaeological site, symbolizing the region's deep connection to its indigenous heritage and history.

17.5084° N / 91.9897° W

Aluxes Ecopark

Aluxes Ecopark near Palenque offers forest trails, swimming areas, and wildlife viewing as a nature stop beside trips to the Maya ruins. Paths run through humid lowland vegetation typical of Chiapas. It provides a green alternative to spending every hour on the archaeological site.
