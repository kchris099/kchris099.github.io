Campeche

Founded by Spanish conquistadors in 1540 over a pre-existing Maya settlement, Campeche became the principal port of the Yucatán Peninsula, exporting precious dyewood and gold. Its wealth attracted relentless attacks from notorious Caribbean pirates, prompting the Spanish Crown to construct a massive hexagonal defensive wall and a system of forts in the late seventeenth century. The city's colonial prosperity and military history are beautifully preserved in the pastel-colored mansions of the historic center, the Fuerte de San Miguel, and the surviving sea gates.

Independence Square

Plaza de la Independencia frames a central garden and traditional kiosk inside Campeche's colonial core. Surrounding facades stay carefully kept, giving the square a neat civic character. Paths and shade trees support everyday sitting and evening paseos.

02 AdmissionFree

Puerta de Mar

Puerta de Mar is one of the two main entrances to the walled city of Campeche, featuring a backdrop for restaurants and cafes. The historic center is adorned with pastel-facade houses, creating a colorful and atmosphere. Visitors can walk along the wall with a museum ticket and explore the old Spanish forts while learning about the city's pirate history.

19.8458° N / 90.5385° W

03 AdmissionFree

Malecón de Campeche

Campeche's malecon follows the Gulf shoreline beyond the fortified historic center. A broad promenade combines walking and cycling routes with monuments, seating and open views across the water. Repeated rebuilding has extended the public waterfront and created a popular evening gathering place, while its exposed coastal position contrasts with the enclosed streets and defensive walls of the old city.

19.8479° N / 90.5398° W

04 AdmissionFree

Letras Campeche

Large freestanding letters spelling Campeche provide a municipal photo point along the waterfront. The installation has no independent historical significance; its context comes from the Gulf promenade, nearby monuments, and views back toward the state capital's modern shoreline.

19.8475° N / 90.5395° W

Puerta de Tierra

Puerta de Tierra, also known as the Land Gate, was constructed in 1732 and used to be the main entrance to the city. Although it's no longer the primary gate to Campeche, it remains a landmark. This unique attraction is an original fortified bastion with a former city gate within surviving and restored city walls. It resembles structures found in Spain or Portugal, adding to its appeal.

19.8414° N / 90.5360° W

06 AdmissionFree

Baluarte de San Francisco

Baluarte de San Francisco is one of the defensive bastions that formed part of the fortifications safeguarding Campeche during its colonial era. In the 17th century, this busy port city faced threats from pirates and privateers, prompting the construction of walls, bastions, and watchtowers for protection. Today, visitors can ascend to the top of Baluarte de San Francisco for views overlooking the city and the Gulf of Mexico.

19.8416° N / 90.5353° W

07 AdmissionFree

Baluarte de San Pedro

Baluarte de San Pedro formed part of Campeche's late seventeenth- and early eighteenth-century defensive circuit against maritime attack. Thick walls, gun positions, a small courtyard, and connections to surviving wall segments show the scale of the fortified city.

19.8431° N / 90.5326° W

08 AdmissionMX$100

Campeche Archaeological Museum, Fort San Miguel

On Campeche's western edge, Fort San Miguel holds the city's archaeological museum inside bastioned walls. Galleries display Maya material from the Campeche coast on posted hours. The fort setting ties museum rooms to Spanish coastal defense architecture.

19.8247° N / 90.5687° W

09 AdmissionMX$200

Playa Bonita

Shingle sand, simple cabanas, and calm swimming water define Playa Bonita outside Campeche's fortified centre. Paddleboards, kayaks, and windsurf gear appear when wind and swell allow. Access comes on foot from roadside parking and beachfront paths along the Gulf shore.

19.7936° N / 90.6217° W

10 AdmissionMX$150 (performance ticket)

Teatro de la Ciudad Francisco de Paula Toro

Francisco de Paula Toro Theatre opened in the 1830s as Campeche's principal performance house, using a neoclassical facade, horseshoe-shaped auditorium, boxes and balconies. Restoration has preserved its civic cultural role for theater, music, dance and ceremonies. Interior access normally depends on performances or organized visits and sightlines vary according to the historic seating plan.

19.8455° N / 90.5347° W
