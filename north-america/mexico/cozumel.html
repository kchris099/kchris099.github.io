Cozumel

Maya pilgrims crossed to Cozumel for the Ixchel sanctuary before epidemics emptied much of the island after contact. Mid-twentieth-century scuba tourism, catalysed by visiting divers and cruise docks, rebuilt the economy around reefs. San Gervasio's ruins and the waterfront together mark sacred island and modern resort. Maya coastal shrines at El Cedral add inland ritual sites beyond the beach hotels.

Coral Reefs Monument

The Coral Reefs Monument is a prominent sculpture located on the waterfront promenade (malecón) of San Miguel de Cozumel. It depicts a pair of scuba divers exploring a coral reef, celebrating the island's global reputation as a premier diving destination. The monument is a popular meeting point and photo opportunity for visitors.

Cozumel Musem

The Museo de la Isla de Cozumel occupies a waterfront building with exhibits on the island's geology, Maya pilgrimage role, and later tourism economy. Natural history and cultural rooms sit above the malecón. It offers a compact overview before or after reef and beach time.

03 AdmissionFree

Atlantis Submarines Cozumel

Atlantis Submarines offers a unique underwater excursion in Cozumel, taking passengers up to 100 feet below the surface in a real, Coast Guard-certified submarine. The tour provides an accessible way for non-divers to experience the active marine life and coral formations of the Chankanaab reef system, including a sunken shipwreck.

04 AdmissionFree

Dolphinaris Cozumel

Dolphinaris Cozumel is a commercial marine-animal interaction facility near San Miguel. Scheduled programs take place in enclosed seawater pools, with support buildings and viewing areas arranged around the shoreline complex. Managed encounters with captive dolphins define the venue's role within Cozumel's tourism industry.

Zona Arqueológica San Gervasio

Roads, platforms and small temples remain among the forested ruins of San Gervasio in Cozumel's interior. Construction and occupation continued across several periods of Maya history, leaving building groups spread over a broad area. The site is traditionally associated with devotion to the goddess Ix Chel. Marked paths connect its arches, plazas and principal architectural remains.

20.5002° N / 86.8466° W

06 AdmissionFree

Isla de la Pasión

A narrow island and sandbar off northern Cozumel form Isla de la Pasion, reached mainly by boat through coastal lagoons and mangroves. Privately managed beach facilities and excursions occupy parts of the shore, separate from the San Gervasio archaeological zone. Shallow water, sand and wetland vegetation define a landscape shaped by both coastal ecology and organized recreation. Mangroves shelter parts of the approach from open water.

07 AdmissionFree

El Mirador

Roadside pullouts allow short walks around the formations, but strong waves and sharp stone make the shoreline unsuitable for casual swimming; vendors and temporary facilities may appear, while the natural coastal geology remains the stable feature. On Cozumel's exposed eastern coast, El Mirador centers on wave-cut limestone arches, rough rock shelves, and views of Caribbean surf.

20.3173° N / 86.9294° W

08 AdmissionFree

Paradise Beach Cozumel

Paradise Beach is a southwest Cozumel shoreline stop with pale sand, shallow swimming water, and beach-club facilities. Palapas and lounge areas sit behind the tide line facing the reef channel. Access runs from the coastal road and beachfront paths used by day visitors.

09 AdmissionFree

Playa Uvas

Playa Uvas is a private beach club on Cozumel's western shore, where a narrow waterfront area provides access to the Caribbean. Shoreline seating, food service, snorkeling and kayaking are organized as commercial activities around nearby reef habitat. The site differs from an open public beach because facilities and marine excursions are managed by a single operator within a defined coastal property.

20.4475° N / 86.9904° W

Chankanaab Beach Adventure Park

Inside Cozumel's National Marine Park, Chankanaab combines a protected snorkeling lagoon with botanical gardens, a crocodile exhibit, and a replica Maya village. Family programs introduce island reefs and local history in one paid park day. Shore facilities sit beside the clear inlet that gives the site its name.
