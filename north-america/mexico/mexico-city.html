Mexico City

Mexico City stands on the site of Tenochtitlan, the Aztec capital conquered in 1521 and rebuilt by Spanish planners around the Zócalo. As capital of New Spain and later independent Mexico, it absorbed baroque churches, 19th-century reforms, and rapid 20th-century growth across the Valley of Mexico. Chapultepec, the historic centre, and surrounding boroughs record layered indigenous, colonial, and modern civic life.

Monument to the Revolution

A glass elevator climbs about 57 metres to an observation deck atop the Monumento a la Revolucion, looking out over Mexico City's broad avenues. The memorial occupies an open plaza with Art Deco massing. Plaza space below holds civic gatherings around the copper dome.

Alameda Central

Alameda Central began in the sixteenth century and developed into Mexico City's historic major public park, and repeated redesign has altered the landscape, while protests, performances, commuting and leisure keep it part of daily urban life. Formal paths, fountains, statues, trees and open plazas stretch beside Palacio de Bellas Artes at the edge of the historic center.

Mexico City Metropolitan Cathedral

Mexico City's Metropolitan Cathedral developed over nearly three centuries on the north side of the Zocalo, above part of the sacred precinct of Tenochtitlan. Gothic, Renaissance, Baroque, Churrigueresque and neoclassical phases appear across its chapels, altars, towers and facades. Arena México

Arena México, located in the Colonia Doctores neighborhood, is a known indoor arena that has witnessed legendary battles from the days of El Santo and Blue Demon to boxing matches during the 1968 Olympic Games. It is considered a cathedral of competition and hosts lucha libre shows promoted by Consejo Mundial de Lucha Libre. The venue attracts both locals and tourists who line up on Tuesdays, Fridays, and Sundays to witness the magic of professional wrestling.
