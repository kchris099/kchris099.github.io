Oaxaca

Oaxaca developed around a Zapotec and Mixtec hinterland that supplied Monte Albán and Mitla centres. Spanish colonial churches, Zócalo arcades, and nearby archaeological zones record how indigenous polities persisted under viceregal rule in southern Mexico. Monte Albán ridge temples and mezcal valleys extend Zapotec heritage beyond the colonial city grid.

Mercado Benito Juárez

Under cover at Mercado Benito Juarez, stalls sell produce, prepared foods, crafts, textiles, and folkloric fabrics in central Oaxaca. Next door, Mercado 20 de Noviembre adds huaraches and embroidered blouses. Shoppers move between the twin halls for both groceries and gifts.

Mercado 20 de Noviembre

Fresh produce, baked goods, and street-food counters fill Mercado 20 de Noviembre beside stalls for hand-crafted huaraches and embroidered blouses. Indigenous vendors and Spanish speakers share the covered aisles. The market's food halls draw midday crowds for grilled meats and local sides.

Zona Arqueológica de Monte Albán

Monte Alban crowns a leveled ridge above the Oaxaca Valley as a major Zapotec capital with plazas, ballcourt, and palace platforms. Stone monuments and tombs open across the hilltop acropolis. Views drop to surrounding mountains that once framed the ancient city's territory.

Templo de Santo Domingo de Guzmán

Oaxaca's Dominican complex combines the Church of Santo Domingo with former monastic rooms now housing the Museum of the Cultures of Oaxaca. A restrained stone facade opens into a richly gilded Baroque interior, while cloisters contain archaeological and historical collections.

Andador Turístico

Craft shops, galleries, and cafes pouring Oaxacan coffee line the pedestrian Andador Turistico through Centro. Street art and church facades break up the retail frontage for walkers. Slow pacing and people-watching define use here more than vehicle traffic.

06 AdmissionFree

TEATRO MACEDONIO ALCALÁ

Teatro Macedonio Alcalá is an elegant early-1900s building that houses a theater hosting performing arts and cultural events. The construction of this French-style structure began in 1903 and was completed in August 1909, later being inaugurated on September 5 of the same year. Formerly known by different names, it was eventually named after Oaxacan musician and composer Macedonian Alcala.

07 AdmissionFree

Catedral Metropolitana de Oaxaca Nuestra Señora de la Asunción

Repeated earthquakes shaped Oaxaca Cathedral, whose construction began in the sixteenth century and continued through major rebuilding into the eighteenth. A broad Baroque facade, twin towers, chapels, paintings, and a spacious nave face the Alameda beside the Zocalo; the building remains the seat of the archdiocese, making worship and structural conservation central to its present use.

17.0616° N / 96.7253° W

08 AdmissionFree

Fuente de las 8 Regiones

The Fuente de las 8 Regiones in Oaxaca is aesthetically pleasing, although it suffers from widespread graffiti. Despite its visual appeal, the fountain can be somewhat unease-inducing at night as it becomes a makeshift shelter for those awaiting near the hospital. However, there are advantages to visiting this location, such as observing the traditional attire of people gathered around the fountain and easy access to numerous public buses in proximity.

17.0824° N / 96.7189° W

09 AdmissionFree

Plaza de la Danza

Built in 1959 from local volcanic stone near Cerro del Fortin, Plaza de la Danza hosts art shows, rallies, concerts, and traditional dance. During Dia de Muertos, large sand designs carpet the square. The hard plaza surface favours performance over quiet lawns.

17.0636° N / 96.7297° W

10 AdmissionFree

Mercado de La Merced

Mercado de La Merced in Oaxaca sells rural produce at the doors and, inside, masa for tamales, fresh cheese, oregano, tasajo, and many styles of local chocolate. Vendors from surrounding towns supply the stalls. Cooking shoppers treat it as a practical pantry rather than a souvenir market.
