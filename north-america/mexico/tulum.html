Tulum

Tulum was one of the last cities built and inhabited by the Maya, peaking between the thirteenth and fifteenth centuries. Uniquely situated on coastal cliffs overlooking the Caribbean Sea, it served as a major trading port for obsidian and jade. The city survived the initial Spanish conquest but was abandoned by the late sixteenth century. Today, Tulum is known for its well-preserved archaeological ruins and its transition into a popular eco-tourism destination.

El Castillo

El Castillo (The Castle) is the largest and most prominent structure within the Tulum archaeological site. Perched on a 12-meter limestone cliff above the Caribbean Sea, it served as both a temple and a navigational lighthouse for Mayan trading canoes navigating the treacherous coastal reef system.

Playa Pescadores

Playa Pescadores is a, public beach located just south of the Tulum ruins. It is characterized by its soft white sand, calm turquoise waters, and the presence of numerous small, colorful fishing boats. The beach offers a more relaxed, traditional atmosphere compared to the upscale beach clubs further south, and provides excellent views of the ruins.
