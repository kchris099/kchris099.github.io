Tulum

Tulum was one of the last cities built and inhabited by the Maya, peaking between the thirteenth and fifteenth centuries. Uniquely situated on coastal cliffs overlooking the Caribbean Sea, it served as a major trading port for obsidian and jade. The city survived the initial Spanish conquest but was abandoned by the late sixteenth century. Today, Tulum is known for its well-preserved archaeological ruins and its transition into a popular eco-tourism destination.

Tulum

Tulum is a world-known destination on the Riviera Maya, known for well-preserved Mayan ruins situated on a dramatic cliff overlooking the Caribbean Sea. The area has evolved into a major tourist hub, offering pristine white-sand beaches, boutique eco-resorts, and a active dining and nightlife scene, while maintaining a bohemian, wellness-focused atmosphere.

El Castillo

El Castillo rises near the Caribbean cliff edge inside Tulum's walled Maya city, combining temple architecture with a commanding position over coastal trade routes. Construction phases altered the building, whose doorways, serpent imagery, and platform face the ceremonial precinct.

03 AdmissionFree

Playa Pescadores

Playa Pescadores is a, public beach located just south of the Tulum ruins. It is characterized by its soft white sand, calm turquoise waters, and the presence of numerous small, colorful fishing boats. The beach offers a more relaxed, traditional atmosphere compared to the upscale beach clubs further south, and provides excellent views of the ruins.

20.2055° N / 87.4309° W

Laguna de Kaan Luum

Kaan Luum forms a wide circle of pale turquoise water in the forest south of Tulum. Most of the lagoon is shallow, but a deep central cenote appears as a sharply defined dark blue area that reveals the submerged change in depth. Wooden piers and simple shoreline structures frame the open water, creating a clear visual contrast between the calm outer lagoon and its geological center.

Casa Tortuga Tulum

Four distinct cenotes form Casa Tortuga's park network, mixing open-air pools with cavernous chambers. Guided tours take swimmers and snorkelers through clear fresh water and stalactite formations. The layout aims at an accessible full-day cenote circuit near Tulum.

Yax-Muul

Near Tulum, Yax-Muul is a privately managed cenote and adventure site in the Tulum region, offering guided access to underground water and limestone caves through organized packages. Equipment, routes, swimming, rappelling and ceremonial elements vary by operator.

Gran Cenote

Gran Cenote near Tulum holds clear freshwater suited to snorkeling, with small fish and turtles often visible among submerged roots and rock. Wooden docks and paths lead to open swimming areas and shaded rest spots. The cenote sits inland from the beach strip among low jungle.

Zacil-Ha is a small open cenote northwest of Tulum with clear water, platforms, steps and privately managed visitor facilities. Depth, jumping rules, accommodation and opening status change, making direct operator information necessary for practical planning.

Cenote Santa Cruz Tulum

Cenote Santa Cruz Tulum is a freshwater swimming cenote on the Tulum corridor, reached through a small park setting with changing areas and deck access to the water. Limestone walls and vegetation frame the pool. Visitors come mainly to swim and cool off away from the Caribbean shore.

Escultura Ven a la Luz

The 'Ven a la Luz' (Come into the Light) sculpture is a massive, well known wooden artwork created by South African artist Daniel Popper. Originally part of an art festival, the sculpture depicts a woman opening her chest to reveal a lush, green interior. It has become a photographed landmarks in Tulum's hotel zone.
