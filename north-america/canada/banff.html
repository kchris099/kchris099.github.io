Banff

Established in the 1880s following the discovery of hot springs by railway workers, Banff became Canada's first national park. The arrival of the Canadian Pacific Railway drove early tourism, transforming a rugged wilderness camp into an internationally known alpine destination. Visitors today can clearly trace the detailed layers of this unique heritage throughout the preserved streets and monuments.

Cave and Basin National Historic Site

Railway workers in 1883 found the Cave and Basin hot springs that later sparked Canada's national parks system. Exhibits and guided cave tours, including lantern walks, interpret that origin story. The Banff site ties thermal water to the parks idea itself.

Banff Gondola

Enclosed gondola cabins climb from the edge of Banff townsite to the upper station on Sulphur Mountain. The summit complex includes indoor exhibits, viewing terraces, restaurants and a boardwalk leading toward the historic weather station at Sanson's Peak; the route opens across the Bow Valley and surrounding ranges, though cloud, wind and wildfire smoke can limit visibility.

Banff Upper Hot Springs

Discovered in the late 1880s, Banff Upper Hot Springs sit above 5,000 feet in the Rockies with mineral pools and mountain views from the decks. Bathers use the historic thermal water for rest after park days. Elevation and pool decks define the classic Banff soak.

Bow Falls Viewpoint

A trail from the south base of a pedestrian bridge follows the Bow River through forest with benches to Bow Falls Viewpoint, a cascade once featured in 1950s films. Easy walking suits a short Banff outing. River and mountain walls frame the fall below.

Surprise Corner Viewpoint

Rounding the bend at Surprise Corner suddenly opens the 1888 Fairmont Banff Springs Hotel and Sulphur Mountain in one frame. The unexpected reveal gives the viewpoint its name. No ticket desk stands between the roadside pull-off and the classic hotel vista.

Mount Norquay

Mount Norquay rises immediately northwest of Banff and has operated as a ski area since the 1920s. Winter lifts serve downhill terrain of varying difficulty, while summer programs may include a sightseeing chairlift and guided via ferrata routes on the cliffs. The mountain's position gives broad views toward Banff, Mount Rundle, and the Bow Valley without leaving the national park's developed corridor.

51.2053° N / 115.6068° W

Cascade of Time Garden

Cascade of Time Garden sits a short walk from downtown Banff, terraced with flower beds facing Cascade Mountain. Paths and lawns frame seasonal blooms against the Bow Valley peaks. Laid out in the 1930s civic era, the garden works as a formal floral overlook rather than a wilderness trailhead.

Banff Visitor Centre

The center occupies a historic stone-and-timber building on Banff Avenue near the town's main services. When exploring the landscapes of Banff National Park, a visit to the Banff Visitor Centre is an essential stop. Located Banff village on Banff Avenue, this hub is staffed by knowledgeable representatives from various local organizations, including Parks Canada and Friends of Banff.

Banff Town Sign

The Banff Town Sign is a popular, rustic wooden landmark located at the entrance to the town of Banff. Featuring the town's name carved in a distinctive, park-style font, it is a welcoming photo opportunity for visitors entering the national park. The sign is set against a backdrop of dense pine forests and the dramatic peaks of the Canadian Rockies.

Hoodoos Viewpoint

Hoodoos Viewpoint offers a vantage point of the Bow River Valley and Mount Rundle, with the added bonus of potentially spotting wildlife like elk crossing the river below. The Surprise Corner to Hoodoos Trail is an easy hike that leads to this viewpoint, passing by the Banff Springs Hotel along the way. There are two scenic trails leading to the Hoodoos, which are unique rock formations offering views of the surrounding area.
