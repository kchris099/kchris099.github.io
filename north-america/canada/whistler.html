Whistler

The Whistler area was historically inhabited by the Squamish and Lil'wat First Nations. European settlement began in the early twentieth century, primarily focused on logging and trapping. The region's change into a resort destination started in the 1960s with the development of Whistler Mountain for skiing. It gained global prominence after co-hosting the 2010 Winter Olympics, solidifying its status as a premier year-round alpine resort.

Whistler Blackcomb

Whistler Blackcomb is a visitor site in Whistler, Canada. Whistler Blackcomb is a world-known alpine resort located in British Columbia, consistently ranked among the top ski destinations globally. The resort offers over 8,000 acres of skiable terrain in the winter and transitions into a premier destination for mountain biking and hiking in the summer. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Squamish Lil'wat Cultural Centre

The Squamish Lil'wat Cultural Centre in Whistler is a collaborative facility dedicated to sharing the history, art, and culture of the local Squamish and Lil'wat First Nations. The striking building, designed to reflect traditional architectural styles, houses extensive exhibits, a theater, and a cafe. Visitors can participate in guided tours, craft workshops, and cultural performances that offer deep insights into indigenous heritage.

Whistler Sliding Centre

The Whistler Sliding Centre, operated by Whistler Sport Legacies, is a recent facility that hosted the bobsleigh, skeleton, and luge events during the 2010 Winter Olympics. It has the world's fastest ice track at nearly 1.5 kilometers in length. Visitors can tour the modern venue or partake in thrilling passenger bobsleigh and skeleton rides, reaching speeds of up to 78mph (125kph).

Located at the edge of the forest near Lost Lake in Whistler, Scandinave Spa offers a Nordic-inspired wellness experience.
