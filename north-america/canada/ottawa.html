Ottawa

Ottawa was chosen as Canada's capital by Queen Victoria in 1857 for its location between English-speaking Ontario and French-speaking Quebec along the Ottawa River. Parliament Hill Gothic revival, Rideau Canal locks, and ByWard Market sheds record how lumber rafting, canal defence planning after the War of 1812, and federal civil-service growth shaped a bilingual government city.

Parliament Hill

Parliament Hill is a majestic neo-Gothic complex that is the Canada's legislature, surrounded by beautifully landscaped grounds and adorned with artworks. This site is not only a hub for political activity but also holds significant historical importance, having been the backdrop for important moments in LGBTQ+ rights in Canada, including the first gay protest in 1971 and landmark legal battles.

Centennial Flame

The Centennial Flame, located on Parliament Hill in Ottawa, is a significant landmark dedicated in 1967 to commemorate Canada's 100th anniversary of confederation. It has become a popular photo spot for visitors, with the Centre Block and Peace Tower providing a backdrop. While it often draws crowds, lucky visitors may find moments to capture the flame without too many strangers in the frame.

The National War Memorial

The National War Memorial is a monument in Ottawa, Canada. Construction and early use date to around 1939. The National War Memorial, located in Confederation Square, is a significant tribute to the Canadian Armed Forces. Originally built in 1939 to honor those who died in World War I, it has since been rededicated to commemorate all Canadians lost in conflicts past and future. The memorial stands in an open plaza or roadside setting.

Rideau Canal, Locks 1 - 8 - Ottawa

Rideau Canal, Locks 1 - 8 in Ottawa is a historic site with manually operated locks dating back to the 19th century. The canal is popular for kayaking, cycling tours, and camping. The Bytown Museum near the last lock tells fascinating stories about Ottawa's history and the construction of the canal supervised by Colonel John By. A Celtic cross commemorates the workers who lost their lives during its construction.

The Ottawa sign in the ByWard Market district marks a pedestrian plaza beside historic produce halls and weekend vendor stalls. The illuminated sign is a common photo stop for visitors exploring Lower Town.
