Explore Toronto

A Brief History

Toronto began as the British town of York on Lake Ontario and became the capital of Ontario after Confederation in 1867. Victorian brick districts, the CN Tower, and waterfront renewal record waves of British, Irish, Italian, Caribbean, and Asian immigration. The city is Canada's largest metropolitan centre and financial capital. Municipal archives, parish records, and surviving architecture help explain how the city connected to wider regional trade routes, court patronage, and modern civic rebuilding after industrial and wartime change.

The Distillery Historic District

The Distillery Historic District in Toronto is a active area that has transformed from a former distillery into a collection of boutiques, galleries, cafes, and restaurants. The pedestrian-only neighborhood has buildings dating back to the 1830s and was once home to the largest distillery in Canada, Gooderham & Worts.

C$47

CN Tower

The CN Tower is a landmark in Toronto, standing at over 553 meters tall. It's known for its glass floor and revolving eatery offering panoramic views of the city. Visitors can also experience EdgeWalk, a thrilling hands-free walk around the outside ledge of the tower. As one of the Seven Wonders of the Modern World, it's a Canadian icon and symbol of Toronto.

C$40

Ripley's Aquarium of Canada

Ripley's Aquarium of Canada, located in downtown Toronto near the Rogers Centre and CN Tower, is a modern and expansive indoor aquarium showing thousands of marine species. From barracudas to rays, jellyfish, octopi, moray eels, and giant sharks, the aquarium offers a diverse range of sea creatures. Visitors can enjoy educational behind-the-scenes tours, interactive displays, and even in-water experiences with Cownose and Southern Stingrays.

C$30

Rogers Centre

Rogers Centre is a visitor site in Toronto, Canada. Records associated with the site include 1989. Rogers Centre, formerly known as the SkyDome, is a versatile indoor stadium that has been a prominent has of Toronto's entertainment scene since its opening in 1989. It is most famous for being the home of the Toronto Blue Jays baseball team and for its innovative retractable roof. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Evergreen Brick Works

Evergreen Brick Works is a visitor site in Toronto, Canada. It was originally the Don Valley brickworks, which played an important role in building the city. After its closure in the 1980s, efforts began to transform the site into an ecological center and social enterprise. The main structures and grounds are the principal points of interest.

Toronto City Hall

Toronto City Hall, designed by Finnish architect Viljo Revell, is a modern and structure that is the mayor's office. It has gained popularity as a filming location for various productions, including Star Trek: The New Generation and The Handmaid's Tale. The building, which opened in 1965, is a favorite among locals and visitors alike. Its adjacent Nathan Philips Square hosts art fairs, special events, and a popular skating rink in winter.

Toronto Old City Hall

Toronto Old City Hall, constructed in 1899, is a Richardsonian Romanesque civic building with a notable clock tower and detailed gargoyles. Designed by local architect Edward James Lennox, it has a Romanesque Revival style featuring arches, towers, and carvings. Once the largest civic building in North America, it remains an emblematic construction in the city's landscape.

Free

Fort York National Historic Site

Located in Toronto, Fort York National Historic Site is a 43-acre landmark that has a glimpse into Canada's rich history. With original War of 1812 buildings and the 1813 battle site, this well-preserved fort provides an immersive experience. From dramatic reenactments to an extensive collection of artifacts, Fort York tells the story of its important role during the war.

Massey Hall

Massey Hall, a historic music venue in Toronto, has been hosting legendary shows for over a century. Originally gifted to the city by the Massey family in 1894, it continues to fulfill its mission of showing international arts and entertainment while supporting Canadian talent. With known acoustics and a capacity of 2753 seats, Massey Hall hosts not only concerts but also lectures, film premieres, comedy shows, and more.

Michael's Cathedral Basilica, constructed in 1848, is a Catholic cathedral in the Gothic Revival style. Little Canada

Little Canada is a world-known theme park that careful recreates the natural wonders and cities of Canada on a 1:87 scale. It's not just for kids - the whole family can enjoy workshops, video rooms, and detailed models of Canadian landmarks like churches, stadiums, mountains, and skyscrapers. The attention to detail is astounding; from Little Ottawa's parliament buildings to the miniature Bay of Fundy with its own tide schedule.

St. Lawrence Market

Lawrence Market is a known foodie destination located in Toronto's historic Old Town district. It has over 100 vendors offering a wide variety of products, including produce, antiques, baked goods, and meats. The market dates back to 1803 and is considered the oldest in Toronto. Visitors can indulge in local favorites such as peameal bacon sandwiches at Carousel Bakery or explore European-style pastries at Future Bakery.

Legislative Assembly of Ontario

The Legislative Assembly of Ontario is situated in the Ontario Legislative Building, at the Queen's Park. This historic building, designed by Richard A. Waite in the Richardsonian Romanesque style, was opened in 1893 after six years of construction. It houses various government offices and is a venue for heated debates and discussions among provincial parliament members. The building itself is made of pink granite and sandstone, showing its architecture.

Roy Thomson Hall is a visitor site in Toronto, Canada. Roy Thomson Hall, a concert venue in Toronto, is celebrated for its acoustics and intimate atmosphere. Casa Loma

Casa Loma, located about 10 minutes north of Toronto's city center, is a castle spanning 64,700 square feet. Constructed in 1914 by Canadian financier Sir Henry Pallatt, the castle showcases his love for European art and Gothic revival architecture. Visitors can explore the lavishly adorned halls and rooms, stroll through the terrace and gardens, and admire the opulent bedrooms and libraries.

The Humber Bay Arch Bridge, a modern and structure in Toronto, is an essential part of the Martin Goodman Trail. Its distinctive bow-shaped arch makes it visually appealing and offers views of the Toronto skyline and Lake Ontario. This popular spot is frequented by cyclists, runners, and walkers who come to capture the sunset or enjoy the contemporary architecture that complements the cityscape.

High Park Zoo

High Park Zoo, situated within Toronto's expansive High Park, is a free-admission zoo that provides an intimate wildlife experience. Visitors can encounter various animal species from around the world, including bison, llamas, capybaras, peacocks, reindeer, highland cattle, wallabies, emus and sheep. The zoo offers fenced-off grassy areas where kids can pet animals like llamas and rabbits.

Ireland Park

Ireland Park is a public park in Toronto, Canada. Ireland Park, located along the waterfront, is a small park that pays tribute to Irish immigration. It tells the story of 38,000 Irish immigrants who sought refuge in Canada during the Great Potato Famine. A limestone wall imported from Ireland bears the names of those who perished during the ship crossings. Paths, lawns, and shaded areas make up the main public facilities.

Toronto Music Garden

Toronto Music Garden is a public park in Toronto, Canada. Designed by cellist Yo-Yo Ma and landscape architect Julie Moir Messervy, this urban oasis is inspired by Johann Sebastian Bach's Cello Suite No. The garden is divided into sections representing the suite's six dance movements, creating a unique and harmonious green space. Paths, lawns, and shaded areas make up the main public facilities.

Toronto Islands

Toronto Islands, a cluster of 13 small islands in Lake Ontario, are easily accessible from downtown Toronto via a short ferry ride. The main islands for visitors are Centre Island, Wards Island, and Hanlan Point. Visitors can enjoy various recreational activities such as walking or biking along the bridges and pathways that connect the islands. There are also swimming beaches, boat rentals, and dining options available on the islands.
