Toronto

Toronto began as the British town of York on Lake Ontario and became the capital of Ontario after Confederation in 1867. Victorian brick districts, the CN Tower, and waterfront renewal record waves of British, Irish, Italian, Caribbean, and Asian immigration. The city is Canada's largest metropolitan centre and financial capital. Municipal archives, parish records, and surviving architecture help explain how the city connected to wider regional trade routes, court patronage, and modern civic rebuilding after industrial and wartime change.

The Distillery Historic District

Brick warehouses and stone lanes preserve the scale of Gooderham and Worts' former nineteenth-century distillery east of downtown Toronto; industrial architecture remains the defining element despite the area's conversion to commercial and cultural use. Production ended in the late twentieth century and the pedestrian district now contains galleries, theaters, shops, restaurants, studios and event spaces.

CN Tower

The CN Tower rises more than 553 metres above downtown Toronto, with glass-floor sections and a revolving restaurant on the main pod. EdgeWalk sends harnessed visitors around an outdoor ledge higher up the shaft. Built for communications and tourism, the tower remains the city's vertical landmark on the waterfront skyline.

Toronto Old City Hall

E. J. Lennox designed Toronto's Old City Hall, completed in 1899 with heavy sandstone walls, arches, carved details, and a central clock tower. The Richardsonian Romanesque building originally contained municipal offices and courts; court functions continued after the new city hall opened. Exterior carvings and the urban relationship with Nathan Phillips Square remain its most accessible elements.

Fort York National Historic Site

Located in Toronto, Fort York National Historic Site is a 43-acre landmark that has a glimpse into Canada's rich history. With original War of 1812 buildings and the 1813 battle site, this well-preserved fort provides an immersive experience. From dramatic reenactments to an extensive collection of artifacts, Fort York tells the story of its important role during the war.

On Toronto's waterfront, Ireland Park remembers the roughly 38,000 Irish immigrants who reached Canada during the Great Potato Famine. A limestone wall brought from Ireland lists names of people who died on the crossings. The small park keeps paths and open ground beside the harbour, tying the memorial to the same shoreline where ships once arrived.

Cellist Yo-Yo Ma and landscape architect Julie Moir Messervy shaped the Toronto Music Garden after Bach's Cello Suite No. 1. Toronto Islands

A short ferry ride crosses Toronto Harbour to a chain of low islands formed from shifting sand and later engineering. Beaches, lagoons, paths, picnic areas, homes; a small amusement park and open views of the skyline spread across Centre, Ward's and Hanlan's areas. Service frequency, flooding, storms and seasonal closures affect access to different parts of the islands.
