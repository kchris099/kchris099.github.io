Victoria

The Hudson's Bay Company opened Fort Victoria in 1843; the Fraser Canyon gold rush of 1858 turned it into a boom port. Chosen as British Columbia's capital, the city kept an Edwardian Inner Harbour of parliament buildings and steamship docks that reflect administrative rather than industrial primacy on Vancouver Island. Chinatown and the Songhees shoreline add layers the fort era did not advertise.

Cadboro-Gyro Park

Cadboro-Gyro Park fronts a sandy beach and calm water suited to swimming and sunbathing in Victoria. The playground mixes climbing forms shaped like sea monsters and a giant octopus with open grass for games. Paths and shade break up the lawns, so families can move between sand, play structures, and quiet sitting spots.

Legislative Assembly of British Columbia

British Columbia's Parliament Buildings occupy the Inner Harbour waterfront in Victoria, with stone domes, lawns, and the ceremonial front facing the harbour. Guided tours explain the legislative chambers, while the grounds and fountain remain open as a civic park when the house is not in session.

Black Ball Ferry Line

The Black Ball Ferry Line operates the MV Coho, providing a vital and scenic maritime link between Victoria, British Columbia, and Port Angeles, Washington. The 90-minute crossing traverses the Strait of Juan de Fuca, offering passengers dramatic views of the Olympic Peninsula and the rugged coastline of Vancouver Island. It has been a reliable transportation service since 1959.

Victoria Bug Zoo

The Victoria Bug Zoo keeps live insects, arachnids and other terrestrial invertebrates in a compact downtown facility. Exhibits explain anatomy, behavior, camouflage and ecological roles through species such as walking sticks, mantises, beetles, scorpions and tarantulas. Staff interpretation may include controlled handling, depending on the animal and operating conditions. The collection emphasizes small animals often overlooked elsewhere.

Christ Church Cathedral

A large Gothic Revival cathedral occupies a downtown Victoria block and stays open for daily tours. Services run seven days a week, with Sunday times commonly listed at 8:00 am, 9:15 am, 11:00 am, and 4:00 pm. Outside those hours the exterior and main interior spaces usually remain viewable for people walking through the nave.

Mile Zero Monument

The Mile Zero Monument in Victoria marks the western terminus of the Trans-Canada Highway, which stretches over 8,000 kilometers across the country to St. John's, Newfoundland. Located in landmark Hill Park overlooking the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the monument is a popular photo spot and a symbol of the vast geographic expanse of the Canadian nation.

Beacon Hill Children's Farm

Beacon Hill Children's Farm occupies paddocks and small barns within Victoria's Beacon Hill Park. Goats, sheep, pigs, birds and other domestic animals introduce farm life at close range, and the seasonal goat run has become a familiar part of the operation. The modest facility is oriented toward families and complements the park's gardens, ponds and walking paths.

Beacon Hill Park

Victoria's main waterfront park at the city's southern edge sits near Mile Zero of the Trans-Canada Highway. Ponds, cricket grounds, and Garry oak meadows share space with shore paths facing the Strait of Juan de Fuca. Formal planting mixes with open grass used daily by walkers and cyclists along the coastal lawns.

Ogden Point Sundial

The Ogden Point Sundial is a prominent public art piece and functional timepiece located at the Ogden Point Breakwater in Victoria. Set against the backdrop of the busy cruise ship terminal and the Strait of Juan de Fuca, the sundial is a popular point of interest for walkers enjoying the scenic breakwater path, offering a blend of maritime history and coastal beauty.

Craigdarroch Castle

Coal baron Robert Dunsmuir commissioned Craigdarroch Castle in the 1880s as a family mansion overlooking Victoria. Stained glass, carved wood, tiled fireplaces, period furnishings and a complex stair rise through four levels of the Romanesque Revival house. Later uses as a hospital, college and conservatory preceded restoration as a museum of wealth and domestic life in late Victorian British Columbia.
