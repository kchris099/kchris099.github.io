Santo Domingo

Santo Domingo's Zona Colonial contains the Americas' oldest cathedral and university foundations laid by Spanish settlers after 1496. Ozama fortress, Alcázar de Colón, and cobbled streets record early Caribbean colonisation on the Ozama River. Malecón sea walls and Ozama river parks extend colonial plazas along the Caribbean capital waterfront. Walking routes through the historic centre help visitors relate these monuments to wider patterns of settlement and civic life in Dominican Republic.

Columbus Park

A bronze statue of Christopher Columbus stands at the center of Parque Colón in Santo Domingo's Colonial City. The square developed beside the sixteenth-century cathedral and is framed by historic houses, cafes, cultural buildings and pedestrian streets; shade trees, benches, performers, pigeons and evening gatherings make it an active urban plaza.

Nuestra Señora de la Encarnación

Construction of Santo Domingo's Cathedral of Santa María la Menor began in the early sixteenth century near Parque Colón; as an early cathedral established in the Americas, it became a model for colonial church organization across the region. Coral limestone walls, ribbed vaults, side chapels, tombs, paintings, and a fortified exterior combine Gothic and Renaissance elements adapted to the Caribbean.

Santo Domingo Malecon

The Malecon follows Santo Domingo's Caribbean coast west of the Colonial City as a broad seafront avenue, and hotels and government buildings face sections of parkland and seawall, while monuments and restaurants appear at intervals along the route. Heavy traffic makes it an important transport corridor, and rough water leaves much of the exposed coast unsuitable for ordinary swimming.

Santo Domingo Obelisk

The Santo Domingo Obelisk, a towering monument standing 131 feet high, was constructed in 1937 to commemorate the change of name from Santo Domingo to Ciudad Trujillo under the rule of dictator Rafael Leonidas Trujillo. Situated on Avenida George Washington, Malecon in Santo Domingo, Distrito Nacional, Dominican Republic, this historical landmark was specifically designed in 1936 to ensure its visibility from any point along the known Malecon Avenue.

National Palace of the Dominican Republic

The National Palace has housed the Dominican Republic's executive branch since its completion in 1947. A large dome rises above symmetrical wings containing offices, reception halls and ceremonial staircases. Formal gardens and guarded entrances reinforce its role as a working seat of government, while public views concentrate on the exterior and surrounding avenues.
