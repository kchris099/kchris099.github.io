Paraty

Gold from Minas Gerais left Brazil through Paraty's harbour in the colonial period. When railways bypassed the town, its cobbled streets and whitewashed churches escaped nineteenth-century rebuilds. That accidental survival, not imperial patronage, made Paraty a coastal heritage centre on the Costa Verde. Carnival and literary festivals later reused the same colonial streets without widening them.

Cachoeira da Pedra Branca

Two cascades descend over broad stone shelves into pools at Cachoeira da Pedra Branca in the rural interior of Paraty. Forest and exposed rock surround the water, reached by road and a short approach through privately managed land; current flow, slippery surfaces, and local access arrangements matter more than fixed swimming conditions, especially after heavy rain.

Cachoeira do Tobogã

Water slides down a smooth rock face at Cachoeira do Tobogã near Penha, forming a natural chute above a pool, and skilled local practitioners sometimes descend standing, while most people watch from the bank or enter more cautiously. The waterfall shares the area with Poço do Tarzan, forest paths, bridges, and roadside access from the route inland from Paraty.

Trinity Beach Paraty

Year-round shoreline at Trinity Beach near Paraty links to adjacent beachfront districts by coastal road or boardwalk. Walkers combine the sand with harbour areas and seaside markets nearby. Access stays on foot from nearby roads and beachfront paths.

Cepilho beach

Cepilho Beach, also known as Praia de Fora or Praia dos Ranchos, is a secluded spot with calm waters, making it perfect for a relaxing family day out. It's located near Trindade village and can be reached by an easily accessible trail. The beach offers sands and is ideal for body surfing or swimming in slightly rougher waters. While the town is dedicated to beaches, it can get crowded during summer and weekends.

Pedra que Engole

Pedra que Engole sits inside the Cairucu protected area near Paraty as a rock formation visited on short hikes. The name refers to a cleft or opening in the stone that local routes highlight. Forest paths and humid Atlantic vegetation frame the stop on nature circuits inland from town. Humidity and insects are part of the short forest approach in warmer months.

Lula Beach

Praia da Lula is a small bay visited by excursion boats from Paraty. Forest reaches close to the pale sand, and sheltered water generally supports swimming and short snorkeling stops when sea conditions permit. Boat schedules can make the beach busy at midday, while independent land access is limited by the surrounding coast and private property.

Paraty

Paraty preserves a colonial port town between the Serra do Mar and an island-filled bay on Rio de Janeiro state's Costa Verde; stone streets, whitewashed houses, churches and former warehouses reflect the gold trade and later coffee economy. The wider historic landscape also includes Atlantic Forest, traditional communities, mountains and coastal waters beyond the compact historic center.

Igreja de Nossa Senhora do Rosário e São Benedito

This Paraty church dedicated to Our Lady of the Rosary and Saint Benedict has a simple colonial facade and modest interior. Built for brotherhoods of African descent, it stands apart from the larger matrix church. Whitewashed walls and a quiet square suit a short visit in the historic center. A small churchyard offers shade after the climb from the waterfront lanes.

23.2188° S / 44.7141° W

09 AdmissionFree (guided tour R$60)

Centro Histórico de Paraty

Paraty's historic center keeps pedestrian cobblestone streets and low Portuguese colonial houses. High tides sometimes flood the stone lanes, a quirk of the coastal plan. White churches and colored window frames define the district that draws overnight visitors to the Costa Verde. Horse carts and pedestrians share the stone streets closed to most cars.

23.2198° S / 44.7156° W

10 AdmissionFree

Praia do Pontal

Pontal Beach begins just across the river from Paraty's historic center and follows a tree-lined urban shore. Kiosks, guesthouses, boat services, and simple restaurants face the bay, while the water is usually calmer than the open ocean; its convenience and views back toward town are stronger attractions than pristine sand or consistently clear swimming conditions.
