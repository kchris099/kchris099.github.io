Rio de Janeiro

Rio de Janeiro served as Brazil's capital from 1763 until 1960, when government offices moved to Brasília. Founded around Guanabara Bay, it expanded through sugar, gold, and later coffee wealth into colonial churches, imperial palaces, and planned beachfront districts. Christ the Redeemer, Sugarloaf Mountain, and neighbourhoods from Centro to Copacabana mark Rio's role as a port city, imperial seat, and cultural capital.

Maracanã

Maracanã opened for the 1950 World Cup and became closely associated with Brazilian football through national-team matches, club rivalries and major finals. Rebuilding before the 2014 World Cup reduced and modernized the vast bowl while retaining its circular profile; the stadium also hosted Olympic ceremonies and concerts and its museum areas interpret players, crowds and landmark games.

Sugarloaf Mountain rises 396 metres at the entrance to Guanabara Bay, where steep granite slopes meet the Urca peninsula. The Pedra da Gávea trail climbs through Tijuca National Park to the summit of a granite monolith rising 844 metres above Rio's western beaches. From the plateau, views stretch across Barra da Tijuca, São Conrado, and the Atlantic coastline.
