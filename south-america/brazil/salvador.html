Salvador

Salvador was Brazil's first colonial capital from 1549, channelling sugar wealth through Pelourinho's baroque churches and port warehouses. African diaspora culture in Candomblé and cuisine records how the Bahian coast shaped Atlantic slavery and Brazilian identity. Elevador Lacerda viewpoints and candomblé terreiros extend Pelourinho heritage above Todos os Santos Bay.

Fonte Nova Arena

Itaipava Fonte Nova Arena, originally built for the Confederations Cup in 2013, is now the home ground for Bahia and a venue for concerts. The stadium, with a capacity of 50,000 people, was completely rebuilt by German architects and hosted key matches in the 2014 FIFA World Cup. It offers guided tours that provide insights into its history and architecture.

Monumento as Meninas do Brasil (Gordinhas)

Eliana Kertesz created the three bronze figures titled As Meninas do Brasil for Salvador's Ondina waterfront. Installed beside a coastal avenue, the sculptures have become public art and a neighborhood meeting point. Their rounded female forms refer to Indigenous, European and African ancestry within the city's population. The waterfront placement keeps the work in daily public view.

Elevador Lacerda

An early hydraulic lift opened in 1873, and later rebuilding produced the tall Art Deco tower and enclosed bridge of Salvador's Elevador Lacerda. Short rides connect Cidade Baixa with the upper historic center near Praça Municipal. Windows and nearby terraces open views toward Mercado Modelo, the port and the Bay of All Saints.

Cathedral of Salvador

Salvador Cathedral began as the church of the Jesuit college and was completed in the seventeenth century on Terreiro de Jesus, and after the earlier cathedral was demolished, the former Jesuit church became the seat of the archdiocese. A stone facade from Portugal fronts a Mannerist interior with side chapels, carved altars, paintings, and a deep central nave.
