Lima

Lima was founded by Francisco Pizarro in 1535 on the Rimac Valley coast as capital of Spanish Peru. Colonial churches, balconied mansions, and the Plaza Mayor anchored viceregal power before independence in 1821. Earthquakes and republican growth reshaped the centre while suburbs spread along the Pacific. Museums, convents, and coastal archaeological sites trace Lima's role as Peru's administrative and cultural capital for nearly five centuries.

Lima Main Square

Lima Main Square, also known as Plaza de Armas, is the historic Lima and a must-visit for anyone exploring this active city. Established in 1535, it is the birthplace of Lima and is surrounded by architectural landmarks like the Government Palace, Lima Cathedral, and Archbishop's Palace.

Palacio Municipal de Lima

Lima's municipal palace faces the Plaza Mayor on a site used by city government since the Spanish foundation. The present twentieth-century neo-colonial building contains council chambers, offices, ceremonial rooms, balconies, and an important library; official functions and security limit interior access, while the facade participates in a deliberately reconstructed colonial civic ensemble.

Government Palace of Peru

Also called the House of Pizarro, the Government Palace occupies the north side of Lima's Plaza de Armas, once Francisco Pizarro's residence and later the seat of governors, viceroys, and presidents. Walls and courtyards appear on permitted routes when open. The facade closes the square's ceremonial edge.

Casa de Aliaga

Descendants of Jeronimo de Aliaga have occupied a residence beside Lima's government palace since the sixteenth century, though repeated remodeling transformed the original structure. Courtyards, salons, tilework, furniture, art, and carved wood reflect several colonial and republican periods. Visits are generally guided or arranged, as the property remains a private family house.

Named for songwriter Chabuca Granda, the alameda follows the Rímac with boardwalk space for fairs, dance, comedy, and concerts. A red central sculpture marks a viewpoint over the historic city. Performances and river walks share the same linear park.

Basilica and Convent of Santo Domingo, Lima

Dominicans established this Lima convent in the sixteenth century, creating cloisters, chapels, tilework, library spaces and a prominent bell tower near Plaza Mayor. The complex is associated with Saint Rose of Lima, Saint Martin de Porres and Saint John Macias through burial and devotional history. Active religious life continues alongside guided access to selected rooms and courtyards.

Museo del Convento de San Francisco y Catacumbas

San Francisco's monastery museum combines cloisters, a library, religious paintings, tilework, chapels and burial crypts beneath central Lima. The so-called catacombs contain human remains arranged through later museum practice, and the collections reflect Peruvian colonial history. Guided routes and conservation rules determine movement through the underground spaces and historic rooms. The underground route is only one part of the monastery.

Archdiocesan Shrine of the Heart of Jesus - San Pedro

Jesuits built San Pedro Church in central Lima with a richly ornamented colonial Baroque interior, multiple chapels, paintings, carved altars, and a broad nave. The dedication and later title of Archdiocesan Shrine of the Sacred Heart reflect continuing Catholic use. Services and religious events shape entry, while the small square outside belongs to the surrounding historic district.

San Martin Square

In Lima's UNESCO Historic Centre, San Martín Square centres on an equestrian monument to José de San Martín, the Protector of Peru in the independence wars. Open pavement and surrounding facades frame the gathering space. Civic memory and everyday foot traffic share the plaza.

Iglesia Las Nazarenas

A mural of the crucified Christ survived earthquakes and became the focus of devotion at Las Nazarenas, rebuilt in the eighteenth century around a community with Afro-Peruvian roots. Each October, processions carry a replica of the Lord of Miracles through Lima. The sanctuary, convent, purple habits, music, and offerings form a living tradition beyond the architecture alone.

Quinta Heeren

Built in the nineteenth century for German merchant Otto Heeren in Barranco, Quinta Heeren later became a cultural centre with wooden balconies, garden patios, and period rooms. Exhibitions and concerts fill the house beside the Bajada de Baños. The fabric recalls Barranco's seaside elite era.

Bridge of Sighs

A wooden bridge crosses the Bajada de Banos ravine in Barranco, linking streets above a shaded path that descends toward the coast. Built in the nineteenth century and repeatedly repaired, the Bridge of Sighs sits among houses, cafes and public art. Local breath-holding stories belong to folklore, while the structure itself offers a close neighborhood view.

A black-and-white iron lighthouse stands in Miraflores' Parque del Faro above the Pacific cliffs. It was relocated from another coastal site in the twentieth century. In Pueblo Libre, Park of the Legends combines zoo collections with archaeological displays across three zones echoing Peru's coastal, highland, and jungle regions. Magic Water Circuit of the Reserve Park

Thirteen fountains occupy Parque de la Reserva in Lima's Magic Water Circuit, combining jets, tunnels, interactive water, lighting, music and scheduled evening projection shows. The park itself dates from the 1920s, while the fountain attraction opened much later. Programs, maintenance, weather and crowd controls affect operation, so not every feature runs continuously.

Fortress of the real felipe

Spanish authorities built Real Felipe Fortress in the eighteenth century to defend Callao's port after earthquakes and pirate threats. Massive walls and bastions enclose courtyards, towers and underground passages. Military collections interpret royalist, republican and modern Peruvian use of the complex, which remains under army control and preserves the strategic scale of colonial coastal defense.

John F. Kennedy park

Kennedy Park, located in Miraflores, is a city park honoring President John F. Kennedy. It is known as parks in Lima and offers views, pleasant green spaces, and leafy trees for visitors to enjoy while strolling through its winding paths. The park is also home to over 100 stray cats protected by the Municipality of Miraflores and has its own organization dedicated to their well-being.

Surquillo Market No. 1 supplies households, cooks and restaurants near Miraflores. Seafood and meat counters operate beside vendors of potatoes, herbs and tropical fruit.
