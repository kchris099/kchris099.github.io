Chan Chan

Chan Chan was the vast capital of the Chimú Kingdom, flourishing from the ninth to the fifteenth century before falling to the expanding Inca Empire. As the largest earthen city in the pre-Columbian Americas, it housed an detailed society of artisans, administrators, and kings within a network of massive walled citadels. The city's prosperity relied on an advanced irrigation system that transformed the desert valley into a fertile oasis. Today, visitors navigate the large adobe walls, detailed geometric reliefs, and ceremonial plazas of the Nik An complex.

PLAZA DE ARMAS

Trujillo's Plaza de Armas forms the civic center of the modern city near the major archaeological zone to the west. The Freedom Monument occupies its broad paved center. Around the square stand the cathedral, municipal buildings and colorful colonial or republican facades. Ceremonies and daily pedestrian activity continue at a place associated with the city's declaration of independence in December 1820.

02 AdmissionFree

Plazuela El Recreo

Plazuela El Recreo is a smaller public square east of Trujillo's main Plaza de Armas. Trees, pedestrian paving and colonial facades surround a marble fountain brought from Europe, while remains of an older water system add a less visible layer to the site. Shaded seating and neighborhood movement give the plazuela an everyday civic role within the historic street grid.

8.1070° S / 79.0237° W

Trujillo Cathedral Basilica

Trujillo Cathedral faces the city's Plaza de Armas with a bright yellow facade, twin towers, chapels, altars, paintings, and an attached museum. Earthquakes destroyed earlier buildings, and the present church developed mainly from seventeenth-century rebuilding with later changes.

8.1108° S / 79.0283° W

04 AdmissionFree

Real Plaza Trujillo

Brand stores and a large grocery anchor Real Plaza Trujillo's retail floors, with a food court for post-shopping meals. Open mall courts and storefronts organize family visits. The complex works as a modern shopping centre rather than a historic plaza.

8.1311° S / 79.0329° W

Trujillo Botanical Garden

Trujillo Botanical Garden provides an urban setting for plant education, recreation and small-scale conservation. Paths wind through collections of ornamental, medicinal and regional plants, with ponds and shaded areas breaking up the grounds. Managed by the municipality, the garden offers a quieter counterpoint to Trujillo's dense streets and helps explain plant use and cultivation along Peru's northern coast.

06 AdmissionPEN 10

La Huaca de la Luna

Moche builders raised Huaca de la Luna as a platform for worship and sacrifice near Trujillo, part of a landscape that later included Chimú capitals as well. Painted adobe reliefs survive on excavated walls. The pyramid pairs with Huaca del Sol across the plain.

8.1349° S / 78.9902° W

07 AdmissionPEN 10

La Huaca del Sol

Huaca del Sol is a vast adobe platform in the Moche Valley, built from millions of mud bricks as a political and ceremonial center. Spanish colonial looting and river diversion destroyed much of its mass, while excavation has focused more heavily on neighboring Huaca de la Luna. The complex belongs to Moche culture and predates the later Chimu capital near Trujillo.

8.1318° S / 78.9946° W

08 AdmissionPEN 10

Museo Huacas de Moche

The Museo Huacas de Moche stands beside Huaca del Sol and Huaca de la Luna in the Moche Valley outside Trujillo, and its galleries interpret the neighboring Moche temples, which predate and remain distinct from the later Chimu archaeological city. Ceramics, metalwork, textiles, models and excavation finds explain ritual practice, craft production and urban life.

8.1418° S / 78.9921° W

09 AdmissionPEN 10

Chan Chan

Vast adobe compounds with carved reliefs make Chan Chan a major pre-Columbian city about ten minutes from Huanchaco. Visits often combine the site with Huacas del Sol y de la Luna and Huaca de la Brujo. Surfers base nearby for Huanchaco waves after ruin walks.

8.1086° S / 79.0753° W

10 AdmissionPEN 10

Huaca Esmeralda

Huaca Esmeralda preserves a Chimu adobe platform within modern Trujillo, decorated with reliefs of fishing nets, animals and geometric patterns around courtyards and ramps. The monument probably related to the wider political territory of the Chimu capital but stands outside its main walled compounds. Urban surroundings and fragile earthen walls require managed access and careful conservation.

8.1046° S / 79.0451° W
