Trujillo

Trujillo was founded by Spanish conquistador Diego de Almagro in 1534 and named after Francisco Pizarro's birthplace. Before Spanish arrival, the region was the center of the Moche and Chimu cultures, home to the massive adobe city of Chan Chan and the Huacas del Sol y de la Luna. During the colonial era, Trujillo became a wealthy administrative center, known for its elegant mansions and churches. Today, it is celebrated for its rich archaeological heritage and active marinera dance traditions.

PLAZA DE ARMAS

Trujillo's Plaza de Armas marks the Spanish colonial center and the site where independence was proclaimed in December 1820. The Freedom Monument by Edmund Moeller occupies the middle, surrounded by cathedral, municipal buildings and colorful mansions; ceremonies, protests, traffic controls and public gathering continue, while the exact 1534 foundation date requires historical caution.

Plazuela El Recreo

Colonial and republican facades, trees, shops, and benches create a smaller civic room along Jiron Pizarro. A marble fountain and pedestrian paving define Plazuela El Recreo east of Trujillo's main square, and stories of tunnels beneath the plaza need archaeological support, while the visible fountain and urban layout offer sufficient historical context.

03 AdmissionFree

Trujillo Cathedral Basilica

Raised around 1646, the cathedral on Trujillo's Plaza de Armas shows Churrigueresque detailing behind a bright yellow facade that dominates the square. Exterior walls and the main nave stay open for viewing when Mass is not underway. Builders left an ornate colonial silhouette that still anchors the city's historic core.

04 AdmissionFree

Plaza de Armas of Trujillo

Trujillo's Plaza de Armas is a broad civic square centered on Edmund Moeller's Freedom Monument. The cathedral, municipal buildings and brightly painted colonial or republican houses define its edges, with wrought-iron balconies and window grilles adding detail to the facades. Public ceremonies and daily pedestrian activity continue at the place associated with the city's 1820 declaration of independence.

Real Plaza Trujillo

Brand-name shops and a large grocery fill the retail floors at Real Plaza Trujillo, while a food court gathers family groups after errands. Open pavement and surrounding facades set the outdoor edge of the complex. Shoppers move between indoor stores and the plaza-side approaches throughout the day.

06 AdmissionFree

Trujillo Botanical Garden

Trujillo Botanical Garden is a compact municipal green space with labeled plant collections, shaded paths and ponds. Native and introduced species grow in small thematic areas, and enclosures have also housed birds or other small animals, giving the garden the character of an urban educational park that combines basic botanical interpretation with neighborhood recreation.

La Huaca de la Luna

Moche builders raised Huaca de la Luna near Trujillo as a place of worship and sacrifice, leaving a stepped adobe pyramid still studied by archaeologists. The wider valley was once capital ground for both Moche and Chimú societies. Excavated walls and plazas show how ceremonial life organized the settlement.

8.1349° S / 78.9902° W

08 AdmissionPEN 10

La Huaca del Sol

Outside Trujillo, Huaca del Sol rises as a Moche adobe pyramid used for elite residence, ceremony, and burial beside Huaca de la Luna. Multicolored friezes survive more fully on the Moon temple across the complex. The paired huacas form the ceremonial heart of the Moche capital at Cerro Blanco.

8.1318° S / 78.9946° W

09 AdmissionFree

Huaca "El Dragon", Complejo Arqueológico Chan-Chan

Huaca El Dragón, also called Huaca Arco Iris, is a Chimú adobe platform near Chan Chan with reliefs of stylized dragons or rainbows linked to fertility and water rites. Courtyards and frieze walls remain unusually clear for an earthen site. The huaca sits on the approach to the wider Chan Chan archaeological zone.

8.0765° S / 79.0487° W

10 AdmissionPEN 10

Chan Chan

The political center of the Chimu kingdom spread adobe compounds, plazas, storerooms, roads and canals across the desert near Trujillo. Relief-decorated walls and burial areas survive within several walled sectors, with the Nik An complex forming the principal interpreted route, and huacas del Sol and de la Luna belong to the earlier Moche culture and remain separate archaeological sites.

8.1086° S / 79.0753° W
