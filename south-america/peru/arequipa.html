Arequipa

Spanish founders in 1540 built with white sillar volcanic stone in an Andean valley prone to quakes. Monasteries such as Santa Catalina and mestizo baroque façades were rebuilt after successive tremors. Seismic resilience, not coastal trade, shaped the White City's fabric. El Misti volcano's silhouette explains both the sillar quarries and the seismic threat.

Plaza de Armas Arequipa

Plaza de Armas Arequipa is a monumental square featuring the city's cathedral and colonial buildings that house various restaurants and cafes. The square offers views of the surrounding area, including a volcano rising behind it. Visitors can enjoy dining at second-floor restaurants with galleries offering views of the square, nearby establishments offer local treats such as ice cream.

Basilica Cathedral of Arequipa

White volcanic stone gives Arequipa Cathedral its broad neoclassical facade across the Plaza de Armas. Earthquakes and fire repeatedly damaged the building, producing major nineteenth- and twentieth-century reconstruction over earlier colonial foundations. A museum, organs, chapels, towers, and religious art accompany active Catholic worship, while Colca Canyon lies far outside the city and is irrelevant here.

Church of the Company of Jesus

Arequipa's Jesuit church shows a densely carved Baroque facade and cloister in white sillar stone near the Plaza de Armas, and seventeenth- and eighteenth-century work combines European religious forms with local plants, animals and Indigenous motifs. Gilded altars, painted chapel decoration and active worship provide substance without relying on rankings as the peak of Peruvian Baroque.

Mundo Alpaca

In Arequipa, Mundo Alpaca is a commercial textile center in Arequipa that demonstrates sorting, spinning, dyeing, weaving and grading of alpaca and vicuna fibers. Small animal pens, craft displays, workshops and retail spaces connect material production with luxury sales. Welfare, demonstrations and inventory change.

05 AdmissionFree

Plaza de Yanahuara

Yanahuara's small main square joins a white-stone church, palms, gardens, civic buildings, and the famous sillar arches of the neighboring mirador. Views of Misti depend on weather and are better associated with the overlook than the plaza alone. Yanahuara Plaza is a large, peaceful park with palm and flower-lined paths.

Sabandía mill

A restored watermill at Sabandia uses channels and stone machinery to interpret agricultural processing in Arequipa's countryside. White sillar walls, terraces, trees, fields, and views toward volcanoes shape the estate. The current site reflects substantial reconstruction and tourism use.

Santa Catalina Monastery

Santa Catalina Monastery was founded in 1580 by a wealthy Spanish widow as a cloistered Dominican nunnery in Arequipa. Brightly painted streets, cells, and cloisters form a city-within-a-city of sillar stone. Parts of the complex remain in religious use while large sections open as a museum.

Museum of Virreinal Art Santa Teresa

A colonial convent now holds the Museum of Virreinal Art Santa Teresa with more than 300 works, including sixteenth- and seventeenth-century paintings, porcelain, and liturgical objects. Galleries open on posted hours. Cloister rooms keep the religious-house plan readable.

10 AdmissionFree

Mercado San Camilo

San Camilo Market fills a large covered hall in central Arequipa. Produce, cheese and grains represent the surrounding agricultural region, while meat and prepared-food counters serve daily shoppers. Herbal stalls, flower sellers and juice stands add color to the aisles. The iron roof has encouraged disputed links with Gustave Eiffel, but the market's long commercial role is better documented.

16.4030° S / 71.5350° W

Templo de San Agustín

San Agustin Church faces a narrow street near Arequipa's university district, preserving carved sillar stonework from a colonial foundation heavily damaged by earthquakes. The present facade and interior reflect rebuilding after the destructive 1868 event. Altars, chapels, and active parish use continue.
