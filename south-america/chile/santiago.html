Santiago

Santiago was refounded by Pedro de Valdivia in 1541 in a valley beneath the Andes, serving as capital of the Captaincy General and later the Republic of Chile. Earthquakes repeatedly reshaped its colonial churches and neoclassical centre before 19th-century nitrate wealth funded boulevards and public buildings. Plaza de Armas, Cerro San Cristóbal, and the Bellavista quarter show how the city became Chile's political, financial, and cultural hub.

National Aerospace Museum

Chile's National Aeronautical and Space Museum occupies hangars at the former Los Cerrillos airfield in Santiago; large airframes fill the main halls, while interpretation covers pioneering flights, national airlines, aerospace technology and the development of air transport. Historic aircraft, engines, uniforms, models, documents and displays trace military and civil aviation in the country.

Plaza de Armas de Santiago

Plaza de Armas has anchored Santiago's civic plan since the Spanish foundation of the city in 1541, and palm trees, monuments, performers, chess players, commuters and demonstrations keep it active as a public crossroads. The Metropolitan Cathedral, former royal court, central post office, museums, arcades and commercial streets frame the square.

Metropolitan Cathedral of Santiago

The Metropolitan Cathedral of Santiago, completed in 1800, is a grand neoclassical structure that is the seat of the city's archbishop. Flanked by Plaza de Armas, it has a rich history dating back to 1541 when Pedro de Valdivia requested its construction. Over many years, it underwent several reconstructions due to fires and earthquakes.

La Moneda Palace

Italian architect Joaquin Toesca designed the Neoclassical Royal Mint between 1784 and 1805; the same building later became Chile's presidential palace. La Moneda still houses the seat of government in downtown Santiago. Guided tours explain the shift from mint to palace and walk visitors through the formal courtyards and state rooms when schedules allow.

Club de la Unión de Santiago

Founded in 1864, Santiago's Club de la Unión became a meeting place for political, business and social elites in the capital. Its early twentieth-century headquarters contains formal salons, paintings, sculpture, woodwork and ceremonial rooms associated with the club's long institutional life. Membership was historically male, with women admitted as members in the twenty-first century.

Church of San Francisco

Franciscans began the present stone Church of San Francisco in 1575 on land connected with an earlier hermitage dedicated to the Virgen del Socorro. The low colonial church survived earthquakes that erased much of early Santiago and now stands beside the Alameda amid later city development. A convent museum and cloister preserve religious art linked to the order's history in Chile.

Santa Lucía Hill

Terraces, fountains, stairways, and small monuments cover Santa Lucía Hill, a rocky rise beside central Santiago's street grid. Governor Benjamín Vicuña Mackenna reshaped the former defensive lookout into a public promenade in the 1870s, adding the elaborate approaches seen today. Paths climb through landscaped sections to viewpoints over downtown and, in clear weather, the Andes.

Above the Bellavista and Providencia districts, San Cristóbal Hill forms the central landmark of Santiago Metropolitan Park. Roads, paths, a funicular, and cable-car lines connect gardens, recreation areas, and the hilltop sanctuary crowned by a large statue of the Virgin Mary.
