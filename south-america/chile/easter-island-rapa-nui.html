Easter Island Rapa Nui

Rapa Nui was settled by Polynesian voyagers whose descendants carved the moai statues that still stand on ahu platforms around the island. European contact from 1722, slave raids, and Chilean annexation in 1888 reduced the population and disrupted traditional land use. Sheep ranching later cleared much of the interior. Today Rapa Nui National Park protects quarries at Rano Raraku, ceremonial sites, and a living indigenous culture centred on Hanga Roa.

Ahu Tahai

Ahu Tahai is a ceremonial complex on Rapa Nui's west coast where restored moai stand on stone platforms facing inland. One figure carries a reddish pukao topknot and reconstructed coral eyes, a rare complete presentation among the island's ahu. Grass and coastal rock surround the aligned statues at sunset.

Ahu Vinapu

Ahu Vinapu is known for finely fitted basalt masonry that some compare to Andean stonework, plus a rare female moai carved from red scoria. Weathering has softened the figure's details, yet the platform still stands apart from the island's more common male statues. The site sits inland from the southern coast road.

Te Ra'ai

Te Ra'ai combines an evening meal with staged Rapa Nui music, dance, body painting, storytelling and food prepared in an earth oven. The program introduces selected cultural practices through a contemporary performance created for an audience. Artists and participants shape the experience, which is a commercial cultural production connected with living island traditions.

04 Admission$100

Puna Pau

Puna Pau is a historic rock quarry known for red scoria, which was used to carve the decorative elements for the moai statues on Easter Island. This site is where the moai headdresses, known as pukao, were crafted from the soft red volcanic stone. The quarry offers a glimpse into the origin of these unique headdresses and their significance in the island's history.

27.1475° S / 109.4035° W

Ahu Akivi

Ahu Akivi is an intriguing archaeological site on Rapa Nui featuring seven moai statues that are positioned to face the setting sun during the Spring Equinox. Unlike most other ahu temples, these moai statues face out to sea, adding to their mystery and beauty. According to local legend, these seven moai represent the scouts sent by King Hotu Matua in ancient times and were strategically placed for astronomical purposes.

Ahu Tongariki

Fifteen restored moai stand along the ceremonial platform of Ahu Tongariki near Rapa Nui's southeast coast, and a tsunami in 1960 scattered the statues inland before a major restoration returned them to the ahu during the 1990s. The figures face former settlement land, with the volcanic slopes of Rano Raraku behind them and the Pacific directly beyond the platform.

Pu o Hiro

Pu o Hiro is a large basalt stone near the Anakena area traditionally linked to fertility rites and called a trumpet stone for the holes used to make sound. Petroglyphs mark the surface, and the boulder sits in open grassland away from the main ahu platforms, so visits are brief stops on coastal routes.

Easter Island

Rapa Nui is a remote Polynesian island in the southeastern Pacific, known for moai, ceremonial platforms, volcanic landscapes, caves and living Indigenous culture. Most residents live around Hanga Roa, while archaeological sites spread across ranchland and protected park areas. Travel requires respect for marked routes, local rules and the distinction between inhabited community and monumental sites.

27.1127° S / 109.3497° W

Rapa Nui National Park

Rapa Nui National Park protects much of Easter Island's archaeological landscape, including moai, ahu, quarries, petroglyphs, caves and ceremonial villages. The sites record a complex Polynesian society and later periods of upheaval, colonial intrusion and cultural survival.

27.0728° S / 109.3486° W

Ahu Nau Nau

Ahu Nau Nau stands behind Anakena's white sand beach, a restored ceremonial platform with moai that once wore pukao. Palm shade and dune grass separate the ahu from the swimming shore, making it one of the few major statue sites set directly on a bathing beach.
