Cartagena

Cartagena de Indias was founded in 1533 as a fortified Caribbean port for Spanish silver and slave trade. Walls, bastions, and coral-stone mansions in the old city defended against pirates and rival empires. Independence hero Simón Bolívar landed here in 1811, and the restored centre is a UNESCO World Heritage site. Municipal archives, parish records, and surviving architecture help explain how the city connected to wider regional trade routes, court patronage, and modern civic rebuilding after industrial and wartime change.

San Felipe de Barajas Fort

Named for Philip IV of Spain, San Felipe de Barajas rose over roughly 120 years on San Lazaro hill above Cartagena. UNESCO lists the fortress, which keeps a tunnel system and audio-tour routes through the works. Hilltop walls look out over the city and approaches that once guarded the Caribbean port.

Las Botas Viejas

Bronze oversized boots honour poet Luis Carlos Lopez at Las Botas Viejas in Cartagena. People step inside the sculpture for photographs beside the waterfront. Morning and late afternoon light are cooler for lingering at the literary monument than midday heat.

Plaza de la Trinidad

Food vendors, painted buildings, and murals fill Plaza de la Trinidad in Cartagena's Getsemani area. Champeta music and street dancers often animate evenings while children play on church steps. Open paving keeps the square as a neighbourhood stage rather than a formal garden.

04 AdmissionFree

Walls of Cartagena

Stone ramparts, bastions, gates and sea defenses encircle Cartagena's colonial core and parts of Getsemaní, and Spanish authorities built and expanded the system from the late sixteenth through the eighteenth century after repeated attacks on the port. Walkable sections overlook the Caribbean, old town roofs and modern districts, while the walls themselves reveal changing military engineering.

La Serrezuela Mall

A restored nineteenth-century shell near Cartagena's walls holds La Serrezuela's boutiques and restaurants under a modern roof. Brick arches remain while contemporary retail floors fill the old volume. An open central void ties the historic fabric to the shopping levels inside.

06 AdmissionFree

Las Bóvedas

Twenty-three vaulted chambers were completed beneath Cartagena's seaward wall in the late eighteenth century. Known as Las Bóvedas, they stored military supplies and later held prisoners during the independence era. Their thick masonry, arcaded frontage, and position between the bastions of Santa Catalina and Santa Clara remain visible, although the cells now contain craft and souvenir shops.

10.4302° N / 75.5465° W

Parque Fernández Madrid

Parque Fernandez Madrid is a shaded square inside walled Cartagena where benches, trees, and a central statue create a quiet pause between colonial streets. Residents read, eat takeaway, and watch children play while carriage and foot traffic pass the surrounding facades.

08 AdmissionFree

Monumento India Catalina

Abducted as a child and later used as an interpreter by Pedro de Heredia, her life is known through colonial accounts shaped by Spanish perspectives. A bronze figure of India Catalina stands near the edge of Cartagena's historic center, commemorating an Indigenous woman linked to the city's conquest period. Eladio Gil Zambrana created the 1974 monument, whose image also became the emblem of Cartagena's film festival.

Catedral de Santa Catalina de Alejandría

Cartagena's cathedral, seat of the Archbishop of Cartagena de Indias, anchors Plaza de la Proclamacion with a pale stone tower and wide nave. Centuries of rebuilds after pirate raids and earthquakes shaped the present church, which remains the old city's principal Catholic landmark.

Santuario de San Pedro Claver

This sixteenth-century church in Cartagena is dedicated to Jesuit priest San Pedro Claver, an early defender of enslaved Africans in the Spanish Empire. A museum on site covers his legacy beside the sanctuary. The complex ties Cartagena's colonial religious architecture to the port's role in the slave trade.

10.4218° N / 75.5510° W

Plaza de Santa Teresa

Inside Cartagena's walled city near a gate toward Getsemani, a small paved square is edged by colonial facades. Vendors sometimes sell mochila bags while taxis wait at the nearby exit. Walkers use the compact court as a rest point between larger squares such as Santo Domingo and the streets leading out of the walls.

10.4217° N / 75.5523° W

15 AdmissionFree

Baluarte de Santo Domingo

Baluarte de Santo Domingo is a bastion on Cartagena's walls raised after Francis Drake's 1586 attack to strengthen the seaward defences. Thick angled ramparts and gun platforms now hold cafes and viewpoints over the Caribbean, while the masonry still reads as military engineering.

10.4244° N / 75.5542° W

16 AdmissionFree

Convent of Santa Cruz de la Popa

Augustinian Recollects established the complex in the early seventeenth century, and later rebuilding shaped its arcaded cloister and chapel dedicated to the Virgin of Candelaria. The Convent of Santa Cruz de la Popa crowns Cartagena's high hill above the Caribbean coast and inland wetlands. Terraces provide broad city views, while the religious rooms preserve colonial art and pilgrimage traditions.

10.4192° N / 75.5255° W
