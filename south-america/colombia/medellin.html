Medellín

Medellín was founded in the Aburrá Valley in the seventeenth century, but its major expansion occurred during the late nineteenth-century coffee boom and subsequent textile industrialization. Following a period of severe violence linked to the drug cartels in the 1980s and 90s, the city underwent a urban change fueled by innovative public transit and social architecture. This trajectory from industrial hub to crisis and rebirth defines the modern city. Visitors witness this urban resilience through the Metrocable network, the revitalized Comuna 13, and the expansive Plaza Botero.

Tour Comuna 13

Comuna 13 in Medellin is a prime example of the city's progressive and innovative approach to its underserved areas. This neighborhood, once plagued by crime, has been transformed with the installation of public escalators and colorful street murals adorning its staircases. The area now has bars, souvenir shops, and active urban artwork that brightens up the previously dilapidated buildings.

Plaza botero

Twenty-three sculptures by Fernando Botero fill Plaza Botero's open pavement in Medellín. The Antioquia Museum sits a short walk away, so art stops often pair outdoor bronzes with indoor galleries. Surrounding facades frame the square as a dense outdoor sculpture court.

Atanasio Girardot Stadium

Opened in 1953, Atanasio Girardot Stadium anchors a larger public sports complex in western Medellín. Atlético Nacional and Independiente Medellín use the football ground, while surrounding arenas, courts, pools, tracks and open spaces support many other sports. Metro access and neighborhood activity make the district a regular gathering place beyond match days, concerts and major tournaments.

Plaza Minorista José María Villa

Downtown Medellín's Plaza Minorista José María Villa operates as a large indoor marketplace. Vendors sell produce, meat, seafood, and household goods under one roof. Busy aisles and stalls define the visit more than outdoor paving despite the plaza name.

Plaza Botero - Medellín, Antioquia

This listing marks the same Botero sculpture square in Medellín's centre used on many walking routes. Guides often include the open pavement when linking downtown museums and civic blocks. The surrounding streets tie the bronzes to the city's broader historic core.

Parroquia La Catedral Metropolitana de Medellín

Medellín's Metropolitan Cathedral was raised with more than a million adobe bricks in a Romanesque design that dominates Villanueva Plaza. As the seat of the archdiocese it holds major liturgical art inside the nave. The brick mass and twin towers remain the city's principal Catholic church.

El Palacio egipcio

El Palacio Egipcio stands in Prado Centro north of downtown Medellin, a small early-twentieth-century house dressed in Egyptian Revival motifs from the years when the district was among the city's wealthiest. Later industrial decline left the street quieter, yet the facade still shows lotus columns and period detail from that building boom.

Pequeño Teatro de Medellín

A collective of professional actors founded Pequeño Teatro de Medellín in 1975 to produce theater and train performers. The company later established itself in a restored house in the city center, where intimate rooms support plays, music, workshops, and cultural programs.

Plaza Cisneros

Renovated in 2005, Plaza Cisneros gained about 300 light poles that form an artificial forest after dark. The square is also called the Park of Lights and honours Cuban engineer Francisco Javier Cisneros of the Antioquia Railway. Daytime paving and nighttime illumination both draw people into the downtown block.

Parques del Río

Parques del Rio is a linear park project that reconnects Medellin to the Medellin River with walkways, sports courts, lawns, and riverside dining. Bridges and underpasses stitch neighbourhoods across the water, so daily exercise and evening strolls share the same engineered green corridor.

Parque Norte

Parque Norte divides into five themed plazas after a major renovation: expedition, fantasy, adventure, events, and extreme. Theme-park rides sit among green spaces across the northern grounds. The layout aims at mixed ages rather than a single thrill corridor.

El Tesoro Parque Comercial

Hillside floors at El Tesoro Parque Comercial hold fashion shops, cinemas, and restaurants from sweets to full meals. Glass fronts look over the Aburra Valley toward city ridges. Shopping trips often pause at the windows for long views below the green slopes.

El Castillo Museum and Gardens

Belgian-style turrets and steep roofs distinguish El Castillo, a 1930s residence built for businessman José Tobón Uribe and later owned by textile industrialist Diego Echavarría. Furnished rooms contain European decorative art, paintings, porcelain, glass, and objects collected by the family. Formal gardens and rotating exhibitions extend the museum beyond the house into its El Poblado grounds.

Casa Museo Otraparte

Casa Museo Otraparte in Envigado preserves the house associated with writer Fernando Gonzalez, with rooms for exhibitions, talks, and a small cafe in the garden. The suburban setting keeps a residential scale, and programmes mix literary history with contemporary cultural events.

Parque de El Poblado

Parque de El Poblado is the main green square of the El Poblado district, with mature trees, open lawns, and paths used as a neighbourhood meeting point. Cafes and churches face the park, and weekend crowds treat it as a pause between the metro and the hillside streets.

Parque de la Conservación

Formerly Medellín's Santa Fe Zoo, Parque de la Conservación now emphasizes rescue, rehabilitation, environmental education and protection of Colombian wildlife. Shaded paths connect habitats for birds, mammals, reptiles and other animals, many affected by trafficking or unsuitable captivity; the change in name reflects a shift from display toward conservation, though the site remains an urban animal park with managed enclosures.

Parque Arví

Parque Arvi is a large ecotourism reserve east of Medellin, reached by Metrocable into cooler highland forest. Nearly fifteen miles of trails cross native vegetation and flower plantings, with picnic areas and guided walks aimed at day visitors leaving the valley floor.

Estación Metrocable Arví

Estacion Metrocable Arvi is the upper gondola terminal for the line that climbs from Medellin's hillside barrios into Parque Arvi. Cabins look down on steep slopes and the city basin before arriving among trees at the park edge, where trails and buses continue deeper into the reserve.

Arvi Comfama Park

Metrocable access reaches Arvi Comfama Park above Medellín with mountain views across the ridges. Zip lines and tree-climbing courses run with safety harnesses for different skill levels. The park combines forest trails with aerial activities rather than a downtown plaza program.

20 AdmissionFree

Mirador el Cielo Medellin

Mirador el Cielo occupies a recreational hillside above Medellin with city views, food service, music and paid activities. Swings, zip lines, terraces and decorative photo points create a commercial viewpoint reached by steep roads on the urban fringe. Visibility across the Aburra Valley depends on cloud, rain, haze and the changing light over the city. Nightfall changes the emphasis from landscape to city lights.
