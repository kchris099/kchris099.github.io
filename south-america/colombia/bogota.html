Bogotá

Bogotá was founded in 1538 on a plateau of the Andes and became the capital of Gran Colombia and later the Republic of Colombia. La Candelaria's colonial houses, the Gold Museum, and Monserrate hill record Spanish administration, independence under Bolívar, and 20th-century urban growth as a regional financial centre. Municipal archives, parish records, and surviving architecture help explain how the city connected to wider regional trade routes, court patronage, and modern civic rebuilding after industrial and wartime change.

Catedral Primada de Colombia

The Primatial Cathedral occupies the eastern side of Bogotá's Plaza de Bolívar on ground used for churches since the city's Spanish foundation. Friar Domingo de Petrés designed the present neoclassical structure, built mainly between 1807 and 1823. Chapels, religious paintings, monuments, and tombs connect the building with Colombia's Catholic hierarchy and national political history.

National Capitol

Colombia's Congress meets beneath the long colonnaded facade of the National Capitol on Plaza de Bolívar. Construction began in 1848 and continued for decades, producing a neoclassical government complex with legislative chambers, courtyards, murals, and sculpture. The building faces the Palace of Justice and stands beside the cathedral, placing it at the center of Bogotá's ceremonial and political landscape.

Teatro Colón Bogotá

Italian architect Pietro Cantini designed Bogotá's Teatro Colón, inaugurated in 1892 on the site of an earlier theater; a horseshoe-shaped auditorium, painted curtain, frescoes, gilded detail and layered balconies create an intimate national performance house. Opera, music, theater, dance and public ceremonies continue inside a building closely tied to Colombia's late nineteenth-century cultural institutions.

Gold Museum

The Gold Museum, located in the historic city center of Bogota, is a contemporary structure that houses an extensive collection of pre-Columbian gold, ceramics, and stones. It is home to over 34 thousand pieces made of gold and tumbaga, showing the art and goldsmithing used by various indigenous communities in Colombia.

Augustinian friars completed the Church of San Agustín in the seventeenth century near Bogotá's colonial center.
