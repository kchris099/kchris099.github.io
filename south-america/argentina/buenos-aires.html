Buenos Aires

Buenos Aires was founded twice on the Río de la Plata, first in 1536 and permanently in 1580 as a Spanish port supplying the silver trade from Potosí. Massive 19th- and early 20th-century immigration from Italy and Spain, together with cattle and grain exports, shaped its plazas, theatres, and Haussmann-style avenues. The Casa Rosada, Teatro Colón, and neighbourhoods such as San Telmo and La Boca record how the city became Argentina's federal capital and principal cultural centre.

Palacio Barolo

Italian architect Mario Palanti designed Palacio Barolo as an office tower in Monserrat whose floors echo Dante's Divine Comedy. Zones nicknamed Hell, Purgatory, and Heaven climb toward a lighthouse cupola once used as a harbor signal light. Guided climbs reach vaulted lobbies, spiral stairs, and the rooftop lookout over Avenida de Mayo.

Floralis Generica

Architect Eduardo Catalano designed Floralis Generica as an eighteen-ton flower of stainless steel and aluminum. Installed in 2002 above a reflecting pool in Plaza de las Naciones Unidas, the sculpture uses hydraulic mechanisms and light sensors to move its six petals. The changing form links an engineered object with the daily cycle of opening and closing flowers.

Casa Rosada

Casa Rosada closes the eastern side of Plaza de Mayo and contains the offices of Argentina's president. The pink complex grew from nineteenth-century government and post-office buildings joined behind an Italianate facade; its balconies, courtyards and museum are closely associated with national ceremonies, political speeches and demonstrations in the square.

Buenos Aires Metropolitan Cathedral has stood on the main plaza since roots in the early 17th century. Upper galleries hold a Carlo Vegezzi Bossi organ from Italy, built in the Ripiena tradition with romantic orchestral registers. Barolo Palace, the Cabildo, Gran Rex Theatre, and Cafe Tortoni lie a short walk away.

Parroquia San Ignacio de Loyola

San Ignacio de Loyola developed from the Jesuit complex established in colonial Buenos Aires. The present church was completed in the eighteenth century, though later repairs altered its towers and interior. Brick vaults, side chapels, carved altarpieces, and links to the adjoining Manzana de las Luces place it among the city's surviving colonial religious buildings.

Obelisk

The Obelisco de Buenos Aires rises 67.5 meters at the crossing of Avenida 9 de Julio and Corrientes. Built in 1936 from Cordoba white stone for the city's four-hundredth foundation anniversary, it is a national historic monument. Four inscribed faces and a narrow interior stair mark the shaft that anchors the theater district skyline.

Teatro Colón

Teatro Colón, a grand theater built in 1908, is known for its acoustics and opulent design. It hosts classical music performances, operas, ballets, and offers tours to visitors. The French Embassy and Lavalle Street are notable landmarks nearby. Considered one of the world's top five opera houses, it has welcomed known artists like Maria Callas and Luciano Pavarotti.
