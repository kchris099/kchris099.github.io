Mendoza

Founded by Spanish settlers in 1561, Mendoza grew as an irrigated farm town until an 1861 earthquake flattened much of it. Reconstruction brought wide avenues and canal networks that still shape the grid. From the late nineteenth century the surrounding valleys turned the city into Argentina's main wine center, and bodega culture remains visible in civic buildings, plazas, and day trips into the foothills.

Monument to the Army of the Andes

Bronze figures crown Cerro de la Gloria above Mendoza in the Monument to the Army of the Andes. Juan Manuel Ferrari's early twentieth-century composition commemorates General Jose de San Martin and the forces that crossed the Andes during the independence campaigns. Reliefs, soldiers, horses, and an allegorical figure of liberty unfold around the stone base.

Plaza Pedro del Castillo

Laid out with Mendoza's original settlement in the sixteenth century, Plaza Pedro del Castillo occupies the city's former civic center. The nearby Museo del Area Fundacional interprets the colonial town and the destruction caused by the 1861 earthquake; open paving, archaeological traces, and surrounding historic buildings distinguish it from Mendoza's later tree-filled plazas.

Square Chacras de Coria

Restaurants around Square Chacras de Coria in Mendoza's wine-country fringe support fair days and evening meals after tastings. Open pavement and surrounding facades keep the plaza as a neighborhood meeting floor. The square reads as a dining and gathering hub for the Chacras district.

Square San Martin

An equestrian statue of General San Martin holds the center of Square San Martin in Mendoza, recalling his role in independence campaigns. Open pavement and surrounding facades frame the monument for civic gatherings. The bronze rider is the fixed landmark of the block.

Peatonal Sarmiento

Cafes, restaurants, and boutique shops line Peatonal Sarmiento, Mendoza's tree-shaded pedestrian artery. Outdoor seating and street performances fill the corridor, which links several main plazas and commercial districts. The car-free stretch is the city's casual dining and strolling spine.

Plaza España

Plaza Espana in Mendoza keeps a quieter civic green than the commercial avenues around it, with open pavement and surrounding facades for local gatherings. Families and neighbors use the square as a pause rather than a transit hub. The layout stays simple: trees, benches, and facing streets.

Plazoleta Pellegrini

Between San Juan, Primitivo de la Reta and Alem streets, Plazoleta Pellegrini preserves a compact urban design with a marble fountain, pergola, iron benches, traditional lamps and a cobbled side lane. Municipal restoration in 2020 renewed paving and accessibility while retaining the square's layout. The space has also been associated with an antiques and collectibles fair.

Plaza Independencia

About 55,000 square meters of green at Plaza Independencia hold rare plantings and the Frieze of Independence monument with historical scenes. Shady pergolas ring parts of the plaza for rest. The scale and central frieze mark it as Mendoza's principal civic square.

Teatro Independencia

Facing Plaza Independencia, Teatro Independencia opened in 1925 as Mendoza's principal stage for opera, drama, dance, and orchestral music. Its neoclassical facade leads to a horseshoe auditorium with balconies and decorative plasterwork, and provincial arts institutions and visiting companies use the theater, which remains central to the city's formal performing arts program.

Fountain of the Continents

Four sculptural groups representing Europe, Asia, Africa and America surround the Fountain of the Continents in Parque General San Martin. The cast-iron work was acquired in France and installed in Mendoza in the early twentieth century, and water jets, mythological figures and animals create a circular composition along the park's main avenue.

32.8891° S / 68.8664° W

12 AdmissionFree

Parque General San Martín

Tree-lined avenues, an artificial lake, sports clubs, gardens and cultural buildings spread across Parque General San Martin on Mendoza's western side. Landscape architect Carlos Thays planned the park around the turn of the twentieth century, linking the city with the foothills and Cerro de la Gloria. Residents use its shaded lawns and broad roads for exercise, boating and gatherings.

Portones del Parque General San Martín

Wrought-iron gates, lamps, and a flying-condor figure mark the Portones entrance to Parque General San Martin in Mendoza. Trails continue into the large park beyond the gates for walkers and longer hikes. The decorated portal is the ceremonial threshold into the city's main green expanse.

Cerro Arco Mirador

A rough vehicle track or steep hike reaches Cerro Arco Mirador in the Andean foothills above Mendoza. From the summit, the city and surrounding vineyards spread in a wide pan; paragliders also use the ridge. The outlook favors those willing to take the climb or the rough approach.

15 AdmissionFree

Parque Central

Parque Central occupies former railway land north of central Mendoza and combines open lawns, a lake, playgrounds, sports areas, and long paths used for walking and cycling. The Nave Cultural and other public venues stand along its edges, while a large sundial marks one of the central plazas. The park is a neighborhood recreation space and event ground.

32.8745° S / 68.8424° W
