Iguazú

The Iguazu region was originally inhabited by the Guarani people before Spanish explorer Alvar Nunez Cabeza de Vaca recorded the falls in 1541. The area remained largely isolated until the creation of Iguazu National Park in 1934, which protected the surrounding subtropical rainforest. Today, the falls are recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site, drawing visitors to their massive cascades and diverse wildlife.

Iguazu Falls

More than two hundred cascades spread across a broad bend of the Iguazu River at the border of Argentina and Brazil. Walkways on both sides approach different sections of the falls through protected subtropical forest, where spray, islands, and changing water levels reshape the view. The surrounding national parks conserve both the waterfall system and habitat for Atlantic Forest species.

Garganta Del Diablo

At Garganta del Diablo, channels of the upper Iguazu River converge along a U-shaped rim before dropping into a mist-filled gorge. On the Argentine side, a raised walkway crosses channels and forested islands to a platform above the fall, and heavy spray frequently obscures the lower cliffs, leaving the sound and movement of water as dominant parts of the setting.

Lookout Devil's Throat

The Lookout Devil's Throat provides a primary vantage point for observing the power of the Garganta Del Diablo. Positioned at the end of a long walkway over the upper Iguazu River, the observation deck places visitors directly at the edge of the horseshoe-shaped falls. The constant spray and thunderous sound of the water create an immersive environment, highlighting the raw natural forces that shape the surrounding landscape.

Paseos de Luna Llena en las Cataratas del Iguazú

On selected nights around the full moon, guided trains and walks continue through Iguazu National Park after normal daytime access ends. The route follows the upper circuit to Garganta del Diablo, where moonlight, mist and the sound of the river create a view distinct from daylight visits. Operation depends on weather, river conditions and the park's published calendar.

Iguazú National Park

Created in 1934, Iguazu National Park protects the Argentine side of the falls and the subtropical forest around them. Boardwalks and trails lead to multiple cataract viewpoints; the forest holds jaguars, toucans, and dense bird life. Park management balances waterfall access with habitat protection across the river corridor shared with Brazil.

La Aripuca

A giant replica of a Guarani trap, built from rescued tree trunks, anchors La Aripuca outside Puerto Iguazu. Grounds around the timber frame interpret Atlantic Forest conservation and indigenous cultures of the region, with regional crafts on sale. The trap form is the unmistakable centerpiece of the park.

07 AdmissionARS 15,000

La Casa de Botellas

Walls and rooms at La Casa de Botellas in Puerto Iguazu are built from recycled plastic bottles and other reclaimed materials. Tours walk through the house and outbuildings to show how waste becomes structural fabric. The project frames sustainable building as a visible, walkable experiment beside the falls corridor.

08 AdmissionARS 30,000

Güirá Oga

Guira Oga receives wild animals injured, orphaned, confiscated or displaced in the Misiones rainforest. Veterinary staff rehabilitate birds, mammals and reptiles for release when possible, while animals unable to return to the wild remain in managed enclosures. Guided visits follow a forest route and explain rescue work, habitat loss, illegal trade and local species.

25.6261° S / 54.5485° W

09 AdmissionFree

Saltos del Arroyo Mbocay

Away from the main Iguazu Falls complex, Saltos del Arroyo Mbocay follows a forested stream near Puerto Iguazu to small cascades. Trails stay quieter and closer to town than the national park circuits. Local vegetation and the Mbocay channel define a short natural retreat without the main falls crowds.

25.6465° S / 54.5794° W
