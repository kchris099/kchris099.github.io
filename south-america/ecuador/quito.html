Quito

Quito stands high in the Andes on the site of a pre-Inca centre incorporated into the Inca empire and refounded by Sebastián de Benalcázar in 1534. Baroque churches, monasteries, and plazas in the historic centre are among the -preserved in the Americas and were inscribed by UNESCO in 1978. The city remains Ecuador's political capital while facing volcanic valleys to the east.

Basílica del Voto Nacional

Stone animals native to Ecuador replace conventional European gargoyles along parts of the exterior. The Basilica of the National Vow rises above Quito's historic center as an ambitious neo-Gothic church begun in the late nineteenth century. Towers, stained glass, a long nave, chapels, and exposed upper walkways provide architectural detail and city views, though steep stairs and ladders demand care.

Independence Square

Independence Square, also Plaza Grande, is the focal plaza of Quito's historic center with a monument to the 1809 independence advocates. The cathedral, presidential palace, and archbishop's palace frame the paved square. Palm trees and open stones host daily foot traffic and formal civic ceremonies.

Quito Metropolitan Cathedral

Quito's Metropolitan Cathedral occupies one side of Plaza Grande on ground used for the city's principal church since the sixteenth century. Earthquakes and rebuilding produced a mixture of Gothic, Moorish, Baroque and neoclassical elements. Chapels, carved altars, paintings, bishops' tombs and the memorial of Antonio Jose de Sucre connect worship with national history.

Church of the Society of Jesus

Jesuit builders spent more than a century completing Quito's Church of the Society of Jesus, consecrated in the eighteenth century. A volcanic-stone Baroque facade leads to an interior covered with carved wood, gilding, painted domes, altars and symbolic ornament; european forms merge with local plants, Indigenous faces and Andean craft traditions throughout one of the historic center's most detailed churches.

Basilica and Convent of San Francisco

The San Francisco Catholic Church in Quito is a grand baroque church known for its ornate interior featuring detailed paintings and sculptures. It is part of the historic center of Quito, which also includes other notable attractions such as the Compania de Jesus church, Basilica del Voto Nacional church, Catedral Primada de Quito, and Santo Domingo church. The area also has landmarks like La Plaza Grande and Carondelet Palace.

Historic Center of Quito

Quito's historic center spreads across a high Andean valley with churches, convents, plazas, civic buildings and houses developed from the sixteenth century onward. Spanish colonial planning and architecture stand beside Indigenous and mestizo craft traditions visible in stone carving, gilded interiors, courtyards and painted decoration. Steep streets and active neighborhoods keep the historic district part of daily city life.

Virgin of the Panecillo

A winged aluminum figure of the Virgin of Quito crowns El Panecillo, the rounded hill south of the colonial center. Spanish artist Agustin de la Herran based the 1970s monument on Bernardo de Legarda's eighteenth-century sculpture and assembled it from thousands of metal pieces. Terraces around the base look across both northern and southern districts of the city.

TelefériQo Cable Car

Cabins on Quito's TelefériQo rise from the city edge to the Cruz Loma area on the eastern slope of Pichincha volcano, and broad views may include Quito's valley and surrounding volcanoes, but cloud, wind, rain and altitude strongly affect conditions. The upper station stands above 3,900 metres, where paths, viewpoints, food stalls and longer mountain routes begin in thin, rapidly changing air.

Iglesia de Santo Domingo in Quito's historic centre traces its Dominican community to the mid-sixteenth century and holds a neo-Gothic altarpiece with sixteenth- and seventeenth-century paintings and sculptures. The church faces a plaza that remains an active parish and tourist stop.

Before the Church of Santo Domingo in Quito's historic center, paved open ground and colonial facades form a southern civic square. The Dominican church and convent complex give the court its religious and architectural focus. Streets from the old town grid feed into the square for daily crossing and evening gatherings.

Calle La Ronda

La Ronda follows a narrow curving street in Quito's historic center, lined with restored houses, workshops, cafes, restaurants and small cultural spaces. The lane has colonial origins but also experienced decline and later public restoration, so its present evening economy is not unchanged from the past. Balconies, courtyards, artisan demonstrations, music and traditional sweets connect tourism with a still-inhabited urban district.

Quito Botanical Gardens

Living collections at Quito Botanical Garden represent highland, cloud-forest, coastal, Amazonian, wetland and dry-zone plants from across Ecuador. Orchid houses, bromeliads, medicinal species and native trees surround ponds and demonstration gardens within La Carolina Park, and the compact grounds introduce Ecuador's varied plant habitats while supporting conservation, propagation and public education.

Parque La Carolina

Parque La Carolina covers about 67 hectares in northern Quito, with cycle paths, football and basketball courts, and paddle boats on a central pond. The botanical garden and weekend markets sit within the same green belt used daily by runners and families.

18 AdmissionFree

Teatro Sucre

Teatro Nacional Sucre opened in 1886 on a former market square in Quito's historic center. Its neoclassical facade fronts a horseshoe auditorium used for opera, theater, dance, music, festivals and public ceremonies. Restoration revived painted decoration, boxes, stage machinery and acoustics, while the building remains part of an active network of municipal performance venues.

Mercado Central

Quito's Mercado Central on Avenida Pichincha is a busy food market known for juice stalls, soups, and tropical fruit piles of papaya, mango, and pineapple. Vendors serve local breakfast and lunch plates in a hall that remains more practical market than polished food hall.
