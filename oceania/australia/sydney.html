Sydney

Sydney Harbour was settled by Britain in 1788 as a penal colony that grew into Australia's largest metropolis. Opera House sails, sandstone convict precincts, and harbour bridges record colonial foundations and post-war immigration on the Pacific rim. Bondi-to-Coogee coastal walks and Rocks laneways extend harbour settlement layers along the Pacific escarpment.

Mrs Macquarie's Chair

Convicts carved Mrs Macquarie's Chair from a sandstone ledge at the end of the Domain peninsula in 1810. The bench was associated with Elizabeth Macquarie, who used the point overlooking the harbor. Today the surrounding path frames one of Sydney's clearest combined views of the Opera House and Harbour Bridge, while the rock itself records an early colonial intervention in the shoreline.

St Mary's Cathedral

Local sandstone walls, twin spires, pointed arches and stained glass define St Mary's Cathedral beside Hyde Park. Construction began in 1868 after fire destroyed an earlier church and the long building program continued across several generations; the cruciform interior contains chapels, carved timber, mosaics and a crypt serving the principal Catholic cathedral of Sydney.

Hyde Park Barracks

Convict architect Francis Greenway designed Hyde Park Barracks, completed in 1819 to house male convicts under government control. Later uses included accommodation for immigrant women, an asylum, courts and offices. Museum displays, archaeological material and restored spaces trace these changing populations within a building included in Australia's historic convict sites.

State Theatre

The State Theatre, located in Sydney's CBD, is a establishment that hosts live music, film screenings, and comedy shows. The building has ornate Gothic, Italian, and art deco architecture and was originally opened in 1929 as a 'Fantasy Palace for the People.' With its opulent design featuring vaulted ceilings, mosaic floors, marble columns and statues, brass and bronze doors, it offers a unique experience for visitors.

Queen Victoria Building

The Queen Victoria Building, a historic shopping center in Sydney's CBD, is a example of Romanesque architecture designed by George McRae. Built in the late 19th century, it now houses over 175 modern stores including known brands like Tiffany and Ralph Lauren alongside tea rooms, florists, and jewelry stores. The building's grand arches, pillars, and stained-glass windows reflect the artistry of its craftsmen from a bygone era.

Steel arches span Sydney Harbour between Dawes Point and Milsons Point on the bridge opened in 1932.
