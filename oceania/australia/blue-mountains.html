Blue Mountains

The Blue Mountains were a formidable barrier to early European expansion from the Sydney colony until a navigable route was crossed in 1813. The region is the traditional country of the Darug and Gundungurra peoples, whose rock art remains scattered throughout the sandstone gorges. In the late nineteenth century, the mountains developed into a fashionable retreat for wealthy Sydneysiders and a center for coal and shale mining. Today, visitors experience this mix of ancient geology and Victorian tourism via the historic Scenic Railway, the Three Sisters rock formation, and the dense eucalyptus forests.

Wentworth Falls

Wentworth Falls drops from the Blue Mountains escarpment into a deep sandstone valley. Marked tracks and lookouts such as the Jamison Valley viewpoints frame the cascade and surrounding cliffs. Mist after rain can hide the base, while clear days show layered ridges stretching west from the falls. Stair flights on the National Pass drop below the cliff line for closer spray.

Lincoln's Rock

Sandstone at Lincoln's Rock, also called Flat Rock, juts from Kings Tableland above the Jamison Valley in the Blue Mountains. Sunset light across the forested valleys draws photographers and wedding parties, yet the ledge still leaves room for picnics. Cliff edge and open rock define the outlook.

03 AdmissionFree

Grand Canyon Walking Track

The Grand Canyon Walking Track is a 6-km loop that provides views of sandstone cliffs, waterfalls, and rainforest. Established in 1907, it's popular walks in the Blue Mountains. The trail takes hikers through diverse ecosystems, from dry sclerophyll forest to cool-temperate rainforests. Guided tours are available for a more immersive experience.

33.6494° S / 150.3214° E

04 AdmissionFree

Evans Lookout

Orange cliff faces at Evans Lookout near Blackheath catch sunrise and sunset light across Blue Mountains gorges. Parking sits close enough for a short stop, and the lookout marks the start of the Grand Canyon Track for longer hikes. Exposed rock and valley depth set the view.

33.6468° S / 150.3263° E

05 AdmissionFree

Govetts Leap lookout

Govetts Leap Lookout stands on the escarpment at Blackheath above the Grose Valley. Sandstone cliffs, forested ridges, and the long drop of Bridal Veil Falls fill the view, with walking tracks continuing toward Evans Lookout and valley routes. The name recalls surveyor William Romaine Govett, although the lookout also lies within a much older Aboriginal cultural landscape.

33.6280° S / 150.3115° E

06 AdmissionFree

Anvil Rock Lookout

A rock shaped like a blacksmith's anvil gives Anvil Rock Lookout its name above Blue Mountains valleys. About ten minutes on foot from parking brings a wide pan with fewer crowds than busier cliff stops. Short approach paths from the road keep the visit compact and quiet.

33.5954° S / 150.3393° E

07 AdmissionFree

Gordon Falls Picnic Area

Gordon Falls picnic area is a spot near the junction of Lone Pine Avenue and Olympian Parade. The 900m return walk from the picnic area to the lookout offers views of the valley and falls. Visitors can enjoy a leisurely picnic at the top before began on longer bush walks or taking a brisk walk to the base of the falls for a different perspective.

33.7255° S / 150.3320° E

08 AdmissionFree

Elysian Rock Lookout

Two small sandstone platforms linked by steps form Elysian Rock Lookout on the escarpment near Leura, and it is reached by a short path from residential streets and can be combined with the Prince Henry Cliff Walk. The position looks across the Jamison Valley toward Mount Solitary and neighboring cliff lines, with eucalyptus forest filling the basin below.

33.7262° S / 150.3299° E

Everglades House & Gardens

Art collector Henri Van de Velde commissioned Everglades House in the 1930s, with landscape designer Paul Sorensen shaping its terraced garden above Leura's escarpment. Formal lawns, stone walls, pools, winding paths, and native woodland lead to views over the Jamison Valley. The Art Deco residence and garden now operate as a National Trust property.

33.7213° S / 150.3383° E

10 AdmissionFree

Leura Cascades Picnic Area

Leura Cascades Picnic Area sits above a Blue Mountains waterfall creek, with picnic lawns and marked tracks to viewing platforms. Short walks follow the cascade gully through eucalypt forest rather than a long ridge trek. Families use the tables and lookouts as a half-day stop between Leura village and nearby Gordon Falls tracks.

33.7194° S / 150.3223° E
