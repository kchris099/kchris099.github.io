Brisbane

Brisbane began as a penal settlement in 1824 and opened to free settlers in 1842. During World War II it was a major Allied headquarters in the Southwest Pacific. Postwar growth turned the colonial river port into Queensland's capital, with surviving nineteenth-century street grids along the Brisbane River and later civic precincts inland from the waterfront.

Mount Coot-Tha Summit Lookout

From the summit of Mount Coot-tha, Brisbane's street grid and high-rise center spread east toward Moreton Bay, while forested ridges extend inland. A terrace, cafe, and short walking links occupy the lookout area, which can be reached by road or trails from the surrounding reserve. The broad orientation view helps place the city within its wider landscape.

Brisbane Botanic Gardens Mt Coot-tha

Brisbane Botanic Gardens Mount Coot-tha occupies sloping land below the mountain, with themed collections arranged around lagoons, lawns and shaded paths. The Japanese Garden, tropical display dome, fern house, bamboo grove, arid zone and Australian plant sections reflect climates from Queensland and abroad. Research, conservation and public education accompany the landscape displays.

Lone Pine Koala Sanctuary

Founded in 1927 on the Brisbane River, Lone Pine Koala Sanctuary cares for koalas and a range of other Australian animals. Enclosures and open areas hold kangaroos, wombats, dingoes, birds, reptiles and monotremes, supported by keeper talks and wildlife-health work. The site combines public viewing with breeding, rescue, research and conservation programs.

Suncorp Stadium

Lang Park developed from a former cemetery reserve into Brisbane's principal rectangular sports stadium, now known as Suncorp Stadium. The steep enclosed stands host rugby league, rugby union, football, and major concerts. Memorial elements outside recall the site's earlier history, while the modern venue remains closely associated with Queensland's representative rugby teams.

Roma Street Parkland

Contemporary garden beds, subtropical plantings, waterways, lawns and an amphitheater fill Roma Street Parkland beside Brisbane's central rail station. The park was created on former railway land and uses bridges and paths to connect areas divided by slopes and tracks; play spaces, picnic lawns and rotating horticultural displays make it both a city garden and an everyday public park.

St John's Cathedral

Construction of St John's Cathedral began in 1906 and continued in stages until completion in 2009, and the long building campaign allows the cathedral to show traditional craftsmanship alongside later work carried out to the original plan. Architect John Loughborough Pearson's Gothic Revival design uses sandstone walls, pointed arches, carved wood, stained glass, and a stone-vaulted nave.

Story Bridge

The Story Bridge in Brisbane is a pedestrian- and bicycle-friendly bridge that offers views of the city skyline and river. It is one of the city's most distinctive landmarks, adding character to the urban landscape. While not as famous as Sydney's Harbour Bridge, it provides an equally vantage point for taking in scenic views of the city.

City Botanic Gardens

Botanical collections, fig-shaded riverside paths, and duck ponds fill Brisbane's City Botanic Gardens along the Brisbane River. Manicured lawns open between denser plantings for rest and walks. The gardens work as a living plant collection inside the CBD edge.

Nepalese Peace Pagoda

Hand-carved timber panels cover the Nepalese Peace Pagoda in Brisbane's South Bank Parklands. Craftspeople in Nepal prepared the structure for World Expo 88, after which a public campaign kept it in the city. A tiered roof, prayer imagery, and small surrounding grove preserve an uncommon complete national pavilions remaining from the exposition.

Eat Street Northshore

Shipping containers, marquees and outdoor stages form Eat Street Northshore on the former Hamilton wharf, and its riverfront setting and temporary industrial architecture distinguish it from Brisbane's conventional covered markets. The weekend market gathers food stalls serving dishes from many cuisines alongside live music, bars and small retail vendors.

The Gabba

The Gabba occupies a compact inner-city site at Woolloongabba and has hosted cricket and Australian rules football for more than a century; its name comes from the suburb, while transport and redevelopment plans continue to shape the precinct. Enclosed stands surround the oval, which is the home ground of the Brisbane Lions and a regular venue for international cricket.

Newstead House

Built in 1846 above the Brisbane River, Newstead House began as a colonial residence and was enlarged by later owners into a more substantial Victorian home. Furnished rooms, verandas and service areas interpret domestic life across several periods of Brisbane's growth. The surrounding park preserves river views and the relationship between the house, gardens and former estate.

Mount Gravatt Lookout

Located just 10 kilometers south of Brisbane's busy CBD, Mount Gravatt Outlook is a destination that has an experience. Perched atop a prominent hill at 187 meters above sea level, this lookout provides sweeping panoramic views of the city and beyond. On clear days, feast visitor eyes on the vistas that include the Moreton Bay islands, D'Aguilar Ranges, and Glasshouse Mountains.
