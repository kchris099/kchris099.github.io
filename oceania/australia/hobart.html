Hobart

British authorities planted a penal station on the Derwent in 1804. Whaling and shipbuilding later paid for Georgian sandstone warehouses along Salamanca Place. When those industries faded, the same waterfront became the stage for Hobart's arts and Antarctic logistics culture. MONA and the waterfront markets occupy warehouses first built for whales and timber.

MONA

MONA, the Museum of Old and New Art, occupies a cliffside complex on the Berriedale peninsula north of Hobart. Private collection galleries mix ancient objects with contemporary installations in tunnel-like underground spaces. Ferry approaches from the waterfront show the museum's industrial silhouette before visitors enter the rock-cut halls.

Salamanca Market

Saturday stalls along Hobart's waterfront at Salamanca Market sell clothing, jewelry, artisan cheeses, fresh produce, and other local crafts. The active lane sits against warehouse facades of Salamanca Place. Trading follows the weekly market schedule rather than daily shop hours.

Tasmanian Travel and Information Centre

Staff at Hobart's Tasmanian Travel and Information Centre provide statewide maps, route advice, accommodation and tour bookings and current guidance on transport or seasonal access. The center is especially useful for planning journeys beyond Hobart, where distances, ferry reservations, park conditions and limited public transport can affect an itinerary.

Constitution Dock

Constitution Dock on the Derwent is Hobart's working and leisure harbor lined with seafood sheds and converted warehouses. Galleries, restaurants, and boat berths fill the colonial-era waterfront buildings. The name recalls early colonial documents associated with the settlement's founding on this shoreline. Race yachts fill the basin when the Sydney to Hobart fleet arrives.

Farm Gate Market

Farm Gate Market takes over Hobart's Bathurst Street on Sunday mornings. Tasmanian producers bring fruit, vegetables, bread and cheese into the city center. Meat, seafood, preserves and prepared dishes broaden the selection. Participation is limited to goods grown or made in the state, encouraging direct exchange with farmers and small food businesses.

Doone Kennedy Hobart Aquatic Centre

Competition and leisure pools share the Doone Kennedy Hobart Aquatic Centre on the edge of Queens Domain. The facility includes a 50-metre pool, diving and program pools, children's water areas, slides, fitness rooms and change facilities. It supports lap swimming, lessons, clubs, rehabilitation.

Royal Tasmanian Botanical Gardens

Established in 1818 beside the Derwent River, the Royal Tasmanian Botanical Gardens combines cool-climate collections with lawns, heritage trees and sheltered paths. The Subantarctic Plant House, conservatory, Japanese garden and Tasmanian native sections reflect the island's climate and scientific work. Historic walls and structures also record the site's long institutional use.

Rosny Hill Lookout

Rosny Hill Lookout sits on a rise across the Derwent from central Hobart on the eastern shore. Clearings and short paths open toward the city skyline, kunanyi/Mount Wellington, and the river mouth. Sunset visits are common; morning light instead picks out detail on the docks and bridge below. Interpretive signs name the peaks and river reaches in the panorama.

Ninja Stadium

Ninja Stadium, the venue long known as Bellerive Oval or Blundstone Arena, hosts cricket on Hobart's eastern shore. Terraces and a cycling track face Howrah Beach beyond the boundary. Grassy banks remain popular with families during matches when the hill seating fills. Ferry and road links from the western shore feed match-day crowds.

Landscaped outdoor holes at Putters Adventure Golf in Hobart send groups through obstacles and water hazards for casual play. Evening lighting extends summer hours when the waterfront stays busy after dinner. Each hole reads as a short comic challenge rather than regulation golf.
