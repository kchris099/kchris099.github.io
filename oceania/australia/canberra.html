Canberra

Canberra was selected as the site for Australia's capital in 1908 as a compromise between rivals Sydney and Melbourne. American architect Walter Burley Griffin designed the city with a focus on geometric motifs and integration with the natural landscape. Construction began in 1913, and it officially became the seat of government in 1927. Today, Canberra houses national monuments, museums, and institutions that reflect Australia's political and local history.

Australian War Memorial

Halls and cloisters at the Australian War Memorial in Canberra honor Australians who served or died in war, combining museum galleries with a commemorative shrine. The complex sits in an open ceremonial setting above the city axis. Exhibits and the Roll of Honour share the same national brief.

Mount Ainslie Lookout

Mount Ainslie Lookout aligns directly with the Australian War Memorial, Anzac Parade, Old Parliament House and Parliament House along Canberra's central land axis. The summit can be reached by road or walking tracks through the surrounding nature reserve. Directional panels identify the planned city, lake, suburbs and distant ranges visible from the paved terrace.

Old Parliament House

From 1927 until 1988, this low white building housed Australia's federal parliament. Now the Museum of Australian Democracy, Old Parliament House retains the House and Senate chambers, party rooms, press spaces and offices used during six decades of national politics. Exhibitions examine elections, protest, citizenship and the workings of representative government.

04 AdmissionFree

Canberra Theatre Centre

Located in Canberra, the Canberra Theatre Centre offers a modern building with three venues catering to various audience sizes. From large and historic to intimate, this premier performing arts venue hosts a wide range of entertainment options, from classic plays by Bell Shakespeare and performances by the Australian Ballet to contemporary shows and concerts. It's a must-visit for those looking for live entertainment experiences in Australia's creative capital.

Australian National Botanic Gardens

Roughly 90 hectares at the Australian National Botanic Gardens hold more than 4,000 Australian plant species across themed plantings such as rainforest gully, eucalypt lawn, and rock garden. Paths thread the collections for a botanical reading of the continent. Lawns and shade support longer walks between beds.

06 AdmissionFree

National Arboretum Canberra

The National Arboretum Canberra is a vast botanical garden featuring 94 forests of rare, endangered, and symbolic trees from around the world. Established following the devastating 2003 bushfires, the site covers 250 hectares and offers extensive walking and cycling trails. The Village Centre provides panoramic views of Canberra and educational exhibits on tree conservation.

National Zoo & Aquarium

Canberra's National Zoo and Aquarium combines land-animal exhibits with marine tanks on one campus. Native Australian species share the grounds with animals from other continents in landscaped enclosures. Paths between the zoo yards and aquarium halls let a single visit cover both collections. Keeper talks schedule feeding times that draw crowds to key yards.

08 AdmissionFree

Royal Australian Mint

Australia's circulating coins are produced at the Royal Australian Mint in Deakin. Public galleries overlook parts of the factory and explain coin design, metal preparation, striking, quality control, and the change from pounds to decimal currency. Displays also cover commemorative issues and medals, while production activity varies with the mint's working schedule.

Stromlo Forest Park

Cycling circuits and a grass running track at Stromlo Forest Park west of Canberra draw runners and riders across former pine country. Paths and open ground support training days and casual loops. The park reads as an outdoor sports landscape rather than a formal botanic garden.

Cockington Green Gardens

Miniature British villages and international landmarks fill carefully tended beds at Cockington Green Gardens near Canberra. Paths wind among the scaled buildings and plantings. The site pairs model architecture with garden walking rather than full-size civic monuments.
