Canberra

Canberra was selected as the site for Australia's capital in 1908 as a compromise between rivals Sydney and Melbourne. American architect Walter Burley Griffin designed the city with a focus on geometric motifs and integration with the natural landscape. Construction began in 1913, and it officially became the seat of government in 1927. Today, Canberra houses national monuments, museums, and institutions that reflect Australia's political and local history.

Mount Ainslie Lookout

The Mount Ainslie Lookout in Canberra offers a paved observation deck with panoramic vistas and information plaques detailing distant landmarks. From this viewpoint, visitors can enjoy views of the capital city and surrounding areas, including main landmarks such as the War Memorial, ANZAC Parade, Old Parliament House, and the striking architecture of Parliament House. While there are no public toilets available at the site, it is just a one-minute walk from the carpark.

Old Parliament House

Old Parliament House served as the seat of the Parliament of Australia from 1927 to 1988. Located in Canberra, the historic building now functions as the Museum of Australian Democracy, offering exhibitions on the nation's political history and democratic heritage. Visitors can explore the preserved legislative chambers, prime minister's office, and historic press gallery.

Australian National Botanic Gardens is a public park in Canberra, Australia. Encompassing approximately 90 hectares, the gardens boast over 4000 species of flora from various Australian ecosystems. National Arboretum Canberra

The National Arboretum Canberra is a vast botanical garden featuring 94 forests of rare, endangered, and symbolic trees from around the world. Established following the devastating 2003 bushfires, the site covers 250 hectares and offers extensive walking and cycling trails. The Village Centre provides panoramic views of Canberra and educational exhibits on tree conservation.
