Auckland

The Crown purchased land from Māori in 1840 for a colonial capital on twin harbours. Wellington later took the capital, but Auckland kept the largest urban concentration through maritime trade. Volcanic cones and the Waitematā waterfront frame that harbour-city origin. Māori pā sites on volcanic cones predate and overlook the colonial street grid. Ferry links to Devonport keep the harbour identity central.

Sky Tower

Auckland's Sky Tower rises 328 meters above the city center as a telecommunications structure and observation attraction completed in the 1990s. Enclosed decks look over the isthmus, harbors, volcanic cones and islands in clear weather, while commercial jumps operate from the tower. Visibility, dining and activity packages change.

Saint Patrick's Cathedral

Auckland's Catholic cathedral grew from a small nineteenth-century chapel on land associated with Bishop Jean-Baptiste Pompallier; restoration strengthened the building, while Mass, music and diocesan ceremonies continue in the city center. Successive Gothic Revival additions produced the present nave, transepts, tower, stained glass and sanctuary dedicated to Saint Patrick and Saint Joseph.

Aotea Square

Beside Queen Street, Aotea Square holds rallies, outdoor concerts, and markets, with Christmas programs of live music, circus acts, visual arts, outdoor cinema, and Santa visits. Regular markets sell New Zealand fashion, retro goods, food, fabric, jewellery, and furniture. The paved civic room stays programmed year-round.

Sweet Axe Throwing Co. Is an indoor recreational facility in Auckland offering the unique and increasingly popular activity of urban axe throwing. Trained instructors guide visitors through safety protocols and throwing techniques before they compete in games and tournaments. It provides a dynamic, unconventional entertainment experience for groups and events.

Spark Arena

Spark Arena opened near Auckland's waterfront in 2007 as a large indoor venue for concerts, sport and public events. Tiered seating surrounds a flexible central floor that can be reconfigured for basketball, stage productions and ceremonies. Its location beside the rail corridor and former industrial land reflects the redevelopment of the eastern city center into a mixed entertainment and residential district.

Auckland Zoo

Auckland Zoo has occupied Western Springs since 1922, developing from traditional cages toward larger habitat-based areas and conservation programs. New Zealand species, international animals, veterinary work, breeding, and education shape the grounds. Species counts and exhibits change, while current welfare standards and construction matter more than claiming a complete global tour in a few hours.

Michael Joseph Savage Memorial Park

A white obelisk and sunken garden mark the burial place of New Zealand prime minister Michael Joseph Savage on Bastion Point. Formal lawns, hedges and paths frame views over Waitemata Harbour toward the city and Rangitoto. Completed after Savage's death in 1940, the memorial occupies a headland with a much longer Maori history connected to Ngati Whatua and later disputes over land and occupation.

Bastion Point

Takaparawhau, commonly called Bastion Point, overlooks Waitemata Harbour and carries deep significance for Ngati Whatua Orakei. A 506-day occupation in 1977-78 resisted state plans to sell ancestral land and became a major event in modern Maori land-rights history. Roads, lawns, memorials, marae connections, and harbor views occupy a politically charged cultural landscape.

SEA LIFE Kelly Tarlton's Aquarium

Diver and explorer Kelly Tarlton created an aquarium in former sewage tanks beneath Auckland's waterfront, opening in 1985 with curved acrylic tunnels and conveyor viewing. Marine exhibits have included sharks, rays, penguins and New Zealand coastal life. Ownership, species, encounters and display names change, while the adaptive engineering remains part of the attraction's history.

Achilles Point

Rocky lookouts at Achilles Point commemorate the 1939 Battle of the River Plate, with Maori carvings honouring historical chiefs. Platforms face the bay and offshore islands on Auckland's eastern shore. Memorial sculpture and open sea views share the same headland.
