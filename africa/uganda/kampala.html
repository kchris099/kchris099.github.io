Kampala

Kampala rose on Kasubi and Mengo hills as the seat of the Buganda kingdom before British colonial rule made it Uganda's administrative capital. Royal tombs, Anglican and Catholic cathedrals, and independence-era monuments record Ganda statecraft and twentieth-century nation-building on the Lake Victoria plateau. Kabaka trails and lake-view ridges connect Buganda royal sites to Lake Victoria basin neighbourhoods.

Kabaka's Palace

Also called Lubiri Palace, Kabaka's Palace has housed Buganda kingdom rulers since the nineteenth century. Courtyards, perimeter walls, and tower forms organize the compound when grounds are open. The residence remains a central reference point for Buganda royal history within Kampala.

St Mary's Cathedral Rubaga

Saint Mary's Cathedral crowns Rubaga Hill as the seat of Kampala's Roman Catholic archdiocese. Built in the early twentieth century, its twin towers, brick exterior, broad nave, stained glass, and memorials reflect the growth of Catholic missions in Buganda. The cathedral remains active for worship and diocesan events, while the hilltop grounds open views over the surrounding city.

0.3025° N / 32.5520° E

Uganda Martyrs Catholic Shrine Basilica, Namugongo

Uganda Martyrs Catholic Shrine Basilica, Namugongo is a Roman Catholic basilica and memorial shrine with a unique hut-shaped exterior. The shrine commemorates the lives of 32 young men who were killed between 1885 and 1887 for refusing to renounce Christianity. The current building was constructed in 1967 and completed in 1975, officially opened by Papal envoy Sergio Cardinal Pignedoli.

0.3856° N / 32.6514° E

Munyonyo Martyrs' Shrine - Uganda Martyrs Basilica

Munyonyo Martyrs' Shrine, also called the Uganda Martyrs Basilica, remembers the late nineteenth-century Christian martyrs of Buganda along Kampala's lakeshore approach. The modern basilica and shrine grounds form a major pilgrimage stop for Ugandan Catholics. Outside Mass the exterior and principal interior spaces are usually available for quiet visits.

0.2379° N / 32.6176° E

Uganda National Mosque

The Uganda National Mosque rises on Old Kampala Hill, built with Libyan support and opened in the 2000s, and the building's former Gaddafi name records the political circumstances of its construction, while its hilltop position makes it a prominent part of Kampala's skyline. A large prayer hall, patterned dome, courtyard and tall minaret support Uganda's Muslim community and major religious gatherings.

Namirembe Cathedral

Namirembe Cathedral crowns Namirembe Hill, replacing a grass-thatched church that stood here from 1903. The later masonry cathedral keeps the hilltop parish role for Kampala Anglican worship. Outside service hours the exterior and main interior spaces are usually open to view.

Independence Monument

The Independence Monument in Uganda is a significant symbol of the country's heritage and history. Situated along Speke Road in Kampala, it commemorates the granting of independence to Uganda by British colonial administrators on October 9, 1962. The monument depicts a man unwrapping and raising a child to the sky, symbolizing the birth of a new nation freed from colonization.

0.3155° N / 32.5817° E

Kampala National Theatre

Kampala National Theatre is a hub of culture and creativity located Uganda's capital. This venue comes alive on Monday nights with an exhilarating jam session that invites both seasoned musicians and daring amateurs to share their talents, creating an atmosphere brimming with joy and appreciation for music. The last Monday of each month transforms.

Baha'i House of Worship

Kampala's Bahá'í House of Worship crowns a hill with gardens and a domed temple open to visitors for quiet reflection and guided introductions to the faith. Paths through landscaped grounds lead to the worship hall used by the community. The site is one of the continental Bahá'í temples and a recognizable skyline marker above the city.

Kasubi Tombs

The Kasubi Tombs, dating to 1882, are the burial ground of four Buganda kabakas and other royals inside the great circular thatched hall known as Muzibu Azaala Mpanga. Traditional plant materials and ceremonial spaces define a living royal shrine on Kampala's edge. Fires and rebuilding have tested the complex, yet it remains central to Buganda heritage.
