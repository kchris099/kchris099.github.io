Algiers

Algiers grew from the Phoenician port of Icosium into a major Ottoman and French colonial capital overlooking the Bay of Algiers. The Casbah's whitewashed lanes, Ottoman palaces, and Ketchaoua Mosque record centuries of Berber, Arab, and European rule, while Haussmann-era boulevards and Martyrs Memorial reflect twentieth-century independence struggles and modern Algerian statehood.

Martyrs Memorial

Three concrete forms shaped like palm leaves meet above Maqam Echahid, the monument raised in Algiers in 1982 for the twentieth anniversary of independence. Sculpted figures at the base represent different phases of the liberation struggle. The hilltop composition is visible across much of the capital and stands beside museums devoted to the war and national memory.

Botanical Garden Hamma

Botanical Garden Hamma is an elegant 1800s French- & English-style garden featuring fountains, a small zoo, and a fine-art museum. The garden has trees from various locations and species, along with restaurants and fast food options. While lacking guides, informative plates next to the trees provide details for interested visitors in three languages. With an entry price of 150da for adults and 100da for kids, it's to maintain cleanliness during a visit.

Algiers Opera House

Algiers Opera House stages theatre, dance, and orchestral programs in a purpose-built auditorium for the capital. Ticketed evening performances are the usual way inside; daytime visits, when offered, focus on the foyer and house rather than backstage areas. The venue anchors the city's formal performing-arts calendar alongside other civic halls.

04 AdmissionFree

Church of Notre Dame d'Afrique

Notre-Dame d'Afrique stands on a coastal ridge above Bologhine, with its dome and semicircular apse facing the Bay of Algiers. Completed in the nineteenth century, the basilica combines Byzantine and Romanesque forms, patterned interiors, mosaics, and votive decoration. An inscription in the apse invokes protection for both Christians and Muslims.

05 AdmissionFree

La grande poste of Algiers

White stone facades, horseshoe arches, domes, and mosaic panels distinguish Algiers' main post office, completed in the early twentieth century. Designed during French rule in a neo-Moorish style, the building occupies a prominent position near the central boulevard and metro station. Its steps and forecourt have also served as a gathering point during public demonstrations.

36.7728° N / 3.0597° E

Martyrs' Square

Martyrs' Square opens as a paved civic plaza in Algiers, edged by surrounding facades and streets that feed into the Old Town fabric. Open pavement leaves room for gatherings, while the square's name marks public memory of the independence struggle. Passersby cross on foot between nearby landmarks without a fixed monument circuit.

07 AdmissionFree

Ketchaoua Mosque casbah

Built as an Ottoman mosque in 1612, Ketchaoua Mosque in the Algiers Casbah later became a Catholic cathedral under French rule and returned to Muslim worship after independence. Facade and main halls remain readable outside prayer times. The Casbah lanes around it keep the building tied to the old city's steep fabric.

36.7850° N / 3.0608° E

08 AdmissionFree

Djamaa el Kebir

Medieval arcades and a broad hypostyle prayer hall define Djamaa el Kebir on the edge of Algiers' Casbah. An inscription dates the Almoravid mosque to the late eleventh century, while its minaret and other elements reflect later rebuilding; reused stone columns and the waterfront setting preserve evidence of the city's layered religious and urban history.

36.7853° N / 3.0643° E

09 AdmissionFree

Great Mosque of Algeria

The Great Mosque of Algeria, also called Djamaa El Djazair, rises above the Bay of Algiers as a modern landmark mosque. Its vast prayer complex was planned for tens of thousands of worshippers, with a tall minaret and extensive underground parking. From the waterfront approach the dome and minaret read clearly against the hillside suburbs behind the capital.

36.7350° N / 3.1402° E

Nelson Mandela Stadium

Football and athletics share the field at Nelson Mandela Stadium in Algiers under federation match calendars. Club and national-team fixtures fill the stands on scheduled evenings, with gates opening from posted approaches. Surrounding roads carry most of the crowd into a working multi-sport bowl.
