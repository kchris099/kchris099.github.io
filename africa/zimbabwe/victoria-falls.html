Victoria Falls

Victoria Falls (Mosi-oa-Tunya) has long held spiritual importance for communities along the Zambezi. David Livingstone's 1855 visit brought the English name into global maps. Colonial-era rail and hotel building created a resort town on the Zimbabwean bank opposite Zambia. The waterfall and gorge remain a shared natural border. Rainforest paths, bridge views, and seasonal spray define visits to one of Africa's major river cataracts.

Victoria Falls

Locally called Mosi-oa-Tunya (The Smoke That Thunders), Victoria Falls pours across a wide basalt cliff with spray visible from marked tracks to viewing platforms and overlooks. Adventure operators base nearby for river and gorge activities. Rainforest paths link the main lookout points on the Zimbabwe side.

The Victoria Falls World Heritage Site

The Victoria Falls World Heritage Site include the dramatic waterfall and the surrounding riverine environment on both the Zambian and Zimbabwean sides. The protected area preserves the unique geological formation of the basalt gorges and the diverse network supported by the constant spray from the falls. It includes a network of trails that provide various vantage points of the cascading Zambezi River.

17.9271° S / 25.8645° E

03 Admission$58

Devil's Cataract

Devil's Cataract is the westernmost curtain of Victoria Falls on the Zimbabwe side, where the Zambezi drops beside viewpoints near the David Livingstone statue, Danger Point, and paths toward the Boiling Pot. Spray and flow change sharply between wet and dry seasons. Most park lookouts on this bank face this section of the falls.

17.9228° S / 25.8475° E

04 Admission$20

Mosi-oa-Tunya National Park

Mosi-oa-Tunya National Park protects the Zambian side of Victoria Falls and a narrow wildlife area along the upper Zambezi River. Paths provide views of the falls in suitable flow, while separate game-drive areas may contain elephants, antelope, giraffes and protected white rhinos under close management. Water level, spray, border procedures, animal welfare and guided-access rules shape different parts of the park.

Victoria Falls National Park

Victoria Falls National Park protects the Zimbabwean viewpoints and rainforest paths facing the main sections of the waterfall on the Zambezi River; spray supports dense vegetation along a sequence of overlooks, while water volume changes dramatically between wet and dry seasons. Slippery paths, intense mist, heat, wildlife, cliff edges, and cross-border conditions require preparation within the historic landscape.

17.8999° S / 25.8166° E

08 Admission$20

Victoria Falls Crocodile Park

The Victoria Falls Crocodile Park is a conservation and educational facility located near the Zambezi River. It houses numerous Nile crocodiles of various sizes, from hatchlings to massive adults. The park offers guided tours that educate visitors about the biology, behavior, and ecological role of these apex predators, providing a safe environment to observe them up close.

17.9009° S / 25.8198° E

09 Admission$17

Zambezi National Park

Zambezi National Park extends upstream from Victoria Falls along the Zimbabwean bank of the river, protecting woodland, floodplain and channels used by wildlife. Game drives and river activities take place away from the waterfall viewpoints, with elephants, buffalo, antelope, hippos and predators present across the wider habitat. Seasonal flooding, heat and road conditions influence access, and sightings vary naturally.
