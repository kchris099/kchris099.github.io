Fez

Fez became Morocco's spiritual and scholarly capital under the Marinid dynasty in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, when theological colleges, fondouks, and tanneries filled Fes el-Bali. The ninth-century Idrisid foundation, the Al-Qarawiyyin university, and the French protectorate's Ville Nouvelle mark imperial patronage, Andalusian refugees, and craft guilds in North Africa's largest walled medina.

Royal palace of Fes

Dar el-Makhzen, the Royal Palace of Fez, occupies a vast enclosed compound whose public face is the brass-studded gates on Place des Alaouites. Ordinary visitors view the ornate gateways and plaza rather than the private palace interiors. The walls mark the edge of the modern ville and the older medina approaches.

Al Attarine Madrasa

Al Attarine Madrasa sits in Fez el-Bali near the spice and perfume markets, built in the fourteenth century as a residential college for religious students. A marble courtyard, carved cedar, and zellij tilework frame the prayer hall. Visitors enter from narrow medina lanes just off the Attarine souk.

Place Seffarine

Coppersmiths work around Place Seffarine in Fes el-Bali, hammering, shaping and repairing trays, pots and cauldrons in small workshops. The irregular square lies near the Qarawiyyin complex and historic craft lanes. Its sounds and working surfaces reflect an active metal trade, while nearby passages lead toward dyers, tanners and other specialized souks.

04 AdmissionFree

Borj Sud

Sixteenth-century Borj Sud keeps a simple square plan without corner bastions, unlike sister fort Borj Nord outside Fez's medina. Access from Bab Jdid leads to a terrace with wide views over the old city. The tower recalls southern defenses beyond the historic walls.

34.0542° N / 4.9703° W

05 AdmissionFree

Bab Boujloud

The present Bab Bou Jeloud dates mainly from 1913, when French Protectorate authorities created a ceremonial triple-arched entrance near an older gate into Fes el-Bali. Blue tile faces the newer city side and green faces the medina, colors associated with Fez and Islam. Through the arches, minarets and food streets lead into the pedestrian old town.

Jnan Sbil

Jnan Sbil, also called Bou Jeloud Gardens, is a historic public park between Fes el-Jdid and Fes el-Bali. Tree-lined paths, fountains, and water channels occupy the green belt that once buffered the royal and medina quarters. City residents use the gardens for shade and afternoon walks.

El Glaoui Palace

Once home to Thami El Glaoui, this Fez medina palace keeps detailed stucco and carved cedar doors from its years as a power seat. Courtyards and rooms open on posted visiting days. Ornament and plan still read as elite residential architecture inside the old city.

09 AdmissionFree

Marinid Tombs

The Marinid Tombs, built in the 14th century, are a collection of mausoleums and graves located on a hill overlooking the city of Fes. The goldstone tombs capture the opulence and beauty of this historical site. While little remains of the original decoration, visitors can still wander through the horseshoe arches and stucco walls to experience a sense of mystery.

34.0699° N / 4.9794° W

10 AdmissionFree

Bab Guissa

Bab Guissa is a 12th-century arched stone city gate that is one of the fourteen entrances to the historic city of Fes. Located beneath the crenellated ramparts of ancient walls, this gateway offers an intriguing starting point for a visit. While walk up from nearby streets, taking a taxi might be more convenient, allowing visitors to leisurely stroll back down and soak in the sights.
