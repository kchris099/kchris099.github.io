Tangier

Carthaginian, Roman, and Arab ports commanded the Strait of Gibraltar before Tangier became an International Zone in the twentieth century. That legal anomaly drew writers and spies into a cosmopolitan medina and ville nouvelle. The kasbah museum and boulevard cafés are leftovers of that extraterritorial era. The American Legation museum occupies a building tied to early U.S.–Morocco diplomacy.

Grand Socco

Grand Socco marks the transition between Tangier's medina and nineteenth- and twentieth-century new town. The circular square, officially Place du 9 Avril 1947, commemorates Sultan Mohammed V's independence-era speech and sits near Cinema Rif, markets, gardens, and St. Andrew's Church. Vendors and traffic patterns change, but the square's connective civic role remains constant.

02 AdmissionFree

Bab Kasbah

Bab Kasbah is a fortified gateway into Tangier's upper kasbah, where steep lanes lead toward the former sultan's palace, houses, small squares and viewpoints over the port. The gate belongs to a living defensive quarter altered under Moroccan and European rule. Its practical role remains visible in the flow of residents and visitors moving between the medina and the elevated neighborhood.

35.7886° N / 5.8149° W

03 AdmissionFree

Bab Al Bahr

Bab al-Bahr, the Sea Gate, opens from Tangier's kasbah toward a terrace above the port and Strait of Gibraltar, and bab Al Bahr, also known as the Kasbah, offers an unparalleled view of the Mediterranean and the southern coast of Spain. Stone walls frame views across shipping lanes, the modern harbor, and, in clear weather, the Spanish coast.

35.7889° N / 5.8121° W

04 AdmissionFree

Phoenician tombs

Rock-cut Phoenician tombs sit between Café Hafa and the upper kasbah entrance in Tangier, carved into the cliff above the sea. Open chambers face the Mediterranean, with Spain sometimes visible on clear days. The site is a compact archaeological stop along the coastal path rather than a large excavated necropolis.

35.7908° N / 5.8200° W

05 AdmissionFree

Marshan Royal Palace

Marshan Palace, a architectural gem in Tangier, has a rich history that dates back to the early 1950s when it was originally built for the Tangier International Zone Legislative Assembly. Following Morocco's independence in 1956, it transitioned into a royal property and underwent renovations by King Mohammed VI in the 2010s to host diplomatic events.

35.7905° N / 5.8257° W

06 AdmissionFree

Tanja Marina Bay

Rows of boats fill Tanja Marina Bay beside cafes and restaurants along a pedestrian boulevard. Night lighting and evening strolls keep the waterfront busy for families after dark. The urban marina concentrates leisure traffic on Tangier's newer harbour edge.

35.7841° N / 5.7968° W

07 AdmissionFree

Villa Harris Park

Built at the end of the nineteenth century for British journalist Walter Burton Harris, Villa Harris overlooks the Mediterranean on Tangier's eastern side. The restored residence now houses a museum devoted to two centuries of art in Morocco and the wider Mediterranean. Gardens and parkland surround the villa. Together they preserve part of the coastal estate while providing public green space beyond the museum rooms.

35.7831° N / 5.7652° W

08 AdmissionFree

Rmilat Park

Also called Parc Perdicaris, wooded Rmilat Park looks out over the Alboran Sea from Tangier's hills. Picnic clearings and climbing trails draw exercise and weekend outings under the canopy. Paths and shade form the main public facilities across the slopes.

35.7930° N / 5.8624° W

Cap Spartel

Cap Spartel is the northwestern tip of Africa near Tangier, where the Atlantic meets the Mediterranean at the Strait of Gibraltar. A lighthouse dating to 1864 stands on the promontory. Cliffs and coastal paths look west toward open ocean and east toward the narrower strait waters.

35.7910° N / 5.9236° W

10 AdmissionFree

Cape Spartel

Cape Spartel forms a wooded headland west of Tangier where the Atlantic approaches the Strait of Gibraltar. A lighthouse completed in the nineteenth century stands above steep coastal slopes and open-water viewpoints. The promontory has long carried navigational importance because of its position at the entrance to the strait, while nearby forest and cliffs create a landscape distinct from Tangier's dense urban waterfront.

35.7856° N / 5.9314° W
