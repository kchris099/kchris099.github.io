Marrakesh

Marrakesh was founded in 1070 by the Almoravid ruler Yusuf ibn Tashfin and later served as capital for the Almohad caliphs who built the Kutubiyya mosque and city walls. Saadian tombs, Bahia Palace, and the Jemaa el-Fna square record how trans-Saharan trade, Andalusian artisans, and French colonial planning turned the red-walled city into a court centre linking the Atlas foothills to Atlantic ports.

Bahia Palace

Late nineteenth-century Bahia Palace, the Palace of the Brilliant, spreads across more than eight hectares of rooms and gardens in Marrakech. Courtyards and painted ceilings organize the route when open. Planted grounds soften the transition from dense medina streets.

Marrakech train station

Marrakech's railway station opened in its present form in 2008 with a large glazed hall, geometric screens, tilework, shops and cafes. Contemporary transport design is combined with decorative references to Moroccan architecture, while platforms connect the city with the national intercity network. The building remains a busy working station close to hotels and central avenues.

Jemaa el-Fnaa

Jemaa el-Fnaa changes character through the day as juice sellers, traders, storytellers, musicians and food stalls occupy Marrakesh's central medina square. Its cultural recognition reflects oral performance and social tradition as much as the physical place, and dense crowds and commercial activity remain part of the setting, while animal entertainment raises concerns.

Souk Semmarine

A broad covered lane leads north from Jemaa el-Fnaa through Souk Semmarine. Shops sell textiles, clothing and leather goods alongside metalwork, lamps, spices and tourist merchandise, while smaller passages branch into specialized workshops. Products, bargaining practices and shop quality vary across this active part of Marrakesh's commercial network.

Madrasa Ben Youssef

Sixteenth-century Madrasa Ben Youssef still shows carved stucco, zellij tile, and cedar ceilings around a central basin. Student cells open onto galleries above the court. The prayer hall and ornamented portals rank among Marrakech's surviving Islamic teaching interiors.

31.6321° N / 7.9863° W

Koutoubia

The twelfth-century Koutoubia minaret rises near Djemaa el-Fna and remains visible across much of Marrakech. Narrow alleys from the square lead into souks of crafts and spices. The mosque's tower sets the city's vertical landmark beside the open plaza.

Park Arsat Moulay Abdesalam

Arsat Moulay Abdeslam is a central Marrakesh park of olive trees, lawns, and play areas near the Koutoubia. Paved walks and shade make it a common pause between medina streets and newer avenues. Local families use the grass and playgrounds through the cooler parts of the day.

Ensemble Artisanal

Ensemble Artisanal Marrakech is a craft shopping complex about a ten-minute walk from Jemaa el-Fnaa. Workshops and stalls sell handmade leather goods, kitchenware, clothing, perfume, and housewares under fixed-price halls. The layout suits visitors who prefer quieter bargaining than in the deep souks.

09 AdmissionMAD 170

Jardin Majorelle

French painter Jacques Majorelle developed this garden over several decades around a cubist studio painted in the blue now associated with his name. Cacti, palms, bamboo, pools, paths, and plants from several climates create a carefully composed landscape later restored by Yves Saint Laurent and Pierre Berge. Museums and timed entry operate separately within the busy complex.

31.6412° N / 8.0032° W

Bab Agnaou

Almohad builders created Bab Agnaou in the late twelfth century as a ceremonial entrance to Marrakesh's royal kasbah. Carved stone bands, a horseshoe arch, and quranic inscriptions distinguish it from the red earthen city walls. The gate led toward palaces and administrative quarters.

31.6176° N / 7.9907° W
