Zanzibar Stone Town

Under nineteenth-century Omani rule, Stone Town became Zanzibar's Swahili port for cloves and the slave trade. Coral-stone houses, narrow alleys, and carved wooden doors mix Arab, Persian, Indian, and European building habits. The 1896 Anglo-Zanzibar conflict lasted under an hour yet fixed British dominance. UNESCO listing now covers that multicultural harbour fabric.

Emerson on Hurumzi

An 1800s villa holds Emerson on Hurumzi in Stone Town, with rooms that can face the ocean and a rooftop restaurant looking over the Indian Ocean and nearby mosques. Local stories link the name to the freeing of enslaved people. Historic fabric and elevated views define the stay.

Emerson Spice

Emerson Spice occupies a former elite Swahili residence in Zanzibar's Stone Town, converted into a hotel with rooms and suites overlooking the Zanzibar Channel. Carved doors, inner courtyards, and rooftop terraces retain the scale of the old merchant house. Guests and diners encounter the building as living heritage architecture set among the narrow lanes between the harbour and the bazaar streets.

Old Fort

Seventeenth-century Omani rulers raised the Old Fort in Stone Town against the Portuguese; market stalls and art shows now occupy the courts near the House of Wonders and Old Dispensary. Walls and towers form the walk when grounds are open. Defense stonework frames today's civic uses.

The Forodhani of Zanzibar

Landscaped paths and seating fill the area by day, while evening food stalls prepare seafood, breads, grilled meat, sugarcane juice, and other dishes under changing hygiene and vendor conditions. Forodhani Gardens opens along Stone Town's waterfront between the Old Fort and harbor. The park's history is tied to colonial waterfront planning and later renovation, not its night-market atmosphere.

Outside Stone Town, Cheetah's Rock is a private wildlife facility outside Stone Town that offers controlled encounters with captive and rescued animals. Species, handling practices, transport, and visitor programs change, while the experience raises welfare and conservation questions.

Zanzibar Spice Community Shop

Zanzibar Spice Community Shop operates on Market Street in Stone Town. Its counters carry cloves, cinnamon and nutmeg, together with teas and related island products. Soaps, oils and small crafts extend the retail selection. The shop reflects Zanzibar's continuing commercial association with spice cultivation and trade, although the origin and production claims attached to individual goods depend on the seller.

Christ Church Cathedral

Christ Church Cathedral was built by the Anglican mission on the site of Zanzibar's former slave market in the late nineteenth century. Gothic and local building elements enclose memorials, an altar traditionally associated with the whipping post location, and interpretation of slavery and abolition; the church remains active, while the surrounding memorial complex addresses a violent history.

East Africa Slave Trade Exhibition (Slave Chambers - Cathedoral Church Of Christ)

On the former slave-market site in Stone Town, the East Africa Slave Trade Exhibition includes cramped underground chambers where enslaved people were held before sale, beside Christ Church Cathedral. Exterior and main spaces can usually be seen outside services. The chambers keep the market's history concrete.

09 AdmissionFree

Tortoise Sanctuary

On Changuu (Prison Island) off Stone Town, a sanctuary protects Aldabra giant tortoises that roam sandy clearings among colonial-era ruins. The island once held a prison linked to the slave trade; today paths lead between enclosure areas and the old fort walls. Boat trips from Stone Town land visitors who walk the short circuit to see the tortoises and the shoreline views back toward Unguja.

6.1195° S / 39.1666° E
