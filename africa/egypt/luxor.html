Luxor

Luxor occupies ancient Thebes, capital of Egypt's New Kingdom from the sixteenth to eleventh centuries BCE. Pharaohs raised temples at Karnak and Luxor, royal tombs in the Valley of the Kings, and mortuary shrines on the west bank. The Avenue of Sphinxes and surviving colossi record how Theban priests and kings projected divine authority along the Nile.

Luxor Temple

Luxor Temple stands on the east bank of the Nile within the modern city. Amenhotep III built much of the sanctuary, and Ramesses II added a monumental forecourt, pylons and statues. Later Egyptian rulers, Romans, Christians and Muslims adapted the complex, while the restored Avenue of Sphinxes links its ceremonial axis with Karnak.

Avenue of Sphinxes

The Avenue of Sphinxes is the processional way that once linked Luxor Temple and Karnak with rows of criosphinxes and human-headed sphinxes. Cleared and partly restored stretches now let walkers follow the ancient axis between the two temple compounds on Luxor's East Bank.

Mortuary Temple of Hatshepsut

Hatshepsut's mortuary temple rises in three terraces beneath the cliffs of Deir el-Bahari on Luxor's west bank. Architect Senenmut organized ramps, colonnades, chapels, reliefs, and sanctuaries around the female pharaoh's royal cult and the god Amun. It is separate from the Valley of the Kings, and modern reconstruction has clarified the original form while leaving ancient carving and later damage visible.

Valley of the Kings

The Valley of the Kings holds rock-cut tombs of New Kingdom pharaohs on Luxor's West Bank, including decorated corridors and burial chambers cut into the desert cliffs. A visitor centre and shuttle serve the tomb entrances, where rotating openings manage heat and conservation.

25.7402° N / 32.6014° E

Deir el-Medina

Deir el-Medina is an ancient village established around 1492 BCE, where workers who built royal tombs and a Ptolemaic temple resided. It's a well-preserved gem that offers insight into the daily lives of ordinary Egyptians. The narrow streets and simple dwellings stand in contrast to the lavish tombs they helped create in the nearby Valley of the Kings.

Colossi of Memnon

Two seated quartzite figures of Amenhotep III remain beside the road across Luxor's west bank. Each once fronted a vast mortuary temple largely destroyed by flood, earthquake, and stone reuse. Greek and Roman visitors associated the northern statue's dawn sounds with the hero Memnon and carved inscriptions on its legs, while ongoing excavation reveals more of the lost temple complex behind them.

Mortuary Temple of Seti I

Seti I began his mortuary temple at Qurna, and ramesses II completed parts of it after his father's death, and royal mummies are not displayed here; those belong to museum collections. Reliefs, columns, chapels, and sanctuaries honored Seti, earlier kings, and major deities within the Theban funerary landscape.

Karnak

Karnak spreads as a vast temple complex at Luxor with more than two hundred structures, including the great Amen-Ra temple. Well-preserved pylons, courts, and columns fill the archaeological ground. On-site exploration centres on Karnak itself rather than distant West Bank monuments sold as add-on packages.

25.7188° N / 32.6573° E

Precinct of Amun-Re

The Precinct of Amun-Re forms the core of Karnak, surrounded by related precincts of Mut and Montu and traces of Amenhotep IV's dismantled works. Hypostyle halls, obelisks, and sacred lakes fill the vast enclosure that dominated Theban religious life.

25.7178° N / 32.6580° E

10 AdmissionEGP 200

Mut Temple

The Precinct of Mut occupies the southern part of the Karnak temple complex beside a sacred crescent-shaped lake. Successive pharaohs built gateways, courts, temples, and hundreds of statues of the lioness goddess Sekhmet around worship of Mut, consort of Amun; excavation and restoration continue across the enclosure, which is quieter and less complete than Karnak's main Amun precinct.

25.7117° N / 32.6552° E
