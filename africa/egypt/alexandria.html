Alexandria

Alexandria was founded by Alexander the Great in 331 BCE and later housed the Ptolemaic court, the Pharos lighthouse, and a celebrated library. Roman, Byzantine, and Islamic layers survive in catacombs, theatres, and fortifications along the Mediterranean. The modern corniche city still anchors Egypt's north coast trade and cultural life. Qaitbay Citadel and the modern library complex extend the city's role as Egypt's principal Mediterranean port.

Montaza Palace

King Fuad I developed Montaza's coastal estate in Alexandria, where the larger Al-Haramlik Palace was completed in the 1930s beside an earlier royal residence. Towers, arcades, gardens, beaches, and sea views formed a summer retreat for Egypt's monarchy. Access to palace interiors is often restricted, so the landscaped grounds and exterior architecture carry most of the public experience.

Alexandria Bibliotheca

The Bibliotheca Alexandrina opened in 2002 on the Alexandria waterfront as a modern library complex meant to echo the ancient Mouseion's scholarly role. A tilted circular form, reading rooms, museums, and planetarium share the campus beside the Mediterranean corniche.

Catacombs of Kom el Shoqafa

A spiral stair descends into the Catacombs of Kom el Shoqafa, a Roman-period burial complex cut beneath western Alexandria. Tomb chambers, banquet rooms, sarcophagi, and reliefs combine Egyptian deities with Greek and Roman dress and artistic conventions; several underground levels survive, although groundwater affects the lowest areas and confined passages require careful movement.

31.1787° N / 29.8931° E

Serapeum of Alexandria

Pompey's Pillar rises from the ruins of Alexandria's Serapeum, a sanctuary dedicated to Serapis on the city's ancient acropolis. The single red-granite column dates to the Roman imperial period and stands near sphinxes, subterranean galleries, and foundations from a complex repeatedly damaged in antiquity. Its modern name is misleading, since the monument has no direct connection to the Roman general Pompey.

31.1823° N / 29.8963° E

Sidi Morsi Abu al-Abbas Mosque, alexandria

Near downtown Alexandria, Sidi Morsi Abu al-Abbas Mosque holds ornate halls and the mausoleum of a thirteenth-century saint. Access has recently been limited to men, with plans discussed to reopen sections for women. Exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside prayer times when rules allow.

Alexandria Naval Unknown Soldier Memorial

Alexandria's Naval Unknown Soldier Memorial honours service members killed in war whose names remain unknown. The monument occupies open plaza or roadside ground for public remembrance. Ceremonies and quiet visits share the same outdoor setting by the memorial stonework.

Mohamed Ali Pasha Statue

The equestrian statue of Mohamed Ali Pasha reached Consuls Square, now Mansheya, in December 1872 after earlier display in France. It commemorates the Ottoman-era ruler who reshaped Egypt's military and administration. The monument still anchors the historic square even where surrounding upkeep varies.

31.1974° N / 29.8929° E

08 AdmissionFree

Alexandria Ancient Roman Theater

Excavations at Kom el-Dikka uncovered a small Roman theater in central Alexandria. Marble seating, a semicircular orchestra, columns, baths, lecture halls, houses and mosaics reveal a district used from the Roman into the Byzantine period. The structure may have supported performances and later public meetings, while nearby remains explain urban life beyond the stage itself.

31.1945° N / 29.9039° E

09 AdmissionFree

Alexandria Opera House

Alexandria Opera House occupies the former Mohamed Ali Theatre, built in 1918 and opened in the early 1920s, and renamed for composer Sayed Darwish in 1962, the ornate theater later underwent major restoration and reopened in 2004. Opera, ballet, orchestral music, Arab music, recitals and festivals use its stage.

31.1973° N / 29.9016° E

St. Mark's Cathedral in Alexandria

The Coptic Orthodox Cathedral of St Mark stands near Alexandria's traditional center of early Christianity. Successive churches were built, destroyed and reconstructed on a site associated by church tradition with St Mark the Evangelist, whose relics were partly returned from Venice in 1968. The modern cathedral contains icons, carved screens, chapels and a shrine linked to the patriarchal history of the Coptic Church.

31.1983° N / 29.8995° E
