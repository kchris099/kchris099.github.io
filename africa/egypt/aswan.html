Aswan

Pharaonic garrisons watched the First Cataract while Nubian gold and ivory moved north. The High Dam of the 1960s drowned villages and forced the relocation of temples such as Abu Simbel's neighbours. Modern Aswan is therefore as much an engineering landscape as a Nile frontier town. Nubian villages relocated above the reservoir keep language and craft traditions beside the new shoreline.

Philae Temple

Ancient temples dedicated to Isis and related deities were moved from Philae to Agilkia Island during the international campaign that preceded flooding by the Aswan dams. Pylons, courts, reliefs, columns and later Christian inscriptions record worship across Ptolemaic, Roman and Byzantine periods. Boats provide a regular approach, making the island setting part of the visit as well as a result of modern conservation engineering.

Unfinished Obelisk

Attributed to Queen Hatshepsut of the Eighteenth Dynasty, the Unfinished Obelisk still lies in Aswan's granite quarry. Tool marks show how ancient stoneworkers shaped monoliths more than three thousand years ago. The abandoned shaft remains in the open quarry rather than standing as a finished temple monument.

Coptic Orthodox Cathedral of the Archangel Michael

The Cathedral of the Archangel Michael rises in modern Aswan near the Nubian Museum and Nile-side districts. Domes, towers, icons, painted biblical scenes, carved screens and a broad nave support the city's Coptic Orthodox community. Services and religious practice remain central to the building, so access follows the rhythms and customs of an active place of worship. The cathedral anchors a modern Christian community in Aswan.

04 AdmissionEGP 200

The High Dam

Completed in 1970, the Aswan High Dam controls the Nile at the northern end of Lake Nasser. Its construction expanded irrigation reliability and hydroelectric generation while ending the old annual flood cycle, displacing Nubian communities, and prompting the relocation of major archaeological sites. Road viewpoints reveal the scale of the embankment and reservoir, but the dam remains critical national infrastructure with controlled access.

23.9709° N / 32.8773° E

05 AdmissionEGP 200

Kalabsha Temple

The Kalabsha Temple, also known as the Temple of Kakabshah or Mandulis, is a revered ancient Nubian temple dedicated to the sun god. It was built in 30 B.C. During the reign of Roman Emperor Augustus and was later relocated due to construction of the Aswan High Dam. Visitors can admire reliefs throughout the site and witness an elephant depicted on one stone block outside.

Aswan Old Souks

Sharia el-Souk runs through central Aswan as a long commercial street used by residents and travelers. Food sections sell spices and produce alongside prepared dishes. Other stalls carry Nubian clothing, textiles, household goods, perfumes, henna and baskets. The mix changes by block and time of day, with the heaviest local trade in the morning.

Sharia as Souq Market

Sharia as Souq Market in Aswan is a must-visit for souvenir hunters. The market offers a wide range of Egyptian items, from perfumes and spices to basketry and scarves, all sold by local vendors. The opening and closing times are flexible, adding to the authentic experience.

08 AdmissionEGP 200

Tombs of the Nobles in Aswan

Rock-cut tombs line the cliffs of Qubbet el-Hawa on the Nile's west bank opposite Aswan. Officials who governed Egypt's southern frontier during the Old and Middle Kingdoms were buried here with inscriptions describing expeditions, family ties, titles, and trade with Nubia.

24.1025° N / 32.8905° E

09 AdmissionEGP 200

Dome of Abu Al-Hawa

A small domed Islamic shrine crowns the ridge of Qubbet el-Hawa above Aswan's Tombs of the Nobles. The hill's Arabic name, often translated as Dome of the Wind, refers to this summit landmark. Sandy paths rise past archaeological areas to broad views of the Nile, Elephantine Island, cultivated riverbanks and the modern city. The exposed ridge receives strong sun and wind.

24.1019° N / 32.8891° E

10 AdmissionEGP 20

Aswan Botanical Garden

Kitchener's Island, now commonly called Aswan Botanical Garden, occupies a narrow strip in the Nile west of Elephantine Island. Exotic and regional trees, palms, flowering plants, shaded paths, and riverside views grew from plant collections established during the British colonial period. Small boats reach the garden, whose greenery contrasts with Aswan's granite hills and arid banks.
