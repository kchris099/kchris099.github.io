Aswan

Pharaonic garrisons watched the First Cataract while Nubian gold and ivory moved north. The High Dam of the 1960s drowned villages and forced the relocation of temples such as Abu Simbel's neighbours. Modern Aswan is therefore as much an engineering landscape as a Nile frontier town. Nubian villages relocated above the reservoir keep language and craft traditions beside the new shoreline.

Philae Temple

Philae Temple, dedicated to the goddess Isis, is a complex located on an island in the middle of the Nile River. Accessible only by boat, it offers a unique and experience. The temple has well-preserved patterns on its walls and columns, creating a poetic and scenery surrounded by the river. Visitors are advised to come with licensed guides as those at the location may provide false information.

Unfinished Obelisk

Unfinished Obelisk is a monument in Aswan, Egypt. This monumental structure, attributed to Queen Hatshepsut of the 18th dynasty, provides valuable insights into the stone-carving techniques of ancient Egyptians over 3,000 years ago. The memorial stands in an open plaza or roadside setting.

Coptic Orthodox Cathedral of the Archangel Michael

Coptic Orthodox Cathedral of the Archangel Michael is a religious building in Aswan, Egypt. Located near the Nubian Museum, it offers a peaceful atmosphere for visitors to explore. The cathedral's exterior is majestic, while its interior holds an unexpected and mystical charm. The exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside service times.

04 AdmissionEGP 200

The High Dam

The Aswan High Dam is a monumental engineering achievement constructed across the Nile River in the 1960s. It created Lake Nasser, one of the world's largest artificial lakes, and plays a important role in controlling floods, providing irrigation, and generating hydroelectric power for Egypt. The dam offers views of the massive reservoir and the river below.

05 AdmissionEGP 200

Kalabsha Temple

The Kalabsha Temple, also known as the Temple of Kakabshah or Mandulis, is a revered ancient Nubian temple dedicated to the sun god. It was built in 30 B.C. During the reign of Roman Emperor Augustus and was later relocated due to construction of the Aswan High Dam. Visitors can admire reliefs throughout the site and witness an elephant depicted on one stone block outside.

Aswan Old Souks

Aswan Old Souks, also known as Sharia as-Souq, is a active bazaar offering a wide variety of goods and local products. From freshly cooked dishes to traditional Nubian outfits and Sudanese trinkets, the market is a busy hub for shopping. Visitors can find everything from spices and perfumes to souvenirs, toys, and even car parts. The market's proximity to the Nile makes it easily accessible from anywhere in town.

Sharia as Souq Market

Sharia as Souq Market in Aswan is a must-visit for souvenir hunters. The market offers a wide range of Egyptian items, from perfumes and spices to basketry and scarves, all sold by local vendors. The opening and closing times are flexible, adding to the authentic experience.

Tombs of the Nobles in Aswan

In the city of Aswan, Egypt, visitors can immerse themselves in a rich history of history and culture. The Tombs of the Nobles offer a glimpse into ancient Egyptian life, with necropolises dating back to the Old, Middle, and New Kingdoms. Perched on a hilltop overlooking the Nile River, these tombs belong to officials and nobles from antiquity. The site is still an active excavation area, promising potential discoveries in the future.

Dome of Abu Al-Hawa

Dome of Abu Al-Hawa, also known as Qubbet el-Hawa, is a small dome-like structure located on top of a sand hill housing noble tombs. Although there's not much to see at the tomb itself, the hike up offers panoramic views of Aswan City, the Nile River, and nearby Nubian Village. The area is well-maintained with manicured gardens and ample seating for visitors to relax and take in the scenery.

Aswan Botanical Garden

Aswan Botanical Garden, located on Kitchener's Island in the Nile, is a lush oasis named after Lord Horatio Kitchener. Covering 17 acres, it has nearly 750 plant species from tropical Africa, India, and Brazil. The well-maintained pathways offer a quiet shoreline with towering palms and fragrant blooms. However, some areas are occupied by market stalls selling typical tourist items.
