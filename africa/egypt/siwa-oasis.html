Siwa Oasis

Siwa Oasis has long been a Berber-speaking settlement in Egypt's Western Desert, watered by springs and famous in antiquity for the Oracle of Amun consulted by Alexander the Great. Mud-brick fortresses such as Shali dominated the oasis until rains and modern building changed the skyline. Date and olive farming remain central. Salt lakes, desert tracks to the Great Sand Sea, and restored dwellings now draw visitors to a community still distinct from the Nile Valley.

Amon Temple

Also called the Temple of Amun, this sanctuary dates from the Twenty-sixth Dynasty and grew through Greek and Roman periods. It sits on high ground with views across Siwa. Local tradition links the site to Ham son of Noah or to Dionysus; exterior and main interiors are usually viewable outside service times.

02 AdmissionFree

Cleopatra Spring

A circular stone pool known as Cleopatra's Spring collects naturally flowing water within Siwa Oasis. The association with Cleopatra comes from local tourism tradition and is not supported by firm historical evidence. Palms and cafes surround the roadside basin, where depth, slippery edges, water quality and local customs shape how the spring is used.

29.1969° N / 25.5502° E

03 AdmissionFree

Fatnas Island

Fatnas lies on the western edge of Siwa's salt lake, reached by a narrow causeway through palms and cultivated land. Simple cafes and seating face the water and desert, making sunset the main reason people gather there. Despite its common name, the site is closer to a small peninsula than a remote island, and its springs and shoreline change with local water conditions.

29.1924° N / 25.4807° E

04 AdmissionEGP 120

al-Mawta mountain

Also called the Mountain of the Dead, al-Mawta is easier to climb than Gabal Dakrur and holds dozens of rock tombs with ancient wall paintings. Local belief once held that spirits of the dead inhabited the hill. Summit views stretch toward the Great Sand Sea for hikers who reach the top.

29.2114° N / 25.5251° E
