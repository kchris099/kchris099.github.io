Cairo

Cairo incorporates Fustat, founded in 641, and later Fatimid, Ayyubid, and Mamluk quarters around the Nile. Al-Azhar Mosque, the Citadel of Saladin, and Islamic Cairo's madrasas record medieval scholarship and trade. Ottoman and khedival reforms added European-style boulevards before the city became capital of modern Egypt in the 20th century, with pharaonic, Coptic, and Islamic monuments still spread across the metropolis.

Khan el-Khalili

Khan el-Khalili fills Cairo's bazaar district with shops selling souvenirs, crafts, jewelry, clothing, and spices. Winding alleys hold incense, tea cups, and mini hookahs among denser tourist pitches. Persistent vendor calls are part of the historic market's working rhythm.

Al-Azhar Mosque

The Fatimid dynasty founded Al-Azhar Mosque in 970 as Cairo's new congregational mosque and center of Ismaili teaching; the mosque remains active for prayer and study within the dense commercial streets of Islamic Cairo. Over centuries it became a leading institution of Sunni scholarship, with courtyards, arcades, teaching spaces, domes, and minarets added under successive rulers.

Qalawun Complex

The Qalawun Complex, located in Medieval Cairo, is a notable building named after the 13th-century ruler who commissioned its construction. The complex has detailed marble and stone buildings with elaborate details, including the world's second-most mausoleum.

Mosque of Ibn Tulun

Ahmad ibn Tulun completed his vast congregational mosque in the ninth century within his new capital of al-Qata'i; later restoration preserved the broad plan, while the elevated outer walkway separates the sanctuary from surrounding streets. A large open courtyard, pointed arcades, stucco ornament, perimeter passage, and an unusual spiral-form minaret create one of Cairo's clearest early Islamic ensembles.

Al-Hakim Mosque

Al-Hakim Mosque, located in Cairo, is a 10th-century landmark with grand entrances and a spacious inner courtyard surrounded by arcades. This ancient mosque has Fatimid era architecture and two minarets that are part of the city walls. Despite its plain appearance compared to other mosques in the area, it stands out for its tranquility and historical significance. Over the years, it has served various purposes including as a school, prison, and psychiatric ward.

Bab al-Futuh

Bab al-Futuh is one of the surviving Fatimid gates of historic Cairo, a fortified northern entrance that once helped defend the city against land threats from the direction of Syria. Stone towers and a deep passage open onto the streets near Al-Hakim Mosque.

Bab El Nasr, the Gate of Victory, was built in 1087 as a fortified entrance to Fatimid Cairo and remains a clear example of early Islamic military architecture. Rounded towers and carved stonework face the northern approach beside the surviving wall walks.

Mosque-Madrasa of Sultan Hassan

Sultan Hassan's fourteenth-century mosque and madrasa rises below Cairo's Citadel with a monumental recessed portal and towering walls. Four vaulted iwans open around a central courtyard, each linked to instruction in a Sunni legal school. Stone carving, marble paving, bronze doors, a mausoleum, and the prayer hall reflect Mamluk royal ambition as well as the building's religious and educational functions.

Al-Rifa'i Mosque

Al-Rifa'i Mosque, located in Cairo, Egypt, is a example of Islamic architecture and historical significance. Commissioned in 1869 by Princess Hoshiyar, the mother of Khedive Isma'il Pasha, it was completed in 1912. The mosque's design incorporates neo-Mamluk and Ancient Egyptian elements, featuring a high ceiling with 44 pillars.

Saladin Citadel

The Citadel of Saladin, an imposing fortress and wall constructed by Saladin in the 12th century as protection against the Crusaders, is a culturally significant site in Islamic Cairo. It houses several mosques, including the Mosque of Mohamed Ali, also known as the Alabaster Mosque for its design and construction.

Sultan Al Nasir Muhammad Mosque

Al-Nasir Muhammad built the Citadel's royal mosque in the early fourteenth century for the sultan, court, and garrison. A broad hypostyle prayer hall surrounds a courtyard, while two minarets retain distinctive green-tiled upper sections. Ottoman forces removed much of its marble decoration after the conquest, but the mosque remains a key record of Mamluk palace life inside the fortress.

Great Sphinx of Giza

The Great Sphinx of Giza is a limestone monument with a lion's body and a human face, carved beside the Giza pyramid plateau more than 4,500 years ago. It faces east toward the Nile and sits near the Valley Temple and Khafre's pyramid complex. Visitors view it from the Giza plateau walkways; climbing the statue is not allowed. Sand, wind, and restoration work have shaped its present outline.

Valley Temple of Khafre

Massive blocks of red granite form the Valley Temple beside the Great Sphinx at Giza. Built for Pharaoh Khafre in the Fourth Dynasty, it connected a Nile-side harbor and causeway with the king's pyramid complex and probably supported purification and funerary rites. Square pillars, fitted stone walls, and surviving statue sockets show the precision of Old Kingdom royal construction.

The Great Pyramid of Giza

Khufu's Great Pyramid has dominated the Giza plateau for more than 4,500 years. Workers assembled millions of limestone blocks around internal passages, chambers, relieving spaces and the Grand Gallery to create the king's Fourth Dynasty tomb. Most outer casing stones are gone, exposing the core, while nearby temples, cemeteries, workers' remains and boat pits place the monument within a much larger funerary landscape.

The Grand Egyptian Museum

Fully opened to the public in November 2025, the Grand Egyptian Museum stands near the Giza pyramids in a vast contemporary complex; the monumental stair and conservation facilities make the museum both an exhibition center and a working research institution. Galleries trace ancient Egyptian history through sculpture, royal objects, daily-life material, and the complete Tutankhamun collection, while Khufu's solar boat occupies a dedicated hall.

Bab Zuwayla

Two rounded towers flank Bab Zuwayla, the southern gate of Fatimid Cairo completed in the eleventh century, and stairs lead to upper levels and city views when open, placing visitors within the fabric of the medieval walls. Processions, trade, punishment, and defense all passed through its stone portal, while later Mamluk minarets rose from the adjoining mosque of al-Mu'ayyad.

El- Tahrir Square

Tahrir Square forms a major traffic and civic space at the eastern end of central Cairo's Nile bridges, and landscaping and monuments have changed over time, but the square's significance comes from public assembly and national politics. Government buildings, hotels, the historic Egyptian Museum, metro entrances, and broad roads frame a place closely associated with demonstrations, especially the 2011 revolution.

Qasr El Nil Bridge

Four bronze lions guard the approaches to Qasr El Nil Bridge, linking central Cairo near Tahrir Square with Gezira Island. The present steel bridge opened in 1933, replacing an earlier crossing from the khedival period. Sidewalks provide views of river traffic, downtown towers, the opera district, and evening lights, while heavy road use and public demonstrations have also shaped its modern identity.

Hanging Church

The Hanging Church stands above a gatehouse of Roman Babylon in Coptic Cairo, giving the nave its traditional suspended position. Rebuilt over centuries, it contains a timber roof, carved screens, icons, a marble pulpit, and steps descending from the street; the church became an important seat of the Coptic patriarchate in the medieval period and remains active for worship.

An early mosque of Amr ibn al-As was founded at Fustat soon after the Arab conquest of Egypt in the seventh century, and the present hypostyle hall and open courtyard reflect later periods within the district where Islamic Cairo first developed. The Nilometer on Rawda Island measured the Nile's flood height to guide taxes and farming expectations in medieval and earlier Cairo. A stone column in a octagonal well sits inside a small historic structure, showing how closely civic life tracked the river's annual rise.

Al-Azhar Park opened in 2005 on a former debris hill east of historic Cairo, creating lawns, gardens, and hilltop views over the mosques.
