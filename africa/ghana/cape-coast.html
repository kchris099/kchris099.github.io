Destination Guide — Cape Coast

Explore Cape Coast

A Brief History

Cape Coast developed out of an 18th-century European trading post on the Gulf of Guinea where the British, Swedish, Danish, and Dutch built overlapping shore forts to compete for gold, ivory, and later the Atlantic slave trade between 1653 and 1664. The Cape Coast Castle that survives today was rebuilt into stone under the British in 1784 after earlier Swedish and English timber fortifications had occupied the same rock shelf, and the site's museums have recorded the broader slave-trade network west to Elmina and east to Accra. Twentieth-century independence in 1957 redirected the town toward an educational and tourism centre anchored by the University of Cape Coast, founded in 1962.

Top Sights & Activities

GHS 80

Cape Coast Castle

Cape Coast Castle is a 17th-century fortified slave-trade museum on Ghana's Gulf of Guinea shore, originally a 1653 Swedish timber post before the British rebuilt it in stone after 1784. It served as the British Gold Coast seat until 1877 and was a principal Atlantic slave-trade embarkation site. Travellers pay GHS80 to enter the ocean-facing Door of No Return and tour the subterranean dungeons and elevated European officers' quarters.

GHS 80

Kakum National Park Canopy Walkway

The Kakum National Park Canopy Walkway is a rainforest suspension-bridge network spanning the upper canopy of Kakum National Park, roughly 30 kilometres north of Cape Coast. Opened in 1995 by the Ghana Heritage Conservation Trust, the walkway climbs up to 30 metres above the forest floor across seven suspended bridges linking six tree platforms. Travellers pay GHS80 per adult and walk the upper-level loop.

Free

Cape Coast Lighthouse

The Cape Coast Lighthouse is a 19th-century cast-iron lighthouse built by British engineers in 1883 on the peninsula adjoining Cape Coast Castle, supplementing the older castle lantern. The current tower stands 28 metres tall and is operated by the Ghana Maritime Authority for vessels approaching the Gulf of Guinea trade corridor. Access to the gallery is via the Cape Coast Castle museum ticket on selected weekday mornings.
