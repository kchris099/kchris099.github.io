Accra

Growing from coastal Ga settlements around 17th-century European trade forts, Accra became the capital of the British Gold Coast in 1877. Its modern urban sprawl traces the nation's journey from colonial administration to leading the charge for African independence in 1957. Visitors today can clearly trace the detailed layers of this unique heritage throughout the preserved streets and monuments.

Accra Mall

Accra Mall is a large modern shopping centre on the city's eastern corridors, with international and local fashion, sports, and jewellery retailers on the main floors. Food courts and cinema screens support longer stays. Escalators and open atriums organise circulation between parking and the retail decks.

W.E.B Du Bois Memorial Centre for Panafrican Culture - Ghana

On the former Accra estate of American civil-rights writer W.E.B. Du Bois, the memorial centre holds rooms and grounds tied to his last years in Ghana. Displays address Pan-African culture and his archive. The compound sits in an open plaza and roadside setting north of the older coastal districts.

Accra International Conference Centre

Accra International Conference Centre opened as a major venue for state, diplomatic, business and cultural gatherings near the city's government district. An auditorium, meeting rooms, foyers, exhibition areas and ceremonial spaces support conferences and public events of different scales.

04 AdmissionFree

Independence Arch

Independence Arch, also known as the Black Star Gate, is a significant symbol of Ghana's fight for independence from British rule. Kwame Nkrumah, it is a tribute to fallen soldiers and a landmark of freedom. The arch is located at Accra's Independence Square and is the focal point for national events such as presidential inaugurations, Independence Day celebrations, military parades, and more.

5.5490° N / 0.1928° W

05 AdmissionFree

National Theatre

Accra's National Theatre opened in 1992 with a distinctive ship-like roof that breaks the skyline near the conference centre. Stages present drama, music, and dance rooted in Ghanaian and wider African repertoires. Daytime exterior views and ticketed performances both define how the building is used.

5.5537° N / 0.2006° W

Kwame Nkrumah Memorial Park

Kwame Nkrumah Memorial Park commemorates Ghana's first president and the independence movement he led. A mausoleum containing Nkrumah and Fathia Nkrumah, museum displays, statues, fountains, lawns, and ceremonial space occupy the former polo ground where independence was declared in 1957. Exhibitions address his political career and pan-African legacy, including debates that extend beyond a purely heroic memorial.

5.5444° N / 0.2026° W

Centre for National Culture

The Centre for National Culture in Accra, often called the Arts Centre, combines craft markets with performance and exhibition space for Ghanaian artists. Stalls sell textiles, woodcarving, beads, and souvenirs directly from makers. Cultural shows periodically animate the courtyards.

08 AdmissionFree

Makola Market

Makola Market spreads through crowded buildings and streets in central Accra. Food traders work beside textile and clothing sellers, supplying everyday urban demand. Household goods, cosmetics and medicines fill adjoining lanes, with hardware and general merchandise extending the trade further. Traffic, loading activity and bargaining define a dense working commercial district.

09 AdmissionGHS 50

Fort James

Fort James stands on Accra's Jamestown coast as a British trading fort completed in the seventeenth century and later used as a prison. Thick walls, cells, courtyards and sea-facing positions connect colonial commerce, enslavement, punishment and local fishing life.

5.5337° N / 0.2112° W

Abossey Okai Central Mosque

White walls and green pillars mark about an acre of prayer space with Turkish-influenced detail. Worshippers and visitors share views of the exterior and main halls outside service times. The large compound anchors the Abossey Okai commercial district.
