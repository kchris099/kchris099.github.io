Dakar

Dakar was originally settled by the Lebou people before the Portuguese arrived in the fifteenth century. It grew significantly under French colonial rule, becoming the capital of French West Africa in 1902 and a major port for the peanut trade. The nearby Goree Island served as a center for the Atlantic slave trade. Today, Dakar is the active capital of independent Senegal, known for its busy markets, rich musical heritage, and strategic position on the Cap-Vert peninsula.

African Renaissance Monument

The African Renaissance Monument rises on a Dakar hill as a 2010 bronze group showing a family emerging toward the sea, and interior stairs lead to exhibits and a viewpoint, while its meaning extends beyond a simple celebration of emancipation. Designed under President Abdoulaye Wade and built with North Korean involvement, it has drawn debate over cost, style, gender, politics, and state ownership.

Phare des Mamelles

Phare des Mamelles crowns one of the twin Mamelles hills above Dakar as a nineteenth-century lighthouse overlooking the Atlantic approaches. Guided climbs reach the lantern level for coastline and city views, while a restaurant-bar at the base uses a shuttle to an open veranda for meals and occasional events. The white tower remains a navigation landmark as much as a leisure stop on the Corniche heights.

Mosque of the Divinity

The Mosque of the Divinity, situated in the Ouakam district near Dakar, Senegal, is a example of modern Islamic architecture. Designed by architect Cheikh Ngom and completed in 1997, it has two towering minarets that provide views of the Atlantic Ocean. The interior is adorned with ornate domes and colorful mosaics. This mosque has become an important pilgrimage site for Muslims and a attraction for travelers.

Place du Souvenir Africain

Place du Souvenir Africain faces Dakar's Corniche as a memorial complex dedicated to African history, diaspora figures, and continental memory. Sculptures, inscribed walls, and open ceremonial space frame views toward the ocean. The site functions as civic remembrance rather than a amusement park, with paths that invite slow reading of names and panels along the waterfront drive.

Grand Mosque of Dakar

Also known as the Grande Mosquée de Dakar or Mosque of the Divinity, the complex dating from around 1906 remains a major Islamic landmark in Senegal's capital. Courtyards and prayer halls open for viewing outside service times. The mosque anchors congregational life on the plateau.

Grand Théâtre National de Dakar

Opened in 2011, Dakar's Grand Theatre provides a national stage for music, dance, drama, ceremonies and major public events. Its main auditorium, rehearsal spaces, formal foyers and production facilities accommodate Senegalese companies and visiting performers. The building reflects state investment in large cultural institutions and supports performance traditions that range from contemporary popular music to staged dance and theater.

The Museum of Black Civilisations

Modern galleries at the Museum of Black Civilisations focus on African culture with emphasis on Senegalese and West African material, while also presenting other African regions. French-language signage can challenge non-French readers. Posted hours govern entry to the public rooms.

Cathedral of Our Lady of Victories

Consecrated in 1936 on the Dakar plateau, the Cathedral of Our Lady of Victories became a central pillar of Catholicism in Senegal. The large church massing dominates its street block. Exterior and main interior remain open for viewing outside liturgies.

Leopold Sedar Senghor French Institute

Institut francais du Senegal in Dakar combines language education with concerts, film, exhibitions, talks, workshops and cultural exchange. Courtyards, halls, classrooms and food service support a changing public program. The institution is named for Leopold Sedar Senghor in some contexts.

House of Slaves

The House of Slaves, also known as Maison des Esclaves, is a museum and memorial located on Ile Goree, an island off the southern tip of Dakar. It was built in the 1700s and is the last remaining slave house out of approximately 30 on the island. The museum documents the history of the Atlantic slave trade and is a powerful reminder of the brutal barbarism of this dark period in history.
