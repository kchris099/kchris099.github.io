Dublin

Dublin began as a Norse longphort on the Liffey in the 9th century and became the seat of English and later British rule in Ireland. Georgian squares, Trinity College, and the Custom House mark 18th-century expansion. The 1916 Easter Rising and independence struggle centred on the General Post Office. Docklands redevelopment and literary museums now sit beside medieval street lines and the River Liffey quays.

St Patrick's Cathedral

St. Patrick's Cathedral developed on a site associated with Dublin's patron saint and became a major medieval Anglican cathedral, and jonathan Swift served as dean and is buried here, linking worship and national history with a substantial literary legacy. The present building dates mainly from the thirteenth century, with later restoration, memorials, choir stalls, stained glass, and patterned tiles.

Croke Park

Croke Park is the headquarters and principal stadium of the Gaelic Athletic Association. Its rebuilt stands surround a pitch used for major hurling and Gaelic football matches, with concerts and other large events held between sporting seasons. The ground also carries national historical importance through the killings of players and spectators during Bloody Sunday in 1920, linking the stadium to both sport and Irish political memory.

Molly Malone Statue

Jeanne Rynhart's bronze Molly Malone statue depicts the fictional street seller from Dublin's well-known song with a barrow of shellfish. Installed in the late twentieth century and later moved to Suffolk Street, it became a heavily photographed city symbol. The figure belongs to folklore, and repeated touching has visibly polished parts of the sculpture. The shellfish barrow identifies the song's central character.

Wellington Monument

The Wellington Testimonial rises near the southeastern entrance of Phoenix Park as a granite obelisk completed in the nineteenth century. Bronze reliefs cast from captured cannon commemorate Arthur Wellesley, Duke of Wellington. The panels depict episodes associated with his military career. Broad steps and open grass isolate the monument within the park, while its imagery reflects the imperial politics of the period that produced it.

Ha'penny Bridge

Opened in 1816, the Ha'penny Bridge is a cast-iron pedestrian crossing over the River Liffey. Its popular name recalls the original halfpenny toll charged in place of ferry service. Ashtown Castle, located in Phoenix Park, is a medieval stone house that was once hidden within a larger Georgian mansion.
