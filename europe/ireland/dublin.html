Destination Guide — Dublin

Explore Dublin

A Brief History

Established as a regional settlement beyond an older fortified core, the city developed through industrialization in the late 19th century, post-war reconstruction, and modern civic expansion that reshaped its public spaces, markets, and surrounding landscape. Restored religious buildings, civic buildings, and natural features now define the contemporary visitor experience.

Top Sights & Activities

St Patrick's Cathedral

The Cathedral of Saint Patrick in Dublin is important religious landmarks in Ireland, and has been a part of the country's history for over 800 years. It is the largest cathedral in Ireland and is home to many beautiful stained glass windows, marble statues, and medieval tiles. The cathedral also plays an important role in literary pilgrimage, as Jonathan Swift once served as dean here.

Reservation required

Croke Park

Croke Park stands as a monumental symbol of Irish culture and sport, being the largest stadium in Ireland and the third largest in Europe. With a staggering capacity of 82,300, it serves as the heart of Gaelic sports, hosting thrilling matches of Gaelic Football and Hurling from April to September each year.

Free

Molly Malone Statue

The Molly Malone Statue in Dublin is a bronze depiction of the fictional fishmonger from the well-known Irish song. Situated in the city's Georgian Quarter on Suffolk Street, this statue attracts hordes of tourists daily who are drawn to its connection with Irish folklore. Although the real Molly Malone may have looked different, this monument has become a symbol of Dublin and is a popular spot for travellers to take selfies.

€25

Irish Whiskey Museum

The Irish Whiskey Museum in Dublin is a must-visit for anyone interested in the rich history and culture of Irish whiskey. The museum offers guided tours that take travellers through interactive exhibits, providing insights into the origins and distillation of Irish whiskey. At the end of the tour, guests can enjoy a tasting experience featuring at least three different whiskeys.

Reservation required

Aviva Stadium

Aviva Stadium is a remarkable venue In Dublin, designed to host international rugby and football matches. Opened in 2010, this modern stadium features an eye-catching glass end and boasts an environmentally friendly design that reflects innovative architectural ideas from around the globe. It serves as a proud home for Ireland's national teams and has become a hub for thrilling sporting events.

€22

Dublin Zoo

Dublin Zoo, the largest zoo in Ireland, offers a diverse range of exhibits including African Plain, Fringes of the Arctic and Asian Forests. Located within Phoenix Park, it is one of the oldest zoos globally dating back to 1831. The zoo is home to a variety of exotic animals such as giraffes, rhinos, gorillas, elephants, penguins and sea lions.

Free

Wellington Monument

The Wellington Monument is an impressive 62m obelisk located at the beginning of Phoenix Park in Ireland. Surrounded by a well-kept park, it serves as a great meeting point and offers a perfect spot to relax on its steps. The monument stands tall and beautiful, making it an ideal location for early morning solitude or photography.

€26

The Book of Kells Experience

The Book of Kells Experience is a attraction in Dublin, Ireland. Housed at Trinity College, this ancient manuscript dating back to 800 AD contains illuminated illustrations of the Christian Gospels. The college itself, established over 400 years ago during Queen Elizabeth's reign, offers a tranquil escape from the city center's hustle and bustle.

Free

Trinity College Dublin

Trinity College Dublin, a 19-hectare university with Georgian buildings, is renowned for its humanities, science, and medical programs. The campus is home to the national treasure of Ireland, the Book of Kells, which can be fully appreciated with an audio guide. travellers can also explore the library gallery, famous for being a filming location in Harry Potter movies, and view the oldest harp in Ireland.

Free

Ha'penny Bridge

The Ha'penny Bridge, a beautifully refurbished cast-iron structure from the 19th century, stands as a beloved symbol of Dublin. Spanning nearly 150 feet across the River Liffey, this pedestrian bridge has welcomed countless travellers since its opening in 1816. Originally requiring a toll of half a penny to cross—hence its charming name—the bridge connects the bustling north and south sides of the city.

Free

O'Connell Monument

The O'Connell Monument, located at the bottom of O'Connell Street in Dublin, is a significant landmark dedicated to Daniel O'Connell, an influential Irish political leader known as "The Liberator." The monument was erected in 1862 and sculpted by John Henry Foley. It stands proudly at the site where O'Connell Bridge crosses the River Liffey.

Reservation required

Abbey Theatre

The Abbey Theatre, founded by W.B. Yeats and Lady Augusta Gregory in 1903, is Ireland's national theatre known for staging new Irish and global plays. The theater offers backstage tours providing insights into the staging of drama and its unique role in Irish history. Additionally, the Theatre Walking Tour explores Dublin's artistic legacies and famous actors and writers associated with the city.

€12

Christ Church Cathedral

Christ Church Cathedral is a medieval place of worship in Dublin, Ireland. It has a rich history dating back to the 11th century when the first Christianized Danish king built a wooden church at the site. The present stone structure was constructed around 1200 and underwent extensive renovations in the 19th century, giving it a Victorian appearance.

€8

Dublin Castle

Dublin Castle, dating back to 1204, is a historic site that has played a significant role in Ireland's history for over 800 years. The castle features state rooms and apartments that have accommodated prominent historical figures, along with a 19th-century coach house, an army barracks from 1811, and a 13th-century Norman Tower. travellers can explore the gardens, museum, library, and chapel free of charge.

€26

Jameson Distillery Bow St.

The Jameson Distillery Bow St. offers an immersive experience in the world of Irish whiskey. While it's no longer the main production site, it provides a fascinating tour of the original distillery and educates travellers about the brand's history and distillation process. The expert guides share captivating stories as guests explore recreated distillery scenes, culminating in tastings and dining experiences.

€7

St Michan's Church of Ireland

St Michan's Church of Ireland is a captivating destination steeped in over 900 years of history, located in the vibrant Smithfield area. While the current structure dates back to the 17th century, it occupies a site that once housed a Viking chapel from 1095. This church is not just an architectural marvel; it also boasts an impressive pipe organ from the 1720s, which was famously played by Handel himself.

Free

The Hell Fire Club

If you're seeking a sunrise experience in Dublin, look no further than The Hellfire Club, perched high in the Dublin Mountains. A short drive from the city leads you to ample parking just a stone's throw away from this historic site. Once an old pub, now abandoned, it offers views after a moderate hike of 20 to 40 minutes up Montpelier Hill.

€22

EPIC The Irish Emigration Museum

EPIC The Irish Emigration Museum, located in the Custom House building, is a captivating exploration of the Irish diaspora's impact on the world. Through 20 interconnected galleries, travellers can delve into the stories and experiences of Irish emigrants who have made significant contributions globally. This award-winning museum offers an interactive and immersive experience that showcases the diverse achievements and struggles of the Irish community throughout history.

Reservation required

The Convention Centre Dublin

Nestled along the banks of the River Liffey, The Convention Centre Dublin stands as a beacon of modern architecture in the vibrant Dublin Docklands. Designed by renowned architect Kevin Roche, this award-winning venue features a glass atrium and 22 versatile event spaces, including an auditorium and banquet areas. Since its completion in 2010, it has proudly held the title of being the world's first carbon-neutral international convention center.

Reservation required

Bord Gáis Energy Theatre

Nestled in the vibrant Grand Canal area of Dublin, the Bord Gáis Energy Theatre stands as a testament to modern architectural brilliance, crafted by the acclaimed Daniel Libeskind. This 2,100-seat venue is a cultural hub that showcases an impressive array of performances including ballet, opera, musicals, and orchestral concerts.

Free

National Botanic Gardens

The National Botanic Gardens in Dublin is a sprawling expanse featuring naturalistic areas, formal gardens, an arboretum, and a Victorian palm house. It serves as both a research and conservation center as well as a recreational park, drawing over 500,000 travellers annually. Families can explore the 50-acre space with its diverse plant species, rose and rock gardens, pond and river walks, organic fruit and vegetable gardens, and Ireland's only indoor rainforest.

Free

Stephen's Green Shopping Centre

Stephen's Green Shopping Centre is a vibrant indoor mall In Dublin's prestigious shopping and cultural district. With over 100 specialty retailers and eateries, it offers a diverse shopping experience. From well-known brands like Dunnes Stores, Argos, Boots, and TK Maxx to unique boutiques such as Asha, Cactus, and Retro Nation, the center caters to a wide range of tastes.

Reservation required

The Gaiety Theatre

The Gaiety Theatre, a classical Victorian auditorium, has been faithfully providing top-quality musical and theatrical performances since its grand opening. As Dublin's longest-running theater, it continues to be the city's primary venue for musicals, operas, events, comedy, storytelling, and choreography. The theater aims to create a warm and welcoming atmosphere for all travellers.

Free

Merrion Square Park

Merrion Square Park is a picturesque public space in Dublin, known for its lush greenery and vibrant floral and heather gardens. The park features a playground and is home to the notable Oscar Wilde statue, making it a must-visit for tourists. Surrounding the park are cobblestone streets lined with charming 18th-century brick townhouses, offering a glimpse into the area's colonial and republican history.

Free

Poolbeg Lighthouse

Poolbeg Lighthouse is an red structure located in Dublin, accessible via a lengthy seawall that attracts sightseers, anglers, and cyclists. It's a favorite spot for watching the sunrise with its panoramic view of the ocean. To get there, it's suggested to rent a car on the GoCar app and drive early in the morning before dawn. The Great South Wall, once the world's longest seawall, offers views of Dublin Bay and leads to Poolbeg Lighthouse.

Free

The Purty Kitchen

The Purty Kitchen is a historic gastropub located near the West Harbour in Dublin. Dating back to 1728, it exudes a warm and sophisticated vibe with its brick fireplaces and cosy alcoves. The large dining room offers a traditional yet elegant atmosphere for enjoying hearty local cuisine and great seafood dishes such as prawn and chorizo linguine, fish pie, Irish beef burgers, tempura plaice, and more.

Free

Dun Laoghaire Sunday Market

The Dun Laoghaire Sunday Market, also known as the Peoples Park Market, is a long-standing market open from 11am to 4pm every Sunday. Located in the People's Park, it offers a wide variety of goods including fruits and vegetables, baked goods, artisan breads, handmade crafts, jewelry, local meats, Irish cheeses and plants. Additionally, there are international food stalls offering diverse cuisines such as Korean noodles, Italian sausages and seafood.

Free

East Pier Lighthouse

East Pier Lighthouse, also known as the East Pier Battery, is a charming spot in Dunleary with seasonal openings. The lighthouse is automated like others along the coast and offers views of the sea and city. travellers can enjoy a leisurely walk from downtown Dunleary to reach this picturesque location, where they can relax on benches at the end of the pier.

Free

Killiney Hill Park

Killiney Hill Park, located just south of Dublin, is a popular recreational park offering 360-degree views of the surrounding area. From the capital city to the Wicklow Mountains and even the Irish Sea, travellers can enjoy vistas. The park features family-friendly walking trails and is often referred to as 'Thousand Dog Park.' Additionally, there's a cozy cafe at the base of the hill for those in need of refreshments.

Free

Drimnagh Medieval Castle and Gardens

Nestled in Dublin, Drimnagh Medieval Castle is a example of Norman architecture that has stood the test of time for over 700 years. This remarkable castle is unique as it boasts Ireland's only water-filled moat, which adds to its enchanting allure. Once falling into neglect during the 1960s, Drimnagh was rediscovered and meticulously restored in the late '90s, opening its doors to travellers eager to explore its rich history.

Free

Howth Head Peak

Howth Head Peak offers a bracing hiking trail with sea, cliff, and lighthouse views. It's particularly tranquil at night, making it an ideal spot for a peaceful evening. The well-signed coastal walk leads to the peak, where travellers can enjoy views of Dublin Bay and South Dublin. The 12km route is quite taxing, requiring a decent level of fitness. However, the flat terrain makes it an amazing hike that allows travellers to disconnect from the city and connect with nature.

Free

Howth Cliff Walk (Green Route)

The Howth Cliff Walk (Green Route) offers a gentle clifftop hiking experience with views of the Baily Lighthouse, bays, beaches, and mountains. This walk is considered in Dublin, featuring easy-to-follow trails that showcase cinematic coastal scenes. The 1.5-hour route starts at Howth Summit car park and leads north to Howth Head Peak, providing views of Irelands Eye and Lambay Island.

Free

Baily Lighthouse

Baily Lighthouse, situated in the picturesque village of Howth, Ireland, is a must-visit attraction offering panoramic views and a glimpse into the area's maritime heritage. This historic 19th-century lighthouse stands tall on the cliffs, boasting its red and white striped tower. travellers can drive or take public transportation to Howth and explore the charming pier before embarking on a scenic hike to Baily Lighthouse.

Free

Howth Lighthouse

Howth Lighthouse is a landmark in Dublin, Ireland, known for its picturesque setting and water views. It's a popular destination for scenic walks, especially during dawn or sunset when the lighting is perfect for capturing incredible photographs. Accessible by DART or bus from the city center, it offers a leisurely pier walk along the seaside with beautiful cliffs as a backdrop. The area is home to resident seagulls adding to the coastal village ambiance.

Free

Phoenix Park

Phoenix Park, known as "Dublin's playground," is a 708-hectare park that was originally a royal hunting ground in the 1660s before opening to the public in 1747. It boasts a large herd of fallow deer, a Zoological Gardens, Aras an Uachtarain, and a Victorian flower garden.

Free

Ashtown Castle

Ashtown Castle, located in Phoenix Park, is a medieval stone house that was once hidden within a larger Georgian mansion. Dating back to the 1430s, the castle was discovered when the surrounding building was demolished in 1978. It has since been restored and now serves as part of the Phoenix Park travellers Centre. The travellers centre provides insight into the history of the castle and the park from 3500BC to present day.

Free

Iveagh Gardens

Nestled in the heart of Dublin, Iveagh Gardens is often referred to as the city's hidden gem or "secret garden." Originally designed in 1865 and beautifully restored since 1995, this enchanting space offers a serene escape from urban life. Once known as Coburg Gardens, it was revitalized by Sir Benjamin Guinness and later gifted to the Irish nation.
