Galway

Fourteen merchant families ran Galway as a medieval Atlantic port tied to Spain and France. Seventeenth-century sieges damaged its fortunes, yet Irish-language culture and a later arts revival kept the city distinct on the west coast. The Spanish Arch and Latin Quarter lanes are remnants of that trading oligarchy. Claddagh, once a distinct fishing village, was absorbed as the city expanded along the bay.

Eyre Square

Eyre Square has functioned as Galway's central civic space for centuries, though repeated redesign produced its present mix of lawn, paving, sculpture, trees, and transport edges. Officially named John F. Kennedy Memorial Park after his 1963 visit, it remains a meeting point surrounded by shops and hotels. Bus activity and public gatherings keep the square urban.

Charlie Byrne's Bookshop

Located Galway City, Charlie Byrne's Bookshop is a beloved destination for book enthusiasts. This shop offers an extensive collection of over 50,000 new, used, and remaindered titles covering a wide range of subjects. It's not just a thrift shop; it provides access to high-end books and is known as literary shopping spots in town.

The Latin Quarter

The Latin Quarter, often referred to as the cultural Galway, is a active area filled with quirky stores, pubs, restaurants, and historic landmarks. Its pedestrianized cobblestoned streets create a festive atmosphere with street performers and entertainers. Visitors can explore interesting shops, trendy cafes, and restaurants reflecting the latest hip trends. The area is home to historic landmarks like Blakes Castle and the Fishmarket area near the Spanish Arch.

Spanish Arch

The Spanish Arch was added to Galway's waterfront defenses in the late sixteenth century as an extension of the medieval town wall toward the quays. Two surviving stone arches stand beside the River Corrib near the former fish market. Originally built as a defense structure to protect the quays from invasion, it now is a symbol of the city's rich history.

53.2697° N / 9.0541° W

Galway Cathedral

A green-domed, Renaissance-style Catholic cathedral near the National University of Ireland, Galway, and University Hospital Galway fills a modern, airy interior. Exterior and main halls usually stay viewable outside service times. The cathedral sits within walking reach of the city museum and downtown streets, anchoring Galway's twentieth-century church architecture.

Galway Races

The Galway Races at Galway Racecourse continue a meeting tradition dating to 1764, mixing thoroughbred cards with food stalls, music, and summer fashion. Race days draw large regional crowds to the track outside the city. The festival feel matters as much as any single race result.

Menlo Castle

Located along the banks of the River Corrib, Menlo Castle is a ruin that dates back to the 1500s. Once a grand residence for the affluent Blake family, who were prominent figures in Galway from 1600 until 1910, this castle has ivy-clad walls and an enchanting atmosphere.

Galway Atlantaquaria

Galway Atlantaquaria, the National Aquarium of Ireland, displays native Irish marine life with feeding talks and touch pools. Tanks focus on Atlantic species rather than tropical imports. The Salthill waterfront location pairs a visit with a promenade walk.

Salthill Prom

Salthill Promenade follows Galway Bay from the city toward Blackrock, passing seawalls, beaches, benches and long views toward the Burren. Walking, running and everyday social use continue in changing Atlantic weather. Tides, wind and designated bathing areas shape activity along the shore, while the promenade connects several distinct waterfront neighborhoods.

Claregalway Castle

Located just 10 kilometers from Galway City, Claregalway Castle is a impressively restored 15th-century Anglo-Norman tower house that stands proudly on the banks of the River Clare. This historic fortress, once home to the Clanricarde De Burgo clan, has a rich and communist period spanning over six centuries. Visitors can explore its medieval corridors and uncover tales of daily life within its walls, as well as the intriguing characters who once roamed its halls.
