Amsterdam

Amsterdam grew from a 13th-century dam on the Amstel River into a major port during the Dutch Golden Age of the 17th century. Canal rings, merchants' houses, and the East India Company headquarters reflect that trading wealth. Jewish history includes the Anne Frank House from the World War II occupation. The canal district was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2010.

Canals of Amsterdam

More than one hundred kilometers of canals form Amsterdam's seventeenth-century ring, designed for drainage, transport, defense and urban expansion. Merchant houses, warehouses, bridges, locks and houseboats record changing commercial and residential life. The protected canal district covers a planned urban system, while individual waterways differ in age, width, traffic and architectural character.

Rijksmuseum

Pierre Cuypers designed the Rijksmuseum as a national museum and monumental gateway between central Amsterdam and Museumplein. Galleries present Dutch and international art, decorative objects, maritime history and material culture across many periods, with Rembrandt's Night Watch at the core of the seventeenth-century collection. Renovation restored the building's murals, courtyards and axial passage.

Begijnhof

A narrow gate off Spui leads into the Begijnhof, a residential courtyard founded for religious women who lived without permanent monastic vows. Historic brick houses, a medieval wooden house, an English Reformed church and a concealed Catholic chapel surround the green. People still live in the enclave, so quiet conduct protects its residential and religious character.

Basilica of Saint Nicholas

Twin towers and a domed crossing mark the Basilica of Saint Nicholas opposite Amsterdam Central Station. Completed in the late nineteenth century after restrictions on public Catholic worship eased, it combines neo-Baroque and neo-Renaissance forms with maritime patronage imagery. Mass, music, stained glass, and painted interiors continue within an active parish near the old harbor.

Royal Palace Amsterdam

Originally constructed as a city hall in 1655, this massive building was a symbol of civic wealth and republican power during the Dutch Golden Age. Converted into a royal palace in 1808, it functions today as one of the official residences of the Dutch monarch.

Rembrandtplein

Rembrandtplein began as a dairy market in 1668 and later became a major Amsterdam square for nightlife and gatherings. In 1876 it was renamed for Rembrandt, whose statue stands at the center. Cafés and theaters now ring the open pavement that once held market stalls.

Amsterdam Centraal Station

Built on artificial islands and opened in 1889, Amsterdam Centraal joined national rail lines while separating the old city from part of the IJ waterfront. Pierre Cuypers, working with engineer Adolf van Gendt, designed its long brick facade, towers, iron roofs and platforms. Later expansion integrated trains with metro, trams, buses, ferries and extensive bicycle facilities.

National Monument

A tall white pillar and sculptural group form the National Monument on Dam Square, unveiled in 1956 for Dutch victims of the Second World War. Later interpretation broadened remembrance to war and persecution more generally. The annual 4 May ceremony gathers at the memorial, whose figures and reliefs express suffering, resistance, liberation, and peace.

Anne Frank, her family and four others hid in rooms behind Otto Frank's business at Prinsengracht 263 from 1942 until their arrest in 1944. The museum preserves the concealed annex, movable bookcase, personal traces and documentation of persecution and the diary's history. Timed entry and narrow stairs shape access, while respectful interpretation avoids turning confinement into spectacle.

Homomonument

The Homomonument in Amsterdam is a significant public sculpture that is a memorial to those persecuted for their homosexuality. Created by sculptor Karin Daan, the monument consists of three pink triangular granite blocks, symbolizing the badges that Nazis forced homosexuals to wear. Positioned in a triangle on the canal bank, each point of the monument holds symbolic significance, pointing towards landmarks like the National War Memorial and Anne Frank House.
