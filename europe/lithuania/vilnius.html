Vilnius

Vilnius served as the capital of the Grand Duchy of Lithuania from the 14th century, with castle hill, cathedral square, and university founded in 1579 shaping a multiconfessional baroque cityscape. Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth and Russian imperial rule added palaces, churches, and later industrial quarters. The city was a center of Jewish learning before the Holocaust and Soviet occupation altered its population and built fabric.

Vilnius Cathedral

Vilnius Cathedral stands on the historic ceremonial square at the foot of castle hill and has been rebuilt across many centuries. It remains one of the main religious and civic monuments of Lithuania. The neoclassical facade and interior chapels preserve the site where grand dukes were honored and where Catholic life has centered since the Middle Ages.

Gediminas Castle

Gediminas Tower is the remaining brick tower of Vilnius's Upper Castle on a hill above the old town. The fortifications developed under Lithuania's medieval rulers and were repeatedly altered, damaged and dismantled. Museum exhibits cover the castle and national symbols, while the summit gives broad views across the cathedral, Neris valley and surrounding districts.

Church of St. Anne

St Anne's Church in Vilnius is a late Gothic brick church beside the Bernardine complex, with slender towers and dense moulded brickwork that have drawn admirers since the sixteenth century. Napoleon famously praised the facade on his march through the city. Outside services the small nave opens onto Maironio Street.

Gates of Dawn

The Gates of Dawn preserve one of the former city gates of Vilnius and contain a chapel with a revered Marian image. The site marks the southern edge of the old town and its pilgrimage tradition. Devotees and tourists pass through the gate daily to see the silver-clad icon visible from the street and to visit the chapel above the arch.

Holy Trinity Church & Basilian Gate

An ornate Baroque gate leads from Ausros Vartu Street into the Holy Trinity Church and Basilian Monastery complex. The site grew from a Ruthenian Orthodox foundation and later became central to the Greek Catholic Basilian order, reflecting Vilnius's eastern Christian communities. Church interiors, monastery buildings, and commemorative spaces have undergone restoration and retain active religious functions.

Saint Catherine's Church

Saint Catherine's Church was rebuilt in the Baroque period for the Benedictine convent and remains part of the dense churchscape of old Vilnius. Its facade and interior record the Catholic rebuilding of the early modern city. The church now is a concert venue, linking sacred architecture with Vilnius traditions of choral and organ music.

Vilnius TV Tower

A concrete shaft and circular upper structure carry Vilnius TV Tower to 326.5 meters above the city's western neighborhoods. Completed in 1980 for broadcasting, it also became a national memorial after Soviet forces killed civilians around the tower on 13 January 1991. Observation and dining facilities occupy the upper level, subject to current operating arrangements.

Church of Sts. Peter and Paul

The Church of Sts Peter and Paul in Vilnius Antakalnis is a seventeenth-century Baroque Roman Catholic church packed with white stucco sculpture. Founded for the Lithuanian Grand Duchy's military elites, it remains one of the city's most ornate interiors. Outside Mass the nave is usually open for quiet visits.

Three Crosses Monument

Three white concrete crosses stand on a hill above Vilnius, continuing a memorial tradition linked by legend to Franciscan martyrs, and woodland paths reach the summit, where the city view and history of suppression are equally important. Earlier wooden and concrete versions were removed or destroyed, including demolition by Soviet authorities in 1950; the present monument dates from 1989.

Bernardine Garden

Bernardine Garden follows the Vilnia River between Vilnius Cathedral, the Bernardine church complex and the slopes below Gediminas Hill. A nineteenth-century public garden was reconstructed with paths, flower beds, fountains, playgrounds, ponds and botanical planting. Seasonal water displays and family facilities supplement.

Angel Of Užupis

A bronze angel blowing a trumpet rises above the main square of Uzupis, installed in 2002 as a symbol of the neighborhood's self-declared artistic republic. Sculptor Romas Vilciauskas created the figure after an earlier egg sculpture occupied the pedestal. Cafes and galleries surround the junction, but the monument's meaning comes from local civic humor and post-Soviet cultural identity.

Verkiai Palace

Verkiai Palace occupies a wooded estate above the Neris on Vilnius's northern edge. Eighteenth-century bishops and nobles developed the ensemble with classical buildings, terraces, service wings and landscaped parkland, although the main central palace no longer survives. Research institutions use parts of the estate and public experience centers on exterior architecture, paths and viewpoints.

Pūčkorių atodanga

The Puckoriai Exposure reveals a high wall of sand and gravel above a bend in the Vilnia River within Pavilniai Regional Park. Geological layers record glacial processes, while a marked viewpoint and valley trails connect the escarpment with former mill sites and woodland. Erosion and steep edges require staying on established routes.

Vincas Kudirka Square

A monument to physician, writer and anthem author Vincas Kudirka stands in the central Vilnius square that bears his name; concerts, demonstrations, seasonal displays and everyday sitting make the space a modern civic square. Government buildings, shops, lawns, trees and paved gathering areas surround the sculpture on Gedimino Avenue.

Cathedral Square

Vilnius Cathedral, its detached bell tower, the Palace of the Grand Dukes and the lower castle grounds define Cathedral Square at the foot of Gediminas Hill. Archaeological traces beneath the paving connect the open space with the city's earliest fortified and religious center. Ceremonies, markets and public events continue its role as the principal civic forecourt of the old town.

Dominican Church of the Holy Spirit

The Church of the Holy Spirit in Vilnius is a baroque Dominican church whose interior is known for dense sculptural decoration. After periods of closure and other uses, including detention in earlier centuries, it returned to Catholic worship. The facade and ornate nave sit among Old Town streets near the former monastery ranges.

Town Hall Square

The neoclassical Vilnius Town Hall faces an elongated square that once served as the old city's main marketplace and administrative center. Merchant buildings, churches, cafes and cultural venues line the surrounding streets. Festivals, fairs, ceremonies and outdoor events use the open paving, while the current town hall functions mainly for representation and culture.

Church of St. Casimir

St Casimir's Church in Vilnius is a seventeenth-century Jesuit Baroque church dedicated to Lithuania's patron saint, funded by nobles of the Commonwealth era. A crown-shaped dome tops the crossing. Outside Mass the ornate interior is usually open for quiet visits.

Orthodox Church of the Holy Spirit, Vilnius

The Orthodox Church of the Holy Spirit in Vilnius has been a significant religious and cultural hub for over four centuries. It was established in 1567 by Helena, the wife of Alexander Jagiellon, and later expanded with a monastery, school, and printing shop. The church houses an ornate Baroque reliquary containing the relics of three saints: Anthony, John, and Eustathius.

Halės Market

A steel-framed brick hall built in 1906 houses Hales Market between Vilnius station and the Gates of Dawn; modern food counters now mix with longstanding daily trade, making the market useful for understanding both neighborhood shopping and recent culinary change. Butchers, fishmongers, produce stalls, bakeries, cheese sellers, cafes and specialty shops share the interior and surrounding stands.

Tauras Hill

Tauras Hill rises above central Vilnius on ground associated with former cemeteries, public buildings and plans for a national concert hall. Open slopes provide city views, walking routes and informal recreation, with winter sledding when snow conditions permit. Construction and redevelopment have periodically changed access and the appearance of the hilltop.

Uptown Bazaar

Uptown Bazaar occupies a commercial hall in Vilnius's Naujamiestis district, where food vendors, bars and small businesses serve nearby offices and residents. The tenant mix changes frequently, and evening dining can bring more activity than daytime shopping. Its industrial-style interior reflects the reuse of buildings within a rapidly changing urban neighborhood.
