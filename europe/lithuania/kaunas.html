Kaunas

Kaunas emerged as a fortified medieval trading post at the confluence of the Nemunas and Neris rivers, frequently contested by the Teutonic Knights. The city's most era occurred between 1920 and 1939, when it served as the temporary capital of independent Lithuania following the loss of Vilnius. This period triggered a massive building boom, giving Kaunas a extensive and cohesive collections of modernist and Art Deco architecture in Eastern Europe. Visitors can trace this interwar golden age along Laisvės Alėja and the surrounding modernist civic buildings.

Pažaislis Monastery and Church

Pažaislis Monastery and Church, constructed in 1664 by Camaldolese monks, is a example of late baroque architecture in North-Eastern Europe. The monastery complex has been an integral part of Kaunas' history for over 350 years and is now a popular destination for cultural and film tourism. Visitors can enjoy guided tours of the monastery, which also offers a hospitality complex for those who wish to extend their stay.
