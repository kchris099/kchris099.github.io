Kaunas

Kaunas emerged as a fortified medieval trading post at the confluence of the Nemunas and Neris rivers, frequently contested by the Teutonic Knights. The city's most era occurred between 1920 and 1939, when it served as the temporary capital of independent Lithuania following the loss of Vilnius. This period triggered a massive building boom, giving Kaunas a extensive and cohesive collections of modernist and Art Deco architecture in Eastern Europe. Visitors can trace this interwar golden age along Laisvės Alėja and the surrounding modernist civic buildings.

Pažaislis Monastery and Church

Italian architects and artists created Pažaislis Monastery in the seventeenth century for the Camaldolese order, producing one of Lithuania's major Baroque ensembles. A domed church, frescoes, stucco, cloisters, courtyards and monastic buildings overlook the Kaunas Reservoir.

Kaunas Castle

At the meeting of the Nemunas and Neris rivers, surviving walls and a reconstructed tower mark Kaunas Castle. The masonry fortress first appeared in the fourteenth century and endured repeated conflict with the Teutonic Order, flooding, and later dismantling; museum displays explain the site, while surrounding parkland reveals how little of the original enclosure remains above ground.

Kaunas Town Hall

Kaunas Town Hall, nicknamed the White Swan, dominates the old town square with a tall baroque tower above white facades. Civic ceremonies and museum displays occupy interiors looking onto the square. Confluence Park, Kaunas Castle, and nearby museums sit within a short walk along the Nemunas and Neris.

Cathedral-Basilica of St. Peter and St. Paul

Kaunas Cathedral Basilica developed from a fifteenth-century brick church into a large Gothic, Renaissance and Baroque religious complex near Town Hall Square. Rebuilding after fires and wars produced its present interior, chapels, altars and tower. As the seat of the Roman Catholic archdiocese, it is an active place of worship.

Aleksotas Observation Deck

A terrace on Aleksotas hill looks across the Nemunas toward Kaunas Old Town, its church towers, bridges, and river junction. The viewpoint can be reached from nearby streets and is often paired with the historic funicular below. Short approach paths link the viewpoint to road access and parking.

Lithuanian Zoo

The Lithuanian Zoo in Kaunas sits among large oak stands and focuses on conservation education alongside animal displays. Paths introduce fauna from multiple continents within a forested city setting. School groups and families use it as the country's main zoological park.

Church of St Michael the Archangel

Built for the Russian imperial garrison in the 1890s, St. Michael the Archangel Church dominates the end of Laisves aleja with a Neo-Byzantine dome and ring of smaller cupolas. The building later served Catholic, Orthodox, and secular uses before returning to Roman Catholic worship. An underground exhibition may operate separately from the main church and follows its own schedule.

National Kaunas Drama Theater

Lithuania's first professional drama company began performing in Kaunas in 1920, and the National Kaunas Drama Theatre continues that tradition on Laisves aleja. Several halls support Lithuanian and international productions, premieres, festivals, and educational programs. The building is primarily a working performance venue, so interiors are normally experienced through scheduled shows or organized events.

Kaunas State Musical Theater

Housed in a late nineteenth-century city theatre building downtown, Kaunas State Musical Theater stages musical works directed by Lithuanian and other European professionals. Each season brings at least two premieres. Warm lighting and carefully tuned acoustics shape the house for singers and orchestra alike.

Church of St. Gertrude

St. Gertrude's Church stands in a secluded courtyard behind buildings near Kaunas's Laisves aleja. The compact late Gothic brick structure dates from the fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries, with later Baroque changes to the interior. Soviet closure interrupted worship before restoration, and the revived church now combines medieval fabric with continuing religious use.

Žalgirio arena

Zalgiris Arena stands on an island in the Nemunas as the home court of BC Zalgiris and a major event venue in Kaunas. Opened in 2011, it changes capacity according to the event. Basketball and ice events use the arena alongside concerts and exhibitions, with seating reaching roughly 15,000 to 17,500 by configuration. Riverside paths connect the building with central Kaunas.

Christ's Resurrection Basilica

A white modernist basilica crowns Zaliakalnis as a monument to Lithuania's national revival and restored independence. Construction began between the world wars, but Soviet authorities converted the unfinished building to industrial use; completion and consecration followed after independence returned. A roof terrace, tower, broad nave, and austere geometry distinguish it from Kaunas's older Baroque churches.

Kaunas Ninth Fort Museum

The 9th Fort of the Kaunas Fortress, originally built by the Russian Tsar in the 1800s, has a haunting history as a Nazi execution site during World War II. It now is a museum and memorial to the 50,000 Lithuanians and Jews who lost their lives there. The massive monument erected on the site is a solemn reminder of this tragic past.

VDU Botanical garden

Vytautas Magnus University maintains this botanical garden on the former Freda estate, combining historical parkland with scientific plant collections. Greenhouses, rose beds, medicinal plants, dendrological areas and ponds support research and teaching. Seasonal displays and public walks reveal different parts of the collection across the year, while the estate landscape adds historical context.
