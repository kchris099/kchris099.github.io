Brașov

Brașov was founded by the Teutonic Knights in the early thirteenth century and subsequently settled by Transylvanian Saxons, who developed it into a heavily fortified and prosperous commercial hub at the crossroads of the Ottoman Empire and Western Europe. The city maintained significant autonomy and wealth, defending itself against numerous invasions from the south. The Saxon mercantile history and medieval defenses remain largely intact. Today, visitors explore the vast Black Church, the Council Square, and the preserved watchtowers of the medieval walls.

The Council Square

Brasov's Council Square developed as the commercial and civic center of the fortified Saxon town. The former council house, colorful facades, churches, museums, cafes, and public events surround an open space rebuilt and altered over centuries. Surrounding the square are baroque buildings and cafes to explore.

Black Church

The Black Church is a Gothic church with a rich history dating back to the 1600s. It earned its name from a fire that blackened its walls, and today it is adorned with striking murals and Ottoman rugs. The church is an important landmark in the Saxon town of Brasov, offering visitors a glimpse into its storied past.

Turnul Negru

Turnul Negru, also known as the Black Tower, is a restored 15th-century tower with a glass roof that now is a weapon exhibit. Despite its name, the tower is actually white and got its name from the blackened soot of a fire in 1599. Originally built as a lookout tower outside of the town's defensive walls, it stands at 11 meters tall on Warthe Hill.

The White Tower

Restored as a fifteenth-century watchtower, Brașov's White Tower holds museum photos of city history inside a horseshoe-shaped plan that once formed a key fortress bastion. Walks continue toward the Black Church and Republicii Street's mixed Renaissance, Classic, and Baroque fronts. The semicircular outline still reads as a defensive remnant.

Catherine Gate

Catherine's Gate survives from Brasov's medieval fortifications as a stone passage crowned by four corner turrets, and saxon authorities controlled movement through this entrance from the Romanian Schei district, with tolls and restrictions reflecting the city's ethnic hierarchy. The adjacent Schei Gate is a later structure.

Saint Nicholas Cathedral

St. Nicholas Church anchors Brasov's Schei district beside a historic Romanian school museum. Medieval origins were transformed through Gothic, Baroque and later Orthodox rebuilding, producing an interior with frescoes, icons, chapels and a cemetery. Its religious and educational connections made the complex an important center of Romanian community life in Transylvania.

The Fortress on the Watchtower

Brasov's Citadel on Straja Hill developed from a watchpoint into a fortified refuge with walls, bastions and interior buildings. Military, storage, prison and later hospitality uses altered the complex over time. The Fortress-on-Straja, originally constructed in the 15th century, served various purposes such as a lookout point, prison, and city archives.

Belvedere

The Belvedere above Brasov is a hillside viewpoint reached from roads and paths climbing beyond the old town. From the open slope, the Black Church, red roofs, newer districts and forested mass of Tampa Mountain appear within a single panorama. The view explains Brasov's position in a mountain basin. It also shows how closely later neighborhoods surround the medieval center.

Fortified Evangelical Church Harman

Harman's fortified Evangelical church grew from a medieval Saxon basilica east of Brasov, then gained defensive walls and towers as regional insecurity increased. Inside the enclosure, storage chambers and protected courtyards show how the village prepared for attack. The church and a separate chapel preserve Gothic details and fragments of wall painting, combining religious life with the architecture of communal refuge.

Seven Ladders Canyon

Seven Ladders Canyon cuts through limestone in the Piatra Mare Mountains south of Brasov. Metal ladders and walkways climb beside a sequence of waterfalls through a narrow, wet gorge reached by forest trail. Closure can follow ice, flooding, maintenance, or storms, and the route requires suitable footwear and physical confidence despite its short canyon section.

Valea Cetatii Cave

Discover the enchanting underground world of Valea Cetatii Cave, a natural formation shaped by water over time. Located a protected area, this cave has the Cathedral Hall, accessible through an ancient forest. Inside, marvel at the 30,000 cubic meter space adorned with calcite formations and hanging white arches reflected in small pools of water. While reaching the cave involves a steep climb from the parking lot, it's manageable for most visitors.
