Destination Guide — Bucharest

Explore Bucharest

A Brief History

Bucharest became capital of Wallachia and later united Romania, growing from a princely court on the Dâmbovița River. Fire and 19th-century rebuilding produced wide boulevards modelled on Paris, earning the nickname 'Little Paris.' The Palace of the Parliament, Orthodox patriarchal cathedral, and Lipscani old town record royal, communist, and post-1989 transformations in Romania's principal political and cultural centre.

Top Sights & Activities

Carol I Park

"Carol I" Park, located in the southern-central area of Bucharest on Filaret Hill, is a historic park established in 1906. It offers a French garden, historical monuments, and a picturesque lake spread across 41 hectares. The park features generous walkways for walking, cycling, and rollerblading as well as playgrounds for children. Visitors can admire important monuments such as the Mausoleum housing fallen soldiers from World War II and the Roman Arenas.

lei15

Palatul Mogoșoaia

Palatul Mogoșoaia, located just outside Bucharest, Romania, is a 17th-century palace that showcases the beauty of Romanian Renaissance architecture. Constructed by Prince Constantin Brancoveanu in 1702, this historical gem features intricate carvings and elegant courtyards surrounded by lush gardens. The expansive park offers a serene escape from the bustling city, complete with picnic areas and a tranquil lake—perfect for leisurely strolls or relaxing afternoons.

Free

Radu Vodă Monastery

Located just 10 minutes from Bucharest's central Unirii Square, Radu Vodă Monastery is one of the oldest monuments in the city. Dating back to 1614, it was built on the site of a previous church that was destroyed by the Ottoman army. The monastery's architecture draws inspiration from the renowned Curtea de Arges Monastery.

Free

The Patriarchal Cathedral

The Patriarchal Cathedral is a significant religious and civic landmark that holds a popular Palm Sunday pilgrimage and various other services. It is part of a building complex that includes the Patriarchal Palace, offering visitors an impressive sight. The area also features attractions like the Carturesti bookstore, University Square, Sfantul Anton church, Lipscani neighborhood for nightlife, Herastrau Park, Cismigiu Park, and Bellu Cemetery.

85 RON

Palace of Parliament

The Palace of Parliament is a colossal building and international conference center with an ornate interior. It was constructed in a traumatic transformation for the city, leading to the destruction of churches, homes, and displacement of families. This mega construction dreamt up by late dictator Nicolae Ceausescu is considered the heaviest building in the world, using massive amounts of steel, marble, and sand sourced locally.

15 RON

The Romanian Athenaeum

The Romanian Athenaeum is a neoclassical concert hall that has been a symbol of national culture for over 120 years. Designed by French architect Albert Galleron, it was funded through a public subscription and national lottery. Located in Revolution Square, it is surrounded by important landmarks such as the former Royal Palace (now the National Museum of Art) and the Memorial of Rebirth.

lei25

The Royal Palace of Bucharest

The Royal Palace of Bucharest, also known as the Palace of the Republic, is a grand former royal residence that was reconstructed in the 1930s. It has a rich history dating back to the 19th century and has been home to significant Romanian art from that period through to the 20th century. The palace served as the primary residence for Romania's kings until communism took over in 1947.

Free

Memorial of Rebirth

Nestled in the heart of Bucharest's Revolution Square, the Memorial of Rebirth stands as a poignant tribute to the victims of the 1989 Romanian Revolution. This striking 25-meter tall marble monument, inaugurated in August 2005, features a soaring white pillar that symbolizes hope and resilience among the Romanian people. Atop this impressive structure rests a metal crown, representing the hardships endured under Communist rule.

Free

Kretzulescu Church

Located in Revolution Square, Kretzulescu Church is a striking Orthodox church dating back to the 1720s. Its red-brick facade and bell towers stand as a testament to its enduring history. Built in the Brancovenesc style, it has survived bombings, demolitions during the communist era, revolutions, and earthquakes. Commissioned by the Cretulescu family, this small yet historically rich church boasts later icons and interior frescoes that have remained untouched by wars or disasters.

Free

Cărturești Verona

Cărturești Verona is a unique and charming bookshop located in the center of Bucharest. Housed in an old mansion, each room features a different design and offers a variety of gifts and books categorized by genre. Despite needing some renovation, this huge bookshop boasts an extensive collection of English language books for all ages, including fiction and nonfiction titles. Visitors can explore numerous nooks and crannies throughout the building, including the basement and attic.

Free

St. Anthony’s Church

Saint Anthony's Church, located next to the Old Court in Bucharest, is the oldest church in the city, dating back to 1559. Its central location makes it visited churches in Bucharest. The building has a rich history, with three previous churches standing on the same site before its current incarnation. Visitors are often impressed by its well-preserved original aspect and rich decorations. The church also features a beautiful rose garden outside.

Free

Domnița Bălașa Church

The Domnița Bălașa Church, a 19th century architectural gem in Bucharest, stands out for its unique features. The interior reflects Western architectural influences and is adorned with stained glass windows from Germany and marble statues that deviate from the traditional Christian Orthodox style.

Free

Arcul de Triumf

The Triumphal Arch, a 27-meter high granite structure located in Bucharest, Romania, serves as a powerful symbol of the country's independence and resilience. Built in 1936 to commemorate the reunification of Romania in 1918, this monument echoes the grandeur of its Parisian counterpart. Adorned with intricate carvings and sculptures that honor significant events in Romanian history, the arch stands as a testament to national pride and solidarity.

Free

Cașin Church

Cașin Church, a grand Orthodox church in Bucharest, was built in the 1930s and is a example of Neo-Romanian architecture. The church features a Greek cross layout with domes and Byzantine-style mosaics that reflect the prosperity of interwar Romania. Designed by architect Dimitrie Ionescu-Berechetit, it stands near the popular landmark Arcul de Triumf.

Free

Herastrau Park

Bucharest Herăstrău Park, home to the oldest man-made lake in the city, offers a 7.4-kilometer scenic path along its banks. The park features magnificent cypress trees, numerous cafes and restaurants, playgrounds for children, and sports grounds including old-school workout equipment. Visitors can enjoy a leisurely walk around the lake or engage in activities like cycling, kayaking, and canoeing.

Free

Monument to the Heroes of the Air

Nestled in the heart of Bucharest's Aviators' Square, the Monument to the Heroes of the Air is a striking tribute that stands 20 meters tall. Inaugurated in 1935, this impressive stone obelisk honors the brave pioneers and pilots who lost their lives while advancing Romanian aviation. Adorned with 13 bronze plaques, it features over 270 names of airmen who perished in crashes or battles up until the mid-1930s.

Free

The House of the Free Press

The House of the Free Press, known as Casa Scanteii during the Communist era, is a striking example of Soviet-style architecture located in Bucharest. Completed in 1957 and towering at 104 meters, it was once the tallest building in the city until 2007. Designed by architect Horia Maicu, this monumental structure was intended to centralize all printing presses and newsroom offices under state control.

Free

Saint Nicholas Russian Church

Saint Nicholas Church, also known as the Bucharest Russian Church, is a captivating gem nestled near University Square. Constructed in 1905 under the guidance of Russian Ambassador Mikhail Nikolaevich Giers, this charming Orthodox church showcases Russian architectural elements with its seven distinctive onion domes that once gleamed with gold. While many Orthodox churches grace Bucharest's landscape, Saint Nicholas stands out for its unique design reminiscent of St. Petersburg.

Free

Macca - Villacrosse Passage

Macca - Vilacrosse Passage, formerly known as the Jewelry Passage, is a charming covered passageway in Bucharest. It connects Calea Victoriei to Strada Lipscani and is lined with various restaurants, cafes, and bars. The 19th-century passage features an elegant stained-glass ceiling and a beautiful rotunda with graceful archways. At night, the space is illuminated by warm overhead orbs, creating a romantic atmosphere.

Free

Palace of the Deposits and Consignments

The Palace of the Deposits and Consignments, also known as Casa de Economii s Consemnatiuni, is a architectural marvel in Bucharest. Erected in 1900, this bank building features a glass-paneled dome and intricate architectural details that exude grandeur and elegance.

Free

Alexandru Ioan Cuza Park

I.O.R. Park, also known as Parcul Alexandru Ioan Cuza or Titan Park, is a large and beautiful park located in Sector 3 of Bucharest. It is a popular destination for both outdoor enthusiasts and families with children due to its picturesque lake featuring five small islands, a charming wooden church, playgrounds, and various sports facilities.

Free

Tineretului Park

Youth Park, located in the south side of Bucharest, is a sprawling 200-hectare park known for its diverse attractions. The park features Sala Polivalenta sports complex and Oraselul Copiilor, the largest entertainment park in Romania during the 80s. Over the years, it has been modernized with a cleaned lake, flower-decorated spaces, playgrounds, outdoor exercise areas, a sky slope, and a section for skateboarders.
