Bucharest

Bucharest became capital of Wallachia and later united Romania, growing from a princely court on the Dâmbovița River. Fire and 19th-century rebuilding produced wide boulevards modelled on Paris, earning the nickname 'Little Paris.' The Palace of the Parliament, Orthodox patriarchal cathedral, and Lipscani old town record royal, communist, and post-1989 transformations in Romania's principal political and cultural centre.

Carol I Park

Carol I Park opened in 1906 on Filaret Hill in southern-central Bucharest. Lawns and tree alleys lead to monuments including a World War II mausoleum and the Roman Arenas amphitheater, so the park mixes everyday walking space with civic memorial ground used for commemorations and open-air events.

Palatul Mogoșoaia

Palatul Mogoșoaia, built by Prince Constantin Brâncoveanu and completed around 1702, stands just outside Bucharest as a landmark of Brâncovenesc architecture. Carved stone details and courtyards open onto lakeside gardens. The palace complex now hosts museum displays and cultural events in the restored wings.

Radu Vodă Monastery

Radu Voda Monastery stands on an archaeological hill near Bucharest's Dambovita River, with earlier religious foundations beneath a church rebuilt in the seventeenth century. Frescoes, icons, tombs and monastic buildings reflect repeated restoration and active Orthodox worship. The layered architecture records several construction phases in place of a single original design.

Bucharest's Patriarchal Cathedral hosts Palm Sunday pilgrimages and regular Orthodox services as a major religious and civic landmark. Domes and courtyard space organize the hilltop complex. Exterior walls and the main interior stay viewable outside liturgies.

Palace of Parliament

Nicolae Ceausescu's regime created Bucharest's Palace of Parliament during the 1980s after demolishing large sections of the historic city. Vast halls, marble, woodwork, offices, parliamentary chambers, conference spaces and unfinished areas reveal both craft and authoritarian excess. Guided access is controlled by security.

The Romanian Athenaeum

The Romanian Athenaeum is a neoclassical concert hall that has been a symbol of national culture for over 120 years. Designed by French architect Albert Galleron, it was funded through a public subscription and national lottery. Located in Revolution Square, it is surrounded by important landmarks such as the former Royal Palace (now the National Museum of Art) and the Memorial of Rebirth.

The Royal Palace of Bucharest

Bucharest's former Royal Palace now houses the National Museum of Art of Romania on Revolution Square. Rebuilt and expanded across the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, it contains Romanian medieval and modern art as well as European galleries. Royal apartments and state history form part of the building's story, while the monarchy ended in 1947 and museum use reshaped the interiors.

Memorial of Rebirth

Inaugurated in August 2005 in central Bucharest, the Memorial of Rebirth rises as a roughly twenty-five-meter marble pillar marking the 1989 revolution. The white column and surrounding plaza sit amid government streets near the Athenaeum district, read as a civic memorial rather than a religious monument.

Kretzulescu Church

Located in Revolution Square, Kretzulescu Church is a striking Orthodox church dating back to the 1720s. Its red-brick facade and bell towers stand as a evidence of its long running history. Built in the Brancovenesc style, it has survived bombings, demolitions during the communist era, revolutions, and earthquakes. Commissioned by the Cretulescu family, this small yet historically rich church has later icons and interior frescoes that have remained untouched by wars or disasters.

Cărturești Verona

Cărturești Verona occupies a former banking house on Bucharest's Strada Verona, converted into a multi-level bookstore with spiral stairs, galleries, and reading nooks that run from basement rooms up into the attic. Shelves fill former offices and corridors, so browsing feels like walking through the building's older layout rather than a single open sales floor near the Athenaeum.

St. Anthony’s Church

St. Anthony Church stands beside Bucharest's Old Princely Court as a sixteenth-century Orthodox foundation rebuilt after fires, earthquakes and political change; it is often called the city's historic surviving church, though that claim depends on how continuity and reconstruction are defined. Brick walls, frescoes, icons and a compact nave continue to support worship in the historic center.

Arcul de Triumf

Bucharest's Arcul de Triumf rises about 27 meters in granite on the northern approach to the city, finished in 1936 to mark Romania's reunification of 1918. Reliefs and sculptures on the piers and attic commemorate campaigns and civic figures from that era. The arch sits in a traffic roundabout, so the carved faces and inscriptions read clearly from the surrounding lawns and sidewalks.

Herastrau Park

King Michael I Park, long known as Herastrau Park, surrounds a large lake in northern Bucharest. Paths, lawns, woodland, boating areas, restaurants, monuments and cultural institutions fill an extensive landscape developed in the 1930s. The water and shoreline were reshaped as part of the park project, while routes and activities vary by section and season. The lake gives the park its broad central axis.

Monument to the Heroes of the Air

The Monument to the Heroes of the Air was inaugurated in 1935 as a stone obelisk for Romanian pilots and aviation pioneers who died in service. It stands near the Arcul de Triumf district, where open pavement and traffic circles leave the shaft visible from approaching avenues and park lawns.

The House of the Free Press rises at Bucharest's northern approach as a monumental 1950s publishing complex built under communist rule. Soviet-influenced massing, a central tower, offices, former presses, and changing media tenants reflect the state's control of information.

Saint Nicholas Russian Church

Built in the early twentieth century near University Square, Saint Nicholas Russian Church introduced a distinctly Russian Orthodox form to Bucharest. Seven onion domes, brick and stone decoration, icons, and a compact interior reflect diplomatic and religious ties with the Russian Empire; later political changes affected the parish, while the building is an active church.

Macca - Villacrosse Passage

Macca-Villacrosse Passage cuts a glazed, horseshoe route between Calea Victoriei and Strada Lipscani in Bucharest's old center. Twin fork galleries hold cafes, bars, and small restaurants under yellow-tinted glass roofs. After dark, overhead orb lamps warm the brick paving, and the short walk stays busy as people cut between the two streets without stepping back onto the main avenues.

Palace of the Deposits and Consignments

The Palace of the Deposits and Consignments, commonly known as the CEC Palace, was completed around 1900 for Romania's public savings institution. An ornate stone facade and large glass-and-metal dome overlook Calea Victoriei opposite the National History Museum. The building remains associated with banking, so its grand exterior is usually the main view from the surrounding streets of Bucharest's historic center.

Alexandru Ioan Cuza Park, also called IOR or Titan Park, surrounds a large lake in eastern Bucharest; management and naming have changed over time. Paths, islands, sports grounds, playgrounds, lawns, public art and community events serve the surrounding residential districts.

Tineretului Park

On Bucharest's south side, this roughly 200-hectare Youth Park holds a cleaned lake, flower beds, playgrounds, outdoor exercise zones, a sky slope, and skateboard space. Paths and lawns carry everyday recreation. Modernization work has steadily expanded the activity mix.
