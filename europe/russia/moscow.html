Moscow

Moscow became the political centre of Muscovy after the fourteenth century and later the Soviet Union. Kremlin cathedrals, Red Square processions, and metro palaces mark Orthodox ritual, autocratic rule, and twentieth-century state power in the Russian heartland. Bolshoi Theatre precincts and Arbat pedestrian lanes still extend Kremlin ceremony into wider Muscovite boulevards.

Cathedral of Christ the Saviour

Moscow's Cathedral of Christ the Saviour is a 1990s reconstruction of the nineteenth-century church demolished under Stalin. Its dome, frescoed interior, icons and ceremonial spaces restore a monument originally dedicated to Russia's victory over Napoleon. The building is an active Orthodox cathedral and a prominent element of the Moscow River skyline, with security and worship shaping public access. The rebuilt cathedral also carries strong political symbolism.

Poklonnaya Hill

Poklonnaya Hill and Victory Park form a large Moscow memorial landscape devoted mainly to the Soviet experience of World War II. Monuments, museums, fountains, religious buildings and military displays line broad ceremonial avenues. The hill's long association with western approaches to the city adds historical context to a complex shaped by state memory and public commemoration.

Kazan Cathedral

Kazan Cathedral on Red Square is a late twentieth-century reconstruction of a seventeenth-century Orthodox church demolished during the Soviet period. Colorful towers and a compact interior recall the earlier building. Icons and religious decoration connect the church with traditions surrounding the 1612 expulsion of Polish-Lithuanian forces and the veneration of the Kazan image of the Virgin.

Moscow Art Theatre

The Chekhov Moscow Art Theatre occupies a historic building at 3 Kamergersky Lane, where the company has performed since 1902. Konstantin Stanislavski and Vladimir Nemirovich-Danchenko founded the theater in 1898. Their ensemble methods and productions of Anton Chekhov reshaped modern drama. Fyodor Shekhtel remodeled the venue, whose stages, foyer and museum material preserve that theatrical history.

Danilov Monastery, named after its founder Daniel of Moscow, has a rich history dating back to 1282. The monastery's defensive walls were built in the mid-17th century and it is now enclosed by seven towers. Sanduny

Sanduny Baths trace their history to 1808, while the present complex took shape during a major rebuilding in the 1890s. Public bathing halls preserve the social customs of the Russian banya. Steam rooms, cold pools, washing areas and private rooms support the bathing ritual. Some interiors remain restrained; others use rich tilework, vaulting and ornament associated with late imperial Moscow.
