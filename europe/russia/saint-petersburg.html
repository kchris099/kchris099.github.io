Saint Petersburg

Saint Petersburg was founded in 1703 as Peter the Great's window on the Baltic, with canals and baroque palaces planned on marshland. Hermitage collections, fortress cathedrals, and Nevsky Prospekt record imperial capital culture until the Soviet era. Peterhof fountains and canal boat routes extend baroque imperial vistas along the Neva delta. Walking routes through the historic centre help visitors relate these monuments to wider patterns of settlement and civic life in Russia.

Peter and Paul Fortress

Peter and Paul Fortress began in 1703 as the military and administrative nucleus of Saint Petersburg on Hare Island. Bastions, gates and riverfront walls enclose the cathedral, mint, former prison and several museums. The complex records imperial government, political incarceration and the city's earliest planned development, even though its defenses were rarely tested in a conventional siege.

Palace Square

Palace Square unites the Winter Palace, General Staff Building, Guards Corps headquarters and Alexander Column in Saint Petersburg's ceremonial center. Imperial processions, the 1905 Bloody Sunday events, the 1917 revolution, Soviet parades and modern gatherings have all used the space. The square's meaning comes from architecture and political history.

Winter Palace

The Winter Palace served as the principal imperial residence in Saint Petersburg before the revolution and now forms the main building of the State Hermitage Museum. Bartolomeo Rastrelli's Baroque facades surround courtyards and state interiors repeatedly altered after fire and political change. Museum collections extend into several buildings.

St. Isaac's Cathedral

St. Isaac's Cathedral dominates central Saint Petersburg with a gilded dome, granite columns, mosaics, paintings and elaborate stone decoration completed in the nineteenth century. The interior is used mainly as a museum, while limited Orthodox services continue. A stairway to the colonnade opens broad views across the historic center and the Neva landscape.

Yusupov Palace

Yusupov Palace on the Moika preserves reception rooms, private apartments, galleries, a small theater and basement spaces associated with the 1916 killing of Grigori Rasputin. The aristocratic residence reflects several owners and remodeling campaigns, producing interiors that range from ceremonial halls to intimate family rooms. Different guided routes emphasize separate parts of the building and its political, social or architectural history.

Cruiser Aurora

The cruiser Aurora is moored on the Neva as a museum ship associated with the Russo-Japanese War, World War I and the 1917 revolution. Reconstructed interiors, naval exhibits, guns, machinery spaces and crew areas interpret a vessel repeatedly repaired and altered. The famous blank shot became a revolutionary symbol, while security and naval management govern access.

Grand Maket Russia

Grand Maket Russia shows a vast animated model of an imagined Russian landscape. Trains, vehicles, cities, industries, countryside, lighting cycles and small scenes unfold across a single indoor display in Saint Petersburg. Interactive controls and detailed observation drive the visit.

Savior on the Spilled Blood

The Church of the Savior on Spilled Blood is one of St. Petersburg's most well known landmarks, featuring a bright, colorful exterior of classic Russian Revival architecture and onion domes. It was built on the exact spot where Emperor Alexander II was assassinated in 1881. The interior is entirely covered with over 7,000 square meters of detailed, active mosaics.

The State Russian Museum, Mikhailovsky Palace

The State Russian Museum uses Mikhailovsky Palace and several related buildings to present Russian art across many periods. Icons, imperial portraiture, nineteenth-century painting, avant-garde work, sculpture, folk art and later movements trace changing artistic traditions. The palace forms the principal historic setting, while branch venues extend the institution beyond one building. Mikhailovsky Palace also contributes its own imperial architectural history.

Bol'shoy Sankt-Peterburgskiy Gosudarstvennyy Tsirk

The Bolshoi Saint Petersburg State Circus occupies a historic roundhouse near the Fontanka, long known as one of Russia's permanent circus buildings. A circular arena, tiered seating, and backstage animal and prop areas support seasonal programs of acrobatics, clowning, and equestrian acts. The facade and dome mark the venue from neighboring embankment streets beside the Savior on the Spilled Blood district.

Park 300-Letiya Sankt-Peterburga

The Park of Saint Petersburg's 300th Anniversary opened on reclaimed Gulf of Finland land for the city's 2003 tercentenary. Lawns, paths, beach areas, fountains and monuments spread across a broad waterfront landscape near newer towers and stadium infrastructure. Open views toward the bay and Lakhta Center give the park a distinctly modern setting within the expanding Primorsky district. The exposed shore gives the park a broad maritime character.

Primorskiy Park Pobedy

Primorskiy Park Pobedy faces the Gulf shore in western Saint Petersburg with tree-lined avenues, memorial plantings, and open sports lawns. Paths link the park toward neighboring waterfront greens, including the 300th Anniversary park farther along the coast, forming a continuous seaside walking belt.

Greenhouse Tauride Gardens

Greenhouse Tauride Gardens occupy part of the historic Tauride park belt in Saint Petersburg, with glasshouses and outdoor plantings beside formal lawns. Paths connect greenhouse halls to open garden rooms used for quiet walks away from the Nevsky crowds, especially in shoulder seasons when beds are refreshed.
