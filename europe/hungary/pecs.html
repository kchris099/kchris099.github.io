Pécs preserves Roman Sopianae cemeteries, an Ottoman mosque, and nineteenth-century civic buildings in southern Transdanubia. Early Christian burial chambers and Zsolnay ceramic heritage record Roman Pannonia, Ottoman occupation, and Hungarian industrial culture. Pécs TV Tower

Pecs TV Tower rises from Misina Hill as a broadcasting structure with public observation levels above the Mecsek landscape. The concrete tower opened in the 1970s and reaches roughly 197 meters, replacing an earlier lookout. Displays and a restaurant occupy the upper structure, where views extend across Pecs and the surrounding hills when visibility is clear. The hilltop setting adds height beyond the tower itself.

Pécs Zoo

Pécs Zoo occupies hillside grounds in the southern part of the city with outdoor enclosures for big cats, other mammals, and varied birds. Paths loop through a compact layout rather than a safari-scale park. English signage appears in many sections, and the site sits below the Mecsek slopes near the TV tower roads.

Nike Statue

Memos Makris's Nike statue, erected in 1975 as a Liberation monument, rises on a hillside site above Pécs with wide views over the city and Mecsek slopes. The winged figure and base occupy an open terrace rather than a street median. Paths approach from the TV tower and park roads.

Zsolnay Fountain

Donated in 1908 by Vilmos Zsolnay's son and designed by Andor Pilch, the ceramic fountain on Szechenyi ter honors the ceramics maker. It stands before the Catholic church on the main square. Warm months usually see the water running for passersby. Glazed tiles catch light on the square paving.

National Theatre of Pécs

Opened in 1895, the National Theatre of Pecs occupies an ornate neo-Baroque building on a central pedestrian street. A decorated auditorium, rehearsal rooms and workshops support drama, opera, dance and musical productions by resident companies and guests, while sculpture and layered exterior ornament reflect the civic ambition of the late nineteenth century.

Downtown Candlemas Church of the Blessed Virgin Mary

A large Ottoman dome dominates the Downtown Candlemas Church on Pécs's main square. The building began as the sixteenth-century Mosque of Pasha Qasim and later became a Catholic church, leaving a mihrab, Arabic inscriptions, and Islamic structural forms beside Christian altars and imagery. The removed minaret and altered interior reveal conversion.

Basilica of Pécs

Pécs Cathedral, also known as the Cathedral Basilica of the Assumption of the Blessed Virgin Mary, is a Catholic church with origins dating back to the 10th century. In Pecs, Hungary, this active cathedral has gardens and religious art. Its rich history and architectural design make it a top tourist attraction in the city. The cathedral's Romanesque Revival look from the 19th century adds to its allure.

Barbican

Pécs's Barbican is a circular fifteenth-century gate tower built to strengthen the approach to the bishop's castle. A narrow passage, stone walls, stairs, and adjacent defensive remains show how attackers were forced into a controlled entry zone; the surviving structure is unusual in Hungary, but it forms one part of a larger fortified precinct.

Vilmos Zsolnay Statue

The Vilmos Zsolnay Monument commemorates the manufacturer who transformed Pécs pottery into an internationally recognized ceramics industry. A central portrait figure and surrounding allegorical sculpture occupy a traffic-facing square, linking civic memory with the nearby district shaped by the Zsolnay factory. Stone and bronze figures form a conventional public monument associated with the city's industrial history.

Zsolnay Mausoleum

A wooded rise within the Zsolnay Cultural Quarter holds the Zsolnay family mausoleum, reached by steps lined with ceramic animal figures. The burial chamber and memorial architecture commemorate the industrial family whose factory shaped modern Pécs. Eosin-glazed details and the carefully staged approach connect funerary design with the company's materials, while interior access may depend on guided or seasonal arrangements.

Tettye Ruins

Tettye's broken stone walls are the remains of a Renaissance palace built for Bishop György Szatmári in the early sixteenth century. Later Ottoman use, quarrying and abandonment reduced the residence to an open ruin above Pécs. Lawns, paths, caves and hillside views now surround the masonry, but the site's value lies in reading a lost elite house within the city's changing frontier history.
