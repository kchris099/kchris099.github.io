Explore Geirangerfjord

A Brief History

The Geirangerfjord was carved by massive glaciers during successive ice ages, creating steep, crystalline rock walls that plunge deep into the sea. For centuries, isolated farming communities clung to the narrow mountain ledges, relying entirely on the fjord for transport and sustenance. The late nineteenth century brought early cruise tourism, transforming the remote fjord into a defining symbol of Norwegian natural heritage. Today, travelers experience this dramatic glacial landscape and the abandoned historic mountain farms by navigating the deep waters past the Seven Sisters waterfalls.

Top Sights & Activities

NOK 330 (toll road)

Dalsnibba Viewpoint

Dalsnibba Utsiktspunkt is a must-visit destination for anyone seeking vistas of Norway's landscapes. Perched high above, it boasts the Geiranger Skywalk, which offers the highest fjord view accessible by road. The journey to this viewpoint can be thrilling, especially for those with a fear of heights! Once you reach the top, you're greeted by awe-inspiring panoramas of snow-capped mountains and dramatic fjords that will leave you speechless.

Free

Flydalsjuvet

Flydalsjuvet is a captivating viewpoint located just behind the village of Geiranger in Norway. It offers a panoramic view of the Geirangerfjord and the surrounding mountains. The well-designed viewing platforms ensure safety while providing an immersive experience. Although parking is limited, it's worth visiting for the scenery and clean, free toilets available.

Free

Fossevandring Geiranger

Fossevandring Geiranger is a leisurely 10-minute walk from the small town's port, making it easily accessible for visitors who prefer not to take an excursion. The metal and concrete trail runs along the river, offering several viewpoints and well-maintained paths. It takes about 30 minutes to descend from the museum to the fjord and around an hour to ascend.

Free

Storsæterfossen

A waterfall where visitors can walk behind the cascading water for a unique experience. It remains a notable local point of interest situated in Geirangerfjord, Norway. Visitors can observe the surrounding area and its connection to the city's broader historical landscape. Local guides sometimes include it on walking routes through the centre.

NOK 350 (tent pitch)

Geiranger Camping

Geiranger Camping is considered the campsite in Geiranger, located right on the fjord in the village center. It offers modern amenities such as wifi and washing machines, and is conveniently close to a grocery store. However, it's recommended to arrive early during peak camping season as spots fill up quickly. The site provides various facilities including washer and dryer, dish cleaning area, cook tops, grey water station with fresh water, and toilet station.

NOK 150

Norwegian Fjord Centre

The Norwegian Fjord Centre is a modern and family-friendly museum that offers an interactive exhibition showcasing the Norwegian fjords. Visitors can explore the world heritage landscape and learn about its formation through informative displays and videos. The centre also features a cafe, gift shop, and EV charging capabilities in the parking lot.
