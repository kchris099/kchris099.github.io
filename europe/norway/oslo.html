Oslo

Oslo traces its origins to a medieval settlement at Bjørvika and was renamed and moved after fires in the 17th century. Union with Denmark and later Sweden shaped its harbour and Akershus fortress before full independence in 1905. Postwar expansion along the fjord, the City Hall, Vigeland sculptures, and museums on Bygdøy illustrate Oslo's development as Norway's capital and maritime gateway.

Akershus Fortress

Akershus Fortress, originally built in the 13th century, was strategically positioned to protect Oslo from external threats. Over the years, it has evolved into a complex that includes a medieval castle, military fortress, Renaissance palace, and active military installations. The fortress also houses two military museums and hosts various events. While the castle is currently closed for renovations, visitors can still explore the large complex and enjoy its historical significance.

The Royal Palace

Oslo's Royal Palace was completed in the nineteenth century for the Norwegian-Swedish monarchy and continues to support official functions of Norway's royal household. A broad neoclassical facade faces Karl Johans gate across Palace Park. Ceremonial rooms and offices occupy the interior, while public life around the grounds reflects the building's role in national representation.

Ekebergparken

More than forty classic and contemporary sculptures sit among Ekebergparken's wooded paths overlooking Oslo. Walkers combine art stops with city panoramas from the hillside clearings. Green space and sculpture share equal weight throughout a circuit of the grounds.

04 AdmissionFree

Oslo Cathedral

Oslo Cathedral opened in the late seventeenth century and later received major interior alterations, including twentieth-century ceiling painting and stained glass, and surrounding arcades and shops belong to the cathedral precinct. The brick church serves the Church of Norway for worship, royal events, national ceremonies, and concerts.

05 AdmissionFree

The Tiger

In Oslo, stands The Tiger, a striking 4.5-meter bronze sculpture created by Norwegian artist Elena Engelsen. This majestic tiger was presented to the city in honor of its millennium celebration in 2000 and has since become one of Oslo's most landmarks. Positioned outside Oslo Central Station, it draws visitors with its powerful presence and is a popular spot for photos and gatherings.

06 AdmissionFree

Oslo Central Station

Oslo Central Station (Oslo S) is the city's main rail terminus, linking regional trains, the airport express, metro, tram, and bus lines under one roof. The hall holds ticket offices, retail, and food outlets used by daily commuters and long-distance passengers. Pedestrian exits open toward the Opera House waterfront and downtown Karl Johans gate. The present complex replaced earlier terminus buildings as Oslo's rail network expanded.

59.9112° N / 10.7520° E

07 AdmissionFree

Oslo Opera House

The Oslo Opera House is a striking and contemporary architectural masterpiece located by the waterfront. It is the home to the Norwegian National Opera & Ballet. Designed by Snohetta, it resembles a glacier floating in the Oslofjord and is considered one of Scandinavia's most buildings. The interior can be fully appreciated through guided tours, offering insights into its thoughtful design that reflects Norway's cultural significance and public engagement.

The Norwegian Opera and Ballet

Snøhetta designed Oslo's opera house as a sloping marble and granite form descending toward the Bjørvika waterfront. Opened in 2008, it contains stages, workshops, foyers, art and the national opera and ballet companies. The Norwegian Opera and Ballet in Oslo is a modern architectural wonder that has become a signature work for the Norwegian National Ballet.

09 AdmissionFree

Deichman Bjørvika

Deichman Bjorvika opened in 2020 as Oslo's main public library near the opera house. Stacked floors and large windows contain reading areas, studios, media facilities, children's spaces and rooms for events and civic services. The building encourages study, production and social use across several levels, extending the idea of a library beyond book storage alone.

The Norwegian Museum of Cultural History

Open-air and indoor collections at the Norwegian Museum of Cultural History include a relocated stave church among farm buildings and urban interiors that track national life. Galleries open on posted hours on the Bygdøy museum peninsula. A single day can cover rural and town histories on the same campus.

Frogner Park

Frognerparken holds Gustav Vigeland's large sculpture installation among lawns, fountains, and flower beds in western Oslo. Paths weave between bronze and granite figures across the formal garden. Shade trees and open grass keep the park in daily recreational use beyond the sculptures.
