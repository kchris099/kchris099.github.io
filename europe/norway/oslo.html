Oslo

Oslo traces its origins to a medieval settlement at Bjørvika and was renamed and moved after fires in the 17th century. Union with Denmark and later Sweden shaped its harbour and Akershus fortress before full independence in 1905. Postwar expansion along the fjord, the City Hall, Vigeland sculptures, and museums on Bygdøy illustrate Oslo's development as Norway's capital and maritime gateway.

Akershus Fortress

Akershus Fortress, originally built in the 13th century, was strategically positioned to protect Oslo from external threats. Over the years, it has evolved into a complex that includes a medieval castle, military fortress, Renaissance palace, and active military installations. The fortress also houses two military museums and hosts various events. While the castle is currently closed for renovations, visitors can still explore the large complex and enjoy its historical significance.

The Royal Palace

The Royal Palace in Oslo, Norway, is a significant 19th-century residence that has played a important role in the country's history since 1814. It is the primary residence of the King and Queen and is where the King presides over the Council of State. During summer, guided tours offer visitors access to important rooms such as the Council Chamber and Banqueting Hall. The palace shares its park with the public, reflecting Norwegian values of humility and closeness to its people.

The Tiger

In Oslo, stands The Tiger, a striking 4.5-meter bronze sculpture created by Norwegian artist Elena Engelsen. This majestic tiger was presented to the city in honor of its millennium celebration in 2000 and has since become one of Oslo's most landmarks. Positioned outside Oslo Central Station, it draws visitors with its powerful presence and is a popular spot for photos and gatherings.

Oslo Central Station

Oslo Central Station (Oslo S) is the city's main rail terminus, linking regional trains, the airport express, metro, tram, and bus lines under one roof. The hall holds ticket offices, retail, and food outlets used by daily commuters and long-distance passengers. Pedestrian exits open toward the Opera House waterfront and downtown Karl Johans gate. The present complex replaced earlier terminus buildings as Oslo's rail network expanded.
