Ohrid

Ohrid stands on a lake that early Slavic missionaries used when Clement and Naum founded schools and churches under the Bulgarian tsars in the ninth and tenth centuries. Samuel's short-lived empire made the city a rival capital before Byzantine reconquest and later Ottoman mosques and town houses filled the amphitheatre slope. Fishing, tourism, and restored frescoed churches now sustain a town whose layered sacral architecture anchors North Macedonian cultural identity.

Ancient Macedonian Theatre of Lychnidos

Unlike later Roman theatres elsewhere in North Macedonia, the Theatre of Lychnidos dates to Greek times, with stone seating still readable on the hillside. Day visits and ticketed performances use the same bowl. Looking across the tiers makes earlier civic gatherings easy to imagine.

Church of the Virgin Mary Peribleptos

Fresco painters Michael and Eutychios signed major works inside the Church of the Virgin Peribleptos, built in 1295 above Ohrid. Dense cycles of saints and biblical scenes helped make the church central to late Byzantine art, and later use preserved important icons and ecclesiastical collections, while the nickname 'Macedonian Sistine Chapel' is promotional comparison.

41.1142° N / 20.7955° E

03 AdmissionFree

Church of St. Sophia

Saint Sophia preserves Ohrid's medieval cathedral in a building developed mainly during the eleventh century over earlier foundations. Byzantine frescoes cover the sanctuary and upper spaces, while Ottoman conversion to a mosque altered the interior before Christian restoration; concerts sometimes use the acoustics, and the church forms a core part of Ohrid's historic religious landscape.

41.1121° N / 20.7940° E

Plaoshnik Archaeological park

A restored ninth-century church stands among earlier ruins at Plaoshnik, including a fifth-century early Christian basilica with mosaic floors of birds and animals. Raised walkways let visitors view the mosaics closely. Excavation and reconstruction share the hilltop above Ohrid.

41.1128° N / 20.7908° E

Samuel's Fortress

Walls and towers crown Ohrid's old-town hill at Samuel's Fortress, built over earlier fortifications and associated with Tsar Samuel's medieval capital. Archaeological excavation revealed residential and military phases within the enclosure. Reconstructed ramparts provide views over Lake Ohrid, while the site's history extends beyond legends about a single ruler.

41.1150° N / 20.7911° E

06 AdmissionFree

Church of Saint Jovan the Theologian at Kaneo

A small cross-in-square church dedicated to Saint John the Theologian stands on a cliff at Kaneo above Lake Ohrid. Medieval construction and later rebuilding produced its brick-and-stone walls, octagonal dome and compact frescoed interior. Paths approach through the old town and shoreline, while worship, conservation and fragile cliff terrain require more context than its frequently photographed silhouette.

41.1111° N / 20.7888° E

07 AdmissionFree

Lake Ohrid

Lake Ohrid, located on the border between Albania and Macedonia, is a large ancient lake with a depth of 300m. It holds significant historical artifacts from the Turkish rule in the 18th century, including personal seals, weapons, and items related to prominent figures like the Robev family and Hristo Uzunov. The lake is also home to a unique aquatic network with over 200 endemic species.

41.0419° N / 20.7190° E

08 AdmissionFree

Chinar Tree

An plane tree stands near Ohrid's old bazaar and is traditionally associated with centuries of public gathering. Age estimates vary widely and hollowing, supports, pruning and urban pressure reflect the difficulty of preserving an ancient living tree; the surrounding square links markets, mosques, shops and pedestrian routes, giving the Chinar civic meaning beyond folklore.

41.1160° N / 20.8003° E

09 AdmissionFree

Mosque of Ali Pasha

Ali Pasha Mosque stands beside Ohrid's old bazaar and dates to the late fifteenth or early sixteenth century. Its square prayer hall and restored minaret reflect the Ottoman religious architecture that developed around the market district. Stone and brick shape the restrained exterior. Worship continues inside, linking the bazaar's present community with centuries of Islamic life in the city.

41.1155° N / 20.8005° E
