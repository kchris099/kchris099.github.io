Granada

Granada was the last Muslim kingdom on the Iberian Peninsula until its surrender to the Catholic Monarchs in 1492, ending centuries of Nasrid rule centered on the Alhambra palaces and Generalife gardens. The Albaicín quarter, cathedral, and royal chapel preserve Moorish street patterns and later Christian institutions. Snow-fed irrigation and mountain trade routes linked the city to the Sierra Nevada and the coast.

Alhambra

Across Sabika Hill, the Alhambra combines Nasrid palaces with defensive walls, towers, gardens, later Christian buildings and remains of a court city. Thirteenth- and fourteenth-century interiors use carved plaster, tile, timber and water to organize royal space. Carefully framed views connect rooms with courtyards and the surrounding landscape. Separate zones and timed routes reflect a complex whose military, residential and ceremonial functions developed over centuries.

Generalife Gardens

The Generalife developed as a rural retreat for Granada's Nasrid rulers on a hillside beside the Alhambra. Courtyards, water channels, terraces, orchards, pavilions and later gardens climb through the estate. Successive restoration shaped much of the planting, while irrigation systems and framed views reveal how productive land and royal leisure were combined.

Palace of Charles V

A square Renaissance exterior encloses the circular two-level courtyard of the Palace of Charles V inside the Alhambra. Pedro Machuca designed the building after the emperor's 1526 visit, asserting an imperial architectural language beside the Nasrid palaces. Construction continued unevenly for centuries, and museum galleries now occupy parts of a palace that was never fully used as planned. The circular court creates a strong spatial contrast with the Alhambra.

Puerta de Elvira

Puerta de Elvira once controlled the main road into Islamic Granada from the northwest. The surviving horseshoe arch formed part of a larger gate complex with towers, courtyards and adjoining walls. Much disappeared as the city expanded, leaving the monument amid busy streets at the edge of the Albaicin. Its name comes from the historic route toward Elvira.
