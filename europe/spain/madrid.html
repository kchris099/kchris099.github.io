Madrid

Madrid became the capital of Spain in 1561 under Philip II, replacing Valladolid. The Royal Palace, Plaza Mayor, and Prado Museum expanded through the Bourbon monarchy and 19th-century reforms. The city remained the seat of government through the Civil War and Franco era. Retiro Park and Gran Vía boulevard date from the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Today it hosts national ministries and museums.

Plaza Mayor

Madrid's Plaza Mayor took its present rectangular form in the early seventeenth century under Philip III. Arcaded buildings, painted facades, entrance arches and the equestrian royal statue enclose a space used over time for markets, ceremonies, punishments, festivals and public entertainment. Fires led to repeated rebuilding, while current restaurants and events occupy a square whose appearance is less uniformly original than it first seems.

Puerta del Sol

Puerta del Sol is Madrid's central square and kilometre-zero for Spain's radial roads. The Casa de Correos clock bells the New Year Twelve Grapes ritual each 31 December. Metro lines, shopping streets, and constant foot traffic cross the oval plaza through the day and night.

El Retiro Park

El Retiro grew from a royal palace garden into Madrid's major central park after opening to the public in the nineteenth century. Formal avenues, woodland, monuments, fountains, exhibition buildings, gardens and an artificial lake fill the former estate. The park forms part of the historic Paseo del Prado and Buen Retiro landscape, while temporary events, boating and individual venues follow separate schedules.

Museo Nacional del Prado

The Prado Museum houses a collection shaped by the Spanish monarchy, with major groups of works by Velazquez, Goya; el Greco, Titian, Rubens, Bosch and many others. Juan de Villanueva designed the neoclassical building for scientific purposes before it opened as a museum in 1819.

The Museo Nacional Centro de Arte Reina Sofía is Spain's national museum of 20th-century art. The museum was officially inaugurated on September 10, 1992, and is named for Queen Sofía. Palacio Real de Madrid

Madrid's Royal Palace rose in the eighteenth century after fire destroyed the old Alcazar. Designed for the Bourbon monarchy, its formal rooms contain tapestries, frescoes, decorative arts, armor and royal collections, while the building remains available for state ceremonies. The current royal family does not use it as an everyday residence and closures can occur when official functions take priority over public visits.

Temple of Debod

An ancient Egyptian temple stands in Parque del Oeste after being dismantled and given to Spain during the international campaign connected with the Aswan High Dam. Reassembled in Madrid in the 1970s, the Temple of Debod preserves reliefs and chambers from several rulers, though relocation changed its original landscape and orientation. Conservation concerns often restrict interior entry, while the surrounding platform remains a popular sunset viewpoint.

Puerta de Alcalá

Puerta de Alcalá is a triumphal arch located near Parque del Buen Retiro, adorned with elegant sculptures and stone carvings. Commissioned by King Charles III in the late 18th century, this neoclassical monument was designed by Italian architect Francesco Sabatini.

The Crystal Palace in El Retiro was built in 1887 as a glass-and-iron pavilion for an exhibition on the Philippines. Its transparent walls, dome, and lakeside setting later became part of the Reina Sofia Museum's program of temporary contemporary art installations; exhibitions and conservation closures change frequently, while the building's colonial exhibition origin deserves acknowledgment alongside its elegant engineering.

Puente de Toledo

Pedro de Ribera designed Puente de Toledo in the eighteenth century to carry the road across the Manzanares, and floods had destroyed earlier bridges at the site, while the present pedestrian setting developed after traffic was redirected and the river corridor was rebuilt. Catedral de Santa María la Real de la Almudena

Almudena Cathedral faces Madrid's Royal Palace on a site discussed for a capital-city cathedral over several centuries. Construction began in the late nineteenth century and continued through major design changes until consecration in 1993. Its neoclassical exterior responds to the palace, while neo-Gothic and modern interior decoration, a crypt, chapels, and a dome reveal the unusually long building history.
