Palma de Mallorca

Palma de Mallorca occupies a bay on the largest Balearic Island, with a cathedral begun after the Catalan conquest of 1229 on the site of a former mosque. The Almudaina palace served Moorish and later Aragonese rulers. Maritime trade and tourism shaped the 19th and 20th centuries. The old quarter retains city walls and narrow lanes. Ferries and an airport connect the city to mainland Spain and Europe.

Catedral-Basílica de Santa María de Mallorca

Begun after James I of Aragon captured Mallorca in 1229 on the site of the Great Mosque, this cathedral took centuries to finish. Catalan Gothic vaults and a vast rose window face the Parc de la Mar. Antoni Gaudí directed some interior restoration work in the early 20th century.

Sants de Mallorca

Sants de Mallorca is a visitor site in Palma de Mallorca. This church in Palma's old quarter reflects the faith communities that settled after the 13th-century Aragonese conquest. Stone portals and a modest bell tower fit the narrow streets of the former Muslim medina. It remains an active parish where local festivals mark saints' days through the year.

Can Forteza Rey

Can Forteza Rey is a visitor site in Palma de Mallorca. Completed in 1909 for a textile merchant, this modernisme townhouse on Plaça Marquès del Palmer shows Gaudí-influenced trencadís and wrought iron. Floral ceramic panels cover the facade. It illustrates how Catalan Art Nouveau reached Mallorca's bourgeois architecture in the early 20th century.

39.5708° N / 2.6513° E

Fundació Joan March Palma

The Fundació Joan March in Palma occupies a seventeenth-century mansion on Carrer de Sant Miquel and shows twentieth-century Spanish art from the banker's collection. Works by Picasso, Miró, Dalí, and Gris hang in period rooms. The foundation was meant to keep modern art visible to island residents.

39.5714° N / 2.6521° E
