Explore Palma de Mallorca

A Brief History

Palma de Mallorca has served as the critical strategic center of the Balearic Islands since its establishment as a Roman camp. It experienced significant cultural transformations during the periods of Byzantine and Moorish rule before being conquered by James I of Aragon in the 13th century. This layered, complex history is deeply reflected in its monumental Gothic cathedral and the surviving labyrinthine street plans of its historic center.

Catedral-Basílica de Santa María de Mallorca

Begun after James I of Aragon captured Mallorca in 1229 on the site of the Great Mosque, this cathedral took centuries to finish. Catalan Gothic vaults and a vast rose window face the Parc de la Mar. Antoni Gaudí directed some interior restoration work in the early 20th century.

Free

Sants de Mallorca

This church in Palma's old quarter reflects the faith communities that settled after the 13th-century Aragonese conquest. Stone portals and a modest bell tower fit the narrow streets of the former Muslim medina. It remains an active parish where local festivals mark saints' days through the year.

Free

Can Forteza Rey

Completed in 1909 for a textile merchant, this modernisme townhouse on Plaça Marquès del Palmer shows Gaudí-influenced trencadís and wrought iron. Floral ceramic panels cover the facade. It illustrates how Catalan Art Nouveau reached Mallorca's bourgeois architecture in the early 20th century.

Free

Fundació Joan March Palma

Housed in a 17th-century mansion on Palma's Carrer de Sant Miquel, this foundation displays 20th-century Spanish art collected by Joan March. Works by Picasso, Miró, Dalí, and Gris hang in period rooms. Admission is free, reflecting the banker patron's wish to share modern art with island residents.
