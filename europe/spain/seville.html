Seville

Seville developed as a Roman port on the Guadalquivir and later capital of Muslim al-Andalus under the Almohads before Christian reconquest in 1248. Its cathedral, alcázar, and archive of the Indies grew with Atlantic commerce after 1492, when the city licensed voyages to the Americas. Plaza districts, watchtowers, and parish churches record civic life in Andalusia's largest city.

Plaza de España

For the 1929 Ibero-American Exposition, Aníbal González shaped Plaza de España in María Luisa Park as a semicircle of tiled provincial alcoves, a canal, and bridges. Open pavement and the curved facade still define the civic setting. The complex remains Seville's most recognizable Expo-era square.

Catedral de Sevilla

Seville Cathedral rose from 1401 on the site of the Almohad mosque after the city's Christian conquest, retaining the Giralda as its bell tower from the former minaret. Gothic naves hold chapels, royal tombs, and the monument long associated with Christopher Columbus. The church remains the seat of the Archbishop of Seville beside the Alcazar walls.

Iglesia Colegial del Divino Salvador

The Church of the Divine Savior occupies the site of Seville's principal medieval mosque and later Christian buildings. Reconstructed in the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries, its Baroque interior contains monumental altarpieces, sculpture, painted vaults, and brotherhood chapels; the courtyard preserves columns and traces linked to the mosque, while the active parish remains distinct from the cathedral despite shared visitor arrangements.

Royal Alcázar of Seville

Seville's Royal Alcazar grew from earlier Islamic fortifications into a palace used by Castilian monarchs and later Spanish rulers, and the complex remains available for royal use and receives heavy demand, while its layered architecture resists simple labels such as a wholly Moorish palace. Mudejar halls commissioned by Pedro I stand beside Gothic rooms, Renaissance additions, courtyards, gardens, waterworks, and archaeological remains.

Triana Bridge

The Isabel II Bridge, commonly called Triana Bridge, crosses the Guadalquivir between central Seville and Triana. Opened in the nineteenth century, its iron arches replaced an earlier bridge of boats and supported the district's closer integration with the city. Road and pedestrian traffic still use the span, while river views connect it with the market, ceramics quarter, and former port landscape on both banks.

Conjunto Arqueológico de Itálica

Italica, founded in 206 BC near Santiponce, was the birthplace of emperors Trajan and Hadrian and preserves amphitheatre seating, street grids, and mosaic floors from the Roman city. Paths cross house foundations and drainage lines outside modern Seville. The archaeological park explains provincial Roman urban life beyond the Alcazar and cathedral.

Royal Tobacco Factory of Seville

The eighteenth-century Royal Tobacco Factory processed imported tobacco under a state monopoly and employed a large industrial workforce later associated with the story of Carmen. Moats, courtyards, gateways, workshops and a chapel form a palace-like complex now used by the University of Seville. Academic functions limit access to some interiors, but the building still records the labor, administration and colonial trade behind tobacco production.

Hercules Columns

Hercules Columns, located at the Alameda de Hercules, are a pair of ancient columns topped with statues of Hercules and Julius Caesar. These columns were originally part of a Roman temple dedicated to Lord March and have been standing in their original location on Calle Marmoles since the 21st century. The statues were sculpted by Diego de Pesquera, with one being a 1574 marble copy by Hercules Farnese.

Archivo de Indias

The General Archive of the Indies occupies Seville's former merchants' exchange beside the cathedral and Alcazar. Created as an archive in the eighteenth century, it holds extensive records of Spanish administration, trade, mapping, and colonial rule in the Americas and Philippines. Exhibitions show selected documents in a Renaissance building by Juan de Herrera.
