Santiago de Compostela

Santiago de Compostela grew around the shrine of Saint James, whose relics drew pilgrims from the 9th century along routes crossing Christian Europe. The Romanesque cathedral with its Baroque façade remains the goal of the Camino de Santiago. Archbishopric power, university foundations, and regional government shaped granite plazas, monasteries, and civic buildings in Galicia's administrative capital.

Cathedral of Santiago de Compostela

The Cathedral of Santiago stands above the traditional tomb of the apostle James and marks the end of major Camino pilgrimage routes; pilgrim rituals and worship remain central, while access to particular areas changes with services, restoration and crowd control. Romanesque structure, later chapels, a Baroque west facade, cloisters, treasury spaces and the vast incense burner reflect centuries of devotion and rebuilding.

Porch of the Glory

The Portico of Glory is Master Mateo's Romanesque entrance ensemble inside Santiago Cathedral, the sculpted threshold pilgrims traditionally reached at journey's end. Apostles, elders, and the seated Saint James fill the arches above the original pilgrim steps. The porch remains central to the cathedral's identity as the Camino's liturgical finish.

Palace of Fonseca

Fonseca Palace was founded in the sixteenth century for the University of Santiago under Archbishop Alonso de Fonseca; stone courtyards and historic rooms face the cathedral approaches, linking episcopal and academic use in one block still tied to university ceremonies. A Renaissance facade leads to a columned courtyard, chapel, library, and academic rooms that remain part of university life.

Museum of the Galician People

The Museo del Pueblo Gallego is a museum dedicated to the history and culture of Galicia, housed within a 13th-century convent complex. For a small fee, visitors can explore this ethnographic museum featuring artifacts dating back to prehistoric times. The museum offers interactive exhibits and performances that bring Galician traditions to life.

Colegiata de Santa María de Sar

Santa Maria de Sar is a Romanesque collegiate church outside Santiago's former walls, known for visibly leaning columns and walls stabilized by later exterior buttresses. A simple nave, carved capitals, cloister remains, and small museum preserve the atmosphere of a medieval religious community. The distortion reflects structural movement over centuries.

Praza de Galicia

Praza de Galicia lies beside Santiago's old town with views toward the cathedral towers and the Hostal dos Reis Católicos. Plane trees shade benches used by pilgrims arriving on the Camino. The square links the historic centre to modern administrative buildings along the Rúa do Vilar axis.

As Duas Marias

Bronze figures of As Duas Marias in Santiago's Alameda park recall two local sisters known for daily walks in traditional dress. Stone benches and pollarded trees line paths between the university quarter and the historic center. Locals treat the statues as a casual meeting point on the park ridge.

08 AdmissionFree

Parque da Alameda

Parque da Alameda above Santiago combines Paseo da Alameda, the oak grove of Santa Susana, and Passeo da Ferradura into one ridge park with cathedral views. Broad walks, bandstands, and tree cover serve evening strollers and student shortcuts. The park is the city's main green overlook rather than a botanical specialist garden.

09 AdmissionFree

Monumento al caminante

A modern sculpture of a walking pilgrim stands along one of Santiago de Compostela's approach routes. The figure's staff, pack and forward posture identify it with travelers completing the Camino. Its outdoor setting places the monument within the everyday movement of pedestrians entering the city. The work represents the repeated journeys that have shaped Santiago's identity as a pilgrimage destination.

42.8847° N / 8.4954° W

Convento de San Francisco de Santiago

Convento de San Francisco de Santiago, originally a 13th-century monastery for Franciscan friars, has been transformed into a refined hotel and restaurant. The historic building is located near a Gothic cathedral and notable monuments. The former convent now offers excellent facilities, delicious food with a special Pilgrim menu featuring typical monastery dishes, and well equipped service.
