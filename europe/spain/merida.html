Destination Guide — Central Spain

Explore Merida

A Brief History

Built in 1542 directly over the ancient Maya city of T'ho, Merida accumulated extensive wealth during the late 19th-century henequen boom. The resulting Parisian-style mansions along the Paseo de Montejo reflect a golden era that significantly shaped the cultural fabric of the Yucatan. Decades of careful preservation have ensured that this profound historical narrative remains a vital part of the modern experience.

Acueducto de los Milagros

Acueducto de los Milagros is a remarkable ancient Roman aqueduct located in Merida, built in the 1st century AD to supply fresh water to the city. This monumental structure spans over 800 meters and features three stories of semicircular arches, distribution towers, and a water tank. The northern end even integrated a pool for purifying the water. It's an extraordinary example of Roman engineering and stands as a testament to their mastery of hydraulic systems.

Teatro Romano de Mérida

The Teatro Romano de Mérida is a historic site that holds the remains of a Roman arena and waiting rooms where gladiator and animal fights took place. Built in 16 BC by the consul Vipsanius Agrippa, it has undergone several renovations over the centuries, including during the reign of Emperor Trajan and Constantine. With its sloped semicircular seating, it could accommodate up to 6,000 spectators.

€13

Roman Amphitheater of Mérida

The Anfiteatro de Mérida is located next to the impressive Teatro Romano and was constructed in 8 BCE. While it may not be as grand as its neighbor, it still held up to 14,000 spectators for thrilling gladiatorial contests. This amphitheater has a rich history and is an important part of the cultural heritage of Mérida.

Free

Roman House of the Amphitheater

Casa Romana del Anfiteatro is an ancient Roman house located next to the Roman theater and amphitheater in Merida. It offers a fascinating glimpse into the past, with well-preserved mosaics and remains of what could have been a mausoleum from that era. The site also features explanatory panels that provide historical context, enriching the travellers's experience.

Temple of Diana

The Temple of Diana is an ancient Roman temple in Merida, featuring columns on a raised pedestal and remnants of a medieval castle. The city offers quiet streets, inviting squares, and green spaces along the Guadiana river for leisurely walks. travellers can explore landmarks like the Arab Citadel and indulge in local gastronomy. Pórtico del Foro Municipal de Augusta Emérita

When you stroll from the Temple of Diana, encounter the Portico del Foro Municipal de Augusta Emérita, a remarkable 1st-century structure that served as an entrance to the Roman Forum. This grand portico features impressive Corinthian columns adorned with intricate decorations such as Medusa heads and Jupiter-Ammon representations. The walls of the portico once held statues of emperors, gods, and mythical figures in their niches.

Free

Trajan Arch

The Trajan Arch in Merida is a 1st-century archway that once led to a Roman temple. The city offers quiet streets, green spaces along the Guadiana river, and landmarks like the Arab Citadel. Nearby, travellers can explore Roman baths in Alange and the Prosperpina reservoir built by the Romans. Merida also boasts impressive Roman-era buildings such as the Temple of Diana and the well-preserved remnants of aqueducts.

Alcazaba of Mérida

The Alcazaba of Mérida is a 9th-century Moorish citadel that boasts Roman and Visigoth relics, including a fish-filled cistern. It offers scenic views from the top of its walls, providing a glimpse into early Spanish history with significant archaeology. The citadel is located near the Plaza del Rastro and the Roman Bridge of Mérida, which has stood for over 2,000 years as part of the Via de la Plata route.

Free

Roman Circus of Mérida

The Roman Circus of Mérida is a significant archaeological site that once hosted chariot races in ancient times. The well-preserved ruins offer a glimpse into the city's rich history and cultural heritage. travellers can also explore the tranquil streets, picturesque squares, and scenic riverbanks along the Guadiana River. Additionally, nearby attractions such as the Arab Citadel and Roman baths in Alange provide further insight into the region's past.

Free

Acueducto de San Lázaro

Acueducto de San Lázaro, located near the circus, is named after a demolished chapel dedicated to Saint Lazarus. Only a few original Roman elements remain, with most of it dating back only a few hundred years. The engineering and architecture of this ancient technology are impressive to witness. While not in a touristy area, the aqueduct itself is worth visiting. A short walk from town leads to the well-preserved three-tier arches in the park.

Free

Basilica of Santa Eulalia

Nestled within the historic city of Merida, the Basilica of Santa Eulalia stands as a testament to the region's rich Christian heritage. Originally constructed in the 4th century over the tomb of Saint Eulalia, this sacred site has undergone numerous transformations, with its current structure dating back to the 13th century. The basilica showcases an intriguing blend of architectural styles that reflect its storied past and significance as a place of devotion.
