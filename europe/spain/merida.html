Merida

Rome founded Emerita Augusta in 25 BCE as a veteran colony on the Guadiana; the theatre, amphitheatre, and bridge remain the landmarks of the modern Extremaduran capital. Visigothic and Moorish periods followed before Christian reconquest. UNESCO listing treats the archaeological ensemble as one of Spain's densest Roman civic cores outside Italy on the Guadiana plain.

Acueducto de los Milagros

Los Milagros aqueduct carried water into Roman Emerita Augusta on towering brick and stone piers that still stride across a valley on Merida's edge. A purifying basin once marked the northern end of the run. The ruined arcade remains one of Spain's clearest lessons in Roman hydraulic engineering.

Teatro Romano de Mérida

The Teatro Romano de Mérida is a historic site that holds the remains of a Roman arena and waiting rooms where gladiator and animal fights took place. Built in 16 BC by the consul Vipsanius Agrippa, it has undergone several renovations over many years, including during the reign of Emperor Trajan and Constantine. With its sloped semicircular seating, it could accommodate up to 6,000 spectators.

Roman Amphitheater of Mérida

Merida's Roman amphitheater opened beside the theater in the late first century BCE for gladiatorial combat and animal spectacles. An oval arena, service trench, gates and surviving seating foundations reveal how performers, animals and spectators moved through the building; much of the upper structure disappeared through quarrying.

Roman House of the Amphitheater

Beside Mérida's Roman theater and amphitheater, Casa Romana del Anfiteatro preserves mosaic floors and remains that may relate to a mausoleum. The house shows domestic Roman layout rather than a stage building. Daytime visits and ticketed performances on the larger sites share the same archaeological zone.

05 AdmissionFree

Temple of Diana

Mérida's Temple of Diana keeps a raised Roman podium and columns in the city centre, later wrapped by a medieval palace fabric. Quiet streets and squares nearby lead toward the Guadiana and other Roman works such as the Arch of Trajan. The temple platform is free to view from the surrounding streets.

Pórtico del Foro Municipal de Augusta Emérita

The portico of Emerita Augusta's municipal forum once framed statues of emperors, gods, and mythic figures in wall niches along a colonnaded civic square. Surviving columns and marble decoration show how Roman Merida displayed imperial cult and local government. The fragment sits among the city's scattered forum remains near other monumental ruins.

07 AdmissionFree

Trajan Arch

The Trajan Arch in Merida is a 1st-century archway that once led to a Roman temple. The city offers quiet streets, green spaces along the Guadiana river, and landmarks like the Arab Citadel. Nearby, travellers can explore Roman baths in Alange and the Prosperpina reservoir built by the Romans. Merida also has Roman-era buildings such as the Temple of Diana and the well-preserved remnants of aqueducts.

Alcazaba of Mérida

The Alcazaba of Mérida is a 9th-century Moorish citadel that has Roman and Visigoth relics, including a fish-filled cistern. It offers scenic views from the top of its walls, providing a glimpse into early Spanish history with significant archaeology. The citadel is located near the Plaza del Rastro and the Roman Bridge of Mérida, which has stood for over 2,000 years as part of the Via de la Plata route.

09 AdmissionFree

Roman Circus of Mérida

Merida's Roman circus hosted chariot races on a long arena whose banks and spina remains outline one of the empire's better-preserved circuses in Spain. Earthworks and stone seating traces explain scale even where fabric is incomplete. The site complements the theatre and amphitheatre in the town's Roman circuit without drawing the same crowd density.

38.9207° N / 6.3332° W

10 AdmissionFree

Acueducto de San Lázaro

San Lazaro Aqueduct preserves tall piers and arches from a Roman water system that supplied ancient Augusta Emerita. Most surviving above-ground masonry belongs to a limited stretch near Merida's railway. A later sixteenth-century aqueduct runs nearby as a separate hydraulic monument. Channels, settling works and water-source routes once extended far beyond the visible arcade.

38.9225° N / 6.3353° W

Basilica of Santa Eulalia

Fourth-century foundations over Saint Eulalia's tomb underlie the Basilica of Santa Eulalia in Mérida, though the standing fabric largely dates to the thirteenth century. Successive rebuilds left mixed architectural styles in one devotion site. Exterior walls and main interior spaces can usually be seen outside liturgy.
