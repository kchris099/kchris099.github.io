Ronda

Ronda occupies a plateau split by the El Tajo gorge in the mountains of Málaga province, settled in prehistoric times and fortified under Roman and Islamic rule. The Puente Nuevo bridge, bullring, and cliff-top quarters reflect Christian reconquest and later 18th-century engineering that linked the old and new towns across the chasm. Bandit lore and Romantic travel writing popularized the setting in the 19th century.

El Tajo de Ronda

El Tajo is the deep gorge that divides Ronda's old and new towns above the Guadalevín River. Limestone cliffs drop more than 100 metres beside the Puente Nuevo bridge. Viewpoints along the rim and paths below illustrate how the chasm defined settlement and fortification on the mesa.

Puerta del Almocábar

Puerta de Almocábar is a medieval gate in the eastern walls of Ronda's old town. It marked the route from the lower quarter toward the Arab quarter after Christian reconquest. The gate and adjacent walls preserve masonry from the fortified edge above the gorge.

Bullring of the Royal Cavalry of Ronda

Ronda's bullring was completed in 1785 for the Real Maestranza de Caballería and is among the oldest purpose-built arenas in Spain. Its stone arcades and diameter influenced later plaza design. A museum documents the town's bullfighting tradition and cavalry history.
