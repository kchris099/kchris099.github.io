Explore Cordoba

A Brief History

Córdoba became capital of Roman Hispania Ulterior and later of the Umayyad caliphate in al-Andalus, when its mosques, libraries, and courts drew scholars from across the Mediterranean. The Great Mosque, enlarged from the eighth century, embodies that Islamic zenith before Fernando III conquered the city for Castile in 1236. Medieval synagogues, Mudéjar houses, and the Alcázar of the Christian kings show how Córdoba kept weaving Roman, Islamic, and Castilian threads into Andalusian civic life.

Top Sights & Activities

Mezquita-Catedral de Córdoba

The Mosque-Cathedral of Córdoba began as a Visigothic church and expanded into a major mosque under Umayyad rule from the 8th century. After reconquest a cathedral was inserted within the prayer hall. Horseshoe arches, mihrab, and Renaissance choir illustrate overlapping religious traditions.

Free

Patio de los Naranjos

Patio de los Naranjos is the courtyard before the mosque-cathedral entrance with rows of orange trees and ablution fountains from the Islamic period. It served worshippers before prayer in the mosque and remains the main outdoor approach to the monument within the former sahn.

€9

Palacio de Viana

Palacio de Viana is a noble house with twelve linked patios accumulated from the 14th century onward in the Santa Marina district. Courtyards, gardens, and collections illustrate aristocratic life in Córdoba. Guided tours pass through rooms decorated across Renaissance and later periods.

Free

Triunfo de San Rafael de la Puerta del Puente

The Triumph of San Rafael statue stands beside Puerta del Puente at the Roman bridge approach. Baroque sculpture commemorates the archangel credited with protecting the city during plague. The monument faces the Guadalquivir and forms part of the riverside ceremonial axis.

Free

Puerta del Puente

Puerta del Puente is a Renaissance gate built in the 16th century at the north end of the Roman bridge. It replaced an earlier Islamic gate and marked ceremonial entry from the river into the city. The structure frames views between the bridge and the mosque-cathedral quarter.

Free

Puente Romano de Córdoba

The Roman bridge spans the Guadalquivir with foundations from the 1st century BC and later reconstructions. Sixteen arches carry pedestrians between the mosque quarter and Torre de la Calahorra. The crossing remained the main river route through medieval and modern Córdoba.

Free

Puerta de Almodóvar

Puerta de Almodóvar is a medieval gate in the western wall of Córdoba's old town. It opens toward the Jewish quarter and Alcázar gardens. The restored arch and tower mark one of the main pedestrian entries into the historic street network west of the mosque.

Free

Mercado Victoria

Mercado Victoria is a covered food market in gardens near the train station opened in 2014. Vendor stalls serve regional dishes and products within a pavilion combining market tradition with contemporary dining. The site lies between the historic center and modern avenues.

€7

Patios Cordobeses

Córdoba's courtyard houses preserve flower-filled patios that are opened to travellers during the annual festival in May and on selected routes year-round. White walls, tiles, and potted plants illustrate domestic architecture from Roman and Islamic traditions adapted in the old quarters.

Free

Palacio de la Merced

Palacio de la Merced was built in the 18th century for a Mercedarian monastery and later became the seat of the provincial government. Its Baroque facade and cloister occupy a block near the city walls. Cultural exhibitions and public events use rooms within the historic complex.
