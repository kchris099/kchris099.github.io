Nis

Constantine the Great was born here when Naissus guarded Roman roads through the Balkans. An eighteenth-century Ottoman fortress and twentieth-century war memorials add later layers. Niš's historical weight is strategic crossroads more than courtly capital. The Skull Tower memorialises a nineteenth-century Ottoman reprisal still taught in local history.

Park Bubanj

Park Bubanj commemorates more than 10,000 people mass-executed by German forces in World War II, sitting just over two kilometers from the Red Cross Concentration Camp site. Paths and lawns carry memorial visits and quiet walks. The ground's meaning is historical before recreational.

02 AdmissionFree

Fortress of Niš

Niš Fortress is an eighteenth-century Ottoman stone enclosure that now holds a park with cafes, shops, and monuments inside the walls. Builders raised it over earlier Roman, Byzantine, and medieval fort remains above the Nišava. Gate towers, bastions, and open lawns make the circuit a central green space for the modern city as well as a military ruin.

43.3252° N / 21.8964° E

03 AdmissionFree

Stambol Gate of Nis Fortress

Stambol Gate opens into Niš Fortress, where routes continue toward mosques, Roman forum remains, and later monuments inside the walls. Courtyards and towers organize the usual walking circuit when the grounds are open. The gate works as the fortress's principal threshold.

43.3233° N / 21.8953° E

04 AdmissionFree

King Milan Square

Shops, outdoor cafes, and flowerbeds ring King Milan Square in Niš, with Banovina Building, Čair Fountain, the Memorial of Constantine the Great, and City Hall nearby. Open pavement carries daily foot traffic. The square functions as the city's busiest downtown room.

43.3214° N / 21.8958° E

05 AdmissionFree

National Theater Niš

Narodno pozorište Niš stages drama, music, and orchestral programs as a long-running house in the city's theater tradition. Ticketed performances provide the main public access. The auditorium remains a civic cultural venue rather than a daytime museum.

43.3202° N / 21.8999° E

06 AdmissionFree

Church of Saint Panteleimon

Saint Panteleimon's church occupies ground associated with kings who convened here during the Crusades, with detailed interior paintings that frame baptisms and other rites. Worship and history share the same nave. Exterior and main interior stay viewable outside ceremonies.

43.3337° N / 21.9100° E

07 AdmissionFree

Holy Trinity Cathedral

Nis's Holy Trinity Cathedral was built during the final decades of Ottoman rule and consecrated after Serbian control expanded in 1878. A broad basilican interior, iconostasis and wall paintings express nineteenth-century Orthodox ambition. The twin-towered facade gives the building a commanding presence, while regular services continue its place in the city's religious life.

43.3152° N / 21.8963° E

08 AdmissionFree

Tinkers Alley

Kazandzijsko sokace preserves a short pedestrian lane in central Nis once associated with coppersmiths and other Ottoman-era crafts; its commercial use has changed substantially. Low houses, restored facades, restaurants, cafes, and a monument to writer Stevan Sremac now shape the street.

43.3179° N / 21.8956° E

09 AdmissionFree

Čegar

Cegar Hill marks the battlefield where Serbian rebels fought Ottoman forces in 1809 during an early Serbian Uprising. A later tower monument commemorates commander Stevan Sindjelic and his soldiers, whose powder-magazine explosion became central to national memory.

43.3655° N / 21.9427° E

10 AdmissionFree

Nazi Concentration Camp "Red Cross”

The Red Cross camp in Nis operated under German occupation as a prison and transit site for Jews, Roma, Serbs, resistance members and other detainees. Preserved cells, guard areas, personal objects and photographs now form a memorial museum. Many prisoners were murdered at nearby Bubanj or deported, and a mass escape took place from the camp in 1942.

43.3304° N / 21.8887° E
