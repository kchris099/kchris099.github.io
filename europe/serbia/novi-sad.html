Destination Guide — Novi Sad

Explore Novi Sad

A Brief History

Founded in the late 17th century by Orthodox merchants seeking refuge across the Danube from the Ottoman Empire, Novi Sad blossomed into the 'Serbian Athens'. Its elegant Habsburg architecture and the formidable Petrovaradin Fortress reflect a legacy of cross-cultural flourishing. The survival of these cultural and architectural milestones provides a powerful window into the region's storied past. Museums, markets, and street plans in Novi Sad still show layers from those periods.

Monument of Svetozar Miletić

The Monument of Svetozar Miletić stands in Novi Sad, Serbia's Liberty Square. Crafted by sculptor Ivan Strović and unveiled in 1939, the bronze statue captures the dynamic presence of Svetozar Miletić, a significant Serbian figure known for his leadership during the struggle for autonomy under Austro-Hungarian rule.

Karađorđe Stadium

Karađorđe Stadium, also known as FC Vojvodina's home ground, is a historic sports venue in Novi Sad. It is named after the known Serbian revolutionary leader, Karađorđe Petrović. The stadium holds significant cultural and sporting importance for the city, being the oldest football stadium in Novi Sad. Despite its age and need for repairs, it remains a point of pride for FC Vojvodina and has witnessed many historic football moments.

Petrovaradin Fortress Clock

Petrovaradin Fortress's Clock, situated on the outskirts of Novi Sad and overlooking the Danube, is a known historical site. As the second largest fortress in Europe, its construction spanned from the late 17th to late 18th century under Ottoman and Austrian control. Visitors can access the tower via a car road or explore its surroundings for views of Novi Sad and the Danube River.
