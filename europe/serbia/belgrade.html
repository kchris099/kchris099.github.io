Belgrade

Belgrade stands at the confluence of the Sava and Danube rivers, with Belgrade Fortress occupying a ridge contested by Romans, Byzantines, Ottomans, and Habsburgs. The city was capital of Yugoslavia and later Serbia. Bombing in 1999 damaged bridges and buildings. Kalemegdan park, Skadarlija quarter, and socialist-era blocks record layers from the 19th century to the present.

Church of Saint Sava

The Church of Saint Sava dominates Belgrade's Vracar plateau above the traditional site linked to the burning of the saint's relics. Construction began in the twentieth century, stopped during war and socialist rule, then resumed over many decades. A vast dome and marble exterior frame an interior covered with mosaics, while the crypt and regular Orthodox worship remain central to the complex.

Belgrade Fortress

At the confluence of the Sava and Danube, this fortress occupies a bluff fortified since Roman times. Celts, Byzantines, Ottomans, and Austrians each rebuilt its walls. Today the upper fort contains museums, parks, and the Pobednik monument with wide river views.

St. Petka Chapel

St. Petka Chapel stands within Belgrade Fortress near a spring associated with Saint Petka. The present small Orthodox chapel dates from the twentieth century, with mosaics, icons, candles, and water collected by worshippers. Religious belief surrounds the spring's healing qualities, while the chapel's location below the ramparts distinguishes it from the older Ruzica Church nearby.

Zindan Gate

Called Zindan or dungeon gate, this entry to the lower fort is flanked by two large round towers from the 15th century. Captives were once held in chambers below. The passage connects the riverside park to inner courtyards where cannons and medieval walls are displayed.

Pobednik

Ivan Meštrović sculpted this bronze figure in 1928 to honor Serbia's Balkan Wars and World War I soldiers. Standing on a pedestal in the upper fortress, it overlooks the Sava-Danube confluence. Locals call it Pobednik, meaning the Victor, and treat it as a city emblem.

St. Mark Orthodox Church

St. Mark's Orthodox Church beside Belgrade's Tasmajdan Park was built in Serbo-Byzantine style between 1931 and 1940, with a tall bell tower and mosaic-rich interiors. The church holds the tomb of medieval emperor Stefan Dusan, linking the parish to the fourteenth-century Nemanjic dynasty. From the surrounding streets the brick mass and dome read as a memorial landmark as much as an active place of worship.

Gardoš Tower

Gardoš Tower, or Millennium Tower, rose in Zemun in the late nineteenth century to mark a thousand years of Hungarian rule in the region. The 36-metre shaft looks over Zemun and the Danube; a spiral stair climbs to the top platform. Brick walls and a steep roof make it the hill's skyline marker.

Kalemegdan

Kalemegdan Park surrounds Belgrade Fortress at the meeting of the Sava and Danube rivers. Paths, monuments, military remains, museums, sports areas, and viewpoints occupy former defensive ground converted into public landscape. The zoo lies within the broader area but is separately managed.

Ružica Church

Ruzica Church occupies a former military structure inside Belgrade Fortress and was converted into an Orthodox church in the nineteenth century. Wartime damage led to rebuilding, while icons and memorial decoration fill the interior. Chandeliers made from military materials give the church an unusual connection with the fortress's long defensive history.

Belgrade Zoo

Belgrade Zoo occupies compact grounds below the fortress walls near the Danube. Founded in the 1930s, it holds a changing collection of mammals, birds, reptiles and domestic species within a historically constrained urban site. Species totals and rare-animal lists quickly become outdated, while enclosure quality, conservation claims and the zoo's wartime history deserve more careful treatment than family marketing.

Republic Square

An equestrian statue of Prince Michael centers Republic Square, Belgrade's old-town transit and gathering hub near Knez Mihailova Street. The National Museum and National Theatre face the paving, with restaurants close by. Shopping, dining, and cultural houses share the same civic room.

National Theatre in Belgrade

The National Theatre in Belgrade, located on Republic Square, is a historic venue that has been showing Serbian culture since 1869. Despite war damage and architectural updates, it remains a revered symbol of the country's artistic heritage. Travellers can take backstage tours during the day to witness wardrobe and scenery maintenance and enjoy performances by resident singers. The square itself is a popular meeting point with landmarks like the National Museum and Prince Mihailo monument.

Cathedral Church of Saint Michael the Archangel

The Cathedral Church of Saint Michael the Archangel in Belgrade was built in the 1830s as a Serbian Orthodox cathedral in neoclassical style. Its altar holds relics of Serbian saints, and the nave remains a working parish church as well as a historic interior. The building stands near the old city core, with a tall iconostasis and painted vaults typical of that rebuilding period.

Rakovica Monastery

Rakovica Monastery on Belgrade's southern edge traces monastic use to the medieval period, with later Baroque rebuilding that shaped the church and residential wings seen today. Associated in memory with the Obrenovic dynasty, the compound sits among wooded slopes away from the central boulevards. Courtyards, the katholikon, and cemetery grounds form a working religious enclosure rather than a downtown plaza monument.

Belgrade Old Railway Station

The Belgrade Old Railway Station, a neoclassical structure established in 1884, is a evidence of the city's rich history. Once a busy hub for travelers, it now is an intriguing relic of the past since train operations ceased in mid-2018. Despite its disuse, the station's art nouveau charm continues to draw travellers and photographers alike.

New Palace

Belgrade's New Palace was built in the early twentieth century for the Karadjordjevic dynasty opposite the Old Palace. War and political change altered the interiors through several institutional uses. The building now houses the Serbian presidency. Formal gardens and the paired palace facades remain visible from surrounding streets, while security limits entry to the active government complex.

House of the National Assembly of the Republic of Serbia

The House of the National Assembly of the Republic of Serbia is a Beaux-Arts structure that is a evidence of the country's rich history and architectural beauty. Located Belgrade, this building was constructed on the site of the former Batal Mosque after Serbia's independence in 1878. Its construction faced numerous delays due to historical events like wars and economic challenges, but it ultimately emerged as a symbol of national pride.

Tašmajdan

Tasmajdan Park occupies a downtown Belgrade plateau above older stone quarries, ringed by St. Mark's Church, Parliament, and the main post office. Paths, playgrounds, and open lawns share space with memorials and sports courts in daily neighborhood use. The park's name recalls the quarry layers beneath, while the surface now works as a green hinge between civic buildings and residential blocks.

Also called Cvetni Trg, Flower Square began as a late-nineteenth-century park and later became an open market ringed by attractive buildings and oak trees. Cafes and outdoor terraces claim the seating. Open pavement keeps the square usable for lingering as well as trading.

About five hectares hold more than 350 tree and bush species at Jevremovac, established in the nineteenth century at Josif Pančić's urging. Paths and shaded lawns organize the historic plantings. The garden ranks among the older botanical collections in the region.

Kneza Mihaila

Kneza Mihaila, a active pedestrian street in Belgrade, is a must-visit for anyone exploring the city. This busy thoroughfare stretches approximately 700 meters and connects the historic Kalemegdan Fortress to Republic Square, making it an essential part of visitor journey. Lined with old buildings and international brand stores, Kneza Mihaila offers a blend of shopping and sightseeing.

Ethnographic Museum

Belgrade's Ethnographic Museum was founded in 1901 and occupies a former commercial building near Studentski Trg. Clothing, textiles, jewelry, tools, furniture, ritual objects; photographs and craft traditions document communities across Serbia and the former Yugoslav region.

Bajrakli Mosque

Bajrakli Mosque survives as Belgrade's principal active mosque from the Ottoman period. A square prayer hall, dome, minaret, courtyard and later repairs reflect conversion, conflict, fire and restoration under changing governments. The building's continuity is more complex than a simple seventeenth-century label, while worship and community use require respectful access.
