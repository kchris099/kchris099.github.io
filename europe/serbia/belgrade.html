Explore Belgrade

A Brief History

Belgrade stands at the confluence of the Sava and Danube rivers, with Belgrade Fortress occupying a ridge contested by Romans, Byzantines, Ottomans, and Habsburgs. The city was capital of Yugoslavia and later Serbia. Bombing in 1999 damaged bridges and buildings. Kalemegdan park, Skadarlija quarter, and socialist-era blocks record layers from the 19th century to the present.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Church of Saint Sava

Dedicated to Saint Sava, founder of the Serbian Orthodox Church, this domed church on Vračar Hill has been under construction since 1935. White marble façades and unfinished interior spaces reflect decades of interrupted work. When completed, it will rank among the largest Orthodox church buildings in the Balkans.

Free

Belgrade Fortress

At the confluence of the Sava and Danube, this fortress occupies a bluff fortified since Roman times. Celts, Byzantines, Ottomans, and Austrians each rebuilt its walls. Today the upper fort contains museums, parks, and the Pobednik monument with wide river views.

Free

St. Petka Chapel

This small chapel inside the fortress covers a spring believed to have healing properties since Ottoman times. Icons and candles fill the dim interior where worshippers collect water. It sits near the ramparts above the rivers, away from the busier upper fort paths.

Free

Zindan Gate

Called Zindan or dungeon gate, this entry to the lower fort is flanked by two large round towers from the 15th century. Captives were once held in chambers below. The passage connects the riverside park to inner courtyards where cannons and medieval walls are displayed.

Free

Pobednik

Ivan Meštrović sculpted this bronze figure in 1928 to honor Serbia's Balkan Wars and World War I soldiers. Standing on a pedestal in the upper fortress, it overlooks the Sava-Danube confluence. Locals call it Pobednik, meaning the Victor, and treat it as a city emblem.

Free

St. Mark Orthodox Church

Built in Serbo-Byzantine style between 1931 and 1940, this church near Tašmajdan Park holds the tomb of medieval emperor Stefan Dušan. Mosaic decoration and a tall bell tower dominate the surrounding streets. It serves as a parish church and memorial to Serbia's 14th-century Nemanjić dynasty.
