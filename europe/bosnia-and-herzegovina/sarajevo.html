Sarajevo

Sarajevo rose as an Ottoman provincial capital in the 15th century, with mosques, bazaars, and the Gazi Husrev-beg complex shaping its Baščaršija core. Habsburg rule after 1878 added tram lines, civic buildings, and a multi-confessional street plan. The 1914 assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand marked the city in world history. Siege damage from the 1990s war is still visible beside rebuilt markets, museums, and hillside neighbourhoods.

Avaz Twist Tower

Avaz Twist Tower rises just outside Sarajevo's center as a twisted glass office skyscraper. An observation level near the top looks over the Miljacka valley and surrounding hills. The spiral facade makes the tower easy to pick out from older low-rise districts. Elevators climb the twisted shaft to a cafe level with glass on all sides.

Baščaršija

Fifteenth-century caravanserai origins grew into Baščaršija, Sarajevo's Old Town bazaar of mosques, hammams, and cobbled lanes. By the seventeenth century workshops packed the trading hub. Craft stalls and cafes still fill the Ottoman commercial core.

Sevdah Art House

Sevdalinka love songs and their interpreters fill Sevdah Art House in Sarajevo, a museum dedicated to Bosnian urban song tradition. Intimate rooms present famous singers and recorded performance heritage. The house keeps sevdah memory indoors rather than as a large concert hall.

04 AdmissionBAM 3

Gazi Husrev-beg Mosque

Gazi Husrev Bey commissioned Sarajevo's principal Ottoman mosque in 1530, with construction completed under architect Acem Esir Ali. A central dome, portico, minaret, courtyard fountain, and adjacent educational and charitable buildings anchor the Baščaršija district; the complex is an active place of worship while also preserving the institutional pattern of a sixteenth-century urban endowment.

43.8592° N / 18.4293° E

05 AdmissionFree

Tašlihan

Stone ruins of Tašlihan recall one of three Sarajevo caravanserais, with Morica Han and Kolobara Han, that once sheltered travellers and horses. Open remains stay visible for photographs beside later streets. The site reads as a fragment of Ottoman roadside lodging.

43.8585° N / 18.4279° E

06 AdmissionFree

Latin Bridge

Latin Bridge, an Ottoman-era stone bridge in Sarajevo, Bosnia and Herzegovina, is a significant historical site. It gained notoriety as the location of the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria in 1914, an event that triggered World War I. The bridge spans the Miljacka River and is a reminder of the city's communist period.

43.8576° N / 18.4290° E

07 AdmissionFree

Emperor's Mosque

The Emperor's Mosque stands beside the Miljacka on a site associated with Sarajevo's earliest Ottoman settlement. The present building dates largely from the sixteenth century, when it was expanded and dedicated to Sultan Suleiman the Magnificent; a broad dome, arcaded porch, minaret, cemetery, and riverside setting distinguish the active mosque and its historic religious complex.

43.8574° N / 18.4307° E

Gradska vijećnica Sarajevo

Sarajevo City Hall opened in 1896 as an Austro-Hungarian municipal building with a richly patterned neo-Moorish interior. It later housed the national library, whose collections were largely destroyed when shelling set the building on fire in 1992; long restoration returned the landmark to public use in 2014, with exhibitions and events occupying reconstructed halls beside the Miljacka.

43.8592° N / 18.4336° E

Yellow Fortress

Located atop a hill, the Yellow Fortress, or Zuta Tabija, is an 18th-century bastion that offers some of the most views of Sarajevo and its surrounding mountains. This historic site has become a beloved destination for both locals and travelers seeking to witness sunsets that bathe the city in golden hues.

43.8614° N / 18.4376° E

White Fortress

Bijela Tabija occupies a limestone outcrop above Sarajevo's eastern entrance, where fortifications guarded the road through the Miljacka valley. Surviving walls reflect medieval, Ottoman and Austro-Hungarian phases, though much of the interior is ruinous. The open plateau looks over the old town, surrounding hills and river corridor, making the strategic purpose of the position immediately clear.

11 AdmissionFree

Pijaca Markale food market

Pijaca Markale food market is housed in a neoclassical building and offers a variety of local produce, meats, and dairy products. After exploring the market, travellers can step outside into the square and relax at one of the nearby cafes. The market's history is marked by significant events, including bombings in 1994 and 1995 that had far-reaching consequences.

12 AdmissionFree

Sacred Heart Cathedral

Twin towers and a rose window mark Sacred Heart Cathedral on Sarajevo's central pedestrian streets. Josip Vancaš designed the neo-Gothic church, completed in 1889 during Austro-Hungarian rule. Stained glass, vaulted aisles, altars, and the tomb of Archbishop Josip Stadler fill the interior, which remains the seat of the Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Vrhbosna.

Orthodox Church of the Nativity of the Theotokos

Built in 1874 with gifts from citizens and merchants across several cities, the Orthodox Church of the Nativity of the Theotokos in Sarajevo shows nineteenth-century interiors with portraits and stained glass. Exterior and main halls remain viewable outside services. The large church marks Orthodox presence in the city center.

14 AdmissionFree

Vječna vatra

Vjecna vatra, the Eternal Flame, burns at a small memorial in central Sarajevo for World War II victims. A continuously lit flame sits in a paved plaza along a main downtown street. Passersby pause briefly; ceremonies gather here on commemorative dates. Trams and foot traffic pass within meters of the flame through the day.

43.8588° N / 18.4219° E

15 AdmissionBAM 15

Sarajevo National Theatre

Sarajevo National Theatre occupies an early twentieth-century building originally planned as a social club during Austro-Hungarian rule. Its formal facade faces a small square beside the Miljacka. Workshops and rehearsal rooms extend behind the auditorium. Founded after World War I, the theater institution has supported drama, opera, ballet, concerts and festivals.

43.8569° N / 18.4207° E

16 AdmissionFree (cable car BAM 30)

Olympic bobsleigh and luge track Trebević

The Olympic bobsleigh and luge track on Trebevic above Sarajevo was built for the 1984 Winter Games. Concrete curves climb through forest, later scarred by conflict and now walked as a hillside path. Graffiti covers sections of the run while views open back toward the city bowl. Cable-car access from the city shortens the climb to the track's upper sections.

43.8392° N / 18.4434° E

17 AdmissionFree

SCC - Sarajevo City Center

Sarajevo City Center combines a multilevel shopping mall, offices, hotel, dining, cinema, and service businesses near Marijin Dvor. Opened in the 2010s, the glass-fronted complex belongs to the city's contemporary commercial district. Its location on a major tram corridor makes it a practical retail stop, but it has limited independent cultural or architectural interpretation.

43.8554° N / 18.4081° E

18 AdmissionFree

Ali Pasha’s mosque

Ali Pasha's Mosque was built in the sixteenth century beside the route west from Ottoman Sarajevo; a single dome covers the square prayer hall, fronted by a small portico and slender stone minaret. The surrounding cemetery contains historic grave markers, while the compact complex is an active neighborhood mosque near modern government and commercial buildings.

43.8580° N / 18.4128° E
