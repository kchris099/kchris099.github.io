A Brief History

Helsinki was founded by King Gustav I of Sweden in 1550 and moved to its present harbor site in 1640 to improve trade access. After Russia annexed Finland in 1809, the city became capital of the Grand Duchy in 1812 and was rebuilt with a neoclassical center around Senate Square under architect Carl Ludvig Engel. Ports, island fortifications, and later art-nouveau districts expanded with industry and shipping.

Helsinki Cathedral

Helsinki Cathedral was designed by Carl Ludvig Engel for the new imperial capital and completed in 1852 after revisions by Ernst Lohrmann. Its white facade and green domes dominate Senate Square and remain one of the clearest examples of Helsinki's 19th-century neoclassical plan.

Free

The National Library of Finland

The National Library building by C. L. Engel opened in 1844 beside Senate Square. Its rotunda, reading rooms, and collections support legal deposit, national bibliography, and research in Finnish cultural history. The library served the Imperial Alexander University before Finland's independence.

Free

Suomenlinna

Suomenlinna is an island fortress begun in 1748 under Swedish rule as Sveaborg. Later used by Russian and Finnish authorities, it now contains museums, docks, residences, and defensive works across linked islands. UNESCO added the site to the World Heritage List in 1991.

Free

Helsinki Central Library Oodi

Oodi opened in 2018 opposite Finlandia Hall and Parliament House as Helsinki's central public library. Designed by ALA Architects, it combines library collections with event space, work areas, studios, and civic meeting functions in a timber-and-glass building tied to Finland's public education culture.

Sibelius Monument

Sibelius Monument is a monument in Helsinki, Finland. Records associated with the site include 1967. The Sibelius Monument, In Sibelius Park in Helsinki, Finland, is a monument to the known Finnish composer Jean Sibelius. Designed by artist Eila Hiltunen and unveiled in 1967, the monument has over 600 steel pipes emerging from natural rock to create a wave-like structure symbolizing Sibelius's music. The memorial stands in an open plaza or roadside setting.

Senate Square

Senate Square is a public square in Helsinki, Finland. It opened or was completed around 1852. Senate Square is a grand public space in Helsinki, showing neoclassical architecture and serving as the focal point of the city's political, religious, and academic life. It was completed in 1852 to honor Tsar Nicholas of Russia and is a key element of Engel's grand city plan. Open pavement and surrounding facades define the visitor setting.

Uspenski Cathedral

Perched atop a hill in Helsinki, Uspenski Cathedral is the epicenter of the Finnish Orthodox faith. Known as the 'Russian Church,' it has an ornate exterior with red brick and distinctive onion domes. Inside, travellers are treated to lavish interior decorations that reflect Orthodox Byzantine style influenced by Italian Art Deco. With its thirteen towers and east-facing crosses symbolizing Jesus and the Twelve Apostles, this cathedral stands out as Scandinavia's largest Orthodox church.

Finnish National Theatre

The Finnish National Theatre, In Helsinki City Center, is a 20th-century architectural gem designed by Onni Törnqvist-Tarjanne. With its three stages for live performances, it is the oldest Finnish-language professional theater in Finland, dating back to its establishment in 1872. The theater played a significant role in Finland's push for independence and is an important cultural institution in the country.

Helsinki Winter Garden

The Helsinki Winter Garden is a 19th-century glass greenhouse located in the Zoo area north of Toolonlahti Bay. It is open to the public and showcases over 200 different plants, including palms, cacti, flowers, pines, limes, magnolias, and camellias. Travellers can also admire parrots and carp in a pond within the garden.

Finnish National Opera and Ballet

The Finnish National Opera and Ballet, located in Helsinki, is a state-owned opera and ballet company housed in the Opera House by the Toolonlahti bay. The venue has two auditoriums - a grand 1,350-seat main hall and a smaller studio with 300-500 seats. Travellers can enjoy opera performances, classical ballets, and active concerts at this cultural hub.
