Plovdiv

Settlement on the three hills predates Rome; Thracian, Roman, and Ottoman rulers each left theatres, mosques, and merchant houses. Nineteenth-century National Revival mansions fill the old town above the Maritsa. Plovdiv ranks among Europe's longest-inhabited urban sites, a claim of continuous occupation rather than a single founding myth. The Roman theatre hosts performances again above the modern tram streets.

Monument of the Red Army "Alyosha"

The Monument of the Red Army, commonly known as Alyosha, is a massive 11-meter tall reinforced concrete statue of a Soviet soldier standing on Bunardzhik Hill in Plovdiv. Erected in the 1950s, it commemorates the Soviet casualties during the liberation of Bulgaria in World War II. The monument dominates the city skyline and offers expansive panoramic views of Plovdiv from its base.

Old Town of Plovdiv

The Old Town of Plovdiv is a very preserved historic district situated on three of the city's hills. It is known for its winding cobblestone streets and distinctive Bulgarian National Revival architecture, featuring colorful, richly decorated houses. The area also contains significant ancient ruins, including the Roman theater, reflecting the city's multi-layered history spanning thousands of years.

Roman Forum of Philippopolis

The Roman Forum of Philippopolis, located at the opposite end of Knyaz Alexander Street, is an collection of ancient ruins dating back to the 1st century AD. Covering a vast area of 20 hectares, with 11 hectares already excavated, it is the largest Roman forum in Bulgaria.

Monument of Unification

The Monument of Unification in Plovdiv commemorates the 1885 unification of the Principality of Bulgaria with the province of Eastern Rumelia. The striking bronze statue depicts a young woman holding a laurel wreath, symbolizing peace and unity. Located in Saedinenie Square, the monument is a focal point for national celebrations and a symbol of Bulgarian independence and solidarity.
