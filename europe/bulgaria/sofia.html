Explore Sofia

A Brief History

Sofia stands on a site occupied since Thracian and Roman times, when it was known as Serdica. Byzantine churches such as Saint Sophia gave the modern city its name. Ottoman administration lasted from the 14th century until Bulgarian liberation in 1878, when Sofia became the capital of the Principality of Bulgaria. Socialist-era housing and public buildings surround older quarters with Roman ruins and 19th-century boulevards.

Patriarchal Cathedral St. Alexander Nevsky

Patriarchal Cathedral St. Alexander Nevsky is a religious building in Sofia, Bulgaria. It opened or was completed around 1877. Alexander Nevsky is a visitor site in Sofia. Bulgaria's largest Orthodox church was conceived after the Russo-Turkish War of 1877-78 to commemorate fallen Russian soldiers. The exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside service times.

Church of St. Nicholas the Wonderworker

Church of St. Nicholas the Wonderworker is a religious building in Sofia, Bulgaria. Construction and early use date to around 1914. Built in 1914 as a Russian diplomatic church, this small Orthodox chapel has colorful ceramic tiles and golden onion domes. Architecturally distinct from local Bulgarian churches, it honors Saint Nicholas and reflects cultural ties between Sofia and Russia during the early 20th century.

Ivan Vazov National Theatre

Named for Bulgaria's national poet, this theatre opened in 1907 with a neoclassical facade facing a city park. As the country's oldest drama venue, it stages Bulgarian and international productions. The building survived wartime damage and remains a focal point for performing arts in Sofia.

Sofia Church of St. George

A 4th-century red-brick rotunda, among the oldest structures in Sofia, sits amid excavated Roman streets of ancient Serdica. Medieval frescoes line the interior. The surrounding archaeological park displays walls, drains, and mosaics from the Roman town beneath the modern capital.

The Monument of Saint Sofia

Erected in 2000, this bronze statue depicts the city's patron holding a cross and an owl. Located on a busy square once dominated by a Lenin monument, it symbolizes the shift between eras. The figure blends Christian and classical imagery rooted in local legend about the city's name.

Boyana Church

Boyana Church is a visitor site in Sofia. At the foot of Vitosha Mountain, this small medieval church is known for frescoes painted in 1259 with realistic portraits and landscapes unusual for their period. A UNESCO site, it lies outside the city center and limits visitor numbers to protect the fragile wall paintings inside.

National Palace of Culture Park

The National Palace of Culture Park is a massive complex in Sofia, serving as a venue for conferences, trade fairs, festivals, and cultural events. It's a landmark of the city and a popular meeting point for both tourists and locals. The park offers free leisure activities such as strolling through its pathways with water ponds, green spaces, and seating areas.

The Russian monument in Sofia

Russian Monument Square is a busy roundabout in Sofia, Bulgaria, featuring the 1882 Russian Monument obelisk surrounded by landscaped areas. The monument symbolizes the end of Ottoman rule in Bulgaria and was funded by the Russian people. Despite historical tensions between Bulgaria and Russia, the monument is a significant landmark.

Vasil Levski National Stadium

Vasil Levski National Stadium, In Sofia, Bulgaria, is a multifunctional all-seater venue and the largest of its kind in the country.
