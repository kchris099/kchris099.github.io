Sofia

Sofia stands on a site occupied since Thracian and Roman times, when it was known as Serdica. Byzantine churches such as Saint Sophia gave the modern city its name. Ottoman administration lasted from the 14th century until Bulgarian liberation in 1878, when Sofia became the capital of the Principality of Bulgaria. Socialist-era housing and public buildings surround older quarters with Roman ruins and 19th-century boulevards.

Patriarchal Cathedral St. Alexander Nevsky

Golden domes rise above Sofia's Alexander Nevsky Cathedral, built in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries as a memorial to those who died in the Russo-Turkish War. The vast neo-Byzantine interior contains marble, mosaics, icons, murals, and multiple altars. A crypt museum displays Bulgarian Orthodox icons beneath the active national cathedral.

Church of St. Nicholas the Wonderworker

Sofia's Russian Church of St Nicholas the Wonderworker was finished in 1914 as a diplomatic chapel, with colourful ceramic tiles and golden onion domes. The small Orthodox interior differs sharply from nearby Bulgarian churches. It still marks early twentieth-century ties between Sofia and Russia on Tsar Osvoboditel Boulevard.

Ivan Vazov National Theatre

Vienna-trained architects Ferdinand Fellner and Hermann Helmer designed Sofia's National Theatre, opened in 1907 and named for writer Ivan Vazov, and a neoclassical facade with sculpture faces the City Garden, while the interior contains several stages for Bulgarian and international drama. Fire, wartime bombing, and reconstruction altered the building without ending its central role in national theater.

Sofia Church of St. George

The red-brick Rotunda of Saint George dates to late Roman Serdica and stands within a courtyard enclosed by modern government buildings. Its circular interior preserves layers of medieval frescoes beneath a simple dome. Excavated streets, heating systems, and building foundations around the church show how the fourth-century structure survived successive transformations of Sofia's center.

The Monument of Saint Sofia

Erected in 2000, this bronze statue depicts the city's patron holding a cross and an owl. Located on a busy square once dominated by a Lenin monument, it symbolizes the shift between eras. The figure blends Christian and classical imagery rooted in local legend about the city's name.

Boyana Church

Boyana Church at the foot of Vitosha is a small medieval church known for frescoes dated to 1259. Realistic faces and landscapes in the paintings stand out among Balkan medieval art. UNESCO listing protects the layered chapels that grew beside the original sanctuary. Timed entry protects the fragile fresco plaster from humidity and crowds.

National Palace of Culture Park

Open lawns, paths, fountains and paved gathering areas extend in front of Sofia's National Palace of Culture. Developed with the large cultural complex in the early 1980s, the park links Vitosha Boulevard with event halls and surrounding neighborhoods; skateboarders, families, commuters, festivals and seasonal markets use the space, while fountains and pools and monuments organize its main axes.

The Russian monument in Sofia

A stone obelisk erected in 1882 stands at the center of Sofia's Russian Monument Square; roads now circulate around the memorial, so its historical symbolism is experienced within a busy modern traffic junction. Reliefs and inscriptions commemorate Russian forces killed during the Russo-Turkish War of 1877-78, which ended Ottoman rule over much of Bulgaria.

Kambanite Park, known as The Bells, displays an outdoor collection of bells donated by many countries as a peace monument near Sofia. Paths and lawns surround the bell field, used for walks and occasional outdoor events. The international bell gifts give the park a memorial theme distinct from ordinary neighborhood greens.

Vasil Levski National Stadium

Vasil Levski National Stadium occupies the edge of Borisova Gradina near central Sofia. The present venue opened in the mid-twentieth century and has been repeatedly renovated for football, athletics, concerts, and public ceremonies. It is the principal home of Bulgaria's national football team, with an athletics track separating the stands from the pitch.

Four bronze eagles on stone columns mark Eagles' Bridge, or Orlov Most, built in 1891 at a busy Sofia junction with a pedestrian underpass beneath the traffic. The sculptures give the crossing its name. Downtown walks often begin from this traffic node.

Monument to the Tsar Liberator

The Monument to the Tsar Liberator, unveiled in 1907, honors Russian Tsar Alexander II for Bulgaria's liberation from Ottoman rule. An equestrian statue rises on a high pedestal along Tsar Osvoboditel Boulevard near the National Assembly. The open plaza setting makes the bronze figure a daily landmark for traffic and pedestrians in central Sofia.

Tsar Samuil Monument

Installed in 2015, the monument commemorates the medieval Bulgarian ruler whose state resisted Byzantine expansion around the turn of the eleventh century. A bronze figure of Tsar Samuil stands near Saint Sofia Church and Alexander Nevsky Cathedral. The statue's illuminated eyes attracted debate, while its placement ties a modern national memorial to Sofia's principal ceremonial quarter.

St. Sofia Church

Saint Sofia Church gave the Bulgarian capital its present name and preserves one of its most important early Christian buildings. The brick basilica took shape mainly in the sixth century above earlier churches and a Roman necropolis; a plain vaulted interior contrasts with the archaeological level below, where tombs, mosaics, and foundations reveal centuries of burial and worship.

Boyana Waterfall

Boyana Waterfall drops through a wooded ravine on Vitosha Mountain above Sofia's southern districts. Several hiking routes reach it from Boyana or higher trailheads, crossing forest, rocks, and steep ground. Spring snowmelt and heavy rain increase the flow, while dry periods reduce the cascade; ice and mud can make the approach hazardous in colder months.

The City Garden

Established in 1872, Sofia's City Garden is a compact downtown park of fountains, flowerbeds, and shade trees. Chess players and lunchtime walkers use the benches beside the National Theatre. As one of the capital's earliest public gardens, it remains a civic green room rather than a large landscape park.

Sofia mosque - Banya Bashi

Banya Bashi Mosque was completed in the sixteenth century beside Sofia's thermal springs. A large dome, brick-and-stone walls, and a single minaret mark the surviving Ottoman mosque, designed within the tradition of architect Mimar Sinan. It is an active place of Islamic worship near the Central Market Hall and former mineral baths, with visitor access shaped by prayer times.

St Joseph's Catholic Cathedral in Sofia replaced a parish complex destroyed in World War II bombing and now stands on the Square of Tolerance near the synagogue and Banya Bashi Mosque. A modern hall and tower serve the city's Latin-rite community. Outside Mass the nave opens for quiet viewing.

Orthodox temple of Saint Paraskeva

Anton Torniov designed the Church of Saint Paraskeva with a broad central dome and unusually rounded interior plan, and construction began in the early twentieth century and continued through periods of war and financial difficulty. Brick, stone, icons, murals, and a spacious nave distinguish the active Orthodox church on Rakovski Street from Sofia's smaller medieval religious buildings.
