Lausanne

Lausanne originated as a Roman military camp called Lousanna before moving to its current hilly location for defense. During the Middle Ages, it became an important bishopric, culminating in the construction of its Gothic cathedral. In 1536, the city was conquered by Bern, introducing the Reformation and establishing a known Protestant academy. Today, Lausanne is a dynamic university city and the headquarters of the International Olympic Committee, overlooking Lake Geneva.

Lausanne Cathedral

Lausanne Cathedral rises above the old city as a major Gothic church begun in the twelfth century and consecrated in 1275, and the traditional night watch still calls the hours from the tower, while worship, concerts, stairs and maintenance govern access. Sculpted portals, stained glass, choir stalls, tombs, organs and a tall tower reflect Catholic origins and later Reformed use.

Place de la Palud

Place de la Palud forms a narrow civic square below Lausanne's old town, bordered by the Renaissance town hall, arcades, shops, cafes, and a historic fountain. An animated clock shows episodes from local history at set times, while market stalls occupy the surrounding streets on scheduled days. The square remains an administrative and commercial space.

03 AdmissionFree

Lausanne Opera

Lausanne Opera traces its origins to a nineteenth-century theater later rebuilt and modernized for opera, ballet, concerts and musical theater. A formal facade fronts an auditorium and stage complex designed for changing productions. Rehearsal, technical and public spaces support a working performance institution whose interior access is tied to scheduled events.

46.5180° N / 6.6369° E

04 AdmissionFree

Esplanade de Montbenon

Montbenon Esplanade stretches along a terrace above Lausanne's railway with lawns, tree-lined walks, monuments and views across Lake Geneva toward the Alps. The Casino de Montbenon and Palais de Justice frame the public space. Festivals and gatherings periodically occupy the grounds. The broad terrace combines the roles of urban overlook, civic park and pedestrian link between central streets and the slope below.

46.5200° N / 6.6259° E

05 AdmissionFree

Sauvabelin Tower

Perched atop a hill in Sauvabelin Park, the Sauvabelin Tower has an experience with its 302-step spiral staircase leading to a viewing platform. This 35-meter wooden tower provides panoramic vistas of Lausanne and its surroundings, including Lake Geneva, the Swiss Alps, and the Jura region. Originally built in 2003 to replace the original tower from 1904, it is a symbol of the turn of the millennium.

46.5353° N / 6.6385° E

AQUATIS Aquarium-Vivarium

AQUATIS in Lausanne combines freshwater aquariums and vivarium habitats, presenting fish, reptiles, and rainforest species under one roof. Displays range from regional Swiss waters to Amazonian tanks and African and Asian crocodiles. Interpretive galleries also cover evolutionary themes and prehistoric life, giving the complex a science-museum character alongside the live animal rooms.

07 AdmissionFree

Pavillon Thaïlandais

Thailand presented Lausanne's Thai Pavilion in recognition of King Bhumibol Adulyadej's ties to Switzerland. The gilded open structure stands in Denantou Park near Lake Geneva. Ornamented columns and layered roofs carry its ceremonial symbolism. The bright decoration creates a strong contrast with the surrounding lawns, trees and lakeside landscape.

46.5062° N / 6.6409° E

08 AdmissionFree

Parc de Valency

Parc de Valency occupies a sloping residential area in western Lausanne with mature trees, lawns, paths, play spaces, and seasonal planting. Views open toward Lake Geneva and the Alps from parts of the park, while neighborhood use defines its character more than formal monuments.

46.5295° N / 6.6118° E

09 AdmissionFree

Parc Louis Bourget

Parc Louis Bourget occupies a wooded stretch of Lake Geneva shoreline in Lausanne, with walking trails, open grass, and a nature reserve used by waterbirds. Dense tree cover keeps parts of the park quieter than the busier quays closer to Ouchy. Locals come for lakeside walks and birdwatching, while travellers use it as a green break between the waterfront and the hillside city.

46.5192° N / 6.5876° E

10 AdmissionFree

Pyramides de Vidy

The Pyramids of Vidy are low earth terraces and concrete forms within the lakeside park west of central Lausanne. Created during twentieth-century redevelopment near the Roman site of Lousonna and the Expo 64 grounds, they now support sitting, play and public events. Their descriptive name refers to the stepped shape within the broad recreational landscape.

46.5117° N / 6.6033° E
