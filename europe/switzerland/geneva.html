Geneva

Calvin's Reformation turned Geneva into a Protestant republic and a refuge for dissidents. Later neutrality drew the Red Cross and League of Nations institutions. The old town's St Pierre and the international organisations district bookend that path from theocratic city to diplomatic hub. Rousseau's island monument and the Reformation Wall narrate civic memory in public sculpture.

Brunswick Monument

Brunswick Monument is a monument in Geneva, Switzerland. Construction and early use date to around 1877. Located in the Jardin des Alpes near Lake Geneva, the Brunswick Monument is a neo-Gothic tomb honoring Duke Charles II of Brunswick. The mausoleum, built in 1877, has an equestrian statue of the duke and is constructed from materials like Carrara marble and Verona stone. The memorial stands in an open plaza or roadside setting.

Broken Chair

The Broken Chair is a 12-meter-high wooden sculpture located in front of the Palais des Nations, created by artist Daniel Bursett in 1997. Commissioned by Handicap International, it is a powerful protest against cluster bombs and landmines, symbolizing the devastating impact of these weapons on civilians during times of war. The chair's broken leg represents the loss of human limbs caused by landmine explosions.

The Geneva Water Fountain

The Jet d'Eau is the well known symbol of Geneva, Switzerland. Located where Lake Geneva empties into the Rhône River, the massive water fountain propels 500 liters of water per second to an altitude of 140 meters. Originally a safety valve for a hydraulic power network, it is now a dramatic, internationally recognized landmark visible from across the city.
