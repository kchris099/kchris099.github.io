Basel

Basel stands on the Rhine where Switzerland meets France and Germany. Its university, founded in 1460, is among the country's oldest. The city joined the Swiss Confederation in 1501 and developed as a printing and trade center. Erasmus is buried in the cathedral. Chemical and pharmaceutical firms established headquarters here in the 19th and 20th centuries. Art museums hold works from Holbein to contemporary collections.

Basel Town Hall

Basel's Rathaus rose in red sandstone in the early sixteenth century after an earthquake damaged earlier civic buildings, around the time the city joined the Swiss Confederation in 1501. The painted courtyard and stepped facade face the Marktplatz, where markets and tram lines still meet. Council chambers upstairs continue municipal work, while the courtyard remains open for passers-by to view the murals.

Basel Cathedral

Basel Minster stands above the Rhine with red sandstone walls, patterned roof tiles, twin towers, cloisters and a long history of rebuilding after the 1356 earthquake. The former Catholic cathedral became a Reformed church and contains tombs, sculpture and the burial of Erasmus. Its terrace overlooks the river, while worship, concerts and controlled tower access shape the active site.

Sevogel Brunnen

Created in the 19th century, this fountain commemorates Henman Sevogel, a prominent 15th-century councilor and military leader who died at the Battle of St. Jakob an der Birs. The monument was part of a movement to celebrate local heroes, keeping the memory of medieval political struggles alive.

47.5555° N / 7.5878° E

04 AdmissionFree

Mittlere brucke

A Rhine crossing at this spot dates to 1226, when an early bridge helped Basel grow as a trading town between the Upper Rhine and the Swiss plateau. The present stone Mittlere Brücke, rebuilt in 1905 for modern traffic, still links Kleinbasel and Grossbasel at the city's historic midpoint. Pedestrians use the spans for river views toward the cathedral ridge and upstream bends.

47.5601° N / 7.5897° E

05 AdmissionFree

Spalentor

Spalentor is one of Basel's surviving medieval city gates, built after the earthquake as part of an expanded fourteenth-century wall, and demolition of most fortifications left it freestanding within modern traffic, so the gate illustrates a lost defensive system. A central passage, tiled roof, sculpted figures, and round side towers once controlled the road toward Alsace.

47.5580° N / 7.5815° E

06 AdmissionFree

Tinguely Fountain

Jean Tinguely's Tinguely-Brunnen of 1977 fills a former theater site in Basel with kinetic metal figures that splash and spin in a shallow basin. The mechanical sculptures move independently, colliding and spraying water in shifting patterns. Benches around the pool let people watch the machines work, a public artwork that ties the artist's kinetic style to everyday city life.

47.5537° N / 7.5906° E

Zoo Basel

Zoo Basel opened in 1874 and now occupies a compact urban site beside the railway, with changing collections of mammals, birds, reptiles, fish and invertebrates. Breeding programs, education and conservation claims accompany public exhibits, while continual rebuilding responds to limited space.

47.5473° N / 7.5790° E

08 AdmissionFree

St. Jakob-Park

St. Jakob-Park opened in 2001 beside the site of an earlier Basel arena and became the home stadium of FC Basel. Red-and-blue seating surrounds the football pitch. The complex also supports concerts and international tournaments. Shopping and transit facilities occupy adjoining areas, while tours and capacity arrangements follow the event program.

47.5416° N / 7.6201° E

09 AdmissionFree

Tierpark Lange Erlen

Tierpark Lange Erlen in Basel has kept animals since 1871, with enclosures for deer, goats, peacocks, wild boar, donkeys, ponies, and small mammals. Woodland trails link the pens, making a compact family zoo inside a riverside park setting north of the center along the Wiese valley.

47.5740° N / 7.6078° E

Border Triangle Basel

At the Rhine port near Basel, the Dreilandereck marks the meeting area of Switzerland, France and Germany. A tall modern monument stands on Swiss ground near warehouses, docks and river traffic, while the exact legal border point lies in the river. Industrial access and construction can affect routes, making it a geographic marker more than a landscaped national monument.

47.5885° N / 7.5897° E
