Explore Basel

A Brief History

Basel stands on the Rhine where Switzerland meets France and Germany. Its university, founded in 1460, is among the country's oldest. The city joined the Swiss Confederation in 1501 and developed as a printing and trade center. Erasmus is buried in the cathedral. Chemical and pharmaceutical firms established headquarters here in the 19th and 20th centuries. Art museums hold works from Holbein to contemporary collections.

Top Sights & Activities

Basel Town Hall

Constructed in the early 16th century following a devastating earthquake, this red-sandstone building reflects the city's entry into the Swiss Confederation in 1501. The facade and inner courtyard feature later frescoes and a Renaissance clock, remaining the active seat of the cantonal government.

Free (CHF6)

Basel Cathedral

Rebuilt in the late 12th century after an earthquake, this red sandstone cathedral blends Romanesque and Gothic architectural styles. Historically the site of the influential Council of Basel, it houses the tomb of humanist scholar Erasmus, reflecting enduring religious significance and deep intellectual heritage.

Free

Sevogel Brunnen

Created in the 19th century, this fountain commemorates Henman Sevogel, a prominent 15th-century councilor and military leader who died at the Battle of St. Jakob an der Birs. The monument was part of a movement to celebrate local heroes, keeping the memory of medieval political struggles alive.

Free

Mittlere brucke

First constructed in 1226, this was one of the earliest bridges across the Rhine, boosting the city's role as a major trading hub. The original structure was replaced by a stone bridge in 1905 to accommodate modern traffic, serving as a historical symbol of strategic and commercial development.
