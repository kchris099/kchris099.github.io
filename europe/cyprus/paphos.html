Paphos

Paphos was a Hellenistic and Roman capital of Cyprus, famed for sanctuaries of Aphrodite and for mosaic-floored villas now in the archaeological park. Medieval castles guarded the harbour under Lusignan and Ottoman rule. British and later independent Cypriot development turned Kato Paphos into a resort strip while Ktima inland kept administrative and residential quarters. Tombs of the Kings and harbour cafes sit on layers of imperial and pilgrimage traffic.

Archaeological Site of the Tombs of the Kings

The Archaeological Site of the Tombs of the Kings is a vast necropolis dating back to the 3rd century BCE. It has underground tombs that were careful carved out of rock. The site offers a glimpse into ancient burial practices and architectural ingenuity, travellers can explore nearby attractions such as the Medieval Castle, waterfront restaurants, ice-cream shops, cafes, and bars with views across the water and harbor.

Paphos Castle

A squat stone fort closes the western edge of Paphos harbor. Lusignan and Ottoman rebuilding followed the destruction of earlier Byzantine defenses and the present structure later served as a prison, storage building and salt warehouse. A central courtyard, roof platform and thick chambers reveal its practical military scale, while the open space outside is used for waterfront events and festivals.

Archaeological Site of Nea Paphos

Nea Paphos preserves the remains of an ancient city founded near the harbor in the late fourth century BCE. Roman villas contain detailed floor mosaics. An odeon, agora and fortifications reveal public life, while streets and later Christian structures extend across the site. The exposed archaeological landscape requires time and attention to sun and uneven ground.

Paphos Mosaics

Paphos Mosaics are ancient Roman structures in Cyprus, showing well-preserved mosaics depicting mythological scenes. The mosaics date back to the Hellenistic period and are considered among the in the world. The site includes mosaics from various periods, including the Hellenistic and Byzantine eras, Paphos Castle, a Byzantine fort, offers a glimpse into history and hosts events like the Paphos Aphrodite Festival.

Paphos Lighthouse

Paphos Lighthouse, or Paphos Faros, was built in 1888 under British colonial administration on the headland near the harbour castle. The white tower still marks the approach for coastal traffic and stands within walking distance of the archaeological park and seaside path.

Agia Solomoni Catacomb

Also known as the Catacombs of Saint Solomonia, this cave complex began as stone extraction chambers. Later use turned the spaces into burials and, by the ninth century, a Christian Orthodox church. The underground rooms remain a focus for Christian pilgrimage and quiet inspection of the rock-cut halls.

Saint Paul's Pillar

A weathered stone column stands among the remains of the early Christian basilica of Chrysopolitissa in Kato Paphos. Local tradition identifies it as the place where the apostle Paul was bound and flogged before converting the Roman governor of Cyprus; mosaic pavements, foundations, and the later Church of Agia Kyriaki surround the pillar, joining archaeology with an active pilgrimage setting.

Agia Kyriaki Chrysopolitissa

Ruins of a thirteenth-century church at Agia Kyriaki Chrysopolitissa keep a colourful mosaic floor among foundation walls. The remaining footprint shows how large the original religious structure once was in Paphos. Open foundations let people read the plan of one of the town's major early churches.

Paphos Aphrodite Waterpark

Slides, pools, a lazy river, children's areas and landscaped sun decks make up Paphos Aphrodite Waterpark in the Geroskipou tourist zone. The privately operated facility is designed for seasonal recreation. Ride availability, height restrictions, weather and annual operating dates determine what is accessible on a given day.

Byzantine Church of Agia Paraskevi

Five domes rise over the compact Church of Agia Paraskevi in Geroskipou, giving the Byzantine building an unusual roofline. Its masonry shell contains layers of wall painting from several periods, including scenes added between the medieval and early modern eras; the church remains a place of worship, and its artistic value lies in the surviving fresco sequence.
