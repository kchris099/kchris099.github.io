Bordeaux

Bordeaux prospered as a port shipping wine and goods from the Garonne estuary to northern Europe, with 18th-century quays and Place de la Bourse built during the Age of Enlightenment. Medieval gates, Saint-André Cathedral, and neoclassical theatres record English and French control. UNESCO-listed waterfront and surrounding châteaux illustrate southwestern France's mercantile and viticultural history.

Monument aux Girondins

The Monument aux Girondins anchors one end of Bordeaux's vast Place des Quinconces. Built around the turn of the twentieth century, it honors Girondin deputies executed during the French Revolution through a tall column, allegorical figures and bronze fountain groups. Horses, sea creatures, Liberty and the Republic create a political sculpture ensemble beside a square used for fairs and public events.

Grosse Cloche

Two round towers support a central bell and astronomical clock, while the passage once controlled movement and contained prison cells. Grosse Cloche occupies a medieval gateway on Bordeaux's former town hall route. The present bell dates from the eighteenth century and rings on selected civic occasions, preserving a public warning function that once regulated fires, curfews, and communal time.

Place de la Bourse

Place de la Bourse faces the Garonne with a crescent of eighteenth-century facades and the Palais de la Bourse's neoclassical columns and sculpture. The Miroir d'Eau pool on the quayside reflects the square, making the ensemble a central image of Bordeaux's riverfront.

Miroir d'eau

A thin sheet of water alternates with mist across Bordeaux's Miroir d'eau beside Place de la Bourse. Landscape artist Michel Corajoud's 2006 installation uses a granite surface, underground reservoir and programmed cycles to reflect the eighteenth-century facades; operation may pause in winter, drought, maintenance or events, so the plaza remains meaningful even when the water system is off.

Place du Parlement

Place du Parlement lies in Bordeaux's Saint-Pierre district, enclosed by eighteenth-century facades with balanced windows, balconies and ground-floor dining. A nineteenth-century fountain occupies the paved center of the former market square. Its compact proportions and pedestrian activity create an intimate urban setting tied closely to the surrounding historic streets.

Tour Pey Berland

Pey Berland Tower stands apart from Bordeaux Cathedral because heavy bells could have threatened the church's foundations and vaults. Construction began in the fifteenth century, producing a late Gothic bell tower later crowned by a gilded statue of the Virgin; a narrow stair reaches upper terraces with views over cathedral roofs, the Garonne, stone neighborhoods, and modern districts.

Librairie Mollat

Founded in 1896, Librairie Mollat occupies a network of rooms on Bordeaux's Rue Porte Dijeaux, including the site of Montesquieu's final residence. Its departments cover literature, art, history and science. Other rooms focus on travel, children's books, academic subjects and music. Author talks and specialist staff continue the shop's role in the city's literary life.

Porte Cailhau

Porte Cailhau rose beside the Garonne at the end of the fifteenth century as both a defensive gate and a monument to Charles VIII. Conical roofs, Gothic openings, sculpture, and early Renaissance detail frame the passage into old Bordeaux. Interior stairs and displays explain the city's walls and river trade, while changes to the waterfront have moved the bank farther from the gateway.

Porte de Bourgogne

Porte de Bourgogne is a landmark Roman-style stone arch that is a symbolic gateway to Bordeaux. Built in the 1750s, it stands at Quai Richelieu on the Garonne River, near the Pont de Pierre. This historic structure was once part of the city walls and marked the beginning or end of the road to Paris, now known as Cours Victor Hugo.

Pont de Pierre

Seventeen masonry arches carry Pont de Pierre across the Garonne between central Bordeaux and La Bastide. Commissioned under Napoleon and opened in 1822, it replaced the boat crossings that had connected the two banks. Trams, cyclists and pedestrians now use the span. Tidal water, broad views and aligned riverfront facades define the experience of crossing the bridge.

Cité du Vin

The Cité du Vin uses a curved glass-and-metal building beside the Garonne to examine wine as an agricultural, commercial, scientific and cultural subject. Interactive galleries cover regions, landscapes, production, trade, sensory analysis and drinking traditions beyond Bordeaux alone. Temporary exhibitions, workshops, tastings and an upper belvedere extend the visit.

Pont Jacques Chaban Delmas

Pont Jacques Chaban-Delmas opened in 2013 as a vertical-lift bridge of concrete and steel spanning the Garonne toward the Bassins a flot. When raised it clears tall ships; when lowered it links the historic quays to newer districts including the Cite du Vin area.

Palais Gallien

Fragments of high walls and arched entrances survive from Burdigala's Roman amphitheater among residential streets in Bordeaux; built when the city prospered under the empire, the arena may have held large crowds for public spectacles. Medieval and later stone reuse removed most of the structure, leaving isolated masonry that requires interpretation to reconstruct its original oval plan.

Basilique Saint-Seurin

An eleventh-century Roman Catholic basilica, Saint-Seurin holds a crypt with marble sarcophagi on a fourth-century necropolis. UNESCO lists it on the Routes of Santiago de Compostela. Local tradition treats the site as an early Christian anchor in Bordeaux.

Château Pape Clément

Chateau Pape Clement has produced wine near Bordeaux since the medieval period, when the estate became associated with Bertrand de Goth before his election as Pope Clement V. Historic buildings, modern cellars and landscaped grounds reflect centuries of changing ownership and production. Scheduled visits explain vineyard practices, winemaking and the estate's red and white Graves wines.

Parc Animalier René Canivenc

Parc Animalier René Canivenc occupies wooded municipal land in Gradignan south of Bordeaux. Paths pass simple enclosures for deer, wallabies, goats, birds, and other domestic or park animals beside streams and picnic areas. The site is designed as a modest family walk and local green space, not as a full zoological institution with extensive conservation exhibits.

Marché des Capucins

Known locally as Bordeaux's belly, Marché des Capucins fills a covered hall and surrounding streets south of the historic center. Fishmongers, butchers, cheese sellers, produce stalls, bakeries, wine merchants. Cafes and prepared-food counters serve residents from early morning. The market's value lies in everyday regional food trade and informal meals.
