Avignon

Avignon became the seat of the papacy from 1309 to 1377 when French-born Clement V and his successors built the fortress-like palace on the Rhône, drawing cardinals, bankers, and artists to Provence. The city remained papal property until the Revolution, with its broken bridge and ramparts symbolising medieval church power. Theatre festivals, wine trade, and rail links later turned Avignon into a cultural gateway between the Mediterranean coast and the interior of southern France.

Palais des Papes

The Palace of the Popes dominates Avignon with fortified walls, towers, chapels, audience halls, private apartments, and courtyards built during the fourteenth-century papacy. Two major construction phases created the older palace of Benedict XII and the more ornate additions of Clement VI; frescoes survive in selected rooms, while lost furnishings and later military use leave many interiors architecturally bare.

The Bridge of Avignon

Four arches and a small chapel survive from the medieval Pont Saint-Bénézet, which once crossed channels and islands of the Rhône at Avignon. Repeated flood damage and costly repairs led to abandonment in the seventeenth century. The remaining span, gatehouse connection, river views, and famous song make the bridge recognizable, but its historical importance lies in regional movement and control of the crossing.

Tour Philippe-le-Bel

Philip IV of France built the tower at Villeneuve-lès-Avignon in the early fourteenth century to guard the western end of the Rhône bridge; the bridge no longer reaches the tower, leaving the monument to explain a former crossing and the political border it once controlled. A square keep, defensive openings, vaulted rooms, and roof terrace represented royal authority opposite the papal city.

Les Halles d'Avignon

Les Halles d'Avignon is the city's covered market, packed with produce stalls, cheese counters, butchers, and prepared-food stands under one roof. A living plant wall faces the exterior, and mornings bring the densest shopping traffic for regional ingredients.

Orange

Orange lies north of Avignon in the Rhone valley and preserves major Roman monuments within a working Provencal town. The ancient theater retains an stage wall, while a triumphal arch marks the former approach to the settlement. Streets, cafes and later buildings surround these remains, placing Roman architecture within the everyday fabric of the modern town. The monuments remain central to Orange's identity.

Notre Dame des Doms

Avignon Cathedral, also known as Cathedrale Notre-Dame des Doms d'Avignon, is a 12th-century Romanesque-style building located next to the Papal Palace. The cathedral is adorned with a gilded statue of the Virgin Mary and is a UNESCO Heritage Site. Its elevated position on a rocky ledge allows it to be visible from various points in the city and beyond.

Jardin des Doms

Jardin des Doms crowns the rock above the Palais des Papes with terraced paths, lawns, and viewpoints over the Rhone and Villeneuve-les-Avignon. The garden offers shade after palace visits and frames the ruined bridge and river bend from the cliff edge.

Remparts d'Avignon

Avignon's ramparts, built between about 1355 and 1370 during the papal period, run more than four kilometres and rise about eight metres. Gates and wall walks still enclose the historic centre, marking the defensive ring that protected the papal city.

43.9427° N / 4.8053° E

Chocolaterie Castelain

In Châteauneuf-du-Pape, Chocolaterie Castelain is a working chocolate manufacturer and visitor shop, with another production presence in the region. Demonstrations, tastings, workshops and pairings may explain cocoa, tempering, fillings and local wine relationships. Programs vary by booking and age, so the site is better described as a commercial craft producer than as a permanent museum with fixed exhibits.

Parc Chico Mendes

Parc Chico Mendes is a large municipal park on Avignon's edge with lawns, playgrounds, sports fields, and tree-lined paths used by neighbourhood families. The open grass gives a green break from the dense stone streets inside the medieval papal ramparts.
