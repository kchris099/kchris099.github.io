Reims

Reims was the traditional coronation city of French kings from Clovis's baptism in the fifth century through Charles IX in 1825, with the cathedral's sculpted façade celebrating that ritual. Roman Durocortorum lay on the road between Paris and the Rhine, while medieval champagne trade later enriched merchants' houses and cellars in the surrounding plain. First World War bombardment scarred the city, but restored monuments and Art Deco public buildings record Reims's place in French monarchy, industry, and twentieth-century reconstruction.

Porte de Mars

Porte de Mars survives near the northern edge of central Reims as a large Roman triumphal arch from the imperial period. Three openings and carved coffers reveal the scale of the former city gate. Mythological reliefs and architectural fragments preserve details of its decoration. Later incorporation into medieval fortifications helped protect the masonry, which can now be studied from the surrounding landscaped area.

Basilique Saint-Remi

The Basilica of Saint-Remi developed around the tomb of the bishop traditionally credited with baptizing Clovis, and wartime damage required major restoration, while the church is an active place of worship within Reims's historic coronation heritage. Romanesque and Gothic building campaigns created a long nave, choir, chapels, stained glass, sculpture, and monastic connections beside the former abbey.

Villa Demoiselle

Villa Demoiselle was built in the early twentieth century beside Reims's Pommery estate, combining Art Nouveau structure with details that anticipate Art Deco. Paul-François and Nathalie Vranken restored the neglected house using period craft, furniture, stained glass, woodwork, and decorative schemes. Visits connect the residence with champagne culture, though it remains distinct from the neighboring production cellars.

49.2433° N / 4.0499° E

Domaine Les Crayères

A mansion estate on the edge of Reims holds fine dining at Le Parc under chef Philippe Mille and a more casual table at Le Jardin. Pastry chef Yoann Normand and Japanese chef Kazuyuki Tanaka work alongside the kitchen team. Gardens and reception rooms give the property the feel of a private residence adapted for guests who come for the cooking as much as the rooms.

49.2412° N / 4.0517° E

05 AdmissionFree

Place Drouet d'Erlon

Place Drouet d'Erlon is the active, pedestrianized Reims. The long, rectangular square is lined with numerous cafes, restaurants, and shops, making it the primary hub for dining and socializing in the city. At its center stands the striking Subé Fountain, topped with a golden statue of the Winged Victory.

Palais du Tau

Archbishops of Reims used the Palace of Tau beside the cathedral for residence, ceremony and coronation banquets. Rebuilding across medieval and early modern periods produced a palace whose museum collections include cathedral sculpture, tapestries, reliquaries, coronation objects and the Sainte-Ampoule tradition. Restoration or closure may affect access, but the building remains essential to understanding royal rituals centered on Reims.

49.2534° N / 4.0348° E

Cathedral of Notre-Dame of Reims

French kings were crowned for centuries beneath the vaults of Notre-Dame de Reims. Construction began in the thirteenth century after fire destroyed an earlier cathedral, producing a sculpted west front, rose windows, stained glass, high nave, chapels and an extensive angel program. Severe First World War damage and later restoration became part of the monument's history alongside its medieval coronation role.

49.2538° N / 4.0340° E

08 AdmissionFree

Parc Léo Lagrange

Parc Leo Lagrange spreads southwest of central Reims with broad lawns, a lake, paths, sports fields, play areas and planted sections. Residents use the grounds for running, cycling, picnics, informal games and seasonal gatherings. Its open recreational landscape provides everyday green space within the modern city.

49.2460° N / 4.0224° E

09 AdmissionFree

Cryptoportique

A semi-subterranean Roman gallery survives beneath Place du Forum in Reims. The cryptoporticus formed part of the ancient civic center, with vaulted passages likely supporting buildings above and possibly used for storage or sheltered circulation. One wing remains accessible during limited seasons and events, while the sunken garden helps reveal the original level of Roman Durocortorum.

49.2566° N / 4.0339° E

10 AdmissionFree

Place Royale

Arcaded facades enclose a bronze statue of Louis XV at the center of this Reims square. The ordered 18th-century composition sits near the Gallo-Roman Cryptoportique and within reach of Porte de Mars, Saint-Remi Basilica, and Villa Demoiselle. Stone galleries and open paving keep the royal plan readable even when traffic moves around the monument.

49.2555° N / 4.0341° E
