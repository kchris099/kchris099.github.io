Caen

Caen rose around the abbeys William the Conqueror and his wife Matilda founded in the eleventh century to atone for their consanguineous marriage, giving the city two Romanesque churches that still frame civic space. As capital of Normandy, Caen developed textile mills and a university that survived the Wars of Religion and Revolution. Heavy bombing in 1944 destroyed much of the centre before reconstruction preserved the abbeys and rebuilt avenues that connect the city to D-Day beaches nearby.

Sword Beach - Queen Red

Sword Beach, the easternmost of the D-Day landing zones on June 6, 1944, is a significant historical site where British and French forces bravely landed amidst fierce German resistance. This stretch of Normandy coastline between Ouistreham and Saint-Aubin-sur-Mer was important for the 3rd British Infantry Division and commandos from the 1st Special Service Brigade as they fought to secure their foothold in France.

Caen Castle

Caen Castle, constructed on the remains of a fortress by William the Conqueror, is among Europe's largest medieval enclosures. It served as a gathering place for the Dukes of Normandy and Kings of England and was a key stronghold during the Hundred Years War. The castle now houses two museums that offer an in-depth look into Normandy's history, while its ramparts provide views of the city.
