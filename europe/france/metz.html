Metz

Metz stands where Roman Divodurum guarded the Moselle crossing and later bishops and German emperors contested a strategic city on the eastern march of France. Its cathedral of Saint-Étienne combines one of Europe's largest expanses of stained glass with Gothic stonework funded by medieval canons. Annexed by Germany from 1871 to 1918, Metz returned to France with streetscapes that mix eighteenth-century French squares, Prussian barracks, and contemporary Pompidou-Metz cultural investment.

Porte des Allemands

A medieval bridge castle over the Seille in Metz's Quartier Outre-Seille now opens as a walking route through the fortification. Quays and embankment paths link to the gate towers. The structure spans river and wall as a defensive crossing rather than a simple ornamental arch.

Stade Saint-Symphorien

Stade Saint-Symphorien is the home ground of FC Metz on the southern edge of the city. Match evenings draw supporters to the stands for Ligue football, while the wider Metz sports scene also includes handball at nearby arenas. The stadium remains the club's principal matchday venue.

03 AdmissionFree

New Temple

The Temple Neuf occupies a small island in the Moselle beside Metz's historic center. German imperial authorities completed the neo-Romanesque Protestant church in 1904, using dark sandstone that contrasts with the city's pale Jaumont limestone. Towers, arcades, stained glass, and a riverside garden connect the building with the period when Metz formed part of the German Empire.

49.1207° N / 6.1721° E

04 AdmissionFree

Metz covered market

Metz covered market, also known as the Episcopal Palace, is a busy hub of activity with stalls open from 8am-6pm Monday-Saturday. Savor a bowl of vegetable or fruit soup at Soupe A Soups or indulge in local meats and cheeses at Chez Mauricette. For a change of pace, visit the market located right in front of the cathedral where explore various stalls and even find stores selling artisan coffee roasting equipment.

05 AdmissionFree

Muse

Muse opened near the Centre Pompidou-Metz and railway station as part of a major redevelopment district. Shops, restaurants, services, apartments and offices occupy a contemporary mixed-use complex connected by public passages. Its architecture and location show how the former rail-side area has been reshaped into an extension of central Metz. The project extends the city center toward former railway land.

06 AdmissionFree

Metz-Ville

Metz is a city with a rich history and architecture. The Metz station and the Imperial Quarter are remnants of the German era, showing Neo-Roman style buildings that were part of William II's efforts to modernize the city. The 300-meter-long Gare de Metz, completed in 1908, is an notable building with the Emperor's apartments and reception pavilion. It has been recognized as one of France's most train stations multiple times.

Serpentine Gate

The gate commonly called Porte Serpenoise is a nineteenth-century reconstruction of Metz's historic southern entrance; the present stone arch near the Esplanade commemorates local history through reused forms and inscriptions. Earlier Roman and medieval gateways occupied the route, but military modernization and later demolition repeatedly changed the structure.

49.1125° N / 6.1710° E

08 AdmissionFree

Palais du Gouverneur Militaire

The nineteenth-century Palais du Gouverneur Militaire in Metz was built as the residence and headquarters of the military governor during the German imperial period. Today the building holds museum displays of art and material from that era, keeping the former stronghold in public cultural use.

Arsenal

Originally built as a military arsenal in 1864, the Arsenal in Metz has been transformed into a cultural hub. Renovated by architect Ricardo Bofill, it now houses one of Europe's most concert halls with acoustics and decor. The complex includes three theaters, including the Grande Salle dedicated to music. It is home to the Lorraine National Orchestra and hosts prestigious temporary exhibitions.

49.1146° N / 6.1708° E

10 AdmissionFree

Cueillette de Peltre

Cueillette de Peltre is a farm southeast of Metz where customers harvest produce directly from seasonal fields and gardens. Strawberries, vegetables, flowers, apples and other crops may be available according to weather and growing conditions. Purchases are weighed after collection, linking the visit with the farm's ordinary agricultural cycle. The available rows shift through the season.
