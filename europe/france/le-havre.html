Le Havre

Le Havre was founded in 1517 at the Seine estuary to give France a controlled Atlantic port when sandbars blocked older harbours upstream. Transatlantic emigration, coffee imports, and shipbuilding shaped quays and warehouses through the nineteenth century. Allied bombing in 1944 levelled the centre before Auguste Perret's reinforced-concrete grid rebuilt housing, churches, and the town hall as a UNESCO-listed model of post-war urban design on the Normandy coast.

Église Sainte Catherine

Eglise Sainte-Catherine in Honfleur is a large wooden church whose roof structure resembles an inverted ship's hull, built by shipwrights with massive timber pillars and broad arcades. The separate bell tower stands nearby in the market square of the old port town.

02 AdmissionFree

Vieux Bassin

Honfleur's Vieux Bassin, created in 1681 by expanding the port after coastal fortifications came down, lines sixteenth- to eighteenth-century houses around calm water. A carousel sits among the quays. Night lighting reflects the facades in the basin for photographers and evening walkers.

49.4196° N / 0.2328° E

03 AdmissionFree

Panorama du Mont-Joli

Panorama du Mont-Joli in Equemauville overlooks Honfleur, the Normandy Bridge, and the Seine estuary from a hillside about a twenty-five-minute walk from the Vieux Bassin. Night views take in the harbour lights, while daytime looks stretch across the river mouth.

49.4201° N / 0.2256° E

04 AdmissionFree

Pont de Normandie

Opened in 1995, the Pont de Normandie is a cable-stayed bridge across the Seine estuary between Le Havre and Honfleur. Its tall pylons and long main span cut the road distance between the two ports, and the crossing remains a clear landmark on the approach to the mouth of the river.

49.4306° N / 0.2742° E

05 AdmissionFree

St Joseph's Church

Auguste Perret designed St Joseph's Church as both a parish church and memorial within postwar Le Havre. A concrete tower more than one hundred metres high rises from an octagonal lantern filled with colored glass by Marguerite Huré. The light changes across the exposed structural grid throughout the day, while the building's scale recalls residents killed during the 1944 destruction of the city.

49.4910° N / 0.1008° E

06 AdmissionFree

Square Saint-Roch

Downtown Square Saint-Roch sits near Porte Oceane and Avenue Foch with dense greenery and two children's playgrounds. Locals and travellers share the lawns despite occasional mosquitoes. Open planting and surrounding streets keep it a neighbourhood park stop.

49.4945° N / 0.1021° E

07 AdmissionFree

Stade Oceane

Stade Oceane seats more than 25,000 for football in Le Havre, with clear sightlines and a layout praised for easy movement. Women's World Cup matches and other fixtures have used the bowl. Staff and volunteers support match days in the modern stadium shell.

49.4993° N / 0.1693° E

08 AdmissionFree

Le Havre Cathedral

Notre-Dame Cathedral survived the bombing that destroyed much of central Le Havre, although the building required substantial postwar repair, and it predates Auguste Perret's rebuilt city and remains the seat of the modern Diocese of Le Havre. Construction began in the sixteenth century and continued through later periods, producing Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque elements around a compact nave and facade.

49.4870° N / 0.1079° E

09 AdmissionFree

Catena Containers Vincent GANIVET

Vincent Ganivet stacked twenty-one brightly painted shipping containers into two leaning arches for Le Havre's five-hundredth anniversary in 2017. Installed near the waterfront, Catène de Containers links the working port's basic freight unit with an improbable self-supporting sculptural form; the open-air work frames views between docks and Perret's city center without functioning as a climbable tower.

49.4848° N / 0.1071° E

10 AdmissionFree (Greenhouses €2)

Hanging Gardens

Le Havre's Jardins Suspendus occupy Fort Sainte-Adresse, a nineteenth-century military enclosure overlooking the Seine estuary and port, and ramparts provide coastal views, while the reused fort walls and powder magazines remain part of the experience. Plant collections are organized by geographic region across bastions, courtyards, lawns, and seasonal greenhouses.

49.5038° N / 0.0942° E
