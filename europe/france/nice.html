Nice

Nice passed from Savoyard rule to France in 1860, preserving Italianate old-town lanes beneath Belle Époque façades along the Baie des Anges. English and Russian aristocrats wintered here from the 19th century, funding promenades, churches, and gardens. The city remains the commercial centre of the French Riviera while mountain villages above supply olive oil and perfumery.

Place Masséna

Nineteenth-century Place Massena gathers neoclassical buildings, cafes, and upscale shops around a busy paved square. Public events use the open floor, and pedestrian links run toward Old Town and the seafront. The plaza works as Nice's formal central crossroads.

Fontaine du Soleil

Fontaine du Soleil stands on Place Masséna in Nice, with a marble Apollo figure raised above the fountain basins. Since the 1950s the statue's rear view has fed local jokes and nicknames, yet the fountain remains the busy square's central marker between the old town and the modern grid.

Opéra de Nice

The Opéra de Nice, originally a small wooden theater built in 1776, has evolved into a known institution hosting grand opera, ballet, and music performances. After being purchased by the city of Nice and redesigned in classical Italian style by architect Benoit Brunat, it has become the main venue for operas, classical concerts, and ballet performances in the city.

Colline du Château

Also called Castle Hill, Colline du Chateau rises on short stone steps above Nice with views of turquoise water and apricot roofs. From the summit, the Old Port's yachts and distant Alps come into view. The hilltop park rewards the climb with a full sweep of city and coast.

Point de vue Colline du Château

The Colline du Château viewpoint crowns the hill above Nice's old port and the Baie des Anges. The fortress itself was destroyed in 1706, but the public park that replaced it keeps terraces, stairs, and lookouts over the rooftops, beach, and harbour. Paths climb from both the old town and the port.

Basilique Notre-Dame de l'Assomption

Basilique Notre-Dame de l'Assomption, also known as Notre Dame de Nice, is a grand neo-Gothic church with imposing twin towers and active stained glass. Located at the end of Saint-Jean-Medecin, it resembles a white-washed version of the famous Notre Dame in Paris. Despite its striking similarity to the Parisian landmark, this basilica was actually modeled after a monastery in Angers, France.

Jardin des Arènes de Cimiez

The Garden of the Arenas of Cimiez surrounds ruins of a Roman amphitheatre that once seated about 5,000, plus baths and other remains of ancient Cemenelum. English Queen Victoria and visiting aristocrats favoured the hill for winter stays. Paths weave between olive trees and the excavated stone seating.

Monastère de Cimiez

Cimiez Monastery, an 800s monastery with gardens overlooking Nice, is a historical site that offers a glimpse into the Franciscan way of life. The monastery includes a church, a 17th-century chapel, and a museum showing the history and development of the Franciscan order. Visitors can admire paintings by the Brea brothers in the church and explore the cemetery where artists Henri Matisse and Raoul Dufy are buried.

Cascade de Gairaut

Cascade de Gairaut is an artificial waterfall above Nice where canal water drops over rock terraces into a shallow pool. Marked paths reach viewing ledges; ducks often gather on the water. The site sits in a quieter hillside quarter, with olive and pine cover around the engineered cascade.

Fort du Mont Alban

Fort du Mont Alban was built in 1557 on a ridge above Nice, controlling approaches along the Riviera coast. Thick ramparts, a rectangular court, and corner bastions define the military plan. When open, walks along the walls look toward Villefranche harbour and the higher Mont Boron slopes.

Grotte du Lazaret

Located at the foot of Mont Boron, Grotte du Lazaret is a 35-meter-long and 14-foot-wide cave that provides insight into the lives of pre-Neanderthal inhabitants. Archaeologists have discovered over 20,000 bone fragments from both humans and ancient animals within the cave, dating back to around 130,000 BC. This seaside cave offers raised walkways and self-guided tours for visitors interested in prehistory.

Trophy of Augustus

Augustus ordered the Trophy of the Alps above La Turbie around 6 BCE to commemorate the subjugation of Alpine peoples and secure routes between Italy and Gaul. A monumental circular structure once carried imperial sculpture and an inscription naming conquered tribes. Medieval reuse reduced the monument, but modern reconstruction and a museum help explain its original form above views of Monaco and the Mediterranean.

43.7448° N / 7.4018° E

The National Sports Museum occupies part of the complex, while tram and road access connect the suburban stadium with central districts on event days.

Prom. des Anglais

The Promenade des Anglais runs about seven kilometres along Nice's seafront from the airport area toward the old town. Palm-lined walkways and a cycle path follow the shingle beach. English winter visitors helped establish the promenade from the eighteenth century onward, and hotels still face the Bay of Angels.

Jardin Albert 1er occupies a strip of green between Nice's Promenade des Anglais and the old town. An open-air theatre hosts summer concerts and festivals, including jazz events. At the garden's heart stands Bernar Venet's Arc de Venet, a large steel sculpture framing the planted walks.

Place Giuseppe Garibaldi

Ochre arcades and painted facades surround Place Garibaldi at the eastern edge of Nice's old town. Laid out in the eighteenth century, the square later received a central statue of Giuseppe Garibaldi, born in Nice before becoming a leader of Italian unification. Tram tracks, cafes, paving, archaeological remains, and links to the port now shape daily use around the formal composition.
