Aigues Mortes

Louis IX ordered a rectangular port on the Camargue in the thirteenth century so Crusaders could embark under royal control. Salt pans funded the walls and the Tour de Constance. When silt closed the harbour and Marseille took the traffic, Aigues-Mortes froze as an inland fortress whose grid still matches the Capetian plan. Flamingos in the surrounding salt lagoons share the landscape Louis's engineers drained for defence.

Towers and Walls of Aigues-Mortes

The Towers and Walls of Aigues-Mortes are a fascinating medieval defensive structure surrounding the historic town of Aigues-Mortes in the Languedoc-Roussillon region of France. Built in the 13th century by King Louis IX, this limestone fortress offers views over courtyards and the surrounding salt marshes, including the Salinas de Aigues Mortes.

Constancy Tower

Constancy Tower, also known as Tour de Constance, is a significant site for Protestant Christians. It is a lasting monument to the faith of 30 women who were imprisoned for their evangelical beliefs, some long running captivity for up to 40 years. The words "resister," etched by Marie Durand, one of the leaders among the women, are still visible on the stone. The tower offers personalized guided tours that enhance the experience and provide insights into its historical significance.
