Nîmes

Nîmes began as a Volcae Celtic centre before Rome made it a key town of Gallia Narbonensis on the Via Domitia. Augustus and later emperors endowed it with an arena, the Maison Carrée, and links to the Pont du Gard aqueduct. After Visigothic and Moorish intervals it became a Huguenot stronghold in the Wars of Religion. Those Roman monuments still stand in the modern street plan.

Amphitheatre of Nîmes

The Roman amphitheater of Nîmes preserves two levels of arcades, internal corridors, stairways and an oval arena built in an early century CE. Its circulation system once moved tens of thousands of spectators toward seats for games and spectacles. Medieval residents converted the structure into a fortified neighborhood, while modern bullfights, concerts and events continue inside after repeated restoration.

Fontaine Pradier

Fontaine Pradier stands on the Esplanade Charles-de-Gaulle in Nîmes. Architect Charles Questel and sculptor James Pradier completed the marble fountain for its 1850 inauguration. Allegorical figures and basins mark the esplanade's civic axis between the station area and the historic centre.

Tour Magne

Tour Magne rises above Nîmes from Mont Cavalier, where Augustus expanded an earlier Gallic tower as part of the Roman city wall. The surviving stone shell overlooks the Jardins de la Fontaine and the urban plain. A narrow internal stair reaches an upper platform when open, clarifying the tower's role as a landmark, lookout, and display of Roman power.

Temple of Diana

The so-called Temple of Diana stands near the sacred spring in Nîmes's Jardins de la Fontaine. Built in the Augustan period as part of an imperial cult complex, the vaulted hall has a basilica-like plan that scholars still debate. Ruined walls and niches remain beside the eighteenth-century garden layout.

Jardin de La Fontaine

Jardins de la Fontaine was created in the eighteenth century around the ancient spring that supplied Roman Nemausus. Formal terraces, canals, balustrades, statues, trees, and paths incorporate the so-called Temple of Diana and climb toward Tour Magne. The garden combines archaeological remains with planned public landscaping.

Porte d'Auguste

Porte d'Auguste preserves four arched passages from a Roman gate on the Via Domitia at Nîmes. An inscription dates the fortifications to the reign of Augustus, when the road crossed the city on its route between Italy and Spain. Modern streets surround the exposed masonry, but the separate pedestrian and vehicle openings still reveal how traffic once entered ancient Nemausus.

Maison Carrée

The Maison Carree preserves the form of a Roman temple built in the early first century CE and dedicated to Gaius and Lucius Caesar. A deep Corinthian porch rises above a high podium before the enclosed cella. Conversion to several later uses helped the structure survive, while modern restoration has clarified its stone proportions and relationship to the civic center of Nimes.

43.8382° N / 4.3561° E

Carré d'Art

Norman Foster designed Carré d'Art as a glass-and-steel counterpart to the ancient Maison Carrée across the square. Opened in the 1990s, the building combines a contemporary art museum, public library, exhibition spaces, reading rooms, and terraces; its restrained facade and internal atrium create a deliberate dialogue between modern civic culture and Nîmes's Roman monument.

43.8380° N / 4.3550° E

Tour de l'Horloge de Nîmes

Nimes's Clock Tower rises above Place de l'Horloge from the city's medieval civic center. Rebuilding in the eighteenth century gave it much of its present appearance, while clock faces and a bell structure continue to mark the square below. The tower is mainly viewed from the surrounding streets, where cafes and pedestrian activity frame the monument.

43.8383° N / 4.3583° E

Castellum Aquae

Castellum Aquae, or Castellum divisorium, is the Roman distribution basin where aqueduct water entered Nîmes before branching into the city network. The circular stone structure shows the engineering of urban supply under the empire. It sits near the line of the aqueduct that once fed Nemausus from the hills.

43.8424° N / 4.3559° E
