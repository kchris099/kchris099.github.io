Explore Arles

A Brief History

Arles flourished as a major Roman provincial capital after Julius Caesar established it as a veteran colony in 46 BC. It became an important commercial port on the Rhône River and an early center of Christianity in Gaul. During the Middle Ages, it served as the capital of the Kingdom of Burgundy. In the late nineteenth century, the city's distinctive light and Provencal atmosphere attracted Vincent van Gogh, who produced hundreds of paintings here. Visitors navigate this layered history through the intact Roman Amphitheater, the Alyscamps necropolis, and the Romanesque St. Trophime.

Arles Amphitheatre

The Arles Arena, a two-tiered Roman amphitheatre located in the southern French town of Arles, is a significant historical site that has stood the test of time. It is part of a collection of eight Roman and medieval monuments that contribute to the city's rich history. This well-preserved colosseum, dating back to around AD 90, once hosted gladiatorial contests and other events for crowds of up to 21,000 spectators.

€9

Roman Theatre of Arles

Roman Theatre of Arles, also known as the Arles Amphitheater, is a monument in Arles, France. With a seating capacity for up to 20,000 spectators, it hosted various events such as bullfighting, concerts, and plays. Along with other historic monuments in Arles like the cathedral and necropolis, this amphitheater showcases the town's transition from Roman to medieval civilization. The memorial stands in an open plaza or roadside setting.

Free (cloister €6)

Saint-Trophime Primatial Catholic Church

Saint-Trophime Primatial Catholic Church is a religious building in Arles, France. Saint-Trophime Primatial Catholic Church is a massive Romanesque-style church adorned with sculptures on its facade and an array of art inside. The church's carved portal has attracted curious visitors for centuries, along with the city's Roman monuments. The exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside service times.

Free

Arles Obelisk

Tarascon Castle

Tarascon Castle, also known as Chateau de Tarascon, is a fortified historic site in Arles, France. Tarascon Castle, also known as Chateau de Tarascon, is a well-preserved 15th-century fortress located on the banks of the Rhône River in the village of Tarascon sur Rhone. Walls, courtyards, or towers form the main visitor route when the grounds are open.

€8

Beaucaire Castle

Beaucaire Castle is a striking medieval fortress that overlooks the town from its position on sheer rocky cliffs. The well-maintained outer walls and interesting ramparts make for a cool exterior, but accessing the inside can be like an escape game with limited access. Climbing the stairs is worth it for the almost 360-degree view of the city.

Free

Roman Aqueduct of Barbegal

Roman Aqueduct of Barbegal is a visitor site in Arles, France. Aqueduc Romain de Barbegal is a well-preserved section of the Roman aqueduct network located near the Alpilles mountains, northeast of Arles. This ancient water source was important for the Romans, who built an extensive aqueduct system to supply water to the city. The main structures and grounds are the principal points of interest.

Free

Moulin De Daudet

Moulin De Daudet in Fontvieille, less than 30 minutes from Arles, is a windmill named after the writer Daudet. The landscape surrounding the isolated windmill offers an stroll and a glimpse of nature at its. Visitors can combine the visit with a hike through the nearby pine forest or explore other villages and attractions in the area.

Free

Langlois Bridge

Langlois Bridge, also known as Van Gogh's Bridge, is a spot on the edge of Arles that Vincent van Gogh captured in five paintings during his stay in the city. The original bridge painted by Van Gogh has been well-preserved and can be visited by following his traces throughout the region.

€5

Alyscamps

Alyscamps is a tree-lined path in Arles, France, adorned with sarcophagi on both sides leading to a Roman cemetery featuring a 12th-century church. The site is part of the historic urban fabric of Arles and includes Roman monuments dating back to the 1st century BC and 1st century AD. It is one of eight Roman and medieval monuments in the city, showing the adaptation of a Roman town to medieval European civilization.

€9

LUMA Arles, Parc des Ateliers

A contemporary art center featuring innovative exhibitions in a unique architectural setting designed by Frank Gehry. It remains a notable local point of interest situated in Arles, France. Visitors can observe the surrounding area and its connection to the city's broader historical landscape. Local guides sometimes include it on walking routes through the centre.
