Explore Ile de Re

A Brief History

Ile de Re was visited by Ligurian and Iberian salt traders before medieval Aquitanian monastic communities worked the south-coast salt pans from the 12th century onward. Richelieu placed the island under royal protection in 1627 to guard La Rochelle, and Vauban's engineers built the 14-kilometre encircling ramparts, the Fort de la Pre, and the citadel of Saint-Martin-de-Re between 1681 and 1690. Twentieth-century oyster farms replaced the salt pans after the latter closed in the 1930s, leaving the whitewashed villages and 100 kilometres of cycle paths that anchor today's visitor circuit.

Phare des Baleines

The Phare des Baleines is a 57-metre 1854 lighthouse on the western edge of Ile de Re, replacing the original 1684 tower whose smaller limestone structure survives next door. The current cylindrical tower was designed by Leonce Reynaud and completed under Napoleon III to extend the light range across the Pertuis d'Antioche, with 257 spiral steps to a circumferent balcony. Travellers pay EUR5 per adult, ascend the balcony, and continue to the on-site museum documenting both towers.

€6

Fort de la Pree

The Fort de la Pre is a 17th-century Vauban-era star fort on the northern coast of Ile de Re, built between 1681 and 1685 as part of the island's 14-kilometre encircling rampart system. The pentagonal compound retains the original curtain walls, gun casemates, an outer moat, and the east-facing Royal Gate bridge. Travellers pay EUR5 per adult, cross the moat bridge, and trace the rampart walk through four surviving gun emplacements.

Free

Saint-Martin-de-Re Centre

Saint-Martin-de-Re Centre is the walled Vauban port town and main commercial centre of Ile de Re, enclosed within an 8-hectare bastioned layout dating from 1685 and encircled by tidal water. The town holds the 17th-century church of Saint-Martin, the Cloitre des Recollets convent, and a harbour promenade dominated by a stork migration colony on the outer walls. Travellers enter via the Porte des Campanistes and walk the arcaded central streets.
