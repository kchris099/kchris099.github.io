Destination Guide — Lyon

Explore Lyon

A Brief History

Lyon developed from the Roman colony of Lugdunum, founded around 43 BC at the confluence of the Rhône and Saône. It later became a major ecclesiastical seat, silk-weaving center, and industrial hub, with traboules—hidden passage-ways—linking Renaissance merchant quarters. Hillside basilicas, riverfront civic buildings, and preserved Roman theatres show the long continuity of urban life.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Basilica of Notre Dame of Fourvière

The Basilica of Fourvière was built in the late 19th century on the hill that overlooks Lyon's old city. Its hilltop position links modern pilgrimage architecture with the historic Fourvière ridge, where Roman Lugdunum once stood. The church and terrace offer wide views over the confluence of the Rhône and Saône.

Free

Théâtre Gallo Romain

The Roman theaters on Fourvière preserve the remains of Lugdunum, the Roman capital of Gaul. The larger theater and adjacent odeon reflect the scale of urban and cultural life on the hill during the imperial period. The site is part of the Lugdunum museum network and can be visited without charge.

Free

Hôtel de Ville de Lyon

Lyon's city hall was built in the 17th century on Place des Terreaux and rebuilt in part after a fire in the 19th century. Its classical facade and courtyard remain a civic landmark at the political center of the city, facing the square where public festivals, markets, and municipal events still take place.

Free

Lyon Zoo

Lyon Zoo opened in the Parc de la Tête d'Or and forms part of a larger 19th-century urban park developed by the city. The zoo is integrated into the public green space rather than operating as a separate gated complex, and visitors can walk the park paths and animal enclosures without an admission fee.

Free

Porte des Enfants du Rhône

The Porte des Enfants du Rhône is one of the main ornamental entrances to the Parc de la Tête d'Or. It reflects the 19th-century design of Lyon's major urban park and its formal approach from the city. The gate marks the transition from boulevard traffic to the lawns, lake, and paths of the park interior.
