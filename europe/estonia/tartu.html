Tartu

Tartu emerged as a medieval trading hub and joined the Hanseatic League in the thirteenth century, but its defining historical turn came when Swedish King Gustavus Adolphus founded its university in 1632. The city evolved into the intellectual center of the region, fostering the Estonian national awakening in the nineteenth century even while under Russian imperial rule. Despite heavy destruction during World War II, Tartu rebuilt its academic heart. Visitors can experience this intellectual heritage on Toome Hill, the classicist University Main Building, and the ruined Cathedral.

Ruins of Tartu Cathedral

Also housing the University of Tartu Museum, the cathedral ruins keep stone walls that once separated the bishop's residence from the town. Exterior and main interior spaces of the ruin complex are usually viewable outside service times. The broken vaults mark Toome Hill as both worship remnant and campus museum.

Cathedral Hill Park

Cathedral Hill Park, located in Tartu, Estonia, holds historical significance as the site of the ancient Tarbatu Fortress and the medieval Tartu Cathedral. The cathedral, dating back to the 13th-16th century, has endured plunder and raids over time. Visitors can enjoy views from this park and engage in various outdoor activities such as off-road longboarding and cycling, there are recreational amenities like an outdoor pingpong table.

Tartu Town Hall Square

Tartu Town Hall Square centers on the town hall facade and the leaning Neitsitorn statue fountain among classical frontages. Paved open ground hosts markets and gatherings in Estonia's university city. Surrounding buildings from the reconstruction eras after fires frame the square as Tartu's civic living room.

Skulptuur "Suudlevad tudengid" / 'The Kissing Students' sculpture and fountain

Mati Karmin's Kissing Students sculpture has stood in Tartu Town Hall Square since 1998 above a circular fountain. The embracing figures refer to the city's large student population and the social life surrounding the University of Tartu; water, umbrellas, paving, cafes, and the town hall facade shape the setting, while seasonal decorations periodically alter the square.

05 AdmissionFree (tower €3)

St. John Church, Tartu

Hundreds of medieval terracotta figures and fragments distinguish St John's Church in Tartu from other Baltic brick Gothic buildings. The church developed mainly in the fourteenth century, suffered wartime destruction and underwent a long reconstruction completed in the early twenty-first century; a tower platform overlooks the old town, while the restored nave supports Lutheran worship, concerts and interpretation of the sculptures.

St. Paul's Church

St Paul's Church in Tartu is a red-brick Lutheran hall church with a plain interior centred on the pulpit and altarpiece. The exterior mass dominates its neighbourhood streets. Outside services the nave stays open for a short look at the restrained Protestant fittings.

Tagurpidi Maja (Peapeal OÜ)

Furniture, floors and household objects appear fixed to the ceiling inside Tagurpidi Maja, an intentionally inverted house near the Estonian National Museum. Sloping surfaces and altered visual cues create balance illusions as visitors move through its rooms. Staged settings encourage playful photographs, while the unusual angles make the building itself the central experience.

08 AdmissionFree

Raadi manor

Raadi Manor, once seat of the von Liphart family, survives mainly as ruins beside the Estonian National Museum on Tartu's edge. War damage left empty walls and cellars among park trees, so the site reads as a fragmentary estate rather than a furnished house museum.

58.3993° N / 26.7407° E

09 AdmissionFree

Peetri kirik

Peetri kirik, a church located just across the river from Tartu's historic old town, is a gem of Gothic European architecture that has stood for around 150 years. Visitors often rave about its beauty and the warm atmosphere created by the friendly community. While some have noted that it could benefit from a fresh coat of paint, this does not detract from its charm.

58.3902° N / 26.7284° E

Aura Waterpark

Aura Waterpark in central Tartu combines a competition pool, teaching areas, slides, children's pools, saunas and a health club under one roof. School swimming, exercise, recreation and spa use share the municipal aquatic center, with age, height, supervision and lane-programming rules determining access to individual sections.
