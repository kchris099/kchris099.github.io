Caltagirone

Caltagirone has produced pottery since antiquity on a hill between Catania and Gela, with Arab and Norman rulers fostering kilns that supplied tiles across Sicily. The seventeenth-century Staircase of Santa Maria del Monte, lined with decorated risers, links the lower town to the upper after earthquake damage. Baroque churches and ceramic workshops express how a craft town rebuilt in the Val di Noto while keeping majolica at the centre of its economy and civic festivals.

01 AdmissionFree

Staircase of Santa María del Monte

A sequence of 142 steps climbs through Caltagirone's historic center to Santa Maria del Monte. The stairway was laid out in the seventeenth century and later faced with ceramic risers, each carrying a different motif drawn from Sicilian decorative traditions. During seasonal displays, flowers or lights emphasize the long perspective, but the permanent attraction remains the town's tile workmanship.

02 AdmissionFree

Cathedral of Saint Julian

Its present exterior includes an early twentieth-century facade and a blue-tiled dome, while the interior preserves devotional art dedicated to Saint Julian. Caltagirone Cathedral occupies Piazza Umberto I on a site associated with Norman-era worship but repeatedly rebuilt after earthquakes. Changes in style reflect the city's long recovery from seismic damage.

37.2373° N / 14.5129° E

03 AdmissionFree

Giardino Pubblico

Designed during the nineteenth century, Caltagirone's Giardino Pubblico occupies terraces near the historic center with broad paths, shaded planting, fountains, and views across the valley. Architectural details and ceramic decoration connect the grounds with the town's craft tradition. The name commonly refers to the Villa Comunale.

37.2322° N / 14.5175° E

04 AdmissionFree

Public Garden Villa Comunale

Across terraces near Caltagirone's old town, the Villa Comunale combines shaded paths with fountains, planted beds and ceramic decoration. Laid out during the nineteenth century, its balustrades and elevated walks frame views toward the valleys below, connecting a formal public garden with the pottery tradition seen throughout the city's architecture and street design.

37.2335° N / 14.5155° E
