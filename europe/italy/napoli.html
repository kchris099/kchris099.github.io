Napoli

Naples, or Napoli, was founded as a Greek settlement Neapolis and later became a Roman resort town with nearby Pompeii and Herculaneum. Angevin, Aragonese, and Spanish viceroys ruled the Kingdom of Naples from the Middle Ages to 1860. The historic center contains castles, churches, and underground Roman remains. UNESCO inscribed the centro storico as a World Heritage site in 1995.

Mount Vesuvius

Mount Vesuvius is an active somma-stratovolcano above the Bay of Naples whose 79 AD eruption buried Pompeii and Herculaneum. A national park wraps the cone, with trails that climb from a road entrance near 1,000 meters to the crater rim. Guides and monitoring stations keep watch on seismic and gas readings.

Castel Nuovo

Charles I of Anjou began Castel Nuovo in 1279 as a royal seat beside Naples' port, later known as Maschio Angioino under Aragonese and Spanish viceroys. Five round towers and a triumphal arch frame the entrance. Interior museum rooms and a Renaissance chapel cover Neapolitan political history.

Castel dell'Ovo

Castel dell'Ovo occupies the islet of Megaride, where ancient Greek settlement preceded a medieval fortress and royal residence. Norman and Angevin rulers developed the stronghold, followed by Aragonese and later military alterations. Walls and terraces now frame views across Naples' harbor, while the castle's name comes from a medieval legend connected with the poet Virgil.

San Carlo Theatre

Opened in 1737 beside Naples' Royal Palace, Teatro di San Carlo predates many of Europe's major surviving opera houses. Fire led to a rapid reconstruction in 1816, preserving the horseshoe plan while renewing the lavish red-and-gold auditorium; opera, ballet and orchestral seasons continue inside and guided visits usually explain the royal box, foyers, stage and acoustic design.

Archaeological Park of Herculaneum

Herculaneum was buried by volcanic surges from Vesuvius in 79 CE, preserving timber, upper floors, food remains and household objects rarely found at Roman sites. Excavated streets reveal compact houses, baths and shops below the modern town of Ercolano. Shrines and former waterfront structures add further detail to a separate archaeological park near Naples.

Underground Naples

Greek quarrying, Roman aqueducts, wartime shelters, cisterns, and later service spaces lie beneath central Naples. 'Napoli Sotterranea' commonly refers to a guided route entered near Piazza San Gaetano, but other operators lead through different underground systems. Narrow passages and stairs reveal how the city repeatedly reused excavated tuff.

In Pozzuoli the so-called Temple of Serapis is a Roman macellum, or covered market, whose columns show marine borings from past bradyseismic flooding. San Giovanni a Carbonara

San Giovanni a Carbonara rose in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries as an Augustinian church closely linked with the Angevin court. The interior holds the monumental tomb of King Ladislaus, the Caracciolo del Sole chapel, sculpture, fresco fragments, and layered Renaissance additions. A stairway lifts the church above the street near Naples' former eastern edge.
