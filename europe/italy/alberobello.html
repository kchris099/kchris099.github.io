Alberobello

Alberobello arose in the sixteenth century on the Murge plateau when the Counts of Conversano ordered dry-stone trulli so the settlement could be dismantled to avoid royal taxes on permanent towns. Conical roofs and whitewashed walls became permanent after Bourbon reforms in the eighteenth century, supporting wine and almond cultivation. The Rione Monti and Aia Piccola quarters now preserve one of Apulia's most distinctive vernacular landscapes above the Itria Valley roads to Bari and Taranto.

Trullo Sovrano

Near the Basilica dei Santi Medici, Trullo Sovrano rises as a rare two-story trullo among Alberobello's single-chamber conical houses. Interior rooms show how the dry-stone form could expand beyond a one-room dwelling. The house museum explains local building methods and the district's residential history.

Casa D'Amore

Casa D'Amore was built in 1797 after Alberobello gained royal recognition that ended local restrictions on mortar-built houses. Francesco D'Amore used lime and mortar in a transitional two-storey home near the main square, marking a legal and architectural change from dry-stone trulli. Terracotta roofing and conventional walls distinguish it from the conical dwellings nearby.

Alberobello - Rione Monti

Rione Monti climbs a slope in Alberobello through hundreds of whitewashed trulli, narrow lanes, shops, small houses, and the trullo-shaped Church of Sant'Antonio. Heavy commercial use mixes with surviving residential fabric inside the property. The district's density and roofscape explain its significance, while individual souvenir interiors may reveal little about traditional domestic life.

Trullo Siamese

Two adjoining conical roofs give Trullo Siamese its modern name within Alberobello's Rione Monti. The unusual dry-stone building has separate entrances and a divided plan often explained through an unverified family story. It now contains commercial activity, but the joined structure and construction method are more dependable subjects than repeating a precise fifteenth-century date without evidence.

Zoosafari Fasanolandia

Zoosafari Fasanolandia combines a drive-through animal park with walk-through enclosures and an amusement area. Large mammals, primates, reptiles and birds are kept in managed habitats. Rides and seasonal entertainment occupy a separate section of the grounds. The privately run complex brings wildlife display and family recreation together within one commercial attraction.

Basilica di San Martino

In Martina Franca's historic center stands the Basilica di San Martino, a Baroque masterpiece dating back to the 18th century. The basilica has an detailed exterior facade adorned with elaborate carvings and a bas-relief of San Martino on horseback sharing his cloak with a beggar. Inside, travellers are treated to an elegant interior featuring frescoes and elaborate architecture housing important artworks by local painters.

Palazzo Ducale

Palazzo Ducale faces the civic heart of Alberobello as the town's former ducal residence and later municipal seat. Stone facades and reception rooms recall the Acquaviva family's control of the settlement. The building still anchors local administration beside the main square and parish churches.

Villa Garibaldi

A public garden near the town center offers planted beds and lawns for everyday walks, with decorative lighting that draws evening crowds at Christmas from the trulli streets. Paths circle the open green. The garden works as a civic pause among Alberobello's denser dry-stone neighborhoods rather than a private villa estate.

Grotta del Trullo - Putignano

Grotta del Trullo, located just outside of Putignano in Italy, is a fascinating cave system that was discovered by chance around 80 years ago. The entrance to the cave is through a trullo and travellers can explore the different caves using a deep metallic staircase. This cave was once used as a temple to honor Athena, the goddess. Travellers can enjoy guided tours with knowledgeable guides who provide clear explanations in both English and Italian.

40.8571° N / 17.1100° E

Grotte di Castellana

Discovered in 1938 about 40 kilometers southeast of Bari, the Grotte di Castellana form a long limestone cave system open on guided routes. The tour path extends roughly 3.2 kilometers and descends more than 70 meters among stalactites and stalagmites. Chambers named for their shapes structure the standard underground itinerary.
