Pienza

Pienza was rebuilt from 1459 when humanist Pope Pius II, born Enea Silvio Piccolomini, transformed his birthplace Corsignano into an ideal Renaissance city above the Orcia valley. Bernardo Rossellino aligned cathedral, papal palace, and municipal buildings around a trapezoidal piazza that embodied classical proportion. The project remained small in scale yet became a reference for planned towns across Italy as older village lanes survived within new civic geometry.

Cipressi di San Quirico d'Orcia

Two compact groups of cypress trees rise from cultivated fields near San Quirico d'Orcia, creating a familiar landmark in the Val d'Orcia landscape. Their exact planting history is not securely documented, despite many repeated local stories. Roadside viewpoints show the dark trees against rolling clay hills.

02 AdmissionFree

Chapel Vitaleta

Cypress trees and cultivated fields surround the small Cappella della Madonna di Vitaleta south of San Quirico d'Orcia. The present stone chapel was rebuilt in the nineteenth century on a site associated with an older Marian devotion. Its restrained facade, isolated rural setting and relationship to the surrounding Val d'Orcia landscape explain why the chapel appears frequently in views of the Tuscan countryside.

Horti Leonini

Geometric box hedges below and a wilder upper grove preserve the garden's original dual plan. The layout opens from Pienza's main square toward the town walls. Paths between the clipped compartments lead walkers from civic pavement into a more open green edge.

43.0586° N / 11.6045° E

04 AdmissionFree

Sanctuary of the Madonna di San Biagio

Antonio da Sangallo the Elder designed San Biagio below Montepulciano as a centralized Renaissance church built from warm travertine. Construction began in the early sixteenth century near a revered image of the Virgin. A Greek-cross plan, balanced towers, and a prominent dome stand apart from the dense hill town, with cypress-lined roads and vineyards surrounding the sanctuary.

43.0908° N / 11.7746° E

05 AdmissionFree

Porta al Prato

At Montepulciano's northern edge, Porta al Prato marks the start of the long uphill route through the historic center. The arched passage has medieval origins with later alterations and opens onto Via di Gracciano nel Corso; from here, streets rise past Renaissance palaces and churches toward Piazza Grande at the summit.

43.0969° N / 11.7851° E

06 AdmissionFree

Piazza Pio II

Piazza Pio II is the trapezoidal square laid out when Pope Pius II rebuilt Corsignano as the ideal Renaissance town of Pienza. The cathedral of Santa Maria Assunta and Palazzo Piccolomini face the paved space with other civic fronts from the 1450s–60s. Travertine paving and coordinated facades show the planned fifteenth-century ensemble.

43.0765° N / 11.6789° E

07 AdmissionFree

Pienza Cathedral

Pienza Cathedral, also known as the Duomo of Pienza, is a church that combines Gothic and Renaissance architectural elements. The travertine facade is a masterpiece of Renaissance style, featuring three portals and a tympanum with the coat of arms of Pius II. Inside, the cathedral maintains a Gothic influence with three naves and an apse divided into chapels.

43.0761° N / 11.6789° E

08 AdmissionFree

Centro Storico Pienza

In the Tuscan hills, Centro Storico Pienza is a historic village that draws travellers with its architecture and local history. As wander through its quaint streets, discover churches, intriguing museums, and shops. The centerpiece of this enchanting town is the Palazzio Pio, where Pope Pius once resided, offering a glimpse into its storied past.

43.0766° N / 11.6796° E

Agriturismo Poderuccio

Estate meals here draw on farm produce and local wines served on the property. The rural setting gives a base for visiting hill towns without lodging inside Pienza's walls. Fields and farm buildings surround the stay, keeping the pace slower than the tourist streets above.

10 AdmissionFree

Corsignano

Pieve di Corsignano is a Romanesque parish church just outside Pienza, with fabric often linked to early medieval origins and a twelfth-century rebuilding. Decorated portals, a cylindrical campanile, and carved details mark the stone exterior. The church is regarded as the settlement nucleus from which later Pienza grew.

43.0771° N / 11.6713° E
