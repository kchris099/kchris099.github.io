Matera

Matera’s Sassi districts are cave dwellings carved into calcareous ravines and inhabited since prehistoric times, later forming dense Christian neighbourhoods with rock churches. By the mid-20th century authorities emptied many caves as unfit housing; residents moved to new quarters. Restoration from the 1980s and UNESCO listing returned life and lodging to the stone. Rock chapels and stair-streets now present Matera as a recovered cave city of Basilicata.

Cathedral Basilica of Matera “Most Holy Mary of the Bruna”

Cattedrale di Maria Santissima della Bruna e Sant'Eustachio rose in the thirteenth century on Piazza Duomo, high on the ridge between Matera's two sassi districts. A tall bell tower and richly finished interior mark the cathedral against the cave-house skyline. From the square, the view drops across stone roofs toward the ravine.

02 AdmissionFree

Church of Saint Peter 'Barisano'

Rock-cut chambers descend beneath San Pietro Barisano, a large rupestrian church in Matera's Sassi. Medieval builders enlarged earlier caves into naves, chapels, crypts, and burial spaces, while later masonry formed the facade above the ravine. Looting removed many fittings in the twentieth century, but surviving frescoes and carved volumes still explain the church's long use.

40.6688° N / 16.6093° E

03 AdmissionFree

Convent of Saint Agostino

The Convent of Saint Agostino, located at the northern end of the Sassi, was founded in 1592 by the Eremitani monks. The complex suffered damage from an earthquake in 1734 and underwent various uses during the nineteenth century. Today, it houses offices of the Ministry for local history and Activities.

40.6700° N / 16.6100° E

04 AdmissionFree

Chiesa di San Giovanni Battista

Carved portals and later furnishings survive in the nave and side chapels of this medieval stone church. It belongs to the cliff-edge group that forms Matera's older religious skyline above the ravine. The approach meets the facade before the full height of the valley wall comes into view.

40.6677° N / 16.6079° E

05 AdmissionFree

Long Cistern

The Long Cistern under Matera's Sasso Caveoso is a nineteenth-century reservoir dug about 15 meters deep to store roughly 5,000 cubic meters of drinking water. Vaulted rock chambers show how the cave city managed scarce supply. Guided access reveals the scale of the underground hydraulic works.

40.6669° N / 16.6065° E

06 AdmissionFree

Crypt of Original Sin

Crypt of Original Sin is a unique cave church featuring frescoes from the 8th and 9th centuries depicting biblical scenes and angels. Accessing the site can be a bit challenging, with limited lighting and some language barriers for English speakers. Travellers have noted that the audio devices for English speakers may not be of high quality, and some paintings are missing or deteriorated. It's recommended to make reservations in advance, as access may require prior arrangements.

40.6205° N / 16.5632° E

07 AdmissionFree

Belvedere di Piazza Giovanni Pascoli

Piazzetta Pascoli sits between Matera's districts and opens a wide view across the cave-house slopes. From the terrace the scale of the sassi becomes easier to read than from streets buried among the dwellings. Short walks from surrounding lanes reach the railing without a long climb.

40.6633° N / 16.6103° E

08 AdmissionFree

Sasso Caveoso

Sasso Caveoso spreads across Matera's southern ravine in terraces of cave dwellings, stone facades, alleys, and rock churches. Unlike a single museum, the district is a lived urban landscape shaped by centuries of excavation and construction; views from upper lanes reveal how roofs become streets and how homes merge with the limestone slope above the Gravina canyon.

40.6635° N / 16.6118° E

09 AdmissionFree

Chiesa di Santa Lucia alle Malve

Benedictine women established Santa Lucia alle Malve in Matera by the early Middle Ages, carving worship and monastic rooms into the rock of Sasso Caveoso. Frescoes from different periods survive on the uneven walls, including figures of saints and the Virgin. The small complex records female religious life in the cave district before the convent moved elsewhere in the city.

40.6633° N / 16.6125° E

10 AdmissionFree

Church of Saint Mary of Idris

Cut into a rocky hilltop, this small Catholic church keeps a rough interior that was never fully restored. Photography is not allowed during visits. Outside service times the exterior and principal spaces remain viewable, giving a brief look at how Christian worship adapted to Matera's living rock.

40.6641° N / 16.6120° E
