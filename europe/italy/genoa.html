Genoa

Genoa was a maritime republic whose merchants and bankers financed expeditions across the Mediterranean from the 11th century onward. Christopher Columbus was born here, and the city's palazzi dei rolli, rebuilt port, and narrow caruggi record republican government, Spanish Habsburg rule, and 19th-century industrial growth along the Ligurian coast.

Acquario di Genova

The Acquario di Genova occupies the Porto Antico waterfront redeveloped for the 1992 Expo with architecture linked to Renzo Piano's harbor renewal. Large tanks present Mediterranean and ocean species inside a long exhibition hall by the old piers. The aquarium anchors the promenade between the ferry terminal and the historic port.

Palazzo San Giorgio

Palazzo San Giorgio dates to 1290 on Genoa's harbor edge, later covered with exterior frescoes of Saint George and civic allegories. The building housed the Bank of Saint George and maritime administration for centuries. Its painted facade still faces the Porto Antico near the aquarium and ferry docks.

Cattedrale di San Lorenzo

Located Genoa, the Cattedrale di San Lorenzo is a example of Romanesque architecture, characterized by its striking black-and-white striped façade. This cathedral, constructed between the 12th and 14th centuries, has an interior adorned with frescoes and houses unique holy relics that draw visitors from all over. As visitors stroll along Via San Lorenzo, encounter this landmark flanked by two majestic lions at its entrance.

Porto Antico

Genoa's Porto Antico occupies the old harbor edge where warehouses, quays and maritime activity were reshaped for the 1992 Columbus celebrations. Renzo Piano's redevelopment introduced public promenades and cultural facilities while retaining views of cranes, ferries and the historic waterfront; the aquarium, maritime museum and restored port buildings sit within a district that remains tied to active shipping.

Basilica della Santissima Annunziata del Vastato

Located Genoa, the Basilica della Santissima Annunziata del Vastato is a evidence of 16th-century architecture and artistry. This historic Catholic cathedral has an opulent interior adorned with detailed frescoes and gilded details that transport visitors into a world of divine beauty. The grand vaulted ceilings are held aloft by pink and white marble columns, creating an atmosphere of awe While exploring its sacred spaces.

Piazza De Ferrari

A circular bronze fountain marks Piazza De Ferrari at the junction of Genoa's historic and nineteenth-century districts. Palazzo Ducale, Teatro Carlo Felice, former financial buildings, and arcaded facades frame the open space. The square's scale and transport connections make it a common setting for civic gatherings, while adjoining streets lead quickly into the narrow lanes of the old city.

Carlo Felice Theater

Genoa's Teatro Carlo Felice opened in 1828 as the city's main opera house beside Piazza De Ferrari. Wartime bombing destroyed much of the original auditorium, and a late twentieth-century reconstruction placed a modern performance hall behind surviving exterior elements. Opera, ballet, orchestral concerts, and recitals continue the building's musical role within the civic center.

Porta Soprana

Two round towers flank Porta Soprana, the principal surviving gate from Genoa's twelfth-century defensive walls. Pointed arches and stone masonry mark the route once approaching from the east, near the cloister of Sant'Andrea and the house traditionally linked to Christopher Columbus. Restoration in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries emphasized its medieval appearance after long domestic use.

Natural Area Parco delle Mura

Parco delle Mura protects about 617 hectares of forested mountain slopes around Genoa's historic fortifications. Trails link old city walls, forts, and ridge viewpoints above the harbor. The park forms a green belt where hikers follow military roads through mixed woodland.

Piazza della Vittoria

Piazza della Vittoria opens south of Genoa's center around the Arco della Vittoria, completed in the 1930s to commemorate residents killed in the First World War. Colonnades, office buildings, lawns, and a large planted slope frame the broad traffic square. Floral designs on the embankment often form the name of the city, visible from the central axis.

Belvedere Castelletto

Long staircases and a public elevator climb to this hillside terrace above Genoa's harbour streets. From the railing the rooftops drop toward the port and open sea, a perspective hard to grasp from the waterfront alone. After dark the city lights spread below the same overlook, so many locals time the walk for evening rather than midday.

Villetta Di Negro

Villetta Di Negro is a hillside park above Genoa's center, rising toward the Castelletto belvedere with terraces and shaded paths. Multiple lookouts open over the dense historic quarters and the port. The grounds also hold the Museo d'Arte Orientale Edoardo Chiossone within the former villa setting.

Piazza Corvetto

Between central Genoa and the road toward the eastern districts, Piazza Corvetto forms a landscaped traffic circle around an equestrian monument to Victor Emmanuel II. Neoclassical buildings and the entrance to Villetta Di Negro define its edges. The square's ramps and connecting streets reveal how the nineteenth-century city expanded beyond the dense medieval lanes below.

Boccadasse Beach

Boccadasse Beach in Genoa is a small and cove with a cobblestone shore, offering a unique and setting. The warm waters are perfect for a relaxing dip, and the evening vibe adds to its special atmosphere. Surrounded by colorful houses, this beach exudes charm and history. It's an intimate spot with cozy restaurants nearby, making it ideal for enjoying a meal with a view.

Parchi Storici di Nervi

The historic parks of Nervi stitch together former villa gardens along Genoa's eastern seafront into a continuous promenade of palms, cliffs, and clipped lawns. Paths run between the Anita Garibaldi walkway and museum villas facing the Ligurian Sea. The ensemble remains a public green corridor outside the dense centro.
