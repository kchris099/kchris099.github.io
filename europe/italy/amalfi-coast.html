Amalfi Coast

Amalfi was a maritime republic that rivalled Genoa and Pisa in the early Middle Ages, earning wealth from Mediterranean trade. After political decline, terraced lemon villages clung to the cliffs until nineteenth- and twentieth-century elites made the coast a resort. Ravello's villas and Amalfi's cathedral square recall both republic and belle-époque tourism.

Amphitheatre of Pompeii

Pompeii's amphitheatre was built around 70 BC at the edge of the Roman city and survives as an early permanent stone arena. Elliptical seating, entrances, stairs and the arena floor accommodated gladiatorial spectacles for thousands of residents. The monument forms part of the Pompeii archaeological park, where conservation routes guide movement through the structure.

Garden of the Fugitives

Within Pompeii's former vineyard district, the Garden of the Fugitives preserves plaster casts of thirteen people who died during the eruption of Vesuvius in 79 CE. Excavators poured plaster into body-shaped voids left in compacted ash, recording clothing, posture, and final movements. The enclosure places the human cost of the disaster beside traces of cultivation and domestic life.

Teatro Grande

Cut into Pompeii's natural slope, the Teatro Grande accommodated several thousand spectators for drama, music and civic ceremonies. Its semicircular seating faces a stage complex rebuilt in Roman times from an earlier Hellenistic theater; surviving stairways, passages and sightlines explain how large audiences moved through the venue before the eruption of 79 CE.

The brothel of Pompeii

The Lupanare in Pompeii is a two-story brothel block with five small cells on each floor. Stone beds, erotic wall paintings, and graffiti record how the building was used before the AD 79 eruption. It sits on the excavated street grid a short walk from the forum and nearby bath complexes inside the archaeological park.

Archaeological Park of Pompeii

Excavations at Pompeii expose streets, houses, and public buildings buried by the 79 AD eruption of Vesuvius. Chance finds from 1599 later led to systematic digs that opened forums, baths, and painted rooms across the site. Stone paving, amphitheatre seating, and plaster casts of victims remain on the main walking routes.

Casa dei Vettii

Mythological frescoes and a reconstructed peristyle garden distinguish the House of the Vettii from Pompeii's many excavated residences. Two prosperous freedmen, Aulus Vettius Conviva and Aulus Vettius Restitutus, owned the property in an early century CE; reception rooms, service areas, painted panels, and garden sculpture reveal both elite display and household organization.

Forum of Pompeii

Foro di Pompei, located in the ancient city of Pompeii, offers a glimpse into Roman life. The well-preserved ruins include temples, municipal buildings, and the basilica at the social and civic center of the town. Buried under volcanic ash from Vesuvius' eruption in AD 79, visitors can wander through Roman streets and explore houses, shops, cafes, amphitheaters, and even a brothel.

Scavi di Pompei

Pompeii preserves streets, houses, baths, shrines, shops, theaters and government buildings from a Roman town buried by Vesuvius in 79 CE. Wall paintings, inscriptions and food vessels provide evidence of domestic life and trade. Voids in the ash were later used to make plaster casts of victims. Routes through different districts reveal sharp contrasts in wealth and work far beyond the forum and principal civic monuments.

Villa of the Mysteries

Villa dei Misteri, a Roman villa dating back to the 2nd century BCE, is located at the northwestern edge of Pompeii. The villa has well-preserved and richly decorated frescoes that depict the initiation of a young bride into the mysteries of the cult of Dionysus. These active frescoes, painted in glowing Pompeiian red, are found throughout many rooms, with the ones adorning the triclinium.

Arab-Norman and later medieval elements shape Villa Rufolo, a thirteenth-century residence overlooking Ravello's coast. Courtyards, towers, cloisters, and terraced gardens reflect additions made by successive owners; richard Wagner visited in 1880, and the sea-facing gardens later became closely associated with Ravello's summer music festival.

Amalfi Coast

Cliffside settlements, terraced lemon groves and narrow valleys define the Amalfi Coast between the Gulf of Naples and Salerno. Towns such as Amalfi, Ravello, Positano and Minori developed around maritime trade, religious foundations and steep agricultural land; ferries and the coastal road connect communities whose built form follows the limited ledges above the Tyrrhenian Sea.

Cathedral of St Andrew Apostle

The Cathedral of St Andrew Apostle, located in Amalfi, is a notable building that has undergone numerous renovations since its inception in the 9th century. Originally constructed by Duke Manson in 987, it was built adjacent to an earlier church on the remnants of a temple, symbolizing Amalfi's rise as a maritime power.

Villa Arianna

Built on the Varano hill above ancient Stabiae, Villa Arianna formed part of a group of Roman seaside estates buried in 79 CE; bourbon excavators removed many paintings, but the remaining plan still conveys the scale of elite coastal life. Rooms open toward terraces that once faced the Bay of Naples, while surviving frescoes show mythological figures, landscapes, and decorative architecture.

Cassa Armonica

Castellammare di Stabia's Cassa Armonica rises in Piazza Principe Umberto as an octagonal iron-and-glass bandstand completed in 1900, and restored after periods of neglect, the pavilion remains a recognizable piece of the town's Liberty-era civic architecture. Eugenio Cosenza designed its slender columns, decorative brackets, and raised platform for public music performances.

Medieval Castle - Castellammare di Stabia

Above the coast of Castellammare di Stabia, the medieval castle occupies a defensive position between Mount Faito and the Bay of Naples. Its fabric reflects repeated rebuilding from the Middle Ages onward, when the site watched routes approaching the harbor and Sorrento peninsula. The privately managed complex is generally appreciated from surrounding roads and viewpoints.

The Chiesa della Santissima Annunziata is a 14th-century Catholic church with Gothic architecture and a bell tower situated on a cliff overlooking the sea. Fiordo di Furore

Fiordo di Furore is a narrow coastal gorge where the Schiato stream cuts through limestone cliffs on the Amalfi Coast. A small beach sits at the waterline beneath an arched bridge that carries the coastal highway. Stairs and paths from the village drop into the cleft between the rock walls.

Grotta dello Smeraldo

Grotta dello Smeraldo, the Emerald Grotto, is a sea cave on the Amalfi Coast where filtered light turns the water green. Access is by boat or lift from the cliff road, depending on sea conditions. The chamber entered wider notice after exploration in the early 1930s and remains a short stop along the coast.

Path of the Lemons

Stone lanes and farm paths link Maiori and Minori along the Sentiero dei Limoni, crossing terraces cultivated with Amalfi Coast lemons. The route climbs above the shoreline through small hamlets, irrigation channels, and retaining walls built for steep agriculture; sections include stairs and uneven paving, while openings between groves reveal both towns and the coastal headlands.

Church of Saint Mary of the Assumption

Positano's tiled dome marks the Church of Santa Maria Assunta above the main beach. The present building reflects eighteenth-century rebuilding on the site of a medieval Benedictine foundation, and its interior centers on a Byzantine-style icon of the Virgin. A separate bell tower, majolica roof, and position among descending houses make the church a visual anchor for the village.
