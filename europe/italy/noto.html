Noto

Noto was rebuilt inland after the catastrophic earthquake of 1693 destroyed the old town on Mount Alveria, with architects Gagliardi and Sinatra laying out a baroque grid of churches, balconies, and civic staircases in golden limestone. The new capital of southern Sicily's Val di Noto expressed Spanish viceroys' taste for theatrical urban space. Almond and olive estates supported nobles and clergy who funded the rebuild, and the town remains one of Italy's most coherent late-baroque ensembles.

Riserva Naturale Cavagrande del Cassibile

A downhill trek of about an hour reaches the Laghetti di Cavagrande pools on the valley floor. Slippery rock makes sturdy boots essential on the descent. Along the path, butterflies and lizards appear among scrub, and the swimming basins wait at the bottom as the clear payoff.

02 AdmissionFree

Porta Reale o Ferdinandea

Porta Reale o Ferdinandea is a grand gate that welcomes travellers to Noto's Old Town. Built in the 18th century, it was named after King Ferdinand III of Sicily and is a symbol of the city's power and significance. The gate has two imposing towers on either side of a central arch, adorned with detailed relief sculptures and carved stone moldings.

36.8901° N / 15.0738° E

Villa Comunale Noto

Villa Comunale in Noto is the town's public garden, laid out with pathways, fountains, and Mediterranean plantings near the baroque center. Shade trees and benches serve residents between visits to churches and palaces. The garden offers a quiet green pause inside the rebuilt eighteenth-century town.

36.8893° N / 15.0749° E

04 AdmissionFree

Cattedrale di San Nicolò

Noto Cathedral dominates the upper side of Piazza Municipio with a broad staircase, twin towers, and pale limestone facade. Rebuilt after the 1693 earthquake, the church later suffered a major dome and roof collapse in 1996 before reopening after reconstruction; its relationship with Palazzo Ducezio across the square defines the ceremonial center of the planned Baroque town.

Palazzo Ducezio (Comune di Noto)

Palazzo Ducezio faces Noto Cathedral as the town hall, built in Sicilian Baroque forms inspired by seventeenth-century French palace design. A long porticoed facade was once planned as a ducal residence before civic use prevailed. Council chambers and reception rooms occupy the piano nobile above the square.

Chiesa di Santa Chiara, also linked to Santa Maria Assunta, is an eighteenth-century Baroque church by Rosario Gagliardi beside a former Benedictine monastery. An exterior terrace looks over Noto's limestone roofs. The oval interior and carved details follow Gagliardi's regional vocabulary.

Nicolaci Palace

Palazzo Nicolaci in Noto was a wealthy family's town residence and still shows furnished rooms to visitors. Outside, carved stone balconies along Via Nicolaci lean into the street with putti, horses, and masks in high Baroque relief. The contrast between that theatrical facade and the quieter interiors is what defines the stop on Noto's main grid.

36.8919° N / 15.0699° E

Riserva naturale orientata Oasi Faunistica di Vendicari

The Vendicari nature reserve protects coastal wetlands, dunes, and beaches south of Noto, important for migratory birds and Mediterranean habitats. Boardwalks and observation points overlook lagoons beside ruined tuna fisheries. Trails link bird hides with sandy coves along the Ionian shore.

36.7986° N / 15.0930° E

09 AdmissionFree

Spiaggia della Pizzuta

Spiaggia della Pizzuta is a sandy beach on the Noto coastline near the Vendicari reserve, backed by low dunes and Mediterranean scrub. Clear shallow water draws swimmers in the warm months. Access roads and paths from inland farm tracks reach the open shore away from denser resort strips.

36.8447° N / 15.1090° E

10 AdmissionFree

Church of Saint John Baptist

The Church of San Giovanni Battista belongs to Noto's Baroque rebuilding after the 1693 earthquake reshaped southeastern Sicily. Pale local stone and a raised approach respond to the sloping street plan, while the curving facade leads into an interior of altars and stucco. Its decorative program places the church within the reconstructed city's larger network of religious architecture.

36.9099° N / 15.1374° E
