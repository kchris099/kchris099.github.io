Venice

Venice grew from lagoon settlements in the 5th century into a maritime republic linking the Adriatic with the eastern Mediterranean through trade and naval power. The Arsenale shipyards and San Marco's basilica grew from mercantile government and artistic patronage on canals and tidal islands. Decline after the 16th century and Austrian rule preceded incorporation into Italy in 1866.

Palazzo Ducale

The Doge's Palace combined the doge's residence with councils, courts, and offices of the Venetian Republic in a Gothic complex largely of the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries. The Scala d'Oro, Senate halls, and Bridge of Sighs route show how the maritime state governed. It remains Venice's central civic monument beside San Marco.

Ponte di Rialto

The Rialto Bridge was completed in 1591 as the main stone crossing of the Grand Canal at Venice's historic commercial center. Its arcades and location near the market made it central to trade and daily movement within the city. Shops line the covered walkway, and the span remains one of the busiest pedestrian routes between San Marco and San Polo.

The Bridge of Sighs, completed in the early seventeenth century, links the Doge's Palace to the New Prison with an enclosed limestone corridor. Views from the canal below made it a recognized emblem of Venetian justice. Palace tours still cross the bridge between court rooms and cells.

Saint Mark's Square grew as Venice's ceremonial forecourt beside the basilica, campanile, and procuratie arcades. Cafés and open paving host daily crowds, state events, and periodic acqua alta flooding. It remains the republic's former public stage and the city's main gathering space.

Basilica di San Marco

Saint Mark's Basilica took shape from the 11th century as the state church of the Venetian Republic and shrine of Saint Mark. Its mosaics, domes, and marble surfaces reflect trade links with Byzantium and the eastern Mediterranean. The Pala d'Oro altarpiece and treasury hold relics and liturgical objects gathered through Venice's commercial networks.

Ca' Pesaro International Gallery of Modern Art

Ca' Pesaro rises from Venice's Grand Canal with a monumental Baroque facade begun by Baldassare Longhena for the Pesaro family; upper floors also contain the Museum of Oriental Art, creating two distinct collections within the historic residence. Biblioteca Nazionale Marciana

The Marciana Library grew from the humanist collections given to Venice in the 15th century and occupies Sansovino's library building on Saint Mark's Square. It remains one of Italy's major research libraries for manuscripts and early printed books. The reading rooms and exhibition spaces display maps, classical texts, and Renaissance book culture tied to Venetian scholarship.

Ettore Ferrari's bronze monument to Victor Emmanuel II stands on the Riva degli Schiavoni east of Piazza San Marco. Unveiled in 1887, it combines an equestrian figure with allegories of Venice under foreign rule and after unification; the sculptural group reflects late nineteenth-century national memory, while its waterfront setting places it among hotels, bridges, and heavy pedestrian traffic.

Arsenale di Venezia

High brick walls enclose the Venetian Arsenal, the state shipbuilding complex that equipped the republic's fleets for centuries. Founded in the Middle Ages and repeatedly expanded, it included docks, workshops, rope production, storage, and an organized labor force capable of rapid construction. Naval functions continue in parts, while Biennale exhibitions use selected buildings and access routes.

Jewish Quarter

Venice confined Jewish residents to the Ghetto Nuovo in 1516, creating the district from which the word 'ghetto' spread internationally. Limited land encouraged unusually tall buildings, while synagogues occupied upper floors behind restrained exteriors. The Ghetto Vecchio and Nuovissimo later expanded the area; museums, memorials, kosher businesses, and active congregations continue around Campo del Ghetto Nuovo.

Chiesa del Santissimo Redentore

Designed by the prominent Renaissance architect Andrea Palladio, Il Redentore was built in the 16th century as a votive offering to thank God for the end of a devastating plague. Located on the island of Giudecca, its classical white facade and luminous interior reflect Palladian ideals of harmony and proportion. It remains the focal point of the annual Festa del Redentore.
