Rome

Rome developed from Iron Age settlements on the Palatine and Capitoline hills into the capital of the Roman Republic and Empire, with forums, baths, and aqueducts still spread across the city. After the western empire fell, the papacy shaped medieval and Baroque Rome with churches, palaces, and pilgrim routes. Capture of Rome in 1871 made it capital of unified Italy, adding ministries, monuments, and boulevards.

Colosseum

The Colosseum was inaugurated in AD 80 under Titus and expanded under Domitian. Built as an amphitheater for spectacles, it later became a quarry, fortress, and archaeological monument. Its surviving arcades remain one of the clearest structures from imperial Rome.

Roman Forum

Temples, basilicas, arches, rostra and government buildings accumulated in the Roman Forum between the Capitoline and Palatine hills. Republican assemblies, imperial ceremonies, legal business, religious rites and public commemoration all shaped the valley; surviving monuments such as the Curia, Temple of Saturn and Arch of Septimius Severus make the area a layered civic landscape.

Trajan's Forum

The complex included a vast porticoed square, Basilica Ulpia, libraries, markets, and Trajan's Column, whose reliefs narrate the Dacian wars. Emperor Trajan dedicated the last and large of Rome's imperial forums in the early second century CE. Modern roads and buildings divide the remains, but excavated sections still show how the project cut into the Quirinal slope and extended Rome's ceremonial center.

Circus Maximus

The long valley between Rome's Palatine and Aventine hills once held the Circus Maximus, a vast venue for chariot racing and public spectacles; the open basin is now an archaeological landscape and event space whose length still conveys the ancient arena's scale. Repeated imperial rebuilding created seating, starting gates, monuments, and a central barrier, although little remains above ground.

Vatican Museums

Collections formed by successive popes fill the Vatican Museums, ranging from ancient Egyptian and Greco-Roman objects to Renaissance frescoes, tapestries, maps and modern religious art. The complex occupies several palaces, courtyards and purpose-built museum wings within Vatican City.

Teatro di Marcello

Julius Caesar began the theater later dedicated by Augustus to his nephew Marcellus in 13 or 11 BCE. Arcaded stone tiers held large audiences for drama and music, and the curved exterior influenced later Roman amphitheaters without being one itself; medieval fortification and Renaissance apartments were built into the upper structure, creating a rare combination of ruin and residence.

Three historical levels descend beneath Rome's Basilica of San Clemente. Moving downward traces the neighborhood's transformation from imperial houses and cult space to early Christian worship and a later monastic church.

Mouth of Truth

A carved marble face known as the Bocca della Verita rests in the portico of Santa Maria in Cosmedin; the ancient disk may have functioned as a drain cover or fountain element before medieval legend claimed it would bite the hand of a liar. Its fame grew through literature and film, while the church behind it preserves a medieval interior and tall Romanesque bell tower.
