Explore Rome

A Brief History

Rome developed from Iron Age settlements on the Palatine and Capitoline hills into the capital of the Roman Republic and Empire, leaving forums, baths, and aqueducts across the city. After the empire's western end, the papacy shaped medieval and Baroque Rome with churches, palaces, and pilgrim routes. Capture of Rome in 1871 made it capital of unified Italy, adding ministries, monuments, and boulevards.

Top Sights & Activities

Colosseum

The Colosseum was inaugurated in AD 80 under Titus and expanded under Domitian. Built as an amphitheater for spectacles, it later became a quarry, fortress, and archaeological monument. Its surviving arcades remain one of the clearest structures from imperial Rome.

€18

Roman Forum

The Roman Forum was the civic and ceremonial center of ancient Rome, containing temples, basilicas, arches, and senate buildings accumulated over many centuries. Today the archaeological area preserves the political landscape of the republic and empire between the Capitoline and Palatine hills.

Free

Trajan's Forum

Trajan's Forum was dedicated in AD 112 and formed the last and largest of the imperial fora. Designed with a large square, Basilica Ulpia, libraries, and Trajan's Column, it celebrated the emperor's Dacian campaigns and reshaped the political center of Rome.

Free (panorama elevator €17)

Monument to Victor Emmanuel II

The national monument to Victor Emmanuel II was built from 1885 to 1935 to honor the first king of unified Italy. Its marble terraces, stairways, and monumental setting between Piazza Venezia and the Capitoline slope became a defining feature of modern Rome.

€15

Lupa Capitolina

The Capitoline Wolf, or Lupa Capitolina, is associated with the legend of Romulus and Remus and became one of the main symbols of Rome. The bronze is displayed within the Capitoline Museums, where it is interpreted alongside the city's civic collections and classical sculpture.

Free

Piazza del Campidoglio

Piazza del Campidoglio was redesigned by Michelangelo in the 16th century for the Capitoline Hill, the ancient political high ground of Rome. Its trapezoidal layout, stairway approach, and palatial facades turned the hill into a civic setting for papal and municipal authority.

Free

Trevi Fountain

The Trevi Fountain was completed in the 18th century from designs by Nicola Salvi and Giuseppe Pannini at the end of an ancient aqueduct route. Its sculptural facade turned a water terminal into a major Baroque urban landmark in the dense street network of central Rome.

€7

Pantheon

The Pantheon was rebuilt under Hadrian in the 2nd century AD on the site of Agrippa's earlier temple. Its concrete dome and portico made it one of the most studied buildings of antiquity. Since the 7th century it has functioned as a Christian church.

Free

Basilica of San Giovanni in Laterano

San Giovanni in Laterano is the cathedral church of the Diocese of Rome and the ecclesiastical seat of the pope as bishop of Rome. Founded in the 4th century and rebuilt several times, it remains one of the principal basilicas of the Catholic Church in Rome.

Free

Circus Maximus

Circus Maximus was the largest chariot-racing venue in ancient Rome, stretched through the valley between the Palatine and Aventine hills. Although the track structures largely disappeared, the long open basin still shows the scale of the imperial entertainment ground.

Free

Piazza del Popolo

Piazza del Popolo developed around the northern gate of Rome and was redesigned in its present neoclassical form in the 19th century by Giuseppe Valadier. The square connects ceremonial routes, twin churches, and the ascent to the Pincian terrace in Rome.

Free

Spanish Steps

The Spanish Steps were completed in 1725 to connect Piazza di Spagna with the Trinita dei Monti church above. The broad stair became a meeting place and visual landmark within the urban sequence of streets, fountains, embassies, and commercial fronts in central Rome.

€18

Castel Sant'Angelo

Castel Sant'Angelo began as Hadrian's mausoleum in the 2nd century AD and was later turned into a fortress, papal refuge, prison, and museum. Its position beside the Tiber and the Vatican route gave it a military and ceremonial role across many centuries.

Free

Villa Doria Pamphili

Villa Doria Pamphili is a historic park in Rome, offering a serene escape from the bustling city. It was once an ancient noble Roman estate and now features nature trails, fountains, and open green spaces for travellers to enjoy. The park hosts various events, including open-air concerts and immersive experiences like Christmas World. This festive event takes guests on a journey through Christmas traditions around the globe, featuring activities, entertainment, and a daily Christmas parade.

Free

Janiculum Hill

Janiculum Hill, or Gianicolo as the locals call it, is a must-visit destination for anyone looking to soak in the beauty of Rome. Nestled just behind the charming Trastevere district, this historic hilltop terrace provides panoramic views that stretch across the cityscape. As you stroll along its lush walkways, be captivated by sights of ancient ruins and magnificent Baroque domes.

Free

Basilica of Santa Maria in Trastevere

Nestled in the vibrant district of Trastevere, the Basilica of Santa Maria in Trastevere is a for anyone exploring Rome. This grand Catholic church, one of the oldest in the city, dates back to the 3rd century and boasts 12th-century mosaics that adorn its lavish interior.

Free (dome climb €10)

Saint Peter’s Basilica

St. Peter's Basilica is a Late Renaissance church designed by a team of renowned architects, including Michelangelo and Carlo Maderno. It is considered the holiest church in Christendom and one of only four Major Basilicas in the world. The site forms part of Saint Peter’s Basilica's documented civic and cultural landscape within Italy, with archives and visitor information available from municipal or regional heritage authorities.

Free

La Pietà di Michelangelo

The Pietà is a renowned 15th-century sculpture created by Michelangelo that can be found inside St. Peter's Basilica. This masterpiece depicts the Virgin Mary holding the lifeless body of Jesus after his crucifixion and is considered to be a revered piece of religious art. Interestingly, this is the only work that Michelangelo ever signed.

Included with Vatican Museums (€20)

Sistine Chapel

The Sistine Chapel, nestled within the Vatican Museums, is a masterpiece that draws travellers from around the globe. Renowned for Michelangelo's 16th-century ceiling frescoes and the Last Judgment on its altar wall, this chapel represents a pinnacle of Renaissance artistry. Despite its unassuming exterior, inside lies an extraordinary collection of works by legendary artists like Botticelli and Perugino.

Free

St. Peter Square Obelisk

The St. Peter Square Obelisk is a magnificent granite structure that dates back to Ancient Egyptian times, standing proudly at the heart of Vatican City. This impressive monument, also known as the Flaminio obelisk, was transported to Rome in 10 BCE by Emperor Augustus as a symbol of his triumph over Anthony and Cleopatra. It is one of eight obelisks brought from Egypt and showcases the Romans' penchant for collecting artifacts from their vast empire.

€20

Vatican Museums

The Vatican Museums, or Musei Vaticani, are a treasure trove of artistic brilliance and historical artifacts that span centuries. This vast complex showcases masterpieces from the likes of Raphael and Michelangelo, alongside ancient treasures from Egypt, Greece, and Rome collected by popes over time.

€8

Baths of Caracalla

The Baths of Caracalla, located in the south of Rome near the Circus Maximus, are the ruins of a vast Roman thermal bath complex that was in use from around 216 A.D. to 537 A.D. This 11-hectare complex, inaugurated under Emperor Caracalla, was thermal establishments of the Roman Empire and could accommodate up to 1,600 people.

Free

Papal Basilica of Saint Mary Major

One of Rome's four major basilicas, known for its mosaics, beautiful interior, and important relics. The site forms part of Papal Basilica of Saint Mary Major's documented civic and cultural landscape within Italy, with archives and visitor information available from municipal or regional heritage authorities.

Free

Santa Prassede

Basilica di Santa Prassede, also known as Saint Praxedes, is a minor Catholic basilica in Rome that dates back to 822. The church features intricate mosaics and ancient frescoes. Within the basilica lies the San Zeno Chapel, a small chapel that is a significant pilgrimage site.

Free

Arch of Constantine

The Arch of Constantine, a 21-meter-high Roman structure with three arches adorned with intricate figures and battle scenes, was commissioned by the Roman Senate to celebrate Constantine the Great's triumph over Maxentius in AD 312. Situated between the Colosseum and the Roman Forum, this well-preserved monument is a popular photo spot in Rome. It marks the end of Via Sacra, an important procession route during the Roman Empire.

Free

Arch of Titus

The Arch of Titus, a magnificent triumphal arch located on the Via Sacra in Rome, stands as a testament to the military achievements of Emperor Titus. Erected in 81 AD by his brother Domitian shortly after Titus's passing, this architectural marvel commemorates the victory over Jerusalem during the Jewish War in 70 AD.

Free

Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi

Nestled in the heart of Rome, the Fontana dei Quattro Fiumi, or Four Rivers Fountain, is a 17th-century masterpiece that captures the essence of Italian artistry. Designed by the renowned Gian Lorenzo Bernini for Pope Innocent X in 1651, this magnificent fountain graces Piazza Navona with its grandeur.

Free

Church of Saint Louis of the French

The Church of St. Louis of the French, located near Piazza Navona in Rome, is the national church of France in Italy. Commissioned by Cardinal Giulio de' Medici in 1518, it is dedicated to St. Louis IX, King of France. The church features a travertine marble facade adorned with statues and motifs honoring French royalty. Inside, travellers can admire three famous Caravaggio paintings: "The Calling of St.".

Free

Villa Borghese

Villa Borghese is a sprawling urban park in Rome, featuring meticulously landscaped gardens, an artificial lake with a temple, and several villas that now house important museums. The park also includes an aviary, an orangery, and various other charming buildings and follies. It was designed in the English style by Scottish artist Jacob More for the Borghese family in the 1770s.

Free

Orologio ad acqua del Pincio

Located in the Villa Borghese Gardens in Rome, Orologio ad acqua del Pincio is an ornate water-powered clock dating back to 1867. Designed by Father Giovanni Battista Embriaco, this monument sits on a green islet at the center of a small lake and consists of four dials. While it may be easy to miss, the surrounding park offers plenty of picturesque spots and activities such as running and rollerblading lessons.

Free

Basilica Parrocchiale di Santa Maria del Popolo

Basilica Parrocchiale Santa Maria del Popolo is a must-visit in Rome, located in the popular Piazza del Popolo. This elegant basilica was restored by Bernini in the 1600s and boasts masterpieces by renowned artists such as Caravaggio and Raphael. The church's decorative richness is evident through the interventions of famous artists like Caravaggio, Raphael, Pinturicchio, and Bernini in its chapels.

Free

Largo di Torre Argentina

Largo di Torre Argentina is an archaeological site in Rome, known as the place where Julius Caesar was assassinated. It is also home to a colony of cats. The area is well-connected by trams, with tram number 8 taking travellers from Largo Argentina to Trastevere. travellers can use the Roma Pass for unlimited access to the transit system and entry to covered sites with discounts.

Free

Portico of Octavia

Nestled in the vibrant Jewish Ghetto, the Portico of Octavia stands as a remarkable remnant of ancient Roman architecture, dating back to the 2nd century B.C. Originally constructed by Quintus Caecilius Metellus after his triumph in Macedonia, this portico was later renovated by Emperor Augustus and dedicated to his sister Octavia.

Free

Teatro di Marcello

Teatro di Marcello, an ancient open-air theater in Rome, has a rich history dating back to Julius Caesar and Emperor Augustus. It is often mistaken for the Colosseum due to its resemblance but predates it by about 100 years. With a capacity of around 20,000 spectators, it was once the largest and most important theater in Ancient Rome. The ruins now serve as a venue for summer concerts and evening events from early June to October.

Free (underground levels €10)

Basilica of San Clemente

The Basilica of San Clemente is a unique church in Rome, known for its multi-layered history and decorations. Built over a pagan temple and Roman house, the present-day 12th-century church sits atop two older churches. Descending into the lower levels reveals a 4th-century basilica with Romanesque frescoes and even further down, excavated foundations of a 2nd-century Roman home with a shrine to the sun god Mithra.

€10

Catacombs of Domitilla

The Catacombs of Domitilla, located along the Appian Way in Rome, offer tours through 15km of underground tunnels. This ancient site features a sunken church and a second-century fresco of the Last Supper. With over 15,000 graves spanning more than 10 miles, it is one of Rome's largest and -preserved underground cemeteries.

Free

Church of Santa Maria della Vittoria

Church of Santa Maria della Vittoria is a 17th-century church in Rome, Italy, known for housing Bernini's famous sculpture 'The Ecstasy of Teresa.' The church was initially dedicated to Saint Paul for the Discalced Carmelites and later rededicated to the Virgin Mary. It is located in Rione Sallustiano, near the Fontana dell'Acqua Felice.

Free

Piazza Barberini

A bustling square featuring the famous Triton Fountain by Bernini, located at the end of Via Veneto. The site forms part of Piazza Barberini's documented civic and cultural landscape within Italy, with archives and visitor information available from municipal or regional heritage authorities.

Free

Trinità dei Monti

Trinità dei Monti is a 16th-century church located at the top of the Spanish Steps, overlooking Piazza di Spagna. Under the responsibility of the French State, this Roman Catholic church boasts elaborate interior decoration and is accompanied by an Egyptian-inspired obelisk called Obelisco Sallustiano. The magnificent stairway, built in the 18th century, connects the slopes of Pincio to Piazza di Spagna and offers views.

€2

Mouth of Truth

The Mouth of Truth, or La Bocca della Verità, is a captivating marble sculpture located just outside the Church of Santa Maria in Cosmedin. This ancient manhole cover features a carved face and has become a attraction due to its intriguing legend. According to medieval lore, if someone places their hand inside the mouth and tells a lie, it would close on them—making it an early form of a lie detector!.
