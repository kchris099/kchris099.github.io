Pisa

Pisa was a maritime republic by the 11th century, competing in Mediterranean trade before decline after defeat at Meloria in 1284. The cathedral, baptistery, and leaning campanile form the Piazza dei Miracoli, begun in 1063. The university, founded in 1343, remains one of Italy's oldest. Allied bombing in 1944 damaged parts of the city. The cathedral square was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1987.

Tower of Pisa

This freestanding campanile for Pisa Cathedral began in 1173 and took nearly two centuries to finish. Soft subsoil caused the famous lean, already noticeable when work resumed after a pause in the 12th century. Stabilization work in the 1990s reduced the tilt; timed tickets regulate climbs to the upper galleries.

Cattedrale di Pisa

Founded in 1064 with spoils from naval campaigns, Pisa's cathedral anchors the Piazza dei Miracoli. Its Romanesque facade combines Byzantine, Islamic, and classical motifs in marble. Inside, a coffered ceiling and pulpit by Giovanni Pisano illustrate the maritime republic's wealth and artistic ambition.

Piazza dei Cavalieri

Medieval Pisa governed from Piazza dei Cavalieri before Florence remade the square in the 1560s under Cosimo I as headquarters of the Knights of Saint Stephen. Vasari's Palazzo della Carovana now houses the Scuola Normale Superiore. The paved space remains the civic counterpart to the Field of Miracles.

Piazza del Duomo

Pisa's Piazza del Duomo gathers the cathedral, baptistery, Leaning Tower, and camposanto within a broad walled lawn. Construction across the eleventh to fourteenth centuries produced a closely related group of marble Romanesque and Gothic monuments, and the historic ensemble expresses the maritime republic's wealth and religious ambition, while each building has a distinct function and access arrangement.

43.7232° N / 10.3947° E

Battistero di San Giovanni

The Baptistery of St. John on Pisa's Field of Miracles is a medieval circular baptistery whose dome and acoustics often draw as much notice as its marble exterior. It stands between the cathedral and the Camposanto, slightly apart from the leaning tower's crowds. Romanesque and Gothic layers meet in the tall interior.

43.7233° N / 10.3941° E

Chiesa di Santa Caterina d'Alessandria

Chiesa di Santa Caterina d'Alessandria is a 13th-century Gothic-style Catholic church located south of the Arno River. It underwent renovations in the 19th century, resulting in a nave with a gabled roof and plastered walls, except for the lower part of the X and XI century apse. The church is famous for housing the pulpit from which St. Thomas Aquinas preached.

Church of Saint Mary of the Thorn

Santa Maria della Spina sits on the south bank of the Arno as a small Gothic church begun in the thirteenth century by the Gualandi family. Its name recalls a thorn from the Crown of Thorns once kept here until the nineteenth century. Marble pinnacles and statues crowd the riverside facade.

Logge dei Banchi

Built in 1605, the riverside loggia once held stalls for silk, wool, money changers, and grain. Markets still return here, especially in the Christmas season. Arcades open toward the Arno, so traders and passersby share the same covered walk along the water.

43.7153° N / 10.4015° E

09 AdmissionFree

Tuttomondo (Keith Haring)

Tuttomondo, a active masterpiece by the known American street artist Keith Haring, is a gem located Pisa. Just a stone's throw from the busy Corso Italia and near the train station, this mural spans an 180 square meters on the wall of the Church of Sant Antonio Abate. Created in just four days back in 1989, it is Haring's final work before his untimely passing.

43.7112° N / 10.3983° E

San Piero a Grado

San Piero a Grado is a Romanesque basilica on the road toward Pisa's marina, built near the ancient port where tradition says St. Peter landed in Italy around 44 AD. Fresco cycles and a simple brick exterior mark the isolated church. The site sits away from the Campo dei Miracoli tourist core.
