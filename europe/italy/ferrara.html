Ferrara

Este dukes between the fourteenth and sixteenth centuries walled Ferrara, raised Castello Estense, and planned a Renaissance extension north of the medieval core. Poets and painters worked at that court; papal rule followed the dynasty. Brick palaces and bicycle-filled streets on the Po plain are the everyday residue of the ducal capital. The diamond-shaped walls of the Addizione Erculea remain a textbook of Renaissance planning.

Ferrara Cathedral

The Cathedral of Ferrara, also known as the Basilica Cattedrale di San Giorgio or Duomo di Ferrara, is a 12th-century structure located in Piazza della Cattedrale. Its ornate three-tiered marble facade showcases a blend of Romanesque, Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque architectural styles. The cathedral houses a baroque interior and is connected to the Archbishops Palace by a secret passage.

Estense Castle

Estense Castle, also known as the Castle of San Michele, is a grand 14th-century moated castle in Ferrara, Italy. Originally built for political and military purposes, it later became the residence of the Este family. The castle has four towers and holds over six centuries of history within its walls. Travellers can explore lavish private chambers, a chapel, prison, and a museum showing precious artworks.

The seating mainly consists of boxes rising around the stage, providing a unique viewing experience. While not the most comfortable for those in the second row of a box, the theater's architecture is undeniably and makes for concert backdrops.

Piazza Ariostea

Piazza Ariostea is a square in Ferrara, Italy, featuring a walking track, lawns, and a monument dedicated to poet Ludovico Ariosto. The square hosts various events throughout the year, such as the Palio di San Giorgio - the oldest in the world - where participants in Renaissance costumes parade and compete in games between eight contrade. Another highlight is the Ferrara Buskers Festival at the end of August, which fills the streets with music and color.

Basilica Santa Maria in Vado

Basilica Santa Maria in Vado is a known church located in the northern quarter of Ferrara, known as the 'Herculean Addition'. It was developed by Ercole d'Este in the 15th century and is home to the Pinacoteca Nazionale di Ferrara. The church gained fame due to a miracle that occurred on Easter day in 1171. Its richly decorated interior showcases Renaissance and Baroque influences.
