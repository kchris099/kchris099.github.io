Lecce

Seventeenth-century wealth paid for an exuberant local baroque carved from soft pietra leccese. Basilicas and palazzi earned Lecce comparisons with Florence, though the style is distinctly Salentine. The historic centre is essentially a Counter-Reformation building campaign in stone. Roman amphitheatre foundations under Piazza Sant'Oronzo prove a much older municipal layer.

Porta Napoli

Porta Napoli marks Lecce's northern approach with a triumphal arch erected in 1548 for Emperor Charles V. Four Corinthian columns support a pediment carrying imperial and civic emblems, replacing an earlier gate in the defensive circuit. The monument opens onto a straight route into the old town and records the city's sixteenth-century political ties more than its later Baroque identity.

Rudiae Gates

Statues of Lecce's patron saints crown Porta Rudiae, the western gate rebuilt in 1703 on an older entrance to the city. Baroque columns frame the arch, while a figure of Saint Orontius stands above Saints Irene and Dominic; via Giuseppe Libertini continues from the gate toward the cathedral area, linking the monument directly with the historic center's religious buildings.

Basilica of the Holy Cross

Dense carvings cover the facade of Lecce's Basilica di Santa Croce, from animals and human figures to foliage, balconies, and a large rose window. Construction and decoration extended from the sixteenth into the seventeenth century, bringing several architects and sculptors into the project. The adjoining former Celestine convent reinforces the church's role in defining Lecce Baroque.

Charles V Castle of Lecce

Charles V ordered a major rebuilding of Lecce's castle in the sixteenth century, incorporating parts of an earlier Norman and Angevin fortress. Thick angled bastions and a broad moat reflected new artillery defenses against possible Ottoman attack. Later military and civic uses altered the interior, which now supports exhibitions and cultural events while preserving the outer defensive form.

40.3525° N / 18.1745° E

Porta San Biagio

Also called the Gate of Saint Blaise, this Baroque portal is one of three historic entries into Lecce's walled centre. The arch faces downtown streets rather than open countryside, so traffic and walkers still pass through it daily. Nearby quay and embankment paths continue the approach for anyone circling the old town on foot.

Museo Faggiano

Museo Faggiano began when a Lecce family uncovered layered remains while renovating a house near Porta San Biagio. Excavated rooms reveal Roman finds, Greek tombs, medieval walls, and traces of a Franciscan chapel and granary. Visitors descend through stacked centuries under an ordinary residential street.

Chiesa di San Matteo

Chiesa di San Matteo was completed in 1667 as a Baroque church whose facade curves in a convex-concave rhythm typical of Lecce limestone carving. Sculptures and statues pack the front elevation above a single portal. The interior continues the theatrical stonework of the city's seventeenth-century building boom.

Cathedral of Saint Mary of the Assumption and Saint Orontius

The Cathedral of Saint Mary of the Assumption and Saint Orontius, also known as the Lecce Cathedral, is an ancient structure featuring multiple chapels and an elaborate façade. Built in 1144 and later restored by Giuseppe Zimbalo, it has a 225-foot bell tower and two facades. The cathedral is dedicated to Maria SS.

09 AdmissionFree

Teatro Romano

Lecce's Roman theater, attributed to the Augustan period, was rediscovered in the early twentieth century beside Palazzo D'Arpe and Palazzo Romano. Surviving cavea seating and stage foundations show entertainment architecture below later houses. It complements the better-known amphitheater a few blocks away in the historic center.

40.3517° N / 18.1703° E

Church of Santa Chiara

The Church of Santa Chiara in Lecce was built between 1510 and 1520, then remodeled in Baroque style between 1687 and 1691. Ornate altars, sculptures, and painted decoration fill the Clarissan church near the cathedral quarter. The building illustrates the city's shift from Renaissance volumes to dense Leccese Baroque detail.

40.3518° N / 18.1713° E

Torre del Parco

Torre del Parco is a restored medieval tower located just outside the city center of Lecce, Italy. This 15th-century fortress has been transformed into a luxurious hotel while still preserving its historical charm, featuring original frescoes, Renaissance art, and antique furnishings throughout. The accommodations offer spacious rooms with vaulted ceilings, air conditioning, and free internet access.

Convitto Palmieri

The former Palmieri boarding school in Lecce now houses the Bernardini Provincial Library and cultural spaces around a large courtyard, and the complex combines an active public institution with architecture adapted from an earlier religious foundation. Historic rooms hold books, archives, and exhibitions, while the connected Fabbrica delle Parole shows equipment associated with printing and typography.
