Cinque Terre

Monterosso, Vernazza, Corniglia, Manarola, and Riomaggiore grew as Ligurian fishing and farming settlements on terraced cliffs. Genoese rule tied them to coastal trade; rail tunnels in the 1870s ended their isolation. Dry-stone vineyards and pastel harbours define the coast. National-park and UNESCO rules now manage paths and visitor numbers along this stretch of the Italian Riviera.

Parco Nazionale delle Cinque Terre

Five Ligurian villages, terraced hillsides and a narrow marine landscape form Cinque Terre National Park, and trail access changes after storms or maintenance, so route status matters more than treating the park as a single entrance-point attraction. Footpaths and railway stations link Monterosso, Vernazza, Corniglia, Manarola and Riomaggiore across slopes long cultivated with vines and olives.

02 AdmissionFree

Manarola viewpoint

The Manarola overlook faces the stacked coloured houses and harbour of the Cinque Terre village from the cliff path above. Sunset draws the heaviest crowds, though midday light still shows the terraces and sea clearly. The path links toward Riomaggiore along the usual coastal walking routes.

44.1075° N / 9.7254° E

03 AdmissionFree

Castello di Riomaggiore

Perched above the sea, Castello di Riomaggiore is a 13th-century stone castle originally built for defense against pirates and invaders. Over many years, it was transformed into a residence for noble families. Today, travellers can explore its well-preserved rooms and halls showing the village's history. The castle's terrace offers sweeping views of Riomaggiore and its surroundings. Despite being a bit of a climb to reach, the vistas make it worth the effort.

44.0999° N / 9.7375° E

04 AdmissionFree

Castello di Vernazza

Vernazza's castle ruins, linked to the Doria and often dated around the year 1000, cling to the headland above the harbor. Steep stairs climb to the tower platform overlooking the colored houses and Ligurian Sea. The site sits on the coastal path network that connects Vernazza with neighboring Cinque Terre villages.

44.1345° N / 9.6820° E

05 AdmissionFree

Chiesa di San Giovanni Battista

Monterosso's Church of San Giovanni Battista dates mainly from the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries. Alternating bands of dark serpentinite and pale marble cover its Gothic facade, where a rose window and pointed portal face the old town. Inside, striped arches and a compact basilican plan reflect Ligurian medieval church design.

44.1468° N / 9.6547° E

06 AdmissionFree

Spiaggia Levanto

Spiaggia Levanto is the main town beach at Levanto, just north of the Cinque Terre park, backed by green hills and a rocky shoreline entry. Clear water rewards swimmers willing to cross the stones near the waterline. The strand links easily to the town promenade and trains serving the national park coast.

44.1702° N / 9.6083° E

07 AdmissionFree

Portovenere

Portovenere, a village located along the Ligurian coast of northwestern Italy, is a true gem of the Mediterranean. This destination has active 12th-century houses that line its harbor, creating an enchanting atmosphere reminiscent of days gone by. The La Palazzata, a row of tall and slender tower houses, is a evidence of the village's history and was originally built for protection against pirates.

44.0509° N / 9.8353° E

08 AdmissionFree

Doria Castle

Vernazza's Doria Castle occupies a rocky spur above the harbor, where a round watchtower and fragments of walls guarded the village from seaborne attack. The fortification developed under Genoese control during the Middle Ages. Steep lanes and stairs reach the remains, and the exposed platform looks over the compact port, terraced slopes, and ligurian coast.

44.0513° N / 9.8327° E

09 AdmissionFree

Grotta di Lord Byron

At Porto Venere, Byron's Grotto names a rocky inlet below the Church of San Pietro. Lord Byron is linked with the place through later local tradition, although stories of his swims are not firmly documented. Cliffs, exposed rocks and open water shape the cove, with rough weather producing powerful waves and spray along the shoreline. The inlet is viewed most clearly from the church-side paths.

44.0502° N / 9.8325° E

10 AdmissionFree

Parco Naturale Regionale di Porto Venere

Porto Venere Regional Natural Park holds hiking trails, village restaurants, and remnants of a nineteenth-century fort among rugged islands and blue-green water. Quays and embankment paths form shoreline walking routes. The reserve pairs wild coastal tracks with the fortified village edge beyond the Cinque Terre trail towns.

44.0430° N / 9.8431° E
