Turin

Turin served as the first capital of unified Italy in 1861 and long capital of the House of Savoy. Baroque squares along the Po grew under ducal patronage before Fiat and other manufacturers remade the industrial suburbs. The Mole Antonelliana and the Egyptian Museum mark that Savoy-to-industry sequence linking Piedmont to French and Swiss trade routes.

Palazzina di Caccia di Stupinigi

The Palazzina di Caccia di Stupinigi is an eighteenth-century Savoy hunting lodge and royal residence on Turin's southern edge, designed by Filippo Juvarra. A central oval hall and winged arms open onto formal parks. Museum rooms display furniture and hunting-related decor from the Savoy court.

Porta Palazzo

Produce, fish, clothing, household goods, and international foods fill the markets around Turin's Piazza della Repubblica, commonly called Porta Palazzo. Indoor halls and broad outdoor rows occupy a district shaped by migration and wholesale trade. Market sections operate on different schedules, and the nearby Balon flea market is related but geographically distinct from the daily food and general-goods stalls.

Basilica of Superga

Perched atop Superga hill, the Basilica of Superga is a baroque church with a commanding dome that offers views of the Alpine surroundings. Designed by known architect Filippo Juvarra in the early 18th century, this grand basilica replaced an existing church and holds great historical significance as it is the burial site for the royal House of Savoy.

Chiesa Parrocchiale della Gran Madre di Dio

Located along the banks of the Po River in Turin, the Chiesa Parrocchiale della Gran Madre di Dio is a evidence of neoclassical architecture. Established in 1831, this church was commissioned by local administrators to commemorate King Vittorio Emanuele's return after Napoleon's defeat. Its design draws inspiration from Rome's Pantheon, featuring grand columns and an dome that captures the eye of every passerby.

Fountain of the 12 Months

Twelve female figures personifying the months surround the broad basin of Turin's Fontana dei Dodici Mesi. Carlo Ceppi designed the monument for the 1898 national exhibition in Parco del Valentino, combining allegorical sculpture, cascades, and a formal riverside setting. It remains the principal architectural survival of that exposition.

Sindone Chapel

Guarino Guarini designed the Chapel of the Holy Shroud as a daring Baroque structure linking Turin Cathedral with the royal palace. Interlaced stone arches rise in narrowing tiers beneath the dome, creating an open geometric lattice. A 1997 fire caused severe damage, followed by a long restoration completed before reopening; the Shroud itself is normally stored elsewhere in the cathedral.

Palatine Gate

The Palatine Gate is a Roman city gate on Turin's north side, once the official entry through the walls of Augusta Taurinorum. Twin brick towers still frame the arched openings beside the cathedral quarter. Excavated paving and wall stubs around the gate show how the imperial street grid met the fortified circuit.

La Venaria Reale

The Savoy dynasty developed Venaria Reale outside Turin as a hunting residence, court complex, gardens and service town beginning in the seventeenth century. Filippo Juvarra's Great Gallery, chapel and stables give the estate much of its ceremonial character. Restored palace rooms and replanted grounds reveal how royal leisure, administration and landscape design were organized across one extensive suburban residence.

Turin's Santuario della Consolata grew around an old Marian devotion and a site with early Christian and medieval roots. Guarino Guarini and Filippo Juvarra contributed to the complex, whose irregular Baroque interior joins several chapels beneath domes and lanterns; polychrome marble, votive offerings, and the revered icon maintain the sanctuary's religious importance beyond its architectural history.

Chiesa di San Lorenzo

No conventional facade announces San Lorenzo from Turin's Piazza Castello; the church appears integrated with the palace frontage, and the building began as a ducal chapel commemorating a military victory on Saint Lawrence's feast day. Guarino Guarini completed its Baroque interior in the seventeenth century around an octagonal plan and a complex ribbed dome pierced by light.

Casa Scaccabarozzi

A sharply tapered corner gives Casa Scaccabarozzi the nickname Fetta di Polenta, or slice of polenta. Alessandro Antonelli designed the narrow nineteenth-century apartment building near the Po River, using bright yellow walls and an unusually thin plan to fit a difficult plot. The interior remains private, while the street view reveals the wedge profile and inventive structural response.

Villa della Regina

Villa della Regina is a 17th-century royal palace located on the hills surrounding Turin. It was originally built as a vineyard and gardens complex for Prince-Cardinal Maurice of Savoy, but later expanded and adorned by Luisa Ludovica of Savoy, who made it her personal residence. The villa has been home to several Savoy queens over many years. Its Baroque beauty is complemented by frescoes, paintings, and Chinese cabinets.
