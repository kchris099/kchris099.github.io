Siena

Siena rivalled Florence as a banking republic until defeat at Montaperti in 1260 and later plague and Medici pressure ended its independence. The fan-shaped Piazza del Campo, Palazzo Pubblico frescoes, and striped cathedral embody Gothic civic art funded by the Monte dei Paschi bank founded in 1472. Seventeen contrade still stage the Palio horse race, preserving neighbourhood rivalries that date to medieval guild organisation in southern Tuscany.

Piazza del Duomo

The cathedral facade and the Capitoline Wolf column dominate this square before the Duomo. Marble pavement and surrounding palace walls keep the space tightly framed. Crowds gather here before entering the cathedral or continuing toward the adjoining museum buildings.

Siena Cathedral

Siena Cathedral, also known as the Duomo, is a 13th-century marvel known for its striking facade with symbolic black and white marble stripes. It is the main place of worship in Siena and dominates the city's skyline alongside the Torre del Mangia. The cathedral has a rich history, originally built to replace a church dedicated to Mary on a site that was once a temple for the worship of Minerva.

Palazzo Salimbeni

Palazzo Salimbeni faces a small Gothic square in Siena as the headquarters of Monte dei Paschi di Siena, a bank founded in 1472. Nineteenth-century neo-Gothic remodeling unified the facade around earlier fabric. The palace remains a working financial seat rather than a pure museum piece.

Sanctuary of Saint Catherine

The Sanctuary of Saint Catherine occupies the hillside complex around the saint's family home in Siena, with oratories, a church, and her frescoed cell. Pilgrims follow rooms tied to Catherine of Siena's life and cult. The site sits below the cathedral ridge in the Terzo di Camollia.

43.3200° N / 11.3288° E

05 AdmissionFree

Basilica Cateriniana di San Domenico

Siena's Dominican basilica rises in austere brick above the Fontebranda district and is closely tied to Saint Catherine of Siena. A chapel preserves relics including her head and finger, while frescoes by Andrea Vanni and Sodoma recount episodes from her life. The broad Gothic interior lacks aisles, reflecting the preaching function of the mendicant order.

43.3201° N / 11.3265° E

06 AdmissionFree

Fortezza Medicea

Medici fortifications now work as a public park and venue for cultural events, with ramparts giving wide views over Siena. Walkers use the walls, courtyards, and towers when the grounds are open. The brick bastions still outline the sixteenth-century military plan.

43.3227° N / 11.3238° E

07 AdmissionFree

Porta Camollia

Travelers arriving from Florence historically entered Siena through Porta Camollia, the northern gate of the medieval walls; the present arch reflects rebuilding in the early seventeenth century after earlier damage and carries a Latin inscription welcoming the visitor. A long street continues uphill toward the center, while an outer defensive structure survives on the approach.

43.3277° N / 11.3254° E

08 AdmissionFree

Porta Pispini

Also called the Gate of the Water Spout, Porta dei Pispini is a surviving piece of Siena's medieval defensive ring on the city's edge. The inner gate still keeps its wooden frame and doors. Embankment paths nearby continue the circuit along the old wall line.

43.3173° N / 11.3425° E

09 AdmissionFree

Porta Romana

Porta Romana controls Siena's southeastern road toward Rome through a deep brick passage built in the fourteenth century. Multiple arches, guard spaces and remnants of painted decoration reveal its ceremonial and defensive roles. The gate remains part of an active street, and the road climbs from here toward the central ridges of the historic city.

43.3133° N / 11.3394° E

10 AdmissionFree

Basilica of San Francesco

Franciscans established their large Siena church in the thirteenth century, and later Gothic enlargement created the present single-nave basilica. Fire and nineteenth-century restoration altered much of the interior, but chapels retain frescoes, tombs, and eucharistic relics associated with a reported seventeenth-century miracle. The striped timber roof and broad brick facade emphasize the building's mendicant scale.

43.3222° N / 11.3344° E
