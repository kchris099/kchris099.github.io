Cefalù

Cefalù developed under Roger II of Sicily, who founded the cathedral with twin towers facing the Tyrrhenian in 1131 after vowing to build it if spared in a storm. Fishermen and farmers lived within medieval walls beneath the Rocca cliff that Greeks and Arabs also fortified. Later Spanish watchtowers and nineteenth-century seaside promenades turned the town toward tourism while laundry stones along the coast and Norman mosaics inside the abbey record earlier layers of Sicilian maritime life.

Porta Pescara (Porta Marina)

A pointed arch in Cefalu's seawall frames the sea at Porta Pescara, a surviving medieval gate opening directly toward the old harbor. Stone steps descend from the passage to a small beach and former landing area. The view through the arch connects the compact Norman town with the fishing activity that shaped its shoreline for centuries.

Lavatoio Medievale

Stone stairs lead below Cefalu's street level to the Lavatoio Medievale, where spring water flows through a series of carved basins. The communal washhouse was altered over time and reached its present arrangement in the early modern period, despite its traditional medieval name; iron spouts, worn washing stones, and the channel of the Cefalino stream record a practical part of domestic life.

Castello di Castelbuono

References to Castello di Castelbuono reach back to 1081, with later Swabian-era rebuilding on a wooded hill between two streams. Towers overlook the valley and the stone village of Castelbuono inland from Cefalu. The fortress now houses museum rooms on local history inside the surviving circuit of walls.

37.9345° N / 14.0885° E

04 AdmissionFree

Ruins of Cefalù Castle

Castello di Cefalù, a Norman castle built on the site of an old Arab citadel, gives visitors a chance to explore its ruins and enjoy views of Cefalu and the surrounding area. The hike up is relatively easy, with well-defined trails and some rocky terrain. Along the way, hikers can also visit the temples of Diana and St. Anna.

38.0374° N / 14.0275° E

05 AdmissionFree

Duomo di Cefalù

Twin towers give Cefalu Cathedral the appearance of a fortress above the town's main square. Norman king Roger II founded the church in the twelfth century and its apse contains a large Byzantine mosaic of Christ Pantocrator. Arab-Norman forms, cloister capitals and later additions place the cathedral within Palermo, Cefalu and Monreale's historic architectural ensemble.

Rocca di Cefalù

Rocca di Cefalù, also known as u castieddu, is a prominent cliff rising 268 meters above the town of Cefalù on the Tyrrhenian coast. This fortified mountain top offers coastal views and is home to the ruins of a Norman castle. The Rocca's base forms a triangular shape with steep ridges facing east, west, and south, making it an important landmark for navigation along the coast.

38.0395° N / 14.0221° E

07 AdmissionFree

Spiaggia di Cefalù

The Spiaggia di Cefalù is a, crescent-shaped sandy beach located directly in front of the historic Sicilian town of Cefalù. The clear, shallow waters make it ideal for swimming, while the beach offers well known views of the town's Norman cathedral and the massive La Rocca promontory towering in the background. It is a popular summer destination.

38.0357° N / 14.0159° E

08 AdmissionFree

Lungomare di Cefalú

Cefalu's lungomare follows the long sandy shore west of the medieval center, with the cathedral towers and La Rocca visible behind the waterfront. A paved promenade runs beside beach access, lodging, restaurants, and seasonal facilities. The open coastal strip provides a clear contrast with the narrow streets inside the old town and remains busy well beyond bathing hours.

38.0322° N / 14.0010° E

09 AdmissionFree

Bastione di Capo Marchiafava

A coastal bastion on Cefalù's rocky edge offers street-level approaches and views toward the sea and old town. Walkers pass the fortification while moving between waterfront districts. The stone work reads as defensive civic fabric rather than a museum hall, with open air and cliff exposure defining the stop.

38.0415° N / 14.0219° E

10 AdmissionFree

Acqua Verde

Acqua Verde, also known locally with Kalura Beach, is a small pebble and rock cove east of Cefalu backed by Mediterranean scrub. Clear water and rocky entry points suit swimming and snorkeling more than soft sand lounging. Paths and a bus link from town reach the sheltered shore below the coastal hills.

38.0215° N / 13.9494° E
