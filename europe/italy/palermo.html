Palermo

Palermo served as capital of Norman Sicily after Arab engineers and Byzantine mosaicists helped Roger II build palaces and churches that blended Latin, Greek, and Islamic traditions in the twelfth century. Spanish viceroys later fortified harbours and baroque churches above Phoenician and Roman street grids. Twentieth-century migration, Allied invasion in 1943, and anti-mafia campaigns reshaped the city while markets, oratories, and Quattro Canti intersections still organise life on the Tyrrhenian coast.

Cattedrale di Monreale

Gold-ground mosaics cover much of Monreale Cathedral's interior, recounting biblical scenes beneath a monumental image of Christ Pantocrator. Norman king William II founded the complex in the twelfth century, combining Byzantine artists, Islamic decorative influences, and western church forms. The adjoining cloister contains paired columns and carved capitals within the historic Arab-Norman ensemble.

Quattro Canti

Quattro Canti is the octagonal intersection at Palermo's historic crossroads, where four nearly identical Baroque facades define Piazza Vigliena. Statues of seasons, Spanish kings, and patron saints fill the curved elevations. The square remains the geometric hinge of the old city's main axes.

Fontana Pretoria

Fontana Pretoria was designed for a Tuscan villa then reassembled in Palermo in 1573, filling Piazza Pretoria with marble gods and allegorical figures. Local nickname history recalls early shock at the nude statues. The fountain still dominates the square between Quattro Canti and the town hall.

04 AdmissionFree

Chiesa e Monastero di Santa Caterina d'Alessandria

Palermo's former Dominican convent church of Santa Caterina overlooks both Piazza Bellini and Piazza Pretoria. Built and decorated across several centuries, its interior layers colored marble, stucco, fresco, sculpture, and gilding in a dense Baroque program. Convent rooms and roof terraces may be included in visitor routes, while the complex also preserves the enclosed life of its nuns.

Church of Saint Mary 'dell'Ammiraglio'

Byzantine mosaics glow beneath the dome and arches of Santa Maria dell'Ammiraglio, commonly called La Martorana. Founded in the twelfth century by George of Antioch, the church later absorbed a convent and major Baroque alterations; greek and Latin traditions overlap in its architecture and worship history, placing it within Palermo's Arab-Norman ensemble.

06 AdmissionFree

Teatro Massimo di Palermo

A monumental staircase and Corinthian portico announce Palermo's Teatro Massimo, opened in 1897 after decades of planning and construction. Giovan Battista Filippo Basile designed the opera house, and his son Ernesto completed it. The large auditorium, domed roof, acoustical galleries, and rehearsal spaces support opera, ballet, and concerts at the edge of the historic center.

Teatro Politeama Garibaldi

Teatro Politeama Garibaldi faces Piazza Ruggero Settimo with a neoclassical facade and triumphal arch entrance. Built in the nineteenth century as a multipurpose theater, it still hosts concerts and productions for Palermo's orchestra and touring companies. The piazza around it is a major traffic and gathering node.

Palermo Cathedral

Palermo Cathedral, a church dating back to the 1100s, is an notable building with four bell towers and royal tombs. The Ballaro market, reminiscent of the souk from the Arab domination era, offers a atmosphere with merchants' chants and authentic street food aromas. Travellers can explore Quattro Canti and Fontana Pretoria before entering Palermo Cathedral to admire its grandeur and Byzantine mosaics.

Villa Bonanno

Villa Bonanno is a public garden near Palermo Cathedral with palms, paths, and shaded benches used for everyday rest. Roman house remains appear among the plantings in places. The park offers a green pause between the cathedral complex and surrounding streets.

Palazzo dei Normanni

Palazzo dei Normanni began as a ninth-century residence for Palermo's emir, then became the Norman royal palace and later Sicily's regional parliament seat. The Cappella Palatina holds celebrated mosaics inside the complex. Courtyards and state rooms span Arab, Norman, and later layers of power.

Porta Nuova

Raised in the late sixteenth century after Charles V's victory over Ottoman forces, the gate opens onto a nearly two-mile street that runs inland from the sea toward Porta Felice. Many of Palermo's major monuments line that axis. The arch still reads as a ceremonial entrance into the historic capital.

Church of Saint John of the Hermits

Five red domes rise above San Giovanni degli Eremiti, a twelfth-century Norman church built near Palermo's royal palace. The compact complex combines a cruciform sanctuary, bell tower, garden and fragments of an older monastery. Its forms reflect the exchange among Byzantine, Islamic and Latin building traditions that shaped medieval Sicily, though the present red finish is a later restoration choice.

Albero Falcone

A magnolia tree outside Giovanni Falcone's former home on Via Notarbartolo has become an informal memorial to the anti-Mafia judge killed in the 1992 Capaci bombing. Notes, photographs, drawings, and flowers gather around its trunk and railings. The living shrine changes continually, expressing public remembrance outside the institutions and museums devoted to Sicily's struggle against organized crime.

14 AdmissionFree

Church of Jesus of Casa Professa

Colored marble inlay, twisting columns, carved reliefs, frescoes, and stucco cover the interior of Palermo's Chiesa del Gesu, also called Casa Professa. Jesuits developed the church from the late sixteenth century, creating one of Sicily's most elaborate Baroque decorative programs, and wartime damage required substantial reconstruction, but the nave and chapels again display the order's theatrical visual language.

Palazzo Comitini

Stucco, frescoes, and grand staircases still show eighteenth-century aristocratic taste inside selected halls. Parts of the palace now open for civic use, so the private house has a second life as a public interior. Visiting those rooms reveals how noble display converted to shared culture.

Porta Felice

Porta Felice forms a paired monumental gateway at the seaward end of Palermo's Cassaro, now Via Vittorio Emanuele. Construction began in the late sixteenth century and continued under later viceroys, producing two tall pylons open toward the sky; the gate aligns the waterfront with the city's ancient east-west axis leading through Quattro Canti toward the Norman Palace.

Santa Maria dello Spasimo

The missing roof was never a disaster; builders simply never finished an over-ambitious plan. Deconsecrated today, the open church hosts outdoor theatre, concerts, and exhibitions under the sky. The unfinished shell has become the venue's defining feature rather than a ruin to restore.

18 AdmissionFree

La Vucciria

La Vucciria is Palermo's historic market quarter around Piazza Caracciolo, long known for food stalls that now mix with evening bars. Daytime vendors and nighttime crowds share the same narrow streets. The name still signals a working market identity even as the scene has shifted toward nightlife.

19 AdmissionFree

Ponte dell'Ammiraglio

Ponte dell'Ammiraglio is a twelfth-century Norman bridge of pointed arches built for Admiral George of Antioch across the Oreto. The river course later shifted, leaving the stone spans on dry ground. The bridge remains a rare medieval engineering landmark on Palermo's southeastern approach.

20 AdmissionFree

Riserva Naturale di Capo Gallo

Riserva Naturale di Capo Gallo is a rugged coastal nature reserve featuring steep cliffs, caves, and a habitat for various bird species like falcons, buzzards, and owls. The area is characterized by its pristine coastline and numerous caves, some of which hold significant archaeological finds and prehistoric wall art. The reserve also has the Capo Gallo beach where dwarf palms grow amidst rocky formations.

21 AdmissionFree

Basilica di San Francesco d'Assisi

A Gothic portal and rose window survive on Palermo's Basilica di San Francesco d'Assisi, founded for the Franciscans in the thirteenth century; works associated with Giacomo Serpotta and the Gagini family connect the church with major Sicilian artistic traditions. Earthquakes, Baroque remodeling, wartime bombing and restoration left a layered interior of chapels, tombs, sculpture and stucco.

38.1165° N / 13.3665° E

Statua della Libertà

At the center of Piazza Vittorio Veneto, Palermo's Monumento alla Liberta commemorates those who died in the First World War and the political idea of national freedom. A tall column, sculptural groups, and surrounding exedra shape the early twentieth-century memorial complex. Its open traffic setting lies north of the historic center near Favorita Park, not beside Palermo's better-known old-city monuments.

Renzo Barbera Stadium

Opened in the early 1930s and renamed several times since, the ground has hosted World Cup matches and regular Serie football. Capacity sits around thirty-seven thousand. Match days follow posted gate and ticketing procedures for home and visiting supporters.

Villino Florio

On Via Libertà among other Liberty-style villas, this early-1900s house keeps ornate wooden interiors restored after a fire. Art Nouveau detailing inside and out shows the Florio family's taste at peak wealth. The surrounding street still reads as Palermo's villa quarter from that era.

Villa Niscemi

Villa Niscemi preserves the former country residence of Palermo's Valguarnera family near the Favorita landscape. Eighteenth- and nineteenth-century rooms retain paintings, furniture, decorated ceilings, and family collections around courtyards and gardens.
