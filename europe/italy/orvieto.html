Orvieto

Orvieto was founded by the Etruscans on a dramatic tuff cliff and later became a significant Roman and medieval center. During the Middle Ages, it served as a papal refuge and developed a powerful commune, culminating in the construction of its Gothic cathedral in the thirteenth century. The city's history is also reflected in its extensive network of underground caves and tunnels. Today, Orvieto is celebrated for its striking topography, historic architecture, and regional wines.

Orvieto Cathedral

The Duomo di Orvieto is a 1300s cathedral with a gold facade adorned with glittering mosaics. The cathedral is known for chapel filled with detailed frescoes. The construction of the cathedral began in 1290 and involved several architects over the years. A museum opposite the Duomo offers a chance to explore Italy's significant collection of Etruscan artifacts, making it a must-visit destination for history enthusiasts.

Orvieto Underground

Orvieto Underground is a network of tunnels, caves, and wells located beneath the city of Orvieto in Umbria. Dating back 2.5 to 3 millennia ago, this subterranean world comprises over 1,200 artificial cavities that were rediscovered by local speleologists in the late 1970s. The underground space served various purposes over time, including storage areas for wine and olive oil.

Rocca Monaldeschi della Cervara is a 15th-century fortress located in the village of Bolsena. The fortress, built on ancient Etruscan ruins dating back to 300 BC, houses the Territorial Museum and the Collegiate Church of Santa Cristina, featuring Romanesque architecture with Renaissance embellishments.

In the town of Orvieto, Italy, stands the historic Palazzo Del Capitano Del Popolo.
