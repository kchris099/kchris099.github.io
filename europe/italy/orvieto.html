Orvieto

Orvieto was founded by the Etruscans on a dramatic tuff cliff and later became a significant Roman and medieval center. During the Middle Ages, it served as a papal refuge and developed a powerful commune, culminating in the construction of its Gothic cathedral in the thirteenth century. The city's history is also reflected in its extensive network of underground caves and tunnels. Today, Orvieto is celebrated for its striking topography, historic architecture, and regional wines.

Orvieto Cathedral

Orvieto Cathedral began in 1290 and developed through the work of many architects, mosaicists, sculptors and painters. Its striped side walls contrast with a richly ornamented facade of mosaics, reliefs, pinnacles and a rose window. Inside, the Chapel of San Brizio contains frescoes by Fra Angelico and Luca Signorelli, while the Chapel of the Corporal preserves the relic tied to the cathedral's foundation.

Orvieto Underground

More than a thousand man-made cavities lie within Orvieto's volcanic tuff plateau, cut over centuries for wells, storage, workshops, quarries and animal keeping. The Orvieto Underground tour shows a selected group reached from Piazza del Duomo. Etruscan shafts, medieval olive presses, dovecotes and later alterations show continuous adaptation beneath the city.

Rocca Monaldeschi della Cervara

Raised on Etruscan ruins dated around 300 BC, this fifteenth-century fortress now holds the Territorial Museum and the Collegiate Church of Santa Cristina. The church mixes Romanesque structure with Renaissance detailing. From the walls, successive rebuildings make clear how later powers reused the same high ground.

Basilica di Santa Cristina

A short way downhill from the lakeside, this basilica is tied to the Miracle of Bolsena, the event that helped establish the feast of Corpus Christi. Centuries of devotion layer the interior. Outside services, exterior and main spaces usually remain open for quiet looking.

Torre del Moro

A clock and bells crown Torre del Moro at the crossing of Orvieto's principal streets. The medieval tower became associated with the papal and civic buildings around it and now offers a high viewpoint after a long stair climb. From the summit, the cathedral roof, tuff plateau, tiled streets, and umbrian countryside reveal the compact geometry of the hill town.

Palazzo Del Capitano Del Popolo

Pointed arches and crenellations give Palazzo del Capitano del Popolo a fortified appearance on Orvieto's Piazza del Popolo. Built in the thirteenth century for civic assemblies and the captain who represented popular authority, it later accommodated other public functions; restored halls now support conferences and events, while the exterior remains a strong record of the commune's political life.

Labirinto Di Adriano

A private discovery beneath an Orvieto bakery led to the guided attraction called Labirinto di Adriano; the route is smaller and more domestic in scale than the municipal underground tour, showing how one property accumulated subterranean functions. Rooms cut into volcanic tuff contain wells, cisterns, storage spaces, extraction marks, and traces of work from Etruscan through modern periods.

Pozzo di San Patrizio

Pozzo di San Patrizio is a sixteenth-century well dug for Pope Clement VII so Orvieto could withstand siege, with a double-helix ramp for mule traffic. The shaft drops deep into the tufa plateau beneath the town. Visitors descend the spiral galleries to the water and climb the parallel path back to daylight.

Rocca Albornoziana, Orvieto

Cardinal Egidio Albornoz commissioned the fourteenth-century Rocca Albornoziana as a papal fortress on Orvieto's tufa edge. Surviving walls now enclose a public park with views over the Umbrian countryside. The site sits near Pozzo di San Patrizio at the plateau's defensive corner.

42.7209° N / 12.1206° E

Funicolare di Orvieto - Scalo

The lower station of Orvieto's funicular stands opposite the railway station at Orvieto Scalo. The line climbs through the hillside to Piazza Cahen near the historic center, replacing a nineteenth-century cable railway with a modern system opened in 1990. Departures follow a public-transport timetable, and onward buses or a walk connect the upper station with the cathedral.
