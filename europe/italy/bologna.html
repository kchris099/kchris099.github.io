Bologna

Bologna claims Europe's oldest university in continuous operation, teaching law and medicine from the eleventh century under communal self-government. Miles of porticoes shelter streets that medieval towers once dominated when Bologna rivalled Florence as a republic. Papal rule after 1506 brought palaces and churches, while nineteenth-century industry and a large left-wing movement made the city a centre of printing, cooperatives, and post-war Italian politics.

Sanctuary of the Madonna of San Luca

The Sanctuary of the Madonna of San Luca crowns the Colle della Guardia southwest of Bologna as a hilltop Marian church reached by a long covered portico. Pilgrims and walkers follow the arcaded path from the city edge to the sanctuary doors. The site remains an active place of worship with wide views over the Bolognese plain.

Piazza Santo Stefano

Also called Piazza delle Sette Chiese, the triangular pedestrian space leads to the Seven Churches complex and is lined with historic palaces and porticos. Cultural events, flea markets, and concerts periodically fill the square. The open wedge of pavement works as an outdoor foyer to the linked Santo Stefano churches.

Two Towers

Asinelli and Garisenda rise together at the meeting of several streets in central Bologna, recalling the many family towers built during the Middle Ages. The taller Asinelli was completed in the twelfth century, while the shorter Garisenda developed a pronounced lean and was partly reduced for safety. Their contrasting heights remain a clear marker of Bologna's competitive communal past.

04 AdmissionFree

Arco del Meloncello

Where the Portico di San Luca crosses Via Saragozza, the Arco del Meloncello lifts the covered pilgrimage route over the roadway. Carlo Francesco Dotti designed the eighteenth-century Rococo structure with curved stairways and a central arch. From this junction, the portico begins its long ascent toward the Sanctuary of the Madonna di San Luca on Colle della Guardia.

05 AdmissionFree

Piazza Maggiore

Bologna's civic life has centered on Piazza Maggiore since the medieval commune cleared space for public buildings and markets. San Petronio, Palazzo d'Accursio, Palazzo del Podesta and Palazzo dei Notai enclose the broad paving, while the Neptune Fountain stands just beyond its northern edge. The square's irregular architecture records centuries of religious, administrative and commercial use.

Basilica di San Petronio

An unfinished facade exposes the long construction history of the Basilica di San Petronio on Piazza Maggiore. Work began in 1390 for Bologna's civic authorities, producing a vast Gothic interior with twenty-two side chapels and a meridian line laid out by Giovanni Domenico Cassini. The church is dedicated to the city's patron but is not Bologna's cathedral.

Neptune's Fountain

Fontana del Nettuno occupies Piazza Maggiore's edge as a sixteenth-century civic fountain by Giambologna, commissioned under Pope Pius IV. Bronze Neptune rises above attendant figures and water jets that once advertised papal authority in Bologna. The basin still centers daily gatherings between the basilica and the municipal palaces.

Metropolitan Cathedral of St Peter

Bologna's Metropolitan Cathedral of Saint Peter stands on Via Indipendenza, distinct from the larger civic basilica of San Petronio. Fires and earthquakes led to extensive rebuilding, leaving a Baroque interior behind a later facade. The cathedral remains the seat of the archbishop and contains chapels, sculpture, and a tall bell tower rising above the central street.

Archiginnasio Municipal Library

Coats of arms cover the courtyards and corridors of the Archiginnasio, built in the sixteenth century to unite Bologna's university schools, and the building now contains the municipal library, while historic rooms include the carved Anatomical Theatre and Stabat Mater Hall. Its inscriptions and heraldry document generations of students, professors, and academic nations associated with the university.

Basilica of San Domenico

The tomb of Saint Dominic forms the artistic center of Bologna's Basilica di San Domenico. Begun by the Dominican order in the thirteenth century, the church was repeatedly enlarged and altered, bringing medieval chapels together with later Baroque decoration. Sculptors including Nicola Pisano, Arnolfo di Cambio, Niccolo dell'Arca, and the young Michelangelo contributed to the saint's marble shrine.

Porta Saragozza

A thirteenth-century southwestern gate begins the continuous portico stretch toward Arco Del Meloncello and the uphill climb to the Sanctuary of San Luca. The rise demands moderate fitness for walkers. The gate marks the practical start of Bologna's famous covered pilgrimage walk rather than a museum interior.

La Piccola Venezia

A surviving canal fragment recalls Bologna's former waterway network, most of which was covered with asphalt in the early twentieth century. Short open stretches still show bridges and mill remnants where boats and workshops once lined the water. The fragment gives a rare street-level glimpse of the buried hydraulic city.

13 AdmissionFree

Mercato delle Erbe

Produce stalls, butchers, fishmongers, bakeries, and small food counters occupy Bologna's Mercato delle Erbe. The covered market took shape in the early twentieth century and continues to combine everyday grocery shopping with informal dining. Its central hall and side spaces offer a working example of the city's food culture without reducing the market to a restaurant complex.

44.4962° N / 11.3383° E

14 AdmissionFree

Basilica di San Francesco

French Gothic influences appear in the pointed arches and flying buttresses of Bologna's Basilica di San Francesco, begun for the Franciscans in the thirteenth century. The brick church suffered suppression, military use, bombing, and later restoration. Monumental tombs of medieval legal scholars stand behind the apse, linking the religious complex with Bologna's university tradition.

Basilica di Santa Maria dei Servi

Servite friars began Santa Maria dei Servi on Strada Maggiore in 1393, extending the Gothic church over later centuries. A long porticoed facade faces the street, and the interior holds chapels with Bolognese painting. The wide nave and cloister ranges remain open between services.

44.4919° N / 11.3521° E

16 AdmissionFree

Porta Mascarella

Porta Mascarella survives from Bologna's final medieval circuit of walls at the northern end of Via Mascarella; the brick gate controlled the road toward Ferrara and was once accompanied by defenses and a drawbridge. Although surrounding walls disappeared during urban expansion, its pointed passage and tower still mark the transition between the historic center and later neighborhoods.
