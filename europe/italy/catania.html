Catania

Catania was resettled repeatedly on the Ionian flank of Mount Etna, with Greek settlers, Roman colonists, and Norman rulers each rebuilding after lava flows and earthquakes. The devastating quake of 1693 prompted a baroque reconstruction using dark lavastone trim on pale limestone façades. Fish markets, Benedictine monasteries, and eighteenth-century theatres express how Sicily's second city tied volcanic agriculture, port trade, and university life to the eastern coast.

Fontana dell'Elefante (uʻ Liotru)

In Piazza Duomo, the Fontana dell'Elefante carries Catania's basalt elephant, Liotru, as the city's emblem. An Egyptian-style obelisk rises from the Roman-era statue above a Baroque basin. Cathedral facades and Via dei Cruciferi frame the square that gathers around the fountain.

02 AdmissionFree

Basilica Cattedrale di Sant'Agata

Lava stone and pale limestone create the strong contrast of Catania Cathedral's Baroque facade on Piazza del Duomo. The church, dedicated to Saint Agatha, was rebuilt after the 1693 earthquake over earlier Norman fabric and contains the composer's tomb of Vincenzo Bellini. Relics of the city's patron remain central to ceremonies during the annual February festival.

03 AdmissionFree

Porta Uzeda

Porta Uzeda closes the southern side of Catania's cathedral square with an arched passage built after the 1693 earthquake. Named for the Spanish viceroy Juan Francisco Pacheco, Duke of Uceda, it joins the cathedral precinct with the former clerical seminary; the gate opens toward Via Etnea and the port, while upper levels belong to the Diocesan Museum complex.

37.5018° N / 15.0876° E

04 AdmissionFree

Catania Fish Market

Under colorful umbrellas, fishermen auction the day's catch amid coastal smells and crowded lanes beside the cathedral quarter. Regulars trade greetings while browsing stalls during market hours. The open-air fish market remains a sensory morning stop rather than a quiet souvenir arcade.

37.5018° N / 15.0870° E

05 AdmissionFree

Chiesa della Badia di Sant'Agata

On Benedictine abbey grounds, this eighteenth-century Baroque church offers Etna views from its dome platform above Catania's historic center and seaport. Climbing the dome is a separate paid ascent from ordinary church entry. The vantage ties the Badia to the volcano that defines the city's skyline.

37.5030° N / 15.0881° E

06 AdmissionFree

Piazza Università

Eighteenth-century buildings frame a Baroque square with four bronze lampposts depicting local legends, near the University of Catania. Day and evening activity mixes shopping, dining, and student traffic, with Mount Etna visible on clear days. The open pavement works as a civic room between university and Duomo streets.

37.5036° N / 15.0872° E

07 AdmissionFree

Basilica della Collegiata

A curved Baroque facade designed after Catania's 1693 earthquake fronts the Basilica della Collegiata on Via Etnea. The church replaced an earlier building and was completed through the work of several architects, including Stefano Ittar; paired columns, statues, and a raised entrance animate the narrow street, while the interior preserves frescoes and elaborate altars.

37.5042° N / 15.0867° E

08 AdmissionFree

Massimo Bellini Theater

Catania's opera house opened in 1890 and carries the name of local composer Vincenzo Bellini; a richly decorated horseshoe auditorium surrounds the stage, while the exterior faces a small square in the historic center. Opera, symphonic music, and ballet form the main program, and ceiling paintings refer to scenes from Bellini's works.

37.5042° N / 15.0900° E

09 AdmissionFree

Roman Amphitheater of Catania

Roman seating and vaulted passages emerge below modern street level at Piazza Stesicoro, where part of Catania's amphitheater is exposed. Built in the imperial period from dark lava stone and brick, the arena once extended beneath surrounding blocks; the visible section communicates the monument's curved scale while also showing how later city construction covered much of the structure.

37.5074° N / 15.0854° E

10 AdmissionFree

Villa Bellini/Chiosco Bellini

Catania's Giardino Bellini occupies a broad hillside between Via Etnea and surrounding streets. Formal avenues, stairways, fountains, old trees, and a dated floral clock developed from a private garden acquired by the city in the nineteenth century. Kiosks and open terraces support daily recreation, while the upper paths provide framed views toward Etna when weather is clear.

37.5107° N / 15.0850° E

11 AdmissionFree

Teatro Antico greco-romano di Catania

Houses press close around Catania's ancient theater, whose Roman remains incorporate parts of an earlier Greek structure. Semicircular seating, vaulted corridors, and an adjoining odeon survive within the modern street network near Via Vittorio Emanuele II; water sometimes enters the lower orchestra area, a reminder of underground flows beneath the lava-built city.

37.5027° N / 15.0839° E

Ursino Castle

Emperor Frederick II raised this thirteenth-century fortress, which still keeps intact walls and towers in Catania. Courtyards and defensive circuits form the walking route when grounds are open. The castle later housed civic collections, but the stone bulk itself remains the clearest surviving medieval stronghold in the rebuilt city.

37.4992° N / 15.0845° E

13 AdmissionFree

Porta Garibaldi

Alternating bands of black lava and pale stone give Porta Garibaldi a distinctive surface west of Catania's center. The triumphal gate was completed in the eighteenth century to commemorate the marriage of Ferdinand of Bourbon and Maria Carolina of Austria. Once called Porta Ferdinandea, it now stands in a residential square away from the main cathedral axis. The alternating stone bands are visible from a distance.

37.4996° N / 15.0739° E

14 AdmissionFree

Basilica Cattedrale di Santa Maria Annunziata

Two bell towers and a patterned dome rise above Acireale Cathedral, dedicated to the Annunciation of the Virgin. The church developed from a sixteenth-century parish and gained cathedral status in the nineteenth century, while later work reshaped its facade in a Gothic Revival manner. Frescoes, chapels, and the position on Piazza Duomo place it at the center of Acireale, north of Catania.

37.6131° N / 15.1658° E

15 AdmissionFree

Basilica Collegiata di San Sebastiano

Acireale's Basilica Collegiata di San Sebastiano shows one of the town's most elaborate late Baroque facades. Rebuilt after the 1693 earthquake, its white stone front carries statues, balustrades, and carved reliefs above a broad staircase; interior fresco cycles recount episodes from the life of Saint Sebastian, whose January feast remains an important local religious event.

37.6116° N / 15.1657° E

16 AdmissionFree

Castello Normanno - Svevo di Aci Castello

The Norman-Swabian castle at Aci Castello crowns a basalt sea rock north of Catania, its ruined walls rising straight from the lava shore. Rooms hold exhibits on sieges and coastal defense. From the terrace, views open across the Ionian water and the clustered village below.

37.5542° N / 15.1496° E
