Mantua

Gonzaga lords ruled Mantua from 1328 to 1707 on islands in the Mincio, hiring Mantegna, Giulio Romano, and later Rubens for palace decoration. The Ducal Palace and Palazzo Te express that court. Austrian and Italian administrations inherited a lagoon city already shaped as a Renaissance stage. Virgil was born nearby; the Renaissance court happily claimed that classical association.

Te Palace

In Mantua, Italy, Te Palace is a Renaissance gem that draws travellers with its whimsical interiors and striking erotic frescoes. Designed by the talented architect Giulio Romano as a retreat for the Maggiore family, this palace has evolved into a cultural hub showing art and history.

Palatine Basilica of Saint Barbara

The Palatine Basilica of Saint Barbara, located just a 12-minute walk from the city center, was once the exclusive church for the noble families residing in the Palazzo Ducale di Mantova. Designed by Bertani, this sacred building is not only a place for religious ceremonies but also is a venue for cultural events and concerts due to its perfect acoustics.

Basilica di Sant'Andrea is an Renaissance church known for its vast vaulted interior and a reliquary that is believed to contain Christ's blood. The basilica was constructed by Andrea Mantegna, a known court painter, and Leon Battista Alberti, a prominent Renaissance architect. The church showcases the new Renaissance humanist style and is a triumphant display of their work.

Teatro Scientifico del Bibiena

The Scientific Bibiena Theater, also known as Teatro Bibiena or Teatro Scientifico dell'Accademia, is an 18th-century neoclassical masterpiece located in Mantua. Designed by architect Antonio Galli Bibiena, the theater has a bell shape with numerous columns and balconies, showing elegant proportions. It holds historical significance as the venue where Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart performed at just 14 years old, marking the start of his international career.
