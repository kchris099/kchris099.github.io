Milan

Milan developed from the Roman settlement of Mediolanum into a medieval commune and later the court of the Visconti and Sforza families, who commissioned churches and the refectory housing Leonardo da Vinci's Last Supper. Spanish and Austrian rule followed before the 19th-century unification of Italy made Milan an industrial and financial engine. Rail terminals, the Galleria, and postwar skyscrapers record commerce and design leadership.

Duomo di Milano

More than five centuries of work shaped Milan Cathedral after construction began in 1386. Pale Candoglia marble covers a dense Gothic structure of pinnacles, flying buttresses, statues, and stained glass, while rooftop terraces reveal the engineering behind the spires. The cathedral remains the seat of Milan's archbishop and the principal religious building on Piazza del Duomo.

The Sforza dynasty rebuilt this fortress in the fifteenth century over an earlier Visconti stronghold on Milan's edge. Courtyards and towers now hold civic museums, including Michelangelo's unfinished Pieta Rondanini. The complex links Renaissance ducal power with later municipal collections.

Nina Akamu's monumental bronze horse stands at Milan's racecourse, completing a project inspired by Leonardo da Vinci's plans for an equestrian monument to Francesco Sforza.
