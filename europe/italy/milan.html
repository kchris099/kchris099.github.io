Explore Milan

A Brief History

Milan developed from the Roman settlement of Mediolanum into a medieval commune and later the court of the Visconti and Sforza families, who commissioned churches and the refectory housing Leonardo da Vinci's Last Supper. Spanish and Austrian rule followed before the 19th-century unification of Italy made Milan an industrial and financial engine. Rail terminals, the Galleria, and postwar skyscrapers record commerce and design leadership.

Duomo di Milano

Milan Cathedral was begun in 1386 and continued across many centuries, producing one of Europe's largest Gothic churches. The cathedral became the main religious monument of the city and a symbol of Milan's civic and episcopal status. Its marble facade, spires, and roof terraces offer close views of the sculptural decoration and the surrounding piazza.

€5

Sforzesco Castle

Sforza Castle was rebuilt in the 15th century by the Sforza dynasty on the site of an earlier Visconti fortress. It later served military functions and now houses civic museums that trace the courtly and urban history of Milan. The courtyards, towers, and Michelangelo Pietà Rondanini display connect Renaissance patronage with later city institutions.

Free

Arco della Pace

The Arco della Pace was begun in the Napoleonic period and completed under Austrian rule in the 19th century. It stands at the end of Corso Sempione as a ceremonial urban gate tied to modern Milanese planning. The neoclassical arch frames the approach to Parco Sempione and the Sforza Castle district from the northwest.

Free

Basilica di San Lorenzo, Milano

San Lorenzo is an early Christian church rebuilt and altered across later centuries near one of the oldest monumental zones of Milan. Its long history and nearby Roman remains connect it with the late antique city. The basilica's chapels and courtyards preserve layers of early Christian, Romanesque, and Renaissance building in the city center.

Free

Colonne di San Lorenzo

The Columns of San Lorenzo are a Roman colonnade relocated to the basilica area in late antiquity. They remain one of the clearest surviving groups of Roman monumental stonework in Milan's center. The sixteen Corinthian columns stand on the piazza in front of the basilica, where evening gatherings and open-air events often take place.

Free

Basilica of Sant'Ambrogio

The Basilica of Sant'Ambrogio was founded in the 4th century by Ambrose, bishop of Milan, and rebuilt in Romanesque form during the Middle Ages. It is one of the main churches associated with the city's religious history. The atrium, gold altar, and crypt of Saint Ambrose draw visitors interested in early Christian Milan.

Free

Galleria Vittorio Emanuele II

The Galleria was built between 1865 and 1877 as a glazed commercial arcade joining Piazza del Duomo with La Scala. It became one of the main symbols of unified Italy's urban modernization in Milan. The iron-and-glass vault, mosaic floors, and shopfronts create a covered passage that remains a busy route for pedestrians and café patrons.

Free

Milan Central Station

Milano Centrale opened in 1931 as the city's main long-distance railway station. Its monumental design and scale reflect the expansion of modern transport networks and the political ambitions of the early 20th century. The vast hall, stone facades, and platform canopies serve high-speed and international trains linking Milan with the rest of Europe.

Free

Porta Garibaldi

The Porta Garibaldi area connects a surviving 19th-century city gate with one of Milan's main modern rail and business districts. It shows the shift from historic fortification lines to contemporary transport infrastructure. The gate and surrounding piazza mark the edge of the old city wall route toward Corso Como and the Isola quarter.

€15

Pinacoteca di Brera

The Pinacoteca di Brera grew from Napoleonic-era collections and remains one of Italy's main public art museums. The former Jesuit college and academy complex presents painting closely tied to the history of Lombardy and the Italian states. Masterworks by Mantegna, Raphael, and Caravaggio anchor a collection focused on northern Italian schools.

Free

Basilica di Santa Maria delle Grazie

Santa Maria delle Grazie was built for the Dominicans in the 15th century and later became famous for Leonardo da Vinci's Last Supper in the adjoining refectory. The church reflects Renaissance patronage under the Sforza court. The refectory mural requires advance booking, while the church nave and cloister can be visited separately.
