Kraków

Kraków served as the capital of Poland until 1596 and houses Wawel Castle and cathedral above the Vistula River. The medieval market square and St. Mary's Basilica survived wartime destruction that devastated Warsaw. German occupation from 1939 to 1945 included persecution in the Jewish quarter of Kazimierz. The historic center was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1978.

Wawel Royal Castle

The Wawel Royal Castle and the Wawel Hill on which it sits constitute the most historically and culturally significant site in Poland. A fortified residency on the Vistula River in Kraków, it was established on the orders of King Casimir III the Great and enlarged over many years into a number of structures around a Polish Renaissance courtyard. It represents nearly all European architectural styles of the Medieval, Renaissance and Baroque periods.

Wieliczka Salt Mine

Salt extraction at Wieliczka developed from medieval shafts into a vast underground network worked for centuries near Krakow; commercial production largely ended in the twentieth century, while conservation, tourism and health uses continue in selected levels. Guided routes pass chambers, timber supports, saline lakes, machinery and chapels carved or furnished by miners, including St. Kinga's Chapel.

Remains of the Ghetto wall

The Remains of the Ghetto wall in Krakow is a reminder of the city's dark history during World War II. This stone wall, built in 1941, once enclosed the Krakow ghetto and now is a memorial to the thousands of Jews who were confined within its boundaries. The area holds significant historical importance, with notable figures such as Wladyslaw Szpilman passing through it during the relocation of the Warsaw ghetto.

Kraków Barbican, a 15th-century defense gateway once connected to the city walls, is now part of the Historical Museum. Henryk Jordan Park

Opened in 1889, Henryk Jordan Park in Kraków was planned as a public ground for youth sports and outdoor education, an early European model of that kind.
