Łodz

Nineteenth-century textile mills under Russian imperial rule turned Łódź into a brick industrial metropolis along Piotrkowska Street. Multi-ethnic manufacturers and workers filled the city before war and nationalisation. After 1989, complexes such as Manufaktura reused those factories as museums and apartments. Piotrkowska's length made it a parade ground for industrial display and later nightlife.

The Rose Passage

Pasaż Róży at 3 Piotrkowska Street in Łódź is a courtyard transformed by artist Joanna Rajkowska, who covered walls with broken mirror fragments. Light catches the mosaic surfaces through the day. The once-neglected passage is now a small public art landmark on the main street.

02 AdmissionFree

Manufaktura

Manufaktura, located in Lodz, is a former 19th-century cotton factory that has been transformed into a active shopping mall and entertainment complex. The project's success has revitalized the city's profile and demonstrated its resilience. Travellers can explore the site's history at The Museum of the Factory and appreciate contemporary art at MS2 gallery.

03 AdmissionFree

Priest's Mill

Ksiety Mlyn developed in the nineteenth century as Karol Scheibler's integrated textile district in Lodz. Mills, workers' housing, a fire station, school, hospital and residences formed a planned industrial community. Restored brick buildings now contain homes, studios, cafes and cultural uses, while the street plan still reveals the scale of the former factory estate.

51.7538° N / 19.4834° E

04 AdmissionFree

Palm House

Lodz's Palm House stands in Zrodliska Park and preserves plant collections begun by the city's industrial families; the institution is a botanical conservatory, with temperature, humidity and occasional renovation affecting public access. Three climate zones contain mature palms, cycads, cacti, succulents, aquatic plants and tropical understory species beneath modern glass roofs.

51.7603° N / 19.4800° E

Plastuś Monument

Plastus is a small sculpture of the children's-book character associated with Lodz's Fairy Tale Trail, while the Holocaust memorial landscape lies elsewhere, and park Ocalałych is a modern oasis in Lodz that is both a quiet shoreline and a memorial. This park honors the resilience of Holocaust survivors, featuring hundreds of trees planted by them, alongside significant WWII monuments.

06 AdmissionFree

Alexander Nevsky Cathedral

Built in the 1880s, Lodz's Alexander Nevsky Cathedral reflects the city's growth under the Russian Empire and the presence of an Orthodox community. A compact neo-Byzantine plan combines colorful masonry, domes, icons and an ornate iconostasis. Its survival among former factories and commercial streets adds a distinct religious layer to the industrial city's architecture.

Piotrkowska

Piotrkowska Street runs through central Lodz as a long commercial axis shaped by nineteenth-century textile wealth. Merchant houses and factory offices preserve the industrial-era streetscape, with courtyards revealing workshops and former business premises, and shops and restaurants now dominate the busiest sections, while monuments and public art interpret the city's later reinvention.

08 AdmissionFree

White Factory

Ludwik Geyer's White Factory introduced mechanized textile production to Lodz in the nineteenth century and gained its name from plastered walls unusual among the city's later red-brick mills. The complex now houses the Central Museum of Textiles, with machinery, fabrics, design, and industrial history. An adjacent open-air collection adds workers' housing and regional wooden architecture.

51.7456° N / 19.4621° E

Orientarium ZOO Łódź

Łódź Zoo, founded in 1938, keeps more than 350 species and ranks among Poland's older municipal zoos. Feeding times and contact enclosures sit among standard exhibit paths. The Orientarium wing focuses on Asian habitats within the larger garden for indoor viewing.

Mandoria

Styled as a sixteenth-century trading town under one roof, Mandoria near Łódź runs more than twenty rides and attractions year-round. Layouts favor children roughly ages five to fifteen while remaining usable for adults. Covered streets keep the park independent of outdoor weather.

Arturówek

Arturówek, situated on the northern edge of Lodz within the Lagiewniki Forest, offers fantastic recreational opportunities for travellers. The area is perfect for leisurely walks, sunbathing, and active family recreation. Travellers can explore Arturówek lake by pedalo or rowboat, relax on the beach, or experience an adrenaline rush at the rope course.
