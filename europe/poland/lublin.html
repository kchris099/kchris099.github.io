Lublin

Lublin linked Polish and Lithuanian realms in the medieval and Renaissance periods and hosted the 1569 union diet that formalised the Commonwealth. Twentieth-century war and occupation devastated communities, yet the old town's castle, gates, and parish churches continue to frame a compact historic core on the hill above the Bystrzyca. University and trade-fair traditions grew beside that plan.

Plac Po Farze

Plac Po Farze, located between the Rynek and the Grodzka Gate in Lublin's Old Town, is a storied plaza with a rich historical significance. It was once home to the 13th-century Parish Church of St. Michael the Archangel, which was later demolished due to its deteriorating condition. However, remnants of this medieval church have been preserved and are now on display in the square.

Brama Krakowska

The Cracow Gate, also known as Brama Krakowska, is a significant Gothic-style landmark in Lublin, Poland. Constructed in the 14th century, it is a symbol of the city and connects the City Centre with the Old Town. This historic gate was part of the royal route from Cracow to Vilnius and has served various purposes over time, including defense, fire observation tower, and residence for the clock-master.
