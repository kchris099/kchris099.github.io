Zamość

Zamość was founded in 1580 by the Polish nobleman Jan Zamoyski, who commissioned the Italian architect Bernardo Morando to design an ideal Renaissance city. Positioned on the trade route linking the Black Sea to northern Europe, the fortress town attracted Armenian, Greek, and Jewish merchants. Miraculously surviving the widespread destruction of World War II, Zamość remains a cohesive example of late Renaissance urban planning. Visitors can walk the original grid layout, exploring the Great Market Square and the surviving bastions of the fortress.

Ogród zoologiczny w Zamościu im. Stefana Milera

Zamosc Zoo began as a small school collection and developed into a municipal zoological garden named for educator Stefan Miler. Modernized enclosures hold mammals, birds, reptiles, and domestic species alongside educational programs and planted grounds.

Great Market Square

Arcaded Renaissance houses wrap Zamość's Great Market Square, with shops and cafes under the ground-floor vaults. The Old City plan keeps the plaza as a preserved trading and gathering ground. Stalls appear during regular market hours while everyday foot traffic fills the open center.

Ratusz w Zamościu

Zamosc Town Hall closes one side of the Great Market Square with a broad fan-shaped staircase, tall clock tower, and Mannerist-Baroque facade. The building reflects the planned Renaissance city's civic administration and later rebuilding under different rulers. Offices still occupy parts of the structure, while interior access and tower entry are limited.

Rynek Wodny

Rynek Wodny is a smaller square within the carefully ordered Renaissance plan of Zamosc. Low historic houses and mature trees create a quieter setting than the nearby Great Market Square, while the rectangular space still follows the geometry established for the ideal town. Its modest scale helps reveal how residential streets and secondary public spaces supported the monumental civic center.

Kładka widokowa zachodnia

A western viewing footbridge crosses part of Zamość's restored defensive landscape, giving elevated views over earthworks, brick walls, former moat areas and the planned old town. The modern walkway supports interpretation of the fortress without imitating an original military crossing. From its deck, the geometry of the bastioned defenses becomes easier to read against the Renaissance street plan.

Nadszaniec

Nadszaniec rises above a section of Zamosc's bastioned fortifications as a brick and earth defensive work later adapted for military and civilian uses. Passages, platforms, walls, and views across the old town help explain the geometry of the fortress. Restoration has altered some areas, and commercial spaces inside is distinct from the structure's original military function.

08 AdmissionFree

Twierdza Zamość

Zamosc Fortress surrounded the planned Renaissance town with bastions, curtain walls, gates, moats and outer defenses developed from the sixteenth through nineteenth centuries. Surviving and reconstructed sections show how repeated wars altered a distributed fortification system, with individual bastions and gates preserving evidence from different construction phases.

Park Miejski w Zamościu

Zamosc City Park occupies former military land beside the old fortress line, where landscaped paths now cross lawns and mature tree cover. A pond and surviving views toward the defensive works connect the recreation area to the city's changing boundaries. The fortifications eventually lost their military purpose. Their former defensive zone then became part of everyday urban life.

10 Admissionzł12

Rotunda Zamojska

The circular brick Rotunda began as a nineteenth-century fortress work outside Zamosc's main walls. German occupation forces later used it as a prison, execution ground and site of mass killing during World War II. Cells, memorial plaques, graves and exhibitions now form a martyrdom museum, requiring a sober description that separates military construction from the crimes committed there.

50.7108° N / 23.2472° E
