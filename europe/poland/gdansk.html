Gdansk

Gdańsk grew as a Hanseatic port on the Baltic whose shipyards and merchants linked Poland to Scandinavian and Dutch trade. The medieval crane, Royal Route, and rebuilt Main Town Hall record Teutonic, Polish, and Prussian rule before 1945 border changes. Solidarity strikes at the Lenin Shipyard in 1980 began the movement that weakened communist rule across Eastern Europe.

Westerplatte

On the Westerplatte peninsula, ruins and memorials mark the Polish Military Transit Depot attacked by German forces on September 1, 1939. Barracks remains, guardhouses, cemeteries, information panels, and the tall Monument to the Defenders extend through woods beside the port channel; the landscape explains the seven-day defense and the opening of World War II without reducing the site to a single monument.

Neptune's Fountain

Neptune's Fountain has stood before Gdansk's Artus Court since the seventeenth century, linking the maritime city with classical imagery. The bronze figure, basin, railings, and surrounding reconstructed merchant facades form a focal point of Dlugi Targ. Golden Gate and Green Gate frame the wider Royal Way.

Długi Targ

Long Market stretches about five hundred meters through Gdansk's old core, a strip that has stood since the thirteenth century. Colorful tenements line the way past St. Mary's Church, the Neptune Fountain, the main town hall, Artus Court, and the Golden Tenement House. Restaurants and souvenir shops fill the ground floors.

St. Mary's Church

Construction of Gdansk's brick Basilica of the Assumption of the Blessed Virgin Mary continued through much of the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries. The high nave and tower express the wealth of the medieval port, while chapels, artworks and an astronomical clock record its religious life. Severe wartime damage was followed by reconstruction after 1945.

Oliwski Park

Oliwa Park grew around the former Cistercian abbey as a sequence of formal gardens, tree-lined avenues, ponds, woodland and later landscape sections. Paths connect the cathedral precinct with botanical collections and open lawns in northern Gdansk. The mixture of monastic grounds, designed vistas and mature planting records several stages of garden development.

Wieża widokowa na wzgórzu Pachołek

A metal observation tower crowns Pachołek Hill above Gdańsk's Oliwa district. Forest paths and stairs lead to the platform, where clear weather opens views toward the city, shipyards, Gulf of Gdańsk and wooded moraine landscape. The tower forms part of a larger network of park and hill trails, with the climb shaped by the chosen approach and conditions underfoot.

ZOO GDAŃSK

ZOO GDAŃSK, located in Oliwa and established in 1954, offers a diverse range of animal species from around the world. The expansive 126-hectare area is home to 181 different animal species, including majestic big cats like lions, leopards, and tigers as well as gentle creatures such as red-necked kangaroos and various turtle species. Visitors can also encounter native animals like the European bison.

Monument to the Fallen Shipyard Workers of 1970

Three steel crosses rise outside the former Lenin Shipyard in memory of workers killed during the December 1970 protests. Unveiled in 1980 after the emergence of Solidarity, the monument combines anchors, reliefs, inscriptions, and a public square tied to Poland's labor and democratic movements. The European Solidarity Centre stands nearby but is a separate museum.

Góra Gradowa

Góra Gradowa, a 19th-century hilltop fort in Gdansk, offers city views and is easily accessible from the main bus and train station. Despite its modest height of 46 meters, the hill provides panoramic vistas of Gdansk. Visitors can explore the remains of the fortress and discover hidden bunkers with small exhibitions inside.

Highland Gate

Completed in the late sixteenth century, Gdansk's High Gate introduced visitors to the city's ceremonial Royal Route, and stonework, coats of arms and Renaissance composition marked the fortified western entrance before later defensive changes reshaped the surroundings. Tourist information now occupies part of the building, while nearby architecture reflects Polish, Dutch, German and local influences.

Golden Gate

Golden Gate spans the western approach to Gdansk's Dluga Street, replacing an earlier Gothic gate in the early seventeenth century. Dutch Mannerist facades, allegorical figures, inscriptions, and heraldic detail turn a defensive threshold into a civic monument; postwar reconstruction restored the gate after severe damage, and the Royal Route continues east toward Dlugi Targ and the river.

The Great Armoury

Gdansk's Great Armoury was built around 1600 to store municipal weapons and equipment behind an unusually ornate Dutch Mannerist facade. Gables, sculpture, gilded details, and painted brickwork face both Targ Weglowy and Piwna Street. Later educational and commercial uses changed the interior, while the exterior still reflects the city's military needs and mercantile display before the partitions of Poland.

Długa

For centuries Long Street carried Gdansk's merchant trade between Gothic gates and wealthy townhouses. The Neptune fountain and the town-hall clock mark the upper end, with St. Mary's Basilica, Zuraw Crane, and other Old Town landmarks close by. Pedestrians still use the street as the main historic spine.

Shakespeare Theatre

Gdańsk Shakespeare Theatre occupies a modern brick playhouse near the Main Town, staging productions for local and international audiences. The city around it carries more than a thousand years of port history and later Solidarity memory. The theatre sits a short walk from the old town and the main station.

Pier in Sopot

Sopot Pier extends from the resort's seafront square into the Gulf of Gdansk as one of Europe's long wooden pleasure piers; seasonal access rules, wind and events affect the experience, while the nearby lighthouse and concert shell remain separate attractions around Skwer Kuracyjny. Rebuilt and enlarged over time, it supports promenades, boat services, marina activity and broad coastal views.

Skwer Kuracyjny

Skwer Kuracyjny forms the public square between Sopot's main pedestrian street and its pier. The spa house, colonnades, concert shell, lighthouse, cafes and open paving reflect the resort's nineteenth- and twentieth-century growth. As the principal approach to the waterfront, the square links the town center with beach facilities and the long wooden pier.

Known locally as Opera Lesna, the Sopot amphitheatre seats about 4,500 and stages room for 110 musicians. Strong natural acoustics support Polish and international opera and concert bills. It remains the Tri-City's main outdoor house for large cultural programs.

Bastion Żubr

The Żubr Bastion, also known as the Bison Bastion, is one of the 14 bastions located in the southern part of Gdansk's old town. This grassy mound offers a nice view of the old moat and city defense system. While it may benefit from some cleaning and maintenance, visitors appreciate its open spaces for photography and its peaceful atmosphere away from the crowds.

Swan Tower

The round brick Swan Tower stands beside the Motlawa as a surviving part of Gdansk's medieval waterfront defenses, and built and altered across several periods, it guarded the northern harbor edge and later supported guild or administrative uses. Nearby mills and fish-market buildings belong to the wider district.

Straganiarska Gate

Straganiarska Gate opened through Gdansk's fifteenth-century river wall, allowing movement between merchant streets and the Motlawa quays. Twin brick turrets, a pointed passage, and restored upper rooms preserve the character of a Gothic water gate despite wartime damage; residential or guest accommodation inside is secondary to its original role in controlling access from the port.

St. Mary's Gate

Mary's Gate is a red-brick structure with origins dating back to the 15th century, reconstructed after World War II and adorned with the Gdańsk coat of arms. It is one of the main entrances to Gdansk and provides access to the cobbled Mariacka street. The gate is viewed from Granary Island across the river, where visitors can admire its Gothic style and restored polychrome featuring two lions holding the city's coat of arms.

Mariacka

After heavy World War II damage, Mariacka's cobbled lane was rebuilt with merchant houses whose entrances sit on raised terraces above street level. Restaurants claim those terrace edges, and low walls carry ornamental reliefs. Film crews still use the narrow street as a period backdrop.

St. Catherine's Church

St. Catherine's is among Gdansk's historic surviving churches, with Gothic brick fabric developed from medieval foundations and later additions. The tower holds a carillon, while chapels, artworks, and the tomb of astronomer Johannes Hevelius connect the building to city history.
