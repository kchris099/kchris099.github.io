Copenhagen

Copenhagen was founded as a fishing village and grew after Bishop Absalon built a fortress on Slotsholmen in the 12th century. It became Denmark's capital under Christian IV, who added Renaissance buildings and canals in the 17th century. The 19th century brought neoclassical institutions and Tivoli Gardens. Neutral in both world wars, the city expanded with modernist housing and harbor redevelopment while retaining its medieval core.

Rosenborg Castle

Christian IV built Rosenborg in the early 1600s as a Dutch Renaissance summer house inside Kongens Have. Crown jewels, coronation thrones, and royal regalia now fill the rooms, while tall hedges and flower beds enclose the compact brick castle north of the medieval city.

David Collection

The David Collection occupies C.L. David's former townhouse near Nørreport and holds Islamic art from the eighth to nineteenth centuries, with European eighteenth-century furniture and early modern painting. Galleries are arranged by region and dynasty across three floors. The museum was founded to keep the private collection open to the public in central Copenhagen.

Frederik's Church

Frederik's Church closes the western axis of Copenhagen's Frederiksstaden district with a large copper-clad dome. Court architect Nicolai Eigtved began the project in the eighteenth century, but financial problems halted work until Ferdinand Meldahl completed it in 1894. Marble, columns, sculpture, and a circular interior frame worship beneath the dome, aligned directly with Amalienborg Palace.

Amalienborg

Amalienborg's four identical rococo palaces face an octagonal courtyard where the Royal Guard changes posts each day. Built in the 1750s for noble families, the complex became the monarchy's winter residence, and one wing museums recent royal history and ceremony.

Thorvaldsens Museum

Thorvaldsens Museum opened in 1848 beside Christiansborg Palace to house the work and collections of Danish sculptor Bertel Thorvaldsen. Architect Michael Gottlieb Bindesbøll designed colored facades and richly painted rooms that contrast with white plaster and marble figures; the artist's tomb lies in the courtyard, while galleries also display antiquities, paintings, and personal objects he assembled in Rome.

The Christiansborg Tower

The tower above Christiansborg Palace rises over Slotsholmen, where Denmark's parliament, Supreme Court and royal reception rooms share one complex. Elevator access reaches an upper viewing platform below the spire, with roofs, harbor channels, church towers and city districts visible in clear weather. The structure belongs to the palace's twentieth-century rebuilding.

Langelinie

Langelinie, located in downtown Copenhagen, is a quayside promenade along the waterfront of Osterbro district. It was originally a boardwalk that encircled Kastellet and extended to the shoreline along Strandboulevard. The area is home to the famous Little Mermaid statue by Edvard Eriksen and offers shops, cafes, and scenic spaces for walkers and cyclists.

Kastellet

Kastellet preserves a seventeenth-century star-shaped fortress north of central Copenhagen. Grassy ramparts, a moat, gates, barracks, commanders' houses, a church and a windmill remain within an active military area that is largely open for walking. The geometric defenses connect the harbor with Copenhagen's former land walls, while memorials and daily institutional use prevent it from being a landscaped park.

Consecrated in 1887, St Alban's Church is Copenhagen's Anglican church for the British community, often called the English Church. Gothic Revival stonework sits near the Gefion Fountain and harbor fortifications. Services in English continue for expatriates and visitors; the exterior and nave can be viewed outside worship times.

Gefion Fountain

The Gefion Fountain near Langelinie shows the Norse goddess Gefion driving oxen that plough the island of Zealand from the Swedish mainland in myth. Cascading basins and bronze animals face the harbour, a short walk from the Little Mermaid and Kastellet.

Tivoli Gardens

Tivoli Gardens opened beside Copenhagen's old fortifications in 1843 and grew into a combined amusement park, performance venue and landscaped pleasure garden; programs change for summer, Halloween and Christmas, so its atmosphere depends strongly on the operating season. Historic wooden rides, modern coasters, theaters, restaurants, ponds, flower beds and seasonal lighting share a compact site near the central station.

National Museum of Denmark

Denmark's National Museum occupies the eighteenth-century Prince's Palace near Christiansborg. Collections range from prehistory and Viking material to medieval church art, royal objects, ethnography, coins, toys, and modern social history. Famous pieces such as the Sun Chariot and Golden Horn reconstructions sit within a much broader institution that interprets both Denmark and cultures beyond Europe.

Kongens Nytorv, laid out around 1680, is a broad cobbled square framed by the Royal Danish Theatre, Thotts Palace (now the French Embassy), and Magasin du Nord. Paths radiate toward Nyhavn and the old shopping streets from this baroque planning node.

The Royal Playhouse and The Old Stage

The Royal Playhouse and The Old Stage are theaters in Copenhagen, each with its own unique charm. The Royal Playhouse, situated on the harbor, offers a oak-boarded promenade and a glass-fronted cafe with water views. Nyhavn is Copenhagen's seventeenth-century canal harbour lined with brightly painted townhouses, cafes, and wooden boats along both quays. Once a working sailors' quarter, the waterfront now draws walkers between Kongens Nytorv and the harbour bus stops throughout the day.

Church of Our Lady

Copenhagen Cathedral took its present neoclassical form after British bombardment destroyed an earlier church in 1807. C.F. Hansen designed the restrained interior, where Bertel Thorvaldsen's statues of Christ and the apostles stand along the nave. Royal weddings, funerals, state services, and regular Lutheran worship give the Church of Our Lady a national ceremonial role beyond its architectural importance.

The Round Tower, Rundetarn, is Christian IV's seventeenth-century observatory in central Copenhagen, climbed by a continuous spiral ramp rather than stairs. The ascent suits mixed ages and ends at a platform with views over the old city roofs and churches.

Strøget

Stroget names a connected network of pedestrian streets between Copenhagen's City Hall Square and Kongens Nytorv. Car restrictions introduced in 1962 made the route an influential example of city-center pedestrianization. Department stores and international brands occupy the busiest sections, while older shops, churches, squares and side lanes add variety around Amagertorv and beyond.

The Hermitage Palace

Eremitageslottet stands on high ground in the royal deer park north of Copenhagen. Lauritz de Thurah designed the compact Baroque hunting lodge for Christian VI in the 1730s, with formal rooms used for meals after court hunts. The building remains a royal property and is normally seen from surrounding paths, while the open grassland around it supports deer and long views across Jægersborg Dyrehave.

Copenhagen Zoo occupies a sloping site in Frederiksberg between city streets and palace gardens. Founded in 1859, it contains historic structures such as the observation tower alongside modern habitats including Norman Foster's elephant house and an Arctic ring for polar bears. Conservation breeding, education, and animal welfare shape the collection, whose species and enclosures change over time.
