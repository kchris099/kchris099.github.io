Lisbon

Lisbon occupies hills above the Tagus estuary and was settled by Phoenicians and Romans as Olisipo. The 1755 earthquake and tsunami destroyed much of the Baixa district, rebuilt with a grid plan under the Marquis of Pombal. Maritime expeditions in the Age of Discovery departed from nearby Belém. Alfama and São Jorge Castle record Moorish and medieval layers. Fado music emerged from riverfront neighborhoods.

Praça do Comércio

Praca do Comercio opens Lisbon's rebuilt Baixa toward the Tagus on the former site of the royal Ribeira Palace destroyed in the 1755 earthquake. Arcaded Pombaline buildings, the equestrian statue of Jose I, Rua Augusta Arch and river steps define the broad civic space. Government offices, transit, events and restaurants continue around a square whose importance exceeds its exact measurements.

Rua Augusta Arch

Rua Augusta Arch closes the northern side of Lisbon's Praca do Comercio, completing the ceremonial entrance to the rebuilt Baixa after the 1755 earthquake. Columns, allegorical sculpture, coats of arms and figures from Portuguese history crown the stone structure. An upper terrace offers city views when open, while the monument commemorates reconstruction and national figures.

Jeronimos Monastery

Jeronimos Monastery was commissioned in the early sixteenth century near Lisbon's Belem waterfront and entrusted to the Order of Saint Jerome. Manueline cloisters, church vaulting, maritime motifs, royal tombs, and later national memorials connect the complex to overseas expansion and the Avis dynasty. Secularization changed its use, while museums occupy adjoining wings separate from the monastic route.

Torre de Belem

Belem Tower stands near the Tagus as an early sixteenth-century defensive and ceremonial structure built during Portugal's maritime expansion. A low bastion, ornate tower, carved stonework, turrets, and royal symbols combine military function with Manueline display; river engineering has altered its shoreline setting, and narrow stairs or tidal conditions affect access to parts of the monument.

Monument to the Discoveries

The Monument to the Discoveries was erected in its present permanent form in 1960 on Lisbon's Belem waterfront, and exhibitions and a viewpoint occupy the interior. A stylized ship carries figures associated with Portuguese navigation, monarchy, religion, mapping, and conquest, led by Henry the Navigator.

Museu do Tesouro Real

Lisbon's Royal Treasure Museum displays crown jewels, ceremonial silver, diplomatic gifts, coins, insignia and objects connected with the Portuguese monarchy. Purpose-built galleries occupy the west wing of Ajuda National Palace with controlled lighting and security. Opened in the twenty-first century, the museum gives the royal collections a dedicated setting. The palace provides their broader dynastic context.

Palácio Nacional da Ajuda

Ajuda National Palace began after the 1755 earthquake displaced the royal household from central Lisbon. Changing plans produced an unfinished neoclassical residence whose state rooms, throne room, dining rooms, furniture, textiles, and decorative arts preserve nineteenth-century court life. The Royal Treasure Museum occupies a newer adjoining wing, while the palace itself remains a separate historic interior.

Castelo de São Jorge

São Jorge Castle crowns the hill of Santa Maria Maior above Lisbon, with human occupation from at least the eighth century BC and fortifications from the second century BC. Moorish and later Portuguese works shape the present walls and towers. Viewpoints inside the enclosure look over the Baixa and the Tagus.

Belém Tower

Belem Tower was built in the early sixteenth century beside the Tagus as part of Lisbon's river defenses and ceremonial entrance. Manueline stonework, carved maritime motifs and projecting watchtowers decorate the compact fort. Shoreline changes have left the structure close to the bank, while narrow chambers, spiral stairs and an upper terrace preserve the scale of its original military and symbolic roles.

Sanctuary of Christ the King

Completed in 1959, the Sanctuary of Christ the King rises above Almada on the south bank of the Tagus. A monumental statue of Christ stands on a tall concrete pedestal containing chapels and exhibition spaces, with an esplanade facing Lisbon and the 25 de Abril Bridge. The sanctuary grew from a national Catholic project and remains both a pilgrimage site and a prominent marker on the metropolitan skyline.

Queluz National Palace

Queluz National Palace developed in the eighteenth century as a royal and princely residence west of Lisbon. Rococo rooms, painted ceilings, tilework and period furniture reveal the ceremonial life of the court before and after the royal family's move to Brazil. Formal gardens extend through canals, fountains, sculpture and small pavilions, creating a close relationship between the decorated interiors and the planned outdoor estate.

Planted displays and conservation programs connect the aquarium's architecture with marine education.

Ponte 25 de Abril

The 25 de Abril Bridge spans the Tagus between Lisbon and Almada with a long red suspension structure carrying both road traffic and rail lines. Opened in 1966, it received its present name after Portugal's 1974 democratic revolution. Towers and cables dominate the estuary skyline, while the bridge's later rail conversion shows how a major piece of transport infrastructure adapted to metropolitan growth.

The frigate Dom Fernando II e Gloria was launched in the nineteenth century for service between Portugal and its overseas territories. After naval, training and ceremonial roles, a major fire left the vessel badly damaged before long restoration; now moored as a museum ship at Cacilhas, its decks, guns, cabins and equipment interpret maritime life without claiming uninterrupted preservation.

Perched atop the Monsanto Forest Park in Lisbon, Portugal, the Panorâmico de Monsanto is an abandoned structure with a storied past.
