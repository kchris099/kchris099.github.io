Évora

Évora grew from the Roman colony of Liberalitas Julia into a favoured residence of Portuguese kings during the Avis dynasty, when the Jesuit university and royal palace filled its walled centre. The Temple of Diana, cathedral, and sixteenth-century aqueduct span layers from antiquity through the Counter-Reformation. As capital of the Alentejo it administered wheat and cork estates, and its whitewashed houses and arcaded squares later gained UNESCO recognition.

Roman Temple of Évora

The Roman Temple of Évora stands on a raised podium in the historic center, its Corinthian columns surviving from the imperial period. The ruin sits outdoors beside later civic buildings, so the columns and podium platform are viewed from the surrounding square rather than from an enclosed hall or museum gallery.

Cathedral of Évora

Completed in 1746, Évora's Roman Catholic cathedral crowns the hill town and opens a tower climb for city views. Stone massing and the high nave define the skyline from surrounding streets. Exterior courts and the main interior stay viewable outside service times.

Giraldo Square Fountain

The fountain on Évora's Praça do Giraldo faces the church of St Antão in the city's central square, named for Giraldo Sem Pavor after the twelfth-century Christian reconquest. Water once arrived here from the Água de Prata aqueduct for public use. Cafés ring the paved square around the basin.

38.5710° N / 7.9097° W

Igreja e Mosteiro de São Francisco

Igreja e Mosteiro de São Francisco in Évora is a Gothic church linked to the Franciscan order and the reign of Manuel I. Beside the church, the Chapel of Bones lines walls with human skulls and bones gathered from overcrowded cemeteries. Twin experiences of nave and ossuary define the visit.

05 AdmissionFree

Ruínas Fingidas

Ruínas Fingidas, the Faux Ruins, are garden follies in Évora assembled from fragments of real antique masonry into staged false ruins. Paths wind among constructed arches and broken columns set for effect. The composition belongs to landscape taste that reused genuine debris as decorative scenery.

38.5676° N / 7.9098° W

07 AdmissionFree

Aqueduct Água de Prata

The Água de Prata Aqueduct brought water to Évora from springs northwest of the city after construction under King João III in the sixteenth century. Long arcades cross the countryside and enter the historic center, where houses and shops fill some lower arches. Several sections are walkable, while the full network of channels extends well beyond the most visible masonry spans.

38.5758° N / 7.9127° W

08 AdmissionFree

Alto de São Bento

Alto de São Bento is a viewpoint a short drive from Évora old town, looking over roofs, walls, and Alentejo countryside. Locals use the hill for picnics and sunset watching along the open crest. Panels on site outline landmarks across the historic skyline below.

38.5809° N / 7.9375° W

09 AdmissionFree

Great Dolmen of Zambujeiro

The Great Dolmen of Zambujeiro is a large Neolithic collective tomb outside Evora, formed by upright stone slabs, a chamber, and a long entrance corridor. Excavation exposed prehistoric burial architecture but also left the monument vulnerable, leading to protective covering and access controls. Its scale is important, though European size rankings require careful archaeological comparison.

38.5392° N / 8.0144° W

Almendres Cromlech

Built and altered during the Neolithic, the monument probably served changing ceremonial or social purposes, but exact astronomical claims remain debated. Almendres Cromlech gathers dozens of standing stones in an oval arrangement near Guadalupe west of Evora, and a rough rural access road reaches the cork-oak landscape, while calling it the peninsula's large site depends on definitions and preservation.

38.5575° N / 8.0612° W
