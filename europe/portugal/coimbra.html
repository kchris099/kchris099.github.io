Coimbra

Coimbra served as Portugal's medieval capital before Lisbon assumed that role and has remained a university city since studies were established here in 1290. The royal palace on the upper town became the university's core, with baroque Joanina Library and colleges stepping down toward the Mondego River. Roman cryptoporticus, the old cathedral, and student fado traditions record how Coimbra linked court politics, Catholic scholarship, and provincial administration in central Portugal.

Forum Coimbra

Near Santa Clara, Forum Coimbra holds more than 140 shops, a Carrefour hypermarket, 27 restaurants, and a six-screen cinema. Terraces overlook the city and the Mondego River. Late shopping hours run until 12:30 a.m., extending the mall's day well past ordinary closing times.

Praça da República

Praca da Republica lies between Coimbra's university quarter and lower commercial streets, serving as a gathering place for students, demonstrations, festivals and nightlife. Cafes, trees, paving and nearby cultural buildings frame the open space. Its daily identity comes from university life and public assembly.

New Cathedral of the Holy Name of Jesus (Coimbra)

Jesuits began Coimbra's New Cathedral in the late sixteenth century, creating a Mannerist church later enriched with Baroque furnishings; after the Jesuit expulsion and diocesan reorganization, it became the cathedral in the eighteenth century. A broad facade, barrel-vaulted nave, choir, chapels, and religious art reflect both institutional histories, while active worship shapes entry.

Old Cathedral of Saint Mary of Coimbra

High crenellated walls give Coimbra's Old Cathedral a fortress-like silhouette, with a main portal that catches warm evening light. Inside, a late-Gothic retable and a thirteenth-century cloister reward a careful walk. Exterior and principal interior spaces stay viewable when Mass is not underway.

Barbican Gate

Coimbra's Barbican Gate forms an outer defensive passage below the Almedina Gate, funneling pedestrians from the lower city into the old walled quarter. Stone arches, narrow turns, and adjoining buildings preserve the logic of medieval access despite later alterations.

Praça do Comércio

Praca do Comercio occupies an elongated space in Coimbra's lower town, historically associated with trade outside the hilltop university precinct. The Romanesque Church of Sao Tiago, Church of Sao Bartolomeu, arcades, cafes, and surrounding houses frame the square; modern shops continue the commercial role.

07 AdmissionFree

Santa Cruz Church

Santa Cruz Monastery was founded in the twelfth century and became an important religious and intellectual house of early Portugal. The church contains the monumental tombs of Kings Afonso Henriques and Sancho I, with Manueline sculpture, tiles, choir stalls, and cloister spaces added over time. Parish worship continues, while the surviving complex is more than a single Romanesque building.

40.2109° N / 8.4287° W

Jardim Botânico da Universidade de Coimbra

The University of Coimbra Botanical Garden dates from the eighteenth century within the historic university precinct (the school itself was settled in Coimbra in 1537). Terraces, glasshouses, and exotic plantings serve teaching and public walking. Paths drop from the upper town toward the Mondego through layered garden rooms.

Mosteiro de Santa Clara-a-Velha

Mosteiro de Santa Clara-a-Velha is an archaeological site featuring the Gothic ruins of a 1300s monastery, along with a travellers center. The monastery, located near the Mondego River, was abandoned and neglected for over 300 years, resulting in well-preserved yet partially submerged ruins. Travellers can explore the hulking remains, including crumbling cloisters and a dilapidated bell tower that now hosts music concerts and events.

Portugal dos Pequenitos

Portugal dos Pequenitos opened in Coimbra in 1940 as a miniature architectural park intended to teach children about Portuguese regions, monuments and former overseas territories. Small-scale houses, pavilions, gardens and interactive spaces combine family entertainment with ideas promoted during the Estado Novo period. The colonial displays require careful historical context.

40.2023° N / 8.4344° W

11 AdmissionFree

Monastery of Santa Clara-a-Nova

Poor Clare nuns moved to Santa Clara-a-Nova in the seventeenth century after repeated flooding from the Mondego damaged their older riverside monastery. The hilltop complex contains a large church, cloisters and former convent rooms overlooking Coimbra. The tomb of Queen Isabel of Aragon reinforces its connection with the city's royal and religious history.

40.2026° N / 8.4372° W

Jardins da Quinta das Lágrimas

Quinta das Lagrimas surrounds a historic Coimbra estate with woodland, formal gardens, channels, springs and later hotel development. Tradition associates the grounds with Ines de Castro and Pedro, though much of the romantic narrative was shaped by literature.

13 AdmissionFree

Ponte Pedonal Pedro e Inês

The Pedro e Ines footbridge crosses the Mondego with an offset central span and colored glass panels designed by Cecil Balmond and Adriao de Sousa. Opened in the early twenty-first century, it links riverside parks and walking routes on both banks. The angled meeting of its two halves gives the bridge a distinctive geometry while supporting everyday pedestrian circulation.

40.2012° N / 8.4270° W

14 AdmissionFree

Church of Santo António dos Olivais

The Church of Santo Antonio dos Olivais stands on a hill in Coimbra where Saint Anthony is traditionally associated with an early Franciscan community. The present eighteenth-century building rose over older religious foundations and contains blue-and-white tile panels illustrating episodes from the saint's life. Chapels and devotional spaces remain in active use, linking the neighborhood church with a wider pilgrimage tradition.
