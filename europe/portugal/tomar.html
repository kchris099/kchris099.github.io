Tomar

Tomar was established in the twelfth century within the lands granted to the Knights Templar, serving as their Portuguese headquarters during the Reconquista. After the Templars were dissolved, the Portuguese Order of Christ took over the town, playing an important role in funding Portugal's maritime discoveries in the fifteenth century. The town expanded in a grid pattern under Prince Henry the Navigator. This legacy of crusaders and explorers dominates the city, culminating in the monumental Convent of Christ, the Templar Castle, and the historic 15th-century synagogue.

Convent of Christ

The Convent of Christ grew from a Templar castle founded in the twelfth century into the headquarters of the Order of Christ, and protected status covers the full complex, where architecture reveals changing functions across a long institutional history. Its circular Charola, Manueline chapter-house window, cloisters and later Renaissance additions record centuries of military, religious and royal patronage.

Tomar castle

Tomar Castle surrounds the Convent of Christ with walls, towers, gates, and a fortified enclosure established by the Knights Templar in the twelfth century. Military architecture and the religious complex evolved together under the Order of Christ. The castle is not merely a separate scenic ruin.

Seven Hills National Forest

Also known as Mata dos Sete Montes, the woodland below and around Tomar's castle walls offers shaded nature walks on municipal paths. Lawns and tree cover keep the ground cooler than the open town streets. Locals treat the forest as an everyday green under the Templar hill.

Church Santa Maria do Olival

Built as a pantheon for Knights Templar leaders, Santa Maria do Olival holds the tomb of Tomar's founder, Gualdim Pais. The church's Templar associations give it weight beyond a standard parish. Exterior and main interior stay viewable outside service times.

Dam Castelo de Bode

Castelo de Bode Dam holds a large reservoir outside Tomar where shoreline paths support walking below and around the water. Nature routes and lake views define the visit more than castle towers. Day visitors come for the open water setting tied to the dam works.

Aqueduto do Convento de Cristo - Troço Pegões Altos

The Pegoes Aqueduct carried water across valleys and countryside to the Convent of Christ from the late sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries. Tall double-tiered arches form its most visible section west of Tomar, while lower channels continue beyond.

Igreja de São João Baptista

In Tomar's old town, this sixteenth-century church displays paintings by Gregório Lopes and seventeenth-century diamond-point azulejos inside. Tile and canvas give the nave its richest detail. Exterior and principal interior spaces remain open when Mass is not underway.

39.6036° N / 8.4146° W

08 AdmissionFree

Sinagoga de Tomar

Tomar's fifteenth-century synagogue preserves a small square prayer hall supported by four columns, with acoustic jars set into the vaulted ceiling. After the forced conversion or expulsion of Portuguese Jews, the building served several other uses. It later became the Abraham Zacuto Luso-Hebrew Museum, connecting the surviving architecture with the history of Jewish life in Portugal. The compact room conveys the scale of the former congregation.

39.6032° N / 8.4138° W

Park "Mouchão"

Park Mouchão occupies a river island green in Tomar, reached by short bridges from the town banks. Lawns, shade trees, and riverside paths make a compact picnic ground beside the Nabão, a quieter counterpoint to the Templar castle on the hill above and the convent streets that lead from the river into the old center.

39.6058° N / 8.4131° W
