Explore Antwerp

A Brief History

Antwerp rose as a North Sea port linked to the Scheldt River, gaining wealth in the 16th century as a center of printing, art, and diamond trade. The Cathedral of Our Lady and guild houses record that commercial era. Spanish and later Austrian rule reshaped civic institutions before Belgian independence in 1830. The port remains one of Europe's largest, while museums hold works by Rubens and other Flemish painters.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Antwerpen-Centraal Station

Constructed between 1895 and 1905, this monumental railway station features a grand stone terminus building and an expansive iron and glass train shed. It served as a vital hub for Belgian rail network expansion and international travel, remaining a major transit center and a symbol of the city's historical diamond trade.

Free

Sint-Annatunnel

Opened in 1933, this pedestrian and bicycle tunnel passes deep beneath the Scheldt River, connecting the left bank with the historic center. Built to provide passage without disrupting maritime traffic, it features original wooden escalators and serves as a functional piece of 1930s engineering history.

Het Steen

Dating back to the early 13th century, this medieval fortress is the city's oldest building, originally constructed to control access to the Scheldt River. It served as a prison and a defensive bastion for centuries, offering insights into the early origins and maritime history of the region.

Free

Antwerp City Hall

Completed in 1565, this Renaissance building dominates the Grote Markt. Its facade blends Flemish and Italian architectural styles, reflecting the city's wealth and international influence as a 16th-century trading hub. Having survived the Spanish Fury in 1576, it remains the seat of local government.

Free

Standbeeld Pieter Paul Rubens

Built in 1840 on the Groenplaats, this bronze statue honors Pieter Paul Rubens, the preeminent Baroque painter who lived and worked in the city. The monument commemorates his profound impact on art history and his role in defining the cultural prestige of the era.
