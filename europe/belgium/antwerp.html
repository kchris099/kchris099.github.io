Antwerp

Antwerp rose as a North Sea port linked to the Scheldt River, gaining wealth in the 16th century as a center of printing, art, and diamond trade. The Cathedral of Our Lady and guild houses record that commercial era. Spanish and later Austrian rule reshaped civic institutions before Belgian independence in 1830. The port remains one of Europe's largest, while museums hold works by Rubens and other Flemish painters.

Antwerpen-Centraal Station

Antwerpen-Centraal was built between 1895 and 1905 as a monumental stone terminus with a vast iron-and-glass train shed. The entrance hall's dome and staircases greet passengers before tracks fan out under the roof. Later tunnel works added through platforms beneath the historic head house without removing the original facade. Upper platforms under the roof remain among Europe's grandest rail interiors.

Sint-Annatunnel

The Sint-Annatunnel opened in 1933 as a pedestrian and bicycle tunnel under the Scheldt. Wooden escalators and tiled passages link the left bank with central Antwerp beneath the river shipping lane. Cyclists and walkers still use it as a weatherproof crossing when bridges and ferries are busy. Vintage wooden escalators are part of the crossing's preserved character.

Het Steen

Het Steen preserves part of Antwerp's medieval river fortress beside the Scheldt. A thirteenth-century gatehouse, walls and later additions recall its roles in controlling shipping, housing prisoners and supporting port administration. Modern adaptation added a visitor center and rooftop viewpoint, while the surrounding quays place the compact stronghold against the scale of the working harbor.

Antwerp City Hall

Antwerp City Hall on the Grote Markt was completed in 1565 in a Renaissance style mixing Flemish and Italian ideas. The stepped facade dominates the guildhalls around the square. Council chambers upstairs and ground-level arcades face the market where civic ceremonies still gather. Festival lighting washes the facade during major city celebrations.

Standbeeld Pieter Paul Rubens

Built in 1840 on the Groenplaats, this bronze statue honors Pieter Paul Rubens, the preeminent Baroque painter who lived and worked in the city. The monument commemorates his profound impact on art history and his role in defining the cultural prestige of the era.

Cathedral of Our Lady

Construction of Antwerp's Cathedral of Our Lady began in 1352 and continued for generations, producing a vast Gothic church with a single completed tower. The nave and chapels hold major altarpieces by Peter Paul Rubens alongside sculpture, stained glass, and memorials. Its spire remains a key point in the old-city skyline, while worship continues within the cathedral parish.

Sint-Carolus Borromeuskerk

Sint-Carolus Borromeuskerk keeps a Baroque facade and portico altar even after lightning destroyed Rubens's original ceiling. Marble and sculpture fill the Jesuit-founded interior near the Meir shopping street. The reconstructed ceiling and surviving artworks still draw visitors into the nave. The reconstructed ceiling still nods to Rubens even without the lost original.

Zoo Antwerpen

Antwerp Zoo opened in 1843 beside what later became Central Station, making it one of Europe's older urban zoological gardens. Historic Egyptian, Moorish and neoclassical pavilions stand among newer animal habitats, botanical planting and conservation facilities; the compact grounds contain mammals, birds, reptiles, fish and invertebrates while preserving traces of nineteenth-century zoo design.

Sint-Pauluskerk

Sint-Pauluskerk combines a Gothic body with a Baroque tower and a richly fitted interior in Antwerp's old town. Blue-tinged stained glass and carved woodwork line the aisles. The church stands near the Scheldt among lanes that once served sailors and merchants. Side chapels hold sculptures and paintings collected across several centuries.

Handelsbeurs Antwerpen

Merchants traded beneath the covered courtyard of Antwerp's Handelsbeurs, a building descended from the city's sixteenth-century exchange. The present nineteenth-century structure uses arcades, iron, glass and elaborate stonework after earlier halls were lost to fire; trading ended in 1997, but restoration reopened the interior for events, dining and public access to the former commercial heart.

51.2193° N / 4.4063° E

St. Andrew's Church

St Andrew's Church in Antwerp is a Baroque parish church with a tall tower above the Sint-Andrieskwartier. Interior fittings and side chapels record Counter-Reformation patronage, including Scottish and local merchant gifts. Outside services the nave and aisles are usually open for quiet visits.
