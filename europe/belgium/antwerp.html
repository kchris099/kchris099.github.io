Antwerp

Antwerp rose as a North Sea port linked to the Scheldt River, gaining wealth in the 16th century as a center of printing, art, and diamond trade. The Cathedral of Our Lady and guild houses record that commercial era. Spanish and later Austrian rule reshaped civic institutions before Belgian independence in 1830. The port remains one of Europe's largest, while museums hold works by Rubens and other Flemish painters.

Antwerpen-Centraal Station

Antwerpen-Centraal Station is a visitor site in Antwerp, Belgium. Construction and early use date to around 1895. Constructed between 1895 and 1905, this monumental railway station has a grand stone terminus building and an expansive iron and glass train shed. It served as a vital hub for Belgian rail network expansion and international travel, remaining a major transit center and a symbol of the city's historical diamond trade.

Het Steen

Het Steen is a visitor site in Antwerp. Dating back to the early 13th century, this medieval fortress is the city's oldest building, originally constructed to control access to the Scheldt River. It served as a prison and a defensive bastion for centuries, offering insights into the early origins and maritime history of the region.

Antwerp City Hall

Antwerp City Hall is a visitor site in Antwerp. Completed in 1565, this Renaissance building dominates the Grote Markt. Its facade blends Flemish and Italian architectural styles, reflecting the city's wealth and international influence as a 16th-century trading hub. Having survived the Spanish Fury in 1576, it remains the seat of local government.
