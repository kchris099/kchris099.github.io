Dinant

Dinant grew in the Middle Ages around the strategic crossing of the Meuse River, developing a successful brass and copper working industry known as dinanderie. The town suffered frequent sieges and destruction due to its border position between the Prince-Bishopric of Liège and the County of Namur, culminating in the devastating sack of 1466 by Charles the Bold. It also endured heavy combat during World War I. Today, the town's resilient history is embodied in the striking Collegiate Church of Notre Dame and the imposing Citadel towering above the river.

The Citadel of Dinant

Dinant Citadel sits on the cliff above the Meuse, a fortress remodelled in the nineteenth century over earlier medieval works. A cable car and a stair of more than four hundred steps climb from the riverfront to the barracks and museum galleries. From the terrace the town, rock church, and valley bridges lie directly below the gun platforms.

The house of Mister Sax

Maison Adolphe Sax on Dinant's riverfront commemorates the inventor of the saxophone, born here in 1814. Rooms display early instruments, patents, and documents from his Brussels workshop years. Giant saxophone sculptures along the quay mark the same shoreline, linking the small museum to the town's musical identity.

Grotte de Dinant La Merveilleuse

Grotte de Dinant La Merveilleuse is a cave located on the left bank of the Meuse, discovered in 1904. It has concretions in various colors such as pink, brown, blue, and bright white, along with subterranean waterfalls. Visitors can explore this show-cave through a guided tour and climb down a light stairway of 120 steps to witness its natural wonders.

Rocher Bayard

Rocher Bayard is a striking 40-meter-tall rock formation located on the right bank of the Meuse between Dinant and Anseremme. It is part of an limestone ridge and can be accessed by driving along Rue Defoin. According to local folklore, Rocher Bayard broke away due to the hooves of the legendary warhorse Bayard carrying four knights.
