Brussels

Brussels developed around a 10th-century fortress on the Senne River and became the capital of the Duchy of Brabant. Grand Place guild halls and the Gothic town hall date from the late Middle Ages. After Belgian independence in 1830 the city served as the national capital. Since the mid-20th century it has hosted European Union institutions. Art Nouveau buildings by Victor Horta line several central streets.

Grand-Place de Bruxelles

Brussels' Grand-Place is framed by guildhalls and the Gothic Town Hall around a rectangular market square. Documented as a trading ground since the tenth century, the ensemble was largely rebuilt after the 1695 French bombardment. Gilded facades and cobbles fill with flower markets, festivals, and evening crowds. Flower carpets occasionally cover the entire paving for special festivals.

Brussels Town Hall

A slender Gothic tower and decorated facade distinguish Brussels Town Hall on the Grand-Place. Construction proceeded in stages during the fifteenth century, producing an intentionally asymmetrical civic building crowned by Saint Michael; guided interiors reveal council rooms, galleries, tapestries, and later decorative programs that record the city's government and ceremonial history.

Parc du Cinquantenaire

King Leopold II commissioned Parc du Cinquantenaire in 1880 for Belgium's fiftieth independence anniversary. A U-shaped colonnade and triumphal arch link museums of art, history, and military collections. Lawns and avenues fill the space between the arches and the surrounding European Quarter streets. Joggers loop the avenues before museum doors open.

Royal Palace of Brussels

Official working palace of Belgium's monarch on Place des Palais, though the royal family resides elsewhere. The present neoclassical facade dates from after 1900 under Leopold II. Summer openings allow public entry to state rooms where ceremonies and receptions are held each year.

Galeries Royales Saint-Hubert

The Galeries Royales Saint-Hubert opened in 1847 to Jean-Pierre Cluysenaar's design as a glass-roofed shopping arcade. Luxury shops, cafes, and a theater line one of Europe's earliest covered passages. Three linked galleries run between the Grand-Place area and the northern shopping streets. Chocolate and bookshop windows line the glass-covered passages.

Royal Gallery of Saint Hubert

Opened in 1847, the Royal Saint-Hubert Galleries form a glass-roofed passage through central Brussels. The King's, Queen's and Princes' galleries combine shops, cafes, theaters, apartments and cultural venues behind matching neoclassical facades; their covered route linked commercial streets near the Grand-Place and became an influential model for later European shopping arcades.

More than 250 European and American cars fill Autoworld in Brussels' Parc du Cinquantenaire, tracing motoring from the late nineteenth century forward. Exhibits sit inside an iron-and-glass hall built for the 1897 World's Fair. The collection reads as a chronological automobile museum.

Grand Place

Grand Place is a massive city square surrounded by elegant historic buildings dating back to the 14th century. It is the all activities in Brussels, featuring the Town Hall with a statue of St. Michael the Archangel and the Maison du Roi, both showing Gothic architecture. The square hosts the Infiorata event every even year, where it's covered with a carpet of flowers from over five hundred thousand begonia plants.

Saint Nicholas Church stands behind commercial facades close to the Brussels Stock Exchange. Medieval fabric survives within a building repeatedly altered after collapse, bombardment and restoration. The irregular exterior reflects shops historically built against the church walls, while the interior contains Gothic stonework, chapels, devotional art and a painting associated with Rubens' workshop.

Atomium

The Atomium, a colossal stainless steel structure in Brussels, was the centerpiece of the 1958 Worlds Fair. It symbolizes peace, progress, and an optimistic vision of the future. The design is based on the elemental structure of iron and represents a shift towards using atomic energy for positive purposes. Initially intended as a temporary installation, it captured the hearts of Belgians and has become one of Brussels' most known attractions.

Brussels Royal Palace

The Royal Palace of Brussels is the administrative residence and workplace for the Belgian royal family, although they do not live there. The palace, dating back to King Leopold II, has reception rooms including the opulent Throne Room and the Mirror Room adorned with a ceiling covered in jeweled scarab beetles. A recent renovation has left its exterior gleaming.

An equestrian statue of Godfrey of Bouillon occupies the center of Place Royale, the neoclassical square laid out above the former Coudenberg palace. Installed in 1848, the bronze figure shows the crusader leader as a national historical hero. Museums, the Church of Saint James, and uniform eighteenth-century facades frame the monument and connect the royal quarter with Mont des Arts.

Mont des Arts

Terraced gardens descend from the royal quarter toward central Brussels at Mont des Arts. The present layout replaced an earlier temporary garden and was completed during the mid-twentieth-century redevelopment of the hillside. Fountains, clipped planting, stairs, libraries, museums and cultural buildings structure the route, with the lower terrace opening a long view toward the old city.

Parc de Bruxelles

Brussels Park occupies the formal space between the Royal Palace and the Belgian Parliament. Laid out in the late eighteenth century over the former grounds of the Coudenberg palace, it later opened for public use. Broad avenues, clipped lawns, fountains, sculptures and pavilions retain the neoclassical plan. The grounds also recall fighting during the Belgian Revolution of 1830.

Cambre Woods

Bois de la Cambre, laid out in 1861 as a naturalistic landscape park, links to the Sonian Forest at Brussels' southern edge. Lakes, lawns, and riding paths give city residents a large green escape. Cafes near the water and looping roads suit both short strolls and longer rides. A lake island and boathouse give the park a focal point near the main lawns.

Public areas include the Hemicycle, Parlamentarium and House of European History, where exhibits explain elections, lawmaking and the institution's multilingual work. National Basilica of the Sacred Heart in Koekelberg

The National Basilica of the Sacred Heart crowns the Koekelberg plateau in Brussels as a vast Art Deco church. Its green copper dome is visible across the western districts; the nave and galleries open outside Mass for visits. Built as a national Catholic memorial church, it differs in scale and style from the older Gothic churches downtown.

Zinneke Pis

Tom Frantzen installed Zinneke Pis in 1998 at a street corner near the Brussels center. The small bronze dog lifts its leg against a bollard, extending the city's playful group of urinating statues beyond Manneken Pis and Jeanneke Pis. Its name uses a Brussels term linked to mixed ancestry and local identity, giving the joke a specifically urban reference.
