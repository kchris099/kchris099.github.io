Ghent

Ghent was one of Europe's largest cities by 1300, when Flemish cloth weavers and grain traders built the Belfry, St Bavo's Abbey, and canals that moved wool to England. The 1447 revolt against Philip the Good and later Calvinist unrest showed the city's assertive commune before Spanish and Austrian rule. Nineteenth-century industry along the Lys and Leie rivers made Ghent a textile and port centre again, layering factories beside medieval towers in Flanders.

Belfry of Gent

Ghent's Belfry rose between the fourteenth and sixteenth centuries as a symbol of municipal independence. A dragon weather vane crowns the tower above the old cloth hall, while bells and a carillon once regulated work, warnings and public ceremony. Interior displays explain the city's privileges and bell mechanisms and upper levels look across the cathedral, churches and dense historic center.

Gent City Hall

Ghent City Hall joins a late Gothic wing to a Renaissance facade built as tastes shifted mid-construction. The contrast of styles on one block shows the city's wealth across those decades. Civic offices still occupy parts of the complex beside the Belfry and Saint Bavo's precinct. Guided tours explain why the two wings never fully matched in style.

Saint Bavo's Cathedral

Saint Bavo's Cathedral is Ghent's principal church, known for housing the Ghent Altarpiece by the van Eyck brothers. A Romanesque crypt underlies later Gothic nave and tower fabric beside the Belfry. Outside services, visitors enter to see chapels, the choir, and the altarpiece chapel under timed viewing rules.

Museum of Industry Ghent

The Museum of Industry Ghent invites travellers to look at the fascinating narrative of industrialization and the textile sector in Belgium. Located a historic cotton-spinning mill, this museum is a portal to the past, showing how industry and technology have evolved from the 18th century onward. Guests can wander through diverse exhibits that highlight machinery, tools, and artifacts important to advancements in textile production and other vital industries.

Castle of the Counts

Philip of Alsace built the present Gravensteen around 1180 as a fortified residence for the counts of Flanders; stone walls, battlements, towers, a gatehouse and a moat form an unusually complete castle within central Ghent. Museum rooms interpret medieval power, weapons, justice and later industrial uses, while the rooftop walk opens views over surrounding streets and waterways.

Huis van Alijn

A restored almshouse holds Huis van Alijn in Ghent, with recreated shops and exhibits of twentieth-century local life: toys, packaging, household goods, and nostalgic games. Visitors walk the former children's hospice rooms. The museum ties daily Belgian culture to a charitable building fabric.

Korenlei

Korenlei lines the Lys in Ghent with stepped guild houses facing the Graslei across the water. Stone facades, boat traffic, and cafe terraces fill the quay that once handled grain trade. Evening reflections double the gables when river tour boats pass between the bridges. Evening light turns the opposite Graslei facades into a continuous reflection.

St Michael's Bridge

St Michael's Bridge crosses the Lys in central Ghent, giving classic views toward St Michael's Church and the Graslei quays. Pedestrian decks and adjacent streets form the usual viewpoints for the canal skyline. The stone arch bridge links Korenlei and the university quarter without carrying a separate toll plaza.

Saint Michael's Church

Builders once planned Saint Michael's Church in Ghent taller than Antwerp's cathedral, but funds stopped the tower at 23 meters. Exterior and main interior spaces remain viewable outside services. The truncated height is part of the church's visible story on the skyline.

STAM - Ghent City Museum

Permanent galleries titled The Story of Ghent at STAM walk from medieval roots to the modern knowledge city inside the Ghent City Museum. Rooms open on posted hours for curated city history. The museum sits as a contemporary civic narrative rather than a single palace tour.

Bourgoyen-Ossemeersen

Wet grasslands, marshes, ditches and reed beds cover Bourgoyen-Ossemeersen on the western edge of Ghent. Seasonal flooding attracts large numbers of waterbirds, especially during migration and winter. Marked loops cross the reserve on mostly level paths, with viewing points and a visitor center explaining meadow management, birdlife and the relationship between the low-lying landscape and nearby city.
