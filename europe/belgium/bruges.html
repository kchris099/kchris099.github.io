Bruges

Cloth exports through the Zwin made Bruges a thirteenth- and fourteenth-century financial capital; belfries, the Béguinage, and canalside warehouses date from that boom. When the harbour silted, the city shrank until nineteenth-century antiquarians rediscovered the medieval streetscape and lace workshops returned around the old guild halls. The Markt and Burg squares concentrate that cloth-era civic architecture in a few blocks.

Belfry of Bruges

The Belfry of Bruges rises above the Markt with a treasure chamber and a carillon of 47 bells. Climbing 366 steps reaches galleries with views over the canals and stepped gables. The tower has signaled civic time and alarms since the medieval cloth hall complex grew around its base. Carillon concerts spill melody over the Markt on scheduled afternoons.

Market Square

Bruges' Markt has functioned as a center of trade and civic life since the medieval period. The Belfry and cloth halls dominate one side, while the Provincial Court, guild-inspired facades and statues of Jan Breydel and Pieter de Coninck frame the remaining edges. Markets, processions, carriage traffic and public events continue across the broad paved square.

Basilica of the Holy Blood

Twelfth-century Romanesque fabric at the Basilica of the Holy Blood sits on Bruges' Burg among civic buildings. Exterior and main interior spaces stay readable outside services. Relic devotion and the upper chapel define the basilica's role on the square.

De Burg

Administrative power in medieval Bruges concentrated around Burg Square, beside the older market district. The Gothic town hall, Basilica of the Holy Blood, former civil registry, and liberty of Bruges present closely packed facades from several periods. Narrow passages link the square with canals and the Markt, while municipal functions remain active in the historic buildings.

Boniface Bridge

Boniface Bridge (Bonifaciusbrug) is a early-20th-century pedestrian bridge that offers romantic views of the canal and the town. Located near the Church of the Blessed Virgin Mary, it is one of Bruges' most photographed spots. The area around the bridge, including Arents Courtyard, provides scenes with its historic buildings and horse-drawn carriages.

Church of Our Lady

The Church of Our Lady, located in Bruges, Belgium, is a example of Gothic architecture with a soaring tower that reaches 115.6 meters high. Dating back to the 13th century, it holds great historical and artistic significance. The church is home to several notable art pieces, including Michelangelo's 'Madonna and Child,' a rare work that left Italy during the artist's lifetime.

Rosary Quay

Rosary Quay in Bruges is a spot that captures the historic charm of the city. Named after the place where nuns used to sell rosaries, it offers views of canals, bridges, weeping willow trees, and medieval architecture. Travellers can stroll through a passageway under the Palace of the Liberty of Bruges to reach the canal and began on boat tours.

Kruispoort gate

Twin round towers and a pointed central passage define Kruispoort, one of four surviving gates from Bruges' medieval defenses. The present brick structure dates mainly from the early fifteenth century and once controlled the eastern road toward the coast and Low Countries; traffic still passes beneath the arch, with canal banks and windmills marking the former line of the city walls.

Belgium Pier

A long walkway projects from Blankenberge beach to a circular pavilion above the North Sea at Belgium Pier. The current structure replaced an earlier Belle Epoque pier and opened in the 1930s, later undergoing repeated repairs after war and storm damage. Sea views, weather exposure, dining, and temporary displays define the visit more than historic interior rooms.

Jan van Eyckplein opens beside Bruges' canals where the Spiegelrei meets the old harbor basin. A statue of the painter stands among guild houses and cafe terraces facing the water. Boat tours pass the square while walkers link the Markt with the northern canal belt. Canal boats idle at the quay while terraces serve drinks facing the water.

Ezelpoort

Ezelpoort is one of four remaining medieval gates that once controlled trade into Bruges. Twin towers and a passage over the moat still mark the approach from the northwest. Cyclists and cars share the opening that once checked carts entering the cloth town. A remnant of the city ramparts still runs beside the water outside the gate.

Huisbrouwerij De Halve Maan

Operating since 1856, Huisbrouwerij De Halve Maan in Bruges brews Brugse Zot and Straffe Hendrik with tours through modern halls, a historic museum, and a roof view over the city. Family ownership ties the brand to the canal quarter. Tasting rooms close the visit after the production walk.

Sashuis

The gabled Sashuis stands at the edge of Minnewater where water control was once essential to Bruges' canals and lake. Dating from the early sixteenth century, the building housed the operator of the sluice system. Its brick facade and small tower now form part of the approach to the beguinage, with nearby paths following the water and former defensive boundary.

Gunpowder Tower (Poertoren)

Poertoren rises beside Minnewater as the surviving round tower of a late-fourteenth-century water gate. Thick brick walls later made it suitable for storing gunpowder, which gave the structure its present name. The closed tower is viewed from the Lovers' Bridge and surrounding paths, where its austere profile marks the southern edge of medieval Bruges.

Concertgebouw Brugge

Concertgebouw Brugge is a modern venue with sleek architecture that hosts a variety of events including concerts, dance performances, plays, and art exhibits. The large red concert hall was completed in 2002 during Bruges' time as the European Capital of Culture. Despite its controversial modern design, the venue is known for its high-quality programming.
