Pula

Rome built a stone amphitheatre here when Pula was a colony on the Istrian coast. Venetian and Austro-Hungarian navies later used the same harbour. The arena and the naval arsenal, more than any medieval commune hall, explain why Pula mattered as a military port rather than a merchant republic. Film festivals now fill the amphitheatre that once staged Roman games.

Pula Arena

Construction developed from the Augustan period through an early century CE, when the arena held gladiatorial contests and other public events. Pula's amphitheater preserves a complete outer ring of Roman arches beside the modern harbor. Four projecting towers, underground service passages, and limestone seating remains explain how the building functioned, while concerts and festivals continue to use the open interior.

Zerostrasse

Zerostrasse is Pula's network of early-twentieth-century underground tunnels and galleries dug as civil shelters. Cool stone passages now open for guided walks beneath the hill near the Roman arena, showing how the Austro-Hungarian naval port prepared for aerial and naval attack.

Pula Citadel

Venetians built the hilltop Kastel, or Pula Citadel, in the 1600s above the Old Town and bay. Walls and towers open views across the city when grounds are accessible. Courtyard routes lead visitors through the fortress rather than a single interior museum wing.

Mosaic The Punishment of Dirce

The Roman mosaic known as The Punishment of Dirce lies in central Pula, exposed after World War II bombing disturbed the ground above it. Mythological figures fill the floor in coloured tesserae, and a protective shelter now lets visitors view the pavement without walking on the ancient surface.

Temple of Augustus

Raised between 27 BC and AD 14, the Temple of Augustus uses regular square stone blocks typical of Roman work in Pula. Later centuries turned it into a Byzantine church, then a granary, and now a museum of stone monuments. The exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside service or exhibit changeovers.

Arch of the Sergii

The Arch of the Sergii is a Roman triumphal arch built about 29-27 BC as a city gate honouring the Sergii family. Carved detail still faces the street that leads toward the forum area, and the monument remains one of Pula's clearest free-standing Roman markers.

44.8683° N / 13.8470° E

Aquarium Pula

Marine exhibits fill the corridors and rooms of Fort Verudela, an Austro-Hungarian coastal defense built near Pula in the late nineteenth century; a sea-turtle rescue center adds rehabilitation and education work, linking regional conservation with the reused military structure. Aquarium Pula shows Adriatic, Mediterranean, freshwater, and tropical species while retaining much of the fortress plan.

44.8352° N / 13.8331° E

Verudela Canyon

Waves have cut Verudela Canyon into the limestone edge of the peninsula south of Pula. Steep rock walls frame a narrow inlet reached by rough coastal paths, with open sea beyond the entrance. The exposed setting has little formal infrastructure, and wind, wet stone and changing water conditions shape access around the rim and shore.

44.8360° N / 13.8287° E

09 AdmissionFree

Church of St. Fosca

Fosca, located near Vodnjan in Istria, is a spiritual destination surrounded by ancient olive trees. Local legends speak of miraculous events associated with this small church, adding to its mystical allure. Visitors have reported experiencing a strong energy field connecting the Brijuni islands to the church. While part of a larger bike or walking tour, the church can also be reached by car despite limited parking.

44.9787° N / 13.7890° E

Chiavalon Extra Virgin Olive Oil

Chiavalon Extra Virgin Olive Oil is produced by the Chiavalon family, who have turned their 100-tree memorial into a growing business with 9,300 trees over 35 hectares. Their modern mill produces a portfolio of six extra virgin olive oils that are enjoyed on tables in Istria and exported to over 23 countries.
