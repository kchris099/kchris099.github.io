Rijeka

Rijeka developed around a deep Adriatic inlet that Romans called Tarsatica and that Habsburg emperors later made their principal naval outlet to the sea. Hungarian-Croatian corpus separatum status after 1868 brought rail links, shipyards, and a cosmopolitan port where Italian, Croatian, and Hungarian communities competed. Twentieth-century border changes, the Free State of Fiume episode, and Yugoslav then Croatian rule reshaped institutions while Korzo promenade and Trsat hill churches frame the harbour.

Trsat Castle

Trsat Castle occupies a ridge above Rijeka and the Rječina gorge on a site used for defense since antiquity. The Frankopan family and later Habsburg authorities shaped the medieval fortress, while nineteenth-century restoration under Laval Nugent added romantic elements and a family mausoleum. Towers, courtyards, walls, and terraces overlook the port city and Kvarner Bay.

Shrine of Mary of God of Trsat

Pilgrims have traveled to the Shrine of Our Lady of Trsat for centuries, climbing the Petar Kružić stairs from Rijeka or approaching by road. The Franciscan complex grew around a Marian image traditionally linked to Pope Urban V, with later Baroque rebuilding, chapels, cloisters, and votive offerings.

Maiden with the Seagull

The Maiden with the Seagull statue was erected in 1956 on Opatija's Lungomare, a young woman with a bird facing the Adriatic. The sculpture became a shoreline emblem of the resort town's promenade. Walkers pass it along the coastal path among hotels and sea walls rather than in a formal hilltop park.

Sports Hall Marino Cvetkovic covers about 11,000 square meters with reception, locker rooms, meeting rooms, and a restaurant. A VIP terrace, medical office, and wellness centre sit in the same complex. Quays and embankment paths nearby link the hall to Rijeka's waterfront walking routes.

City Clock Tower

Rijeka's City Clock Tower, also called Gradski Toranj, began as a medieval gate from the port into the town. After an earthquake, architect Filbert Bazarig rebuilt it as a tower in the late 1700s while keeping the original entrance arch. The clock face and arch still mark the old harbour approach.

Rijeka Tunnel

A concrete air-raid passage runs beneath central Rijeka between the cathedral area and Dolac. Italian military authorities built the roughly 330-metre tunnel during the Second World War to protect civilians and personnel from bombing. Its plain walls, side chambers, signs, and changing temperature preserve the practical character of a shelter, while current public opening depends on city programming and maintenance.

Petar Kružić Stairway

Petar Kružić Stairway, located near the Rjecina River in the city center, is a historic pilgrimage route leading to the Church of Lady Trsat at the top of the hill. Built in 1531, this stairway consists of over 500 steps and offers views of Rijeka and its harbor. While it can be quite a challenge, especially during summer, reaching the top rewards travellers with panoramas.

Church of Our Lady of Lourdes

Capuchin friars built the Church of Our Lady of Lourdes opposite Rijeka's railway station between 1904 and 1929, and the intended upper tower was never completed, leaving the present front asymmetrical compared with early plans. A red-and-white neo-Gothic facade, twin-level design, pointed arches, sculpture, and patterned masonry distinguish it from the surrounding transport district.

Torpedo - Launch Station

A weathered concrete launch ramp extends into Kvarner Bay beside the former Whitehead torpedo factory in Rijeka, and ruined industrial structures communicate the city's engineering history, but unstable surfaces and restricted areas require cautious access. The site recalls nineteenth-century collaboration between inventor Giovanni Luppis and engineer Robert Whitehead, whose self-propelled naval weapon was developed and tested here.
