Zagreb

Zagreb grew from twin medieval settlements on the Sava slopes into the capital of Croatia after union with the Habsburg monarchy and later Yugoslav federation. Baroque churches on Kaptol, the secular Gradec upper town, and 19th-century Lower Town blocks record Catholic institutions, Austro-Hungarian administration, and modern Croatian statehood. Municipal archives, parish records, and surviving architecture help explain how the city connected to wider regional trade routes, court patronage, and modern civic rebuilding after industrial and wartime change.

Croatian National Theatre

The Croatian National Theatre in Zagreb is housed in a grand neo-Baroque building dating back to 1895. Designed by the Viennese firm Hellmer and Fellner, it was opened by Emperor Franz Josef himself. The theater's stately interior can be admired during its opera, ballet, and drama performances. Outside the theater stands Ivan Strovic's lifelike sculpture 'Fountain of Life,' depicting four couples around a small pool of water.

Ban Josip Jelačić Statue

The equestrian statue of Ban Josip Jelačić occupies Zagreb's main square, a common meeting point day and night. Pedestrian streets around the plaza stay car-free. The open paving lets crowds gather at the monument without separating it behind fences.

Zagreb 360° observation deck

A viewing floor near the top of the 1959 Zagreb skyscraper on Ban Jelačić Square provides a high central outlook over the capital. The tower was an early modern high-rise in Yugoslavia, and its upper level has operated under names including Zagreb 360°; roofs of the Lower Town, cathedral spires, the Upper Town, Medvednica, and newer districts appear in different directions when visibility is clear.

Trg Kralja Tomislava

An equestrian statue of Croatia's first crowned king stands at the center of Trg Kralja Tomislava, the southern end of Zagreb's Green Horseshoe. Lawns and flower beds extend between the main railway station and the yellow Art Pavilion. Outdoor concerts, seasonal installations, public gatherings, and winter skating periodically change the square, while its formal nineteenth-century layout remains visible.

Stone Gate

A thirteenth-century portal in Zagreb's Upper Town once formed part of the Gradec fortifications. A small chapel to the Virgin Mary now occupies the passage, where pilgrims leave candles and votive plaques. Pedestrians still move through the gate between Gradec streets as everyday traffic shares the sacred threshold.

St. Mark's Church

St Mark's Church on Markov trg shows a brightly tiled roof with the coats of arms of Croatia, Dalmatia, Slavonia, and Zagreb. The parish fabric goes back to the thirteenth century, with later Gothic and nineteenth-century work. It faces the Croatian Parliament and government buildings on the Upper Town square.

Lotrščak Tower

Lotrscak Tower is a thirteenth-century Romanesque tower built to guard the southern gate of Gradec in Zagreb's Upper Town. A noon cannon still fires from the upper level, and the climb gives views over the red roofs toward the Lower Town and cathedral spires.

Arena Zagreb

Opened around 2009 for the World Handball Championships, Arena Zagreb seats about 15,000 spectators. The hall has since hosted other sports fixtures and international pop concerts under the same roof. Event access follows posted ticket and gate procedures around the modern arena shell on show days.

Cathedral of Zagreb

Earlier churches occupied the site, but the present appearance owes much to reconstruction after the 1880 earthquake and continuing repairs following the 2020 earthquake. Twin Gothic Revival spires rise above Zagreb's medieval Kaptol district from the Cathedral of the Assumption. Stone portals, chapels, tombs, liturgical art, and the long nave record its role as the center of Croatia's Roman Catholic archdiocese.

Medvedgrad

Medvedgrad, a medieval fortress located in Zagreb, has the chance to explore its intriguing history and surroundings. The castle provides a full experience for those who enjoy hiking through the forest to reach its top, where the Queen well serves refreshing mountain water. Visitors can also immerse themselves in the legend of the Black Queen by watching motion pictures projected on the walls.

Zoo Zagreb

Zagreb Zoo occupies grounds inside Maksimir Park, with enclosures for lions, lemurs, red pandas, capybaras, tapirs, wolves, wallabies, and many birds, fish, reptiles, and insects. Paths weave under park trees, so a zoo visit doubles as a walk through the larger historic park.

13 AdmissionFree

Park Maksimir

Maksimir opened to the public in the late eighteenth century and later developed as a Romantic landscape park on Zagreb's eastern side; historic structures such as the Echo Pavilion and Bellevue remain scattered through terrain used for walking, rowing, sport and everyday recreation. Wooded paths, meadows, streams, five lakes, pavilions and the city's zoo occupy a broad area much larger than a formal garden.

45.8244° N / 16.0176° E

14 AdmissionFree

Bundek City Park

Bundek City Park, situated in the southwestern part of Zagreb, is a popular destination for both locals and tourists. Spanning approximately 45 hectares, this urban oasis centers around the Bundek Lake. During the summer months, it attracts visitors seeking relaxation and recreation. The park offers various activities such as picnicking by the lake, fishing, feeding swans, and enjoying playgrounds and rides for children.
