Split

Split grew within the palace Emperor Diocletian built for his retirement after 305 CE, whose corridors and mausoleum later became the medieval cathedral and civic core. Byzantine, Venetian, and Austrian rulers adapted the Roman shell into houses, markets, and fortifications facing the Adriatic. The waterfront Riva and palace cellars still organise the city, while ferries to the islands extend a role Split has played for seventeen centuries as Dalmatia's main harbour.

Klis Fortress

Hilltop Fortress Klis sits by the village of Klis about nine kilometers from Split. Centuries of fortification left walls and courtyards with long views over the pass. When open, visitor routes follow towers and ramparts rather than a reconstructed interior palace.

Church of Our Lady

Church of Our Lady near Split dates from around 1937 as a parish church outside Diocletian's Palace core. The exterior and main interior can usually be viewed outside service times from the public nave. Its twentieth-century fabric contrasts with the Roman and medieval churches clustered inside the palace walls and peristyle.

Diocletian's Palace

Roman emperor Diocletian built a fortified palace beside the Adriatic around the turn of the fourth century for his retirement; later residents adapted its streets, gates, cellars, temples and defensive walls into the living center of Split. Homes, shops, churches and squares now occupy the ancient fabric, with the cathedral rising from Diocletian's former mausoleum.

43.5083° N / 16.4402° E

Jupiter's Temple

A barrel-vaulted chamber beside the Peristyle began as the Temple of Jupiter within Diocletian's Palace. Christians later converted it into the baptistery of St John, adding a medieval font and religious sculpture while retaining Roman stone carving and a coffered ceiling. An Egyptian sphinx stands outside the richly ornamented doorway, linking the small building with the palace's broader imperial decoration.

43.5083° N / 16.4396° E

Diocletian's Cellars

Diocletian's Cellars form the substructure of the emperor's palace in Split, built around AD 300 as basement vaults under the imperial apartments. Stone arches and corridors open toward the Riva waterfront and support the peristyle above. The vaults show Roman engineering under the later medieval town that grew inside the palace walls.

43.5075° N / 16.4398° E

Split Riva

Split's Riva is the palm-lined waterfront promenade outside Diocletian's Palace, where cafes face the harbour and ferry traffic. Evening crowds walk the full length between palace gates and the marina, treating the quay as the city's main outdoor living room.

43.5078° N / 16.4382° E

Split Old Town Hall

Split's Old Town Hall on the People's Square dates to 1443, with Gothic windows and an open loggia facing the plaza. The building anchors civic gatherings beside Diocletian's Palace walls, and the square remains a daily meeting ground between the harbour and the palace streets.

43.5090° N / 16.4390° E

Golden Gate

The Golden Gate is the northern Roman entrance to Diocletian's Palace, a fourth-century stone portal that once faced the road toward Salona. A niche above later held a statue of Bishop Gregory of Nin, and the gate still opens onto the cardo leading into the palace grid.

43.5092° N / 16.4407° E

Green Market

Beside the east wall of Diocletian's Palace, Split's Green Market sells fruit, vegetables, and meats from local farms. Outdoor stalls fill the paving during regular market hours. Old Town streets feed directly into the produce rows without a separate indoor hall.

43.5075° N / 16.4417° E

Poljud Stadium

Built for the 1979 Mediterranean Games, Poljud Stadium seats about 34,000 for football matches and large events in Split. Music festivals and civic gatherings also fill the bowl when the calendar allows. Gate procedures follow posted ticketing rules on event days around the seaside arena.

43.5195° N / 16.4319° E
