Prague

Prague became the seat of Bohemian kings and, from 1344, an archbishopric centered on Prague Castle above the Vltava River. Charles IV expanded the New Town and founded Charles University in 1348. Hussite wars and Habsburg rule added Gothic, Renaissance, and baroque fabric across the river districts. Czechoslovak independence in 1918 and later division in 1993 kept Prague as the national capital; its historic center is a UNESCO site.

Prague Castle

Ninth-century beginnings put Prague Castle on the ridge above the Vltava as a long seat of Bohemian rulers. Kings, Holy Roman emperors, and later Czechoslovak and Czech presidents used the complex. Today it is the official residence and workplace of the Czech president, and locals still say "Hrad" for the office around him.

St. Vitus Cathedral

Construction of St Vitus Cathedral began in 1344 within Prague Castle and continued, with long interruptions, until the twentieth century. Gothic chapels, stained glass, royal tombs, sculpture and the St Wenceslas Chapel fill the metropolitan church of Saints Vitus, Wenceslas and Adalbert; coronations and burials tied it closely to Bohemian monarchy, and it remains the seat of Prague's archbishop.

Zámecké schody

The Castle Stairs climb from Malá Strana toward Prague Castle along a steep route between walls and historic houses. Landings open toward red roofs, domes and the city across the Vltava as elevation increases. The present stone stair is a practical pedestrian connection as well as a scenic approach, with crowds, ice and uneven surfaces affecting the ascent at different times of year.

Church of Our Lady Victorious and The Infant Jesus of Prague

A small wax-coated wooden figure of the Christ Child draws pilgrims to the Church of Our Lady Victorious in Malá Strana, and princess Polyxena of Lobkowicz donated the Spanish-origin statue to the Discalced Carmelites in 1628, and changing embroidered robes became part of its devotional tradition. The Baroque church also contains chapels, paintings, and a museum of garments associated with the Infant Jesus of Prague.

Charles Bridge

Sixteen stone arches carry Charles Bridge across the Vltava between Prague's Old Town and Malá Strana. Charles IV ordered construction after the earlier Judith Bridge was damaged by flood, with work beginning in 1357 and continuing into the fifteenth century. Baroque statues were later added along the parapets, while bridge towers, river views, artists, and heavy foot traffic define its present public life.

Prague Astronomical Clock

Mounted on Prague's Old Town Hall, the astronomical clock combines a medieval astronomical dial with a calendar display and animated figures. Core working components date from 1410, while later additions created the hourly procession of apostles. Symbols track time, the sun, moon, zodiac and feast days through a visual system that rewards attention beyond the brief mechanical show.

Church of Our Lady before Tyn

Dark twin spires rise behind Old Town Square from the Church of Our Lady before Týn. The Gothic church developed from the fourteenth century as the principal parish for merchants and residents of Prague's Old Town, later becoming associated with the Hussite movement. Baroque furnishings, tombs, paintings, and the grave of astronomer Tycho Brahe occupy an interior reached through the surrounding building line.

Prague Metronome

A red kinetic arm swings above Letná on the stone base once occupied by a giant Stalin monument. Vratislav Novák installed the Prague Metronome in 1991 after the communist period, using repeated movement to suggest the passage of time. The terrace also provides open views across bridges and the Old Town, while skateboarders and public gatherings give the former propaganda site an informal civic life.

Franz Kafka

The Head of Franz Kafka, also known as the Statue of Kafka, is an outdoor kinetic sculpture by David Černý depicting Czech German-language writer Franz Kafka, installed on 31 October 2014 outside of the Quadrio shopping mall in Prague, Czech Republic. The price was 30 million crowns, paid by the investor CPI Property Group together with the adjacent Quadrio complex. Kafka himself worked in the nearby building of a saving bank.

Statue of Saint Wenceslas Riding an Upside-Down Dead Horse

David Černý's sculpture in Lucerna Passage turns the familiar monument to Saint Wenceslas upside down. The patron saint sits on the belly of a dead, inverted horse suspended from the ceiling, directly echoing Josef Václav Myslbek's equestrian statue in nearby Wenceslas Square. Its satire is intentionally open-ended, inviting readings about Czech politics, national symbols, authority, and public memory.

Petrin Tower

On Petrin Hill, a slender observation tower modelled after the Eiffel Tower overlooks Prague's skyline. A funicular climbs the hill, then 299 wooden steps lead to the top platform. From there, Charles Bridge, the Dancing House, and Prague Castle sit in a single sweep of rooftops.

Dancing House

The Dancing House on the Vltava embankment was designed by Vlado Milunic and Frank Gehry as a pair of towers that lean and curve against Prague's older street wall. Offices and a rooftop restaurant occupy the deconstructivist forms that broke from the city's historicist skyline in the 1990s.

Lennon Wall

After John Lennon's death, fans covered a wall in Prague's Mala Strana with graffiti, lyrics, and peace messages that the communist authorities repeatedly whitewashed. The Lennon Wall remains a changing mural surface where new layers of paint and slogans replace older ones.

Municipal house

Municipal House opened in 1912 on the site of Prague's former Royal Court as a center for Czech civic and cultural life. Art Nouveau mosaics and stained glass set the decorative tone around Smetana Hall. Murals, sculpture and metalwork continue through the salons. The building also has restaurants and guided interiors alongside its concert program.

Prague Zoo

Prague Zoo occupies steep terrain beside the Vltava in the Troja district, where paths climb between habitats and city viewpoints. Founded in 1931, it participates in international breeding and conservation programs for species including Przewalski's horse. Pavilions, open enclosures, wetlands, and a chairlift spread across a large site whose elevation changes and flood history shape both animal care and visitor movement.

Vitkov Hill carries the National Memorial, a monumental complex begun in the interwar years and later tied to Czechoslovak and World War history. Exhibitions inside explain the period, while the equestrian statue of Jan Zizka and the ridge paths overlook Zizkov and the wider city.

Built between 1715 and 1720 in Italian baroque style, the Vrtba Garden steps down terraces with sculptures and a pool. Paths and shade organise the planted levels on the Lesser Town slope. The formal layout rewards a slow climb through each terrace rather than a single flat lawn.

Baroque buildings, courtyards and towers form the Klementinum complex near Prague's Charles Bridge. Jesuits expanded the site from the sixteenth century for education, worship, astronomy and a major library. Guided routes commonly include the decorated Baroque Library, Meridian Hall and Astronomical Tower, while working library departments occupy other areas.

The Spanish Synagogue opened in 1868 in Prague's Josefov district on the site of an older Jewish prayer house; moorish Revival arches, geometric ornament, gilding, stained glass and a richly patterned dome cover the interior. Exhibitions from the Jewish Museum interpret emancipation, modern community life, music and persecution, while concerts also use the former worship space.

National Theatre

Public donations helped finance the neo-Renaissance building, which reopened in 1883 after fire damaged an early auditorium. Prague's National Theatre stands beside the Vltava as a symbol of the nineteenth-century Czech national revival. Opera, ballet, and drama continue beneath painted ceilings, gilded balconies, and a roofline crowned by sculptural groups associated with Czech arts and history.

Memorial to the Victims of Communism

Unveiled on Prague's Petrin slope, this memorial shows seven bronze figures descending stairs to recall political prisoners from 1948 to 1989. The sculptures grow more fragmented as they step downward, a visual metaphor for suffering under the regime. The open hillside setting faces Ujezd and the city below.

National Museum

The National Museum's main neo-Renaissance building closes the upper end of Wenceslas Square. Founded in 1818, the institution holds natural history, archaeology, history, art, music and scientific collections developed during the Czech national revival. Restored interiors, a monumental stair, dome and connecting passage to the adjacent new building frame exhibitions that range far beyond a single national chronology.

The Powder Tower

The Powder Tower marks one of the medieval entrances into Prague's Old Town and the traditional start of the coronation route. Construction began in 1475 beside the Royal Court, while later storage of gunpowder gave the gate its present name; a dark neo-Gothic exterior, sculpture, spiral stair, and upper gallery preserve the form of a ceremonial city tower despite nineteenth-century restoration.

Orthodox Cathedral of Saints Cyril and Methodius

The Orthodox Cathedral of Saints Cyril and Methodius occupies a former Baroque Catholic church in Prague's New Town. After the 1942 assassination of Reinhard Heydrich, Czech and Slovak parachutists sheltered in its crypt and died after a prolonged siege by Nazi forces. Bullet marks, memorial displays, and the underground refuge now document the resistance operation within an active cathedral.

Neo-Renaissance architecture surrounds a richly decorated auditorium used today for opera and ballet under the National Theatre organization; wartime, postwar, and communist-era changes altered its name and administration, while recent restoration renewed stage systems and historic finishes.

The Old-New Synagogue

Built in the late thirteenth century, the Old-New Synagogue remains the central prayer house of Prague's historic Jewish quarter. Heavy Gothic ribs, a two-aisled hall, medieval furnishings and later additions reflect centuries of continuous community use. Legends connect its attic with the Golem, but the building's greater importance lies in surviving persecution, clearance, war and repeated floods as an active synagogue.

The Estates Theatre

Count Franz Anton Nostitz opened the Estates Theatre in 1783 as a public stage for German and Czech performances. Mozart conducted the premiere of Don Giovanni there in 1787, giving the compact classical auditorium an long-standing place in opera history; drama, ballet, and opera still use the building, whose blue-and-gold interior preserves one of Europe's historic continuously operating theater spaces.

The City of Prague Museum occupies a neo-Renaissance building in the Florenc district and covers the capital's urban development from prehistory to the twentieth century.
