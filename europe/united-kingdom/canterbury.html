Canterbury

Canterbury's cathedral has been the senior church of the Church of England since Augustine's mission of 597 CE. Roman walls, medieval cloisters, and pilgrimage routes associated with Thomas Becket mark ecclesiastical authority in Kentish history. St Augustine's Abbey ruins and city walls still frame the cathedral precinct within Kent's historic landscape.

Reculver Towers and Roman Fort

Reculver preserves the twin towers of a medieval church within the remains of a Roman coastal fort on the Kent shore. Erosion has brought the sea close to walls that once stood inland, while earthworks, interpretation and paths explain the site's military and religious phases. The exposed setting is affected by wind, tides and cliff conditions and fragments of both complexes survive.

Herne Bay Pier

Herne Bay Pier extends a short distance from the seafront because storms and demolition removed much of the original structure, and detached pier remains can be seen farther offshore, making the site a record of changing seaside engineering. The surviving landward section contains kiosks, small businesses, seating, and seasonal activity above the beach.

51.3726° N / 1.1206° E

03 AdmissionFree

Herne Bay Clock Tower

Herne Bay Clock Tower was completed in the 1830s near the seafront and is often described as one of Britain's early purpose-built freestanding clock towers. Its stone base, narrow shaft, clock faces, and coastal position reflect the town's development as a nineteenth-century resort. Restoration and surrounding public space make it a civic landmark.

51.3730° N / 1.1263° E

Wildwood Trust

Wildwood Trust, located in Herne Bay, is a top British wildlife park spanning 40 acres of ancient woodland. The park houses over 200 animals, including native species like deer, owls, and red squirrels as well as rarer creatures such as lynx and wild horses. Visitors can enjoy various events such as talks, feeds, zoo keeper days, photography days, and festive activities during the Christmas season.

51.3315° N / 1.1174° E

Canterbury Cathedral

Canterbury Cathedral developed from an early Christian foundation into the seat of the Archbishop of Canterbury and a major pilgrimage destination after the murder of Thomas Becket. Romanesque crypts, Gothic choir, stained glass, cloisters, tombs and monastic buildings record repeated rebuilding. Worship, conservation, security and large visitor numbers shape access to the active cathedral precinct.

51.2798° N / 1.0828° E

Canterbury Punting Company

Canterbury Punting Company operates guided boat trips on calm sections of the River Stour through the historic center. Flat-bottomed boats pass beneath low bridges and beside gardens, old walls, timber-framed buildings and private riverside properties. A guide propels each boat with a pole while explaining landmarks and local history, offering a water-level view of streets that are otherwise seen on foot.

51.2785° N / 1.0772° E

07 AdmissionFree

Franciscan Gardens

Franciscan Gardens occupy part of the Greyfriars site beside the River Stour, where a surviving medieval building bridges the water. Lawns, trees, paths, and planted borders provide a quiet setting associated with the arrival of Franciscan friars in Canterbury; the garden and Greyfriars Chapel have separate access arrangements, and the landscape is distinct from the cathedral precinct.

51.2791° N / 1.0766° E

08 AdmissionFree

Westgate Gardens

Westgate Gardens follow the River Stour beside Canterbury's medieval Westgate, using lawns, flower beds, mature trees, paths and bridges around former private gardens. Remnants of the city wall and views toward historic buildings connect the park to Canterbury's defenses and waterways. Flooding, seasonal planting and busy riverside use change the character across the year.

51.2807° N / 1.0742° E

09 Admission£9

St Augustine's Abbey

Founded in AD 597 as a burial ground for Anglo-Saxon kings, St Augustine's Abbey helped restart organized Christianity in southern England. Ruined walls and cloister lines still outline the early monastic plan near Canterbury Cathedral. Exterior remains and open grounds form the usual visit.

51.2781° N / 1.0882° E

Harbour Market Whitstable

Along Whitstable's working harbour, Harbour Market stalls sell hand-crafted goods, fine art, and cooked food including local fried fish. Premium regional produce and homewares fill neighboring tables. Dockside walking keeps the market tied to the fishing town's daily waterfront.

51.3630° N / 1.0282° E
