Belfast

Belfast industrialised rapidly in the 19th century through linen, shipbuilding, and engineering that launched the Titanic from Harland and Wolff. Victorian civic buildings and sectarian murals record unionist and nationalist communities within Northern Ireland established in 1921. Peace agreements after 1998 spurred waterfront renewal. Municipal archives, parish records, and surviving architecture help explain how the city connected to wider regional trade routes, court patronage, and modern civic rebuilding after industrial and wartime change.

Titanic Belfast

Titanic Belfast occupies the former Harland and Wolff shipyard where RMS Titanic was designed and built. Galleries use plans, objects, oral histories, reconstructed spaces and multimedia to examine Belfast industry, ship construction, the 1912 sinking and later discovery of the wreck. The angular building stands beside the surviving slipways, linking the exhibition to the city's maritime labor history.

Peace Wall Belfast

Belfast's Peace Walls are high barriers that still separate some loyalist and republican neighborhoods, covered in murals, messages, and layered paint. Gates open on schedules that shape local movement. Walking tours explain the Troubles context while the walls remain working security architecture rather than pure memorial sculpture.

Stormont Estate

Stormont Estate surrounds Parliament Buildings, the seat of the Northern Ireland Assembly, with formal avenues, woodland, lawns, statues and public paths. The neoclassical building and processional approach reflect the creation of Northern Ireland's devolved institutions in the twentieth century; security measures, demonstrations and government business may affect access to buildings or parts of the grounds.

The Crown Liquor Saloon

The Crown Liquor Saloon occupies a richly decorated Victorian pub near Belfast's Great Victoria Street. Polychrome tiles, stained glass, carved wood, etched mirrors and private drinking booths reflect restoration by the National Trust. It remains a licensed business, so seating, service and crowd levels follow normal pub operations alongside its architectural significance. Private booths preserve the pub's distinctive internal layout.

Grand Opera House

Belfast's Grand Opera House opened in 1895 to a design by theater architect Frank Matcham; curving balconies, ornate plasterwork, painted decoration and a richly layered auditorium survive within a venue expanded and restored for modern productions. Musicals, drama, opera, comedy and pantomime fill the changing program, while interior access normally depends on a performance or scheduled tour.

Ulster Hall

Ulster Hall, a grand Victorian music hall in Belfast, has been a prominent part of the city's music scene for 150 years. From orchestral performances to rock concerts, this venue offers a diverse lineup of events. Notable acts such as Led Zeppelin and All Time Low have graced its stage, and it continues to host known artists like Mitski and Passenger. The Ulster Orchestra also holds regular concerts here.

The SSE Arena Belfast

The SSE Arena Belfast is a versatile indoor stadium with a seating capacity of 10,000. Located within the Titanic Quarter, it is a prime venue for various events including ice-hockey matches, ice shows, music concerts, and comedy events. It is the home stadium for the Belfast Giants ice hockey team and has hosted known music artists like Shania Twain, Bryan Adams, and Westlife.

Belfast Castle

Event hire occupies much of Belfast Castle's interior, while the grounds keep an adventure playground, antiques shop, restaurant, and visitor center open to day callers. Estate paths loop the hillside setting above the city. Walls and courtyards appear along the outdoor circuit when grounds admit walkers.

Belfast Zoo

Belfast Zoo occupies a hillside above the city with more than a thousand animals across about 140 species, many of them threatened in the wild. Paths climb between outdoor paddocks and indoor houses with views back toward Belfast Lough. The collection emphasizes conservation breeding alongside everyday family visiting.

St George's Market

St George's Market occupies a Victorian covered hall near Belfast's waterfront, where traders sell produce, baked goods, crafts, and hot food on scheduled market days. Brick arches and ironwork frame the aisles. The market has long been a civic trading floor rather than a shopping mall, and regular hours keep the stalls turning over through the week.

Waterfront Hall

Waterfront Hall opened beside the River Lagan in the 1990s as a concert and conference venue linked to Belfast's waterfront regeneration; the building's public foyers offer river views, but auditorium access and atmosphere depend on the scheduled program. Its circular main auditorium and smaller studio host orchestras, popular music, comedy, talks, ceremonies, and sporting events.

Belfast city hall

Belfast City Hall occupies Donegall Square as the civic headquarters built after Belfast received city status; exhibitions and tours address municipal history, while public ceremonies, demonstrations and council business continue around the building. Portland stone, a copper dome, stained glass, memorials, formal rooms and surrounding gardens express the wealth and politics of the Edwardian city.

Botanic Gardens

Established in 1828 across 28 acres, Belfast's Botanic Gardens combine tropical plant collections with a domed conservatory and open lawns. Festivals and concerts use the grounds through the year, and an on-site cafe supports longer stays. Paths weave between glasshouse and outdoor planting rooms.

The Salmon of Knowledge

The Salmon of Knowledge, usually called the Big Fish, is a ten-meter ceramic sculpture installed beside the River Lagan in 1999. Tiles covering its body reproduce images and texts from Belfast history, while the form refers to an Irish legend about wisdom; the outdoor artwork marks the regenerated waterfront and can be viewed from the riverside path at any time.

Albert Memorial Clock

Belfast's Albert Memorial Clock leans enough that locals compare it to Pisa, while seasonal fountains at its base run from April through October. The memorial occupies an open plaza setting in the older street grid. Photographers often frame the tower with the water jets in the foreground.

Belfast Cathedral, Church of Ireland Cathedral Church of St Anne

Saint Anne's Cathedral was begun in the late nineteenth century and gradually expanded as Belfast's Church of Ireland cathedral; the building anchors the Cathedral Quarter and remains active for worship, music, remembrance and civic services. Romanesque arches, mosaics, stained glass, memorials, textiles and the slender Spire of Hope reflect several construction phases.

Knockagh Monument

Thirty-four meters tall, Knockagh Monument honors County Antrim men killed in the two world wars from a hill above Carrickfergus. A narrow lane and winding road reach a small car park and history boards. Clear weather makes the shaft visible across wide stretches of the coast.
