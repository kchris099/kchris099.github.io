Durham

Durham was established in the late tenth century when monks from Lindisfarne sought a secure resting place for the relics of St. Cuthbert. The city grew around its Norman cathedral and castle, which served as the seat of the powerful Prince-Bishops who defended the English border against the Scots. In the nineteenth century, Durham became a center for coal mining and established one of England's oldest universities. Today, its historic peninsula remains a UNESCO World Heritage site and a center of academic excellence.

Durham Cathedral

Durham Cathedral was begun in 1093 above the River Wear as the shrine church of Saint Cuthbert and a symbol of Norman authority in northern England. Massive Romanesque columns, rib vaults, cloisters, tombs, stained glass and monastic buildings survive within the historic peninsula. Worship continues, while tower climbs, exhibitions and filming-related areas have separate arrangements.

Durham Castle

Raised in 1072 after the Norman conquest, Durham Castle now houses a university college with bed-and-breakfast rooms in term gaps. The fortress shares the peninsula with the cathedral above the Wear. Walls and courts appear on guided routes when the college admits visitors.

Durham Market Hall

Durham Market Hall, housed under a metal and glass canopy, has been operating since 1852. Following a restoration in 1996, the market now hosts over 50 traders rooted in the Durham community. Most businesses source their products locally, supporting both quality and the local economy. Despite the rise of online shopping, this Victorian Indoor City Market Hall remains active with its busy Saturday market offering a wide array of goods.

National Trust - Crook Hall Gardens

A thirteenth-century manor sits inside five acres of themed English gardens at National Trust Crook Hall, including Secret Walled, Silver and White, Shakespeare, maze, and Quiet Corner rooms. Paths thread between those planted enclosures. The house and gardens read as one medieval-to-domestic ensemble.

Hall Hill Farm

Hall Hill Farm near Durham offers animal contact with cows, rabbits, and guinea pigs plus a play barn, toddler area, and donkey rides. The working-farm setting suits younger children more than wilderness hiking. Indoor and outdoor zones keep visits viable in mixed weather.

Finchale Priory

Finchale Priory beside the River Wear began as a hermitage and became a Benedictine cell from 1196, leaving thirteenth-century ruin walls in a wooded bend of the river. Stone outlines of church and claustral ranges remain for open-air exploration. The site pairs monastic history with a quiet riverside walk north of Durham city.

Adventure Valley

Adventure Valley near Durham mixes indoor soft play, giant slides, a petting farm, and a miniature railway on a rural campus open year-round. Families rotate between covered and outdoor zones without needing city-centre facilities. The park's scale suits a dedicated children's day out in County Durham.

Brancepeth Castle

Brancepeth Castle developed from a medieval stronghold into a large country house shaped by repeated rebuilding. Towers, halls, courtyards and later domestic interiors record changes made under several owners, especially during the Gothic Revival period. The property remains privately occupied. Selected tours and events provide limited public access to parts of the complex.

54.7338° N / 1.6548° W

Diggerland Durham

Diggerland Durham is a unique construction-themed amusement park where children and adults can ride, drive, and operate full-sized construction machinery, including diggers, dumpers, and JCBs. The park offers a hands-on, interactive experience with modified heavy equipment, providing an unconventional and very entertaining day out.
