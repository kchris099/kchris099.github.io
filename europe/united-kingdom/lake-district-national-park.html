Lake District National Park

The Lake District's valleys held farming and mining communities long before Romantic poets such as Wordsworth made the fells a literary landscape. Victorian railways and hotels opened Windermere and Keswick to visitors. National park designation in 1951 aimed to protect lakes, commons, and stone villages under rising tourism. Boat landings, dry-stone walls, and ridge paths still organise travel through Cumbria's glaciated valleys.

Brockhole on Windermere

Brockhole on Windermere is the official travellers center of the Lake District, situated on the shores of Lake Windermere. It offers a wide range of activities such as mini-golf, zip-lines, kayaking, and archery. Travellers can enjoy walking through meadows and woodland or try the Treetop Trek course. The site also provides opportunities for hiring canoes to explore Lake Windermere.

Stock Ghyll Force

Stock Ghyll Force is a 70-ft. Waterfall located in a wooded area near Ambleside village in the Lake District. The falls, which drop in two stages to form a V shape where two streams meet, are easily accessible from the village and offer excellent photo opportunities along the well-signposted footpath. Castlerigg Stone Circle

Castlerigg Stone Circle, an English Heritage site, is believed to be around 5000 years old and offers panoramic views. This Neolithic stone circle, possibly one of the oldest in Britain, consists of 38 large stones with a dramatic mountainous backdrop. While its purpose remains a mystery, some speculate that the stone placement may have had astrological significance.

Friars Crag is a promontory that extends into Derwentwater, offering views of the surrounding landscape.
