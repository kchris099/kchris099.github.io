Glasgow

Glasgow expanded from a medieval bishopric on the Clyde into Britain's leading industrial city during the 19th-century shipbuilding and textile boom. Georgian and Victorian sandstone architecture, the University of Glasgow, and regenerated waterfront districts record engineering wealth and later deindustrialisation. As Scotland's largest city, Glasgow combines mercantile history with contemporary design, music, and civic museums.

Glasgow Cathedral

Glasgow Cathedral, also known as St. Mungo's, is a significant example of Scottish Gothic architecture and the oldest building in Glasgow. It was consecrated in 1197 and is the only medieval cathedral to have survived the Protestant Reformation in 1560. The lower crypt houses the tomb of Glasgow's patron saint, St. Mungo. Visitors are captivated by its stained glass windows and ornate interior.

The Glasgow Necropolis

The Glasgow Necropolis is a Victorian-era cemetery that houses the graves and mausoleums of prominent figures from Glasgow's past. Spanning 37 acres, it is a rich source of architecture, sculptures, and historical narratives from the city's Victorian era. With an estimated 50,000 burials and around 3,500 monuments marking them, this cemetery has been described as a 'city of the dead.'.
