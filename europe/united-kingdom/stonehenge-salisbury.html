Stonehenge Salisbury

Stonehenge was raised in stages in the Neolithic and Bronze Age on Salisbury Plain, aligned with solstice sunrises and tied to nearby burial mounds. Antiquarians and later archaeologists debated its purpose for centuries. Salisbury itself grew as a medieval cathedral city after the bishop moved from Old Sarum in the 13th century. The cathedral close and the monument's visitor routes now link prehistoric ritual ground to a Gothic market city.

Stonehenge

Bluestones hauled from Wales form part of the prehistoric ring at Stonehenge, though the monument's original purpose remains unknown. The standing stones draw visitors onto the managed field circuit in Wiltshire. Dogs stay out of the stone field, while surrounding trails remain available for longer walks.

Woodhenge

Near Amesbury, Woodhenge marks a late Neolithic monument near Amesbury formed by concrete markers indicating former timber-post rings discovered through archaeology. Earthworks, burials, and its relationship with nearby Durrington Walls and Stonehenge place it within a ceremonial landscape. The open field reveals the plan, while the original appearance and rituals remain uncertain.

Cholderton Rare Breeds Farm

Cholderton Rare Breeds Farm, also called Cholderton Charlie's, keeps registered rare livestock with feeding sessions and a comic pig race among family activities. Trails and barns interpret traditional British breeds. The farm sits in the wider Stonehenge-Salisbury countryside as an agricultural counterpoint to prehistoric sites.

Salisbury Cathedral

Salisbury Cathedral was begun in the thirteenth century after the city's religious center moved from Old Sarum. Early English Gothic architecture, a tall spire, cloisters, chapter house, stained glass, tombs and one surviving Magna Carta manuscript define the complex. Worship continues, while tower tours, library access and displays follow separate schedules and conservation rules.

51.0650° N / 1.7973° W

Mompesson House

Mompesson House in Salisbury Cathedral Close is an eighteenth-century National Trust townhouse with period paintings, furniture, a walled garden, and a tea room. Rooms interpret genteel Close life beside the cathedral. The visit pairs naturally with walking the surrounding cloistral streets.

St Thomas’s Church

St Thomas's Church in Salisbury began in the thirteenth century as a chapel for workers building the nearby cathedral and later grew into a parish church in the medieval centre. Stained glass and a famous doom painting draw visitors inside. The tower sits a short walk from the cathedral close.

Salisbury Playhouse

Salisbury Playhouse is a modern producing theater within the city's cultural quarter. Its main auditorium and smaller studio host drama, new writing, touring productions, family shows and community programs. Rehearsal rooms and public foyers support the performance spaces. The venue's identity comes from live production and local arts activity within a contemporary building.

Harnham Water Meadows

Harnham Water Meadows below Salisbury preserve irrigated grassland systems with origins in earlier agricultural management, now valued for wildlife including birds and small mammals. Guided walks look across to the cathedral spire. Paths suit quiet nature time on the city's southern edge.

Old Sarum

Old Sarum, located two miles north of present-day Salisbury, is an ancient hill fort with a rich history dating back to the Iron Age. It was once home to a Norman cathedral and a strategically positioned castle built by William the Conqueror in 1075. The site also housed a Roman fort and later became a busy town settlement under Saxon rule. However, by 1220, the inhabitants were relocated to New Salisbury, leading Old Sarum into decline.

51.0932° N / 1.8049° W

Wilton House

Wilton House is a grand country manor located just three miles from Salisbury, and it's the ancestral home of the Earl of Pembroke. This stately home has an internationally important art collection featuring works by known artists such as Rembrandt, van Dyke, and Brueghel. The estate also offers an adventure playground for travellers to enjoy and a cafe to relax in.

51.0786° N / 1.8593° W
