Liverpool

Liverpool rose as Britain's major Atlantic port in the 18th and 19th centuries, shipping cotton, slaves, and emigrants before becoming a centre of transatlantic liners. Albert Dock warehouses, two cathedrals, and football clubs record mercantile wealth, Irish immigration, and post-industrial culture recognised by UNESCO on the waterfront. Municipal archives, parish records, and surviving architecture help explain how the city connected to wider regional trade routes, court patronage, and modern civic rebuilding after industrial and wartime change.

Royal Albert Dock Liverpool

Royal Albert Dock's brick warehouses and cast-iron colonnades enclose a restored dock basin on Liverpool's waterfront, now holding museums, galleries, restaurants, and apartments. The enclosed water and colonnades define one of the city's clearest maritime heritage ensembles. Pedestrian routes link the dock to Pier Head and the wider UNESCO waterfront setting.

Anfield

Anfield is a known football stadium and the home of Liverpool FC, one of England's most successful football clubs. Since its establishment in 1892, Anfield has been witness to numerous historic moments in the club's history. The stadium, with over 50,000 seats, is easily accessible and offers a variety of food kiosks for visitors. It also houses the Liverpool FC museum, showing monuments dedicated to the team and the sport.

Goodison Park

Goodison Park, located in Liverpool, is the historic home stadium of Everton Football Club. Constructed in 1892, it has a rich football history and can accommodate over 40,000 fans. The stadium's Bullens Road Stand and Gwladys Street End contribute to its electrifying atmosphere. Despite being an old stadium, its close proximity to the pitch provides an intimate experience for spectators. The stadium tour is recommended for those interested in exploring its heritage.

Royal Liver Building

The Royal Liver Building was completed in 1911 at Liverpool's Pier Head as the headquarters of the Royal Liver Assurance company, and offices still occupy much of the building, while scheduled tours may reach selected interiors and upper viewpoints. Its reinforced-concrete frame, clock towers, and copper Liver Bird sculptures helped define the city's waterfront skyline.

The Black Pearl is a community-built driftwood sculpture shaped like a pirate ship on the beach at New Brighton.
