Cambridge

Evolving around one of the world's oldest continuous universities founded in 1209, Cambridge has been shaped entirely by profound academic legacy. The majestic Gothic colleges and peaceful river 'Backs' reflect eight centuries of extensive wealth, royal patronage, and intellectual dominance. Visitors today can clearly trace the detailed layers of this unique heritage throughout the preserved streets and monuments.

The Church of Our Lady of the Assumption and the English Martyrs, Cambridge

Completed around 1890 at the Hills Road and Lensfield Road crossroads, the Church of Our Lady of the Assumption and the English Martyrs shows Gothic Revival massing in busy south Cambridge. Stone spires read clearly above the junction traffic. Outside Mass the exterior and main nave are usually viewable.

Cambridge Market Square

Cambridge Market Square is a historic outdoor market that has been a central gathering place for locals since medieval times. It offers a wide variety of goods, including gourmet food stalls, fresh produce, herbs and spices, preserves, oils, condiments, and locally made crafts. The market reflects the city's active multicultural community with its diverse range of offerings from around the world.

52.2054° N / 0.1190° E

03 AdmissionFree

Great St Mary's

Great St Mary's is Cambridge's university church, a fifteenth-century parish building whose tower climb overlooks the colleges and market square. Eighteenth-century galleries and a tall bell tower define the interior and silhouette. Travellers use the tower view to orient the historic centre after walking King's Parade.

52.2053° N / 0.1183° E

King's College Chapel

Founded by Henry VI and finished across nearly a century by later monarchs, King's College Chapel holds a fan-vaulted ceiling, carved woodwork, and original stained glass. The sixteenth-century chapel closes one side of the college court on the Cam. Evensong and daytime visiting follow posted college hours.

52.2048° N / 0.1166° E

05 AdmissionFree

The Corpus Clock

Unveiled in 2008 outside Corpus Christi College, the Corpus Clock by John C. Taylor displays time with concentric LED rings and a grasshopper escapement sculpture instead of ordinary hands. The gold-plated face draws crowds on King's Parade. It functions as public kinetic art tied to the college rather than a conventional tower clock.

52.2037° N / 0.1177° E

06 AdmissionFree

Cambridge Corn Exchange

Cambridge Corn Exchange opened in the nineteenth century as a grain-trading hall and later became a major concert and performance venue. Its brick and stone exterior encloses a flexible auditorium used for music, comedy, talks, family shows, and touring productions; little of the experience resembles an active commodity market today, and public access normally depends on the event schedule.

52.2044° N / 0.1197° E

07 AdmissionFree

Newton's Apple Tree

A living descendant of the apple tree linked to Isaac Newton's gravity story grows in the gardens of Trinity College, Cambridge, where visitors may view it from permitted paths. Plaques explain the scientific legend and the tree's propagation history. The stop is brief but concrete, tying campus walking to Newton's association with the college.

52.2073° N / 0.1178° E

Mathematical Bridge

Straight timbers alone form the Mathematical Bridge, a precise wooden footbridge over the Cam beside the Backs. College gardens and river paths supply the usual viewpoints. Walkers cross or photograph the span while moving between neighboring colleges.

52.2022° N / 0.1151° E

09 AdmissionFree

Lets Go Punting Cambridge

Punting craft from Lets Go Punting Cambridge put travellers on the Cam between college backs and riverside meadows. Chauffeured and self-hire boats share the same stretch of water used for classic Cambridge river views. Booking desks sit near the college-side landing points.

52.2116° N / 0.1183° E

10 AdmissionFree

Castle Mound

Rising above northern Cambridge, Castle Mound preserves an earthwork, created as part of the Norman castle established after the conquest. Most stone structures disappeared, leaving a grassy motte with views across church towers, colleges, and modern development. The short climb offers a visible reminder that royal and military authority once occupied a part of the city now dominated by civic buildings.

52.2121° N / 0.1147° E
