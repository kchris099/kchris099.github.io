Edinburgh

Edinburgh grew from a hill fort on Castle Rock into Scotland's royal capital after David I founded Holyrood Abbey and burgh privileges along the Royal Mile in the twelfth century. Medieval parliaments, the Reformation, and the 1707 Union with England reshaped the Old Town closes while Enlightenment planners laid out the Georgian New Town from the 1760s. Courts, university buildings, and printers earned the city its nickname as the Athens of the North, and festivals now fill kirks and squares above the drained Nor Loch valley.

The Real Mary King's Close

The Real Mary King's Close operates guided tours through preserved and reconstructed spaces beneath Edinburgh's Royal Mile. The closes were partly built over during construction of the eighteenth-century Royal Exchange, not sealed solely as a plague measure; costumed interpretation introduces crowded housing, work, disease, and urban change, while the route includes stairs and confined interiors.

St Giles' Cathedral

Known as the High Kirk of Edinburgh, St Giles' Cathedral rises on the Royal Mile as a medieval Gothic church that remains a principal Presbyterian place of worship. The crown steeple marks the skyline, and the Thistle Chapel holds the stalls of the Order of the Thistle. Outside services, the nave and side chapels stay open for stonework, stained glass, and memorials.

Royal Mile

The Royal Mile follows the Old Town ridge between Edinburgh Castle and Holyroodhouse. Castlehill and Lawnmarket descend toward the High Street, where closes open between churches, civic buildings and former tenements. Canongate continues past museums, shops and residences toward the palace. Festival crowds can dominate the route, but the street remains part of a lived urban district.

Holyrood Park surrounds Arthur's Seat with crags, lochs, grassland, paths, archaeological remains and views over Edinburgh. The royal park includes Salisbury Crags, Duddingston Loch and the ruins of Saint Anthony's Chapel, with routes ranging from paved roads to steep rocky climbs.

Arthur's Seat

Arthur's Seat rises above Edinburgh within Holyrood Park's ancient volcanic landscape. Several paths cross grassland and exposed crags before reaching the rocky summit, where the city, Firth of Forth and surrounding hills spread below. Archaeological traces and unusual geology add context to the climb, while the open terrain and uneven routes distinguish it from a conventional urban park walk.

Usher Hall

The Usher Hall, located on Lothian Road in Edinburgh, is a known concert hall with a rich history. Built in 1914 by architects Stockdale Harrison and Howard H Thomson, it stands out with its beaux-arts-style architecture amidst the Victorian Gothic buildings of the city. The hall was funded by Andrew Usher, a prominent whisky distiller. Today, it is known as the home of the Royal Scottish National Orchestra and hosts various concerts and recitals.

The National Monument of Scotland, situated atop Calton Hill in Edinburgh, is a neoclassical memorial dedicated to the soldiers and sailors from Scotland who lost their lives during the Napoleonic Wars. Designed by known architects William Playfair and Charles Robert Cockerell, this 19th-century landmark bears a striking resemblance to the Parthenon in Athens.

Dugald Stewart Monument

Perched atop Calton Hill in Edinburgh, the Dugald Stewart Monument is a monument to the known Scottish philosopher, Dugald Stewart. Completed in 1831 and designed by William Henry Playfair, this monument resembles the Tower of the Winds in Athens. Stewart's contributions to Edinburgh University are commemorated through this structure, which offers panoramic views of the city and has become a popular subject for postcards and tourist brochures.
