Snowdonia

Welsh princes held these glacial valleys against English advance; later Edwardian castles ringed the coast. Nineteenth-century slate quarries cut the mountains before national-park designation protected the massif. Princely resistance and slate industry, not a single borough charter, structure Snowdonia's past. Narrow-gauge slate railways now carry visitors along lines built for quarry export.

The Smallest House In Great Britain

The Smallest House In Great Britain is a visitor site in Snowdonia, United Kingdom. Records associated with the site include 1900. This minuscule former fisherman's residence, known as Quay House, measures just 1.8 meters wide and 3 meters high. With only two rooms, this bright red one-up-one-down dwelling was inhabited until May 1900 by the last owner, Robert Jones. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Plas Mawr

Plas Mawr, meaning "Great Hall," is a 16th-century residence located on Conwy High Street. Built between 1576 and 1585 by Robert Wynn, it is preserved by Cadw and offers self-guided audio tours. The house showcases original plasterwork in the bedrooms and Great Chamber, as well as Tudor technology in the kitchen. Visitors can explore the garden, courtyard, and climb to the tower for views of Conwy.

Conwy Castle

Conwy Castle is a fortified historic site in Snowdonia, United Kingdom. Construction and early use date to around 1283. Conwy Castle is a well-preserved 13th-century fortress located in an estuary setting. Built by King Edward between 1283 and 1287, it has scenic battlement views and royal apartments that are the most intact in Wales. Walls, courtyards, or towers form the main visitor route when the grounds are open.

Llandudno Promenade

Llandudno Promenade is a visitor site in Snowdonia, United Kingdom. Llandudno Promenade is a stretch of Victorian-era architecture along the Irish Sea, offering panoramic views. The promenade itself spans almost two miles from Llandudno Pier to Craigside, with hotels and traditional attractions like Punch and Judy shows during the summer months. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Pont-y-Pair Bridge

In the village of Betws-y-Coed in Snowdonia National Park, North Wales, stands the historic Pont-y-Pair Bridge. Dating back to the 1500s, this architectural gem offers views of the River Conwy and its surroundings. Originally built for packhorses, it was later expanded to accommodate stagecoaches.
