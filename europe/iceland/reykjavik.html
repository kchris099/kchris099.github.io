Reykjavik

Norse settlers are traditionally dated to 874, yet Reykjavík stayed a fishing hamlet until the eighteenth century. Twentieth-century industry and independence culture scaled it into a Nordic capital. Hallgrímskirkja and the old harbour district bookend that leap from trading post to national centre. The Alþingi's modern building continues a parliamentary tradition older than the city itself.

Hallgrimskirkja

Guðjón Samúelsson designed Hallgrímskirkja with a stepped concrete facade often compared with Icelandic basalt formations. Construction lasted from 1945 until 1986, producing a 74.5-metre Lutheran church visible across Reykjavík. A high nave, large pipe organ, tower, and Einar Jónsson's Leif Erikson statue define the site, while worship and concerts remain central to its use.

The Reykjavik Food Walk

The Reykjavik Food Walk is a guided tasting tour through downtown streets, stopping at cafes and restaurants for small portions of Icelandic dishes. Guides explain local ingredients while the group moves between venues on foot. The route uses the capital's compact center rather than a single fixed dining hall.

03 AdmissionFree

Sun Voyager

Sun Voyager is a stainless-steel sculpture located on the granite shores of Reykjavik, Iceland. Created by Jón Gunnar Árnason, it resembles a Viking longship and was unveiled in 1990 to celebrate the city's 200-year anniversary. Despite its appearance, the sculpture is actually an ode to the sun, symbolizing hope and light in a place with long hours of winter darkness.

04 AdmissionFree

Höfði House

Höfði House, built in 1909, once housed poet Einar Benediktsson and later hosted the 1986 Reagan-Gorbachev summit. The white wooden villa stands alone on Reykjavik's waterfront near the Sun Voyager sculpture. Exterior viewing from the shore path is the usual visit; the building itself remains closed for private use.

64.1465° N / 21.9064° W

Laugardalslaug

Laugardalslaug is Reykjavik's main outdoor geothermal pool complex, with a full-size lap pool, hot pots, and smaller basins for children. Geothermally heated water stays open through winter under open sky. Changing rooms and slide areas make it a daily recreation site for residents as much as for travellers.

National Museum of Iceland

Iceland's National Museum traces settlement, conversion and political development from the Viking Age to the modern republic, and archaeological finds and church art establish the early periods, with the carved Valthjofsstadur door among the central objects. Tools, clothing and domestic possessions then follow work and daily life, while rotating loans and conservation affect individual displays.

Sky Lagoon

At Kársnes Harbour in Kópavogur near Reykjavík, the geothermal spa places infinity-edge soaking against the ocean rim. Themed indoor rooms and outdoor heat channels structure the visit. Despite the short ride from the city, the site feels set apart on the peninsula edge.

Perlan

On Öskjuhlíð hill, a glass dome over six water tanks holds the Wonders of Iceland exhibits, an indoor ice cave, a Northern Lights planetarium, and a 360-degree observation deck. The tanks once stored hot water for the city. Views sweep Reykjavík and the surrounding hills.

Lava Show

Lava Show stages a controlled demonstration in which molten rock is poured and manipulated inside a purpose-built theater, and it is a commercial educational performance and schedules or formats differ between the Reykjavík and Vík branches. Explanations cover volcanic processes, cooling, texture and the practical dangers of eruptions, while heat, sound and protective barriers make the presentation physical.

FlyOver Iceland

In the Grandi harbour district, motion seats and a wraparound screen simulate flight over lava plains, geysers, and mountain terrain. The show runs as a timed attraction with assigned seating. The warehouse-district setting places it among other new waterfront venues.
