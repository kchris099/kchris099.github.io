Explore South Coast

A Brief History

Iceland's South Coast was settled during the 9th- and 10th-century Norse landnam period along the coastal farms of the Ranga and Markarfljot river valleys, with Skalholt founded c. 1056 as a religious centre and elevated to a bishopric in 1106. The 1783 Laki fissure eruption devastated the Myrdalssandur plain and reshaped the glacial Eyjafjallajokull and Myrdalsjokull catchments, with the latter's 1918 Katla eruption still defining local road closures. The 20th-century Ring Road of 1974 and the 2010 Eyjafjallajokull eruption together established the present visitor circuit through Seljalandsfoss, Skogafoss, and Vik.

Top Sights & Activities

Free (parking 1000 ISK)

Seljalandsfoss

Seljalandsfoss is a 60-metre waterfall on the south coast of Iceland, dropping over the original sea cliff of the Eyjafjallajokull volcanic system into a narrow canyon above the Seljalandsa river. A footpath continues behind the curtain of water into a cave cut into the surrounding soft tuff, allowing visitors to walk a full circuit around the falling stream. Travellers pay ISK1000 for the parking lay-by on the Hvolsvollur-Skogar stretch of the Ring Road.

ISK 600

Skogafoss

Skogafoss is a 62-metre waterfall on the Skoga river on Iceland's south coast, draining the Myrdalsjokull ice cap into a wide rock pool at the village of Skogar. The falls sit on a basalt and tufa columnar structure and can produce a single or double rainbow on sunny days. Travellers pay ISK600 for parking, climb a 524-step staircase to the top of the cliff, and continue on the Fimmvorduhals trail to Thorsmork.

Free

Reynisdrangar Sea Stacks

Reynisdrangar is a group of basalt sea stacks rising from the Atlantic Ocean at the foot of the Myrdalsjokull ice cap, immediately west of the village of Vik on Iceland's south shore. According to medieval Icelandic folklore the stacks are petrified trolls caught by the dawn, a tradition recorded in the Svarfdcela saga. Travellers reach the black-sand beach via a short marked path from the Vik harbour car park and continue on to Dyrholaey promontory.
