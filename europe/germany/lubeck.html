Explore Lübeck

A Brief History

Lübeck was the leading city of the Hanseatic League from the twelfth century, with merchants using its Holstentor gate and salt stores on the Trave to control Baltic trade. Brick Gothic churches, the town hall, and patrician courtyards express wealth from herring, wax, and furs before the League declined. Though stripped of imperial status in 1937 and damaged in 1942, Lübeck's island old town remains a reference for north German civic architecture and marzipan commerce.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Holstentor (Holsten Gate)

The Holsten Gate is a brick Gothic city gate built in 1478, now leaning slightly due to subsidence. It houses a museum on Lübeck's role in the Hanseatic League and the city's medieval trade networks. Twin round towers and a central passage make it the most recognized symbol of the old town.

Free

St. Mary's Church (Marienkirche)

St. Mary's Church is a brick Gothic basilica that served as a model for many churches around the Baltic Sea. Inside, the fallen bells remain where they dropped during the 1942 air raid, left as a war memorial. The vast interior includes a massive astronomical clock and a rose window above the west portal.

Free

Lübeck Old Town

Lübeck's old town occupies an island in the Trave river, recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site. Behind the main streets, narrow alleys and enclosed courtyards called Gänge und Höfe reveal smaller houses where artisans and sailors once lived. The layout preserves a medieval urban structure rare in northern Germany.

Free

Niederegger Marzipan Museum

The Niederegger café and marzipan shop on Breite Straße has sold almond confections since 1806. A small museum upstairs displays life-sized marzipan figures and explains how Lübeck became a center of marzipan production. The city has a long tradition of trading almonds and sugar through Hanseatic shipping routes.

Free

Lübeck Town Hall (Rathaus)

Lübeck's Town Hall combines Gothic and Renaissance sections built between the 13th and 16th centuries. Its dark brick facade, open arcades, and ornate staircases reflect the political power of the merchant patricians. The building remains in use for city council meetings and can be visited on guided tours.

€16

European Hansemuseum

The European Hansemuseum opened in 2015 on the site of a former monastery near the castle gate. Interactive exhibits trace the Hanseatic League from its 12th-century origins to its decline, with Lübeck as the leading kontor city. Archaeological finds from the harbor are displayed in the basement level.
