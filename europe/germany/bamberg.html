Bamberg

Bamberg spread across seven hills where Henry II founded a bishopric in 1007 that later became a prince-bishopric within the Holy Roman Empire. Romanesque cathedral with the Bamberg Rider, Baroque Neue Residenz, and smoked-beer breweries line the Regnitz where medieval citizens famously built the town hall on an artificial island. The city's relatively intact fabric illustrates Franconian ecclesiastical power and river trade between Nuremberg and the upper Main.

Diözesanmuseum (Diocesan Museum)

Bamberg's Diocesan Museum occupies the cathedral chapterhouse beside the imperial cathedral. Liturgical textiles, reliquaries, sculpture, metalwork, manuscripts, and objects from churches across the archdiocese explain worship and episcopal patronage. Among its major holdings are medieval robes associated with imperial and saintly traditions.

Neue Residenz (New Residence)

Bamberg's Neue Residenz on Domplatz housed the prince-bishops after they left the Alte Hofhaltung. Building began in 1604, with Renaissance wings later joined by Baroque extensions, and episcopal residence ended with secularisation in 1803. State rooms and the rose garden overlook the cathedral island.

Rosengarten Bamberg (Rose Garden)

A formal rose garden fills the upper courtyard behind Bamberg's New Residence. Geometric beds, clipped hedges, statues, lime trees, fountains, and hundreds of rose varieties frame views across the old town and toward Michaelsberg. The design reflects eighteenth-century court landscaping, while flowering depends on season and weather.
