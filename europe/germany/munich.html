Munich

Munich developed from a 12th-century market settlement under the Wittelsbach dynasty into the capital of Bavaria, with residence palaces, churches, and civic gates expanded through Renaissance and Baroque patronage. The 19th century added museums, boulevards, and the Oktoberfest tradition tied to a royal wedding celebration of 1810. Wartime destruction and postwar reconstruction reshaped parts of the center while preserving major dynastic monuments.

Nymphenburg Palace

Nymphenburg Palace was begun in 1664 as a summer residence west of Munich and expanded into a Baroque complex with pavilions and canals. State rooms, the Gallery of Beauties, and park buildings trace Wittelsbach court life. The grounds remain open parkland popular for walks and museum visits.

Marienplatz and the New Town Hall

Marienplatz has been Munich's central square since the city's founding and is dominated by the Neo-Gothic New Town Hall completed in 1909. The Glockenspiel performs daily for crowds in the square below. Markets, festivals, and orientation for the old town still concentrate here.

Residenzmuseum (Munich Residence)

The Munich Residence was the seat of Bavarian dukes, electors, and kings from the 16th to the 19th century and now houses a museum of state apartments and treasuries. Rooms span Renaissance, Baroque, and Rococo decoration with collections of porcelain and regalia. The complex forms the core of the palace district east of Odeonsplatz.

St. Peter's Church (Peterskirche)

Munich's historic parish foundation stands behind Marienplatz at St. Peter's Church, known as Alter Peter; the stair climb opens over the old town when available, while worship and funerary traditions remain central to the church below. Gothic rebuilding, Baroque decoration, postwar restoration, side chapels, relics, artworks and a tall tower record centuries of change.

English Garden (Englischer Garten)

Elector Karl Theodor opened land beside the Isar as a public park in 1789, beginning Munich's English Garden. Meadows and woodland spread around streams, bridges, beer gardens and monuments for several kilometres north of the center. Sports, the Eisbach surfing wave and busy gathering places coexist with quieter habitat and long riverside paths. The park remains woven into Munich's daily life.

Alter Hof

Wittelsbach rulers used Munich's Alter Hof as an early urban residence from the thirteenth century, and the complex marks the dynasty's move into Munich, although much of its visible fabric has been restored or rebuilt. Courtyards, a gateway tower, reconstructed Gothic elements, and later wings survive among modern offices and shops after wartime damage and redevelopment.

Hofbräuhaus München on Platzl is the city's long-running state brewery beer hall, with cavernous rooms, house beer, and regular brass music. Feldherrnhalle

Feldherrnhalle on Odeonsplatz is a nineteenth-century loggia modelled on Florence's Loggia dei Lanzi, built to honour Bavarian military leaders. The open hall faces the Residenz and Theatinerkirche. In 1923 it became the site of Hitler's failed putsch march, a history now marked nearby.

Theatine Church

Yellow facades and twin towers frame Munich's Theatine Church on Odeonsplatz. Elector Ferdinand Maria and Henriette Adelaide commissioned the seventeenth-century church after the birth of their heir, bringing Italian Baroque forms and extensive white stucco into Bavaria. The nave, dome, side chapels, crypt, and royal tombs remain active Catholic space shaped by wartime damage and later restoration.

Frauenkirche

Two domed towers define Munich's Frauenkirche, a late Gothic brick cathedral completed in the fifteenth century, and legend adds color, but the building's real importance lies in its episcopal role, urban scale and relationship to the Bavarian ruling dynasty. A broad nave, chapels, tombs, stained glass, postwar reconstruction and the mark popularly called the Devil's Footprint fill the interior.

St. Michael München

Munich's Jesuits built St. Michael's Church in the late sixteenth century as a major Counter-Reformation statement. A broad barrel-vaulted nave, richly ordered facade, side chapels, choir, and Wittelsbach crypt connect Renaissance architecture with dynastic burial and Catholic education. Wartime destruction required extensive rebuilding, while services and concerts continue within the pedestrian center.

Wittelsbacher Brunnen

Sculptor Adolf von Hildebrand designed the Wittelsbach Fountain on Lenbachplatz around the turn of the twentieth century, and traffic surrounds the monument, but its layered composition remains visible from the paved square and nearby sidewalks. Stone basins, bronze figures, horses, and allegories of water's destructive and beneficial forces commemorate Munich's modern water supply.

Siegestor

Munich's Siegestor is a nineteenth-century triumphal arch commissioned by King Ludwig I, echoing Roman models and later compared to Berlin's Brandenburg Gate. War damage left it partly ruined before decades of repair. An inscription for peace replaced purely martial messaging after 1945.
