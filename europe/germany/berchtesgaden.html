Berchtesgaden

Berchtesgaden was established in the twelfth century around an Augustinian monastery and grew wealthy through salt mining. It became an independent prince-provostry within the Holy Roman Empire before being annexed by Bavaria in 1810. The region's dramatic alpine scenery attracted Bavarian royalty and, later, tourists. Today, Berchtesgaden is known for its historic salt mines, the Watzmann mountain, and the pristine Konigssee.

Kehlsteinhaus

Perched at an elevation of 1834m, The Eagle's Nest was originally built as a mountain retreat for Hitler and has since been repurposed into a restaurant with panoramic views. Accessible only by a special shuttle bus from the Kehlsteinhaus bus station, this historic site has a rich World War II history and was once used by top officials of the Third Reich regime.

The Rossfeld Panorama Strasse is a mountain road that spans 15.4 kilometers along the high alpine terrain, offering views of the Berchtesgaden Alps, Dachstein Massif, and Salzkammergut. It is the highest panoramic road in Germany, reaching an altitude of 1,600 meters with winding hairpin turns.

Berghof

Berghof, once the mountain retreat of Adolf Hitler, was a chalet that hosted not only Hitler but also his companion Eva Braun, her friends, and the families of Nazi officials. Although the original Berghof no longer stands due to bombing during World War II, travellers can stay at Hotel zum Turken nearby and explore underground bunkers that once connected to Hitler's residence.
