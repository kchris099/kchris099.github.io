Mainz

Mainz stands where the Main River joins the Rhine and was a Roman military base known as Mogontiacum. Archbishop Willigis began the cathedral around 975, and the city became an electorate of the Holy Roman Empire. Johannes Gutenberg developed movable type printing here around 1450. French sieges and annexations in the 18th and 19th centuries altered its borders. Today Mainz is the capital of Rhineland-Palatinate.

St. Augustine's Church

St. Augustine's Church in Mainz was built for Augustinian hermits between 1768 and 1771 in late Baroque style. It survived World War II bombing with its ornate interior and ceiling frescoes largely intact. The church remains a rare preserved eighteenth-century sacred interior in the city.

St. Stephan's Church

St. Stephan's Church in Mainz was founded in 990 and later rebuilt in Gothic form as a major ecclesiastical landmark on the city's hill. Marc Chagall's blue stained-glass windows define the modern interior experience. The church continues parish life above the old town.

Fastnachtsbrunnen

Mainz's Fastnachtsbrunnen, unveiled in 1967, celebrates Carnival with more than two hundred bronze figures drawn from Fastnacht lore. Satirical characters recall the season's licensed mockery of authority. The fountain sits in the old town as a public emblem of Rosenmontag tradition.

Mainz Cathedral

Mainz Cathedral began under Archbishop Willigis around 975 and grew through repeated fires, rebuilding and additions. Six towers, red sandstone, Romanesque massing, Gothic chapels, Baroque memorials, cloisters. Tombs and liturgical furnishings reflect the power of Mainz's archbishops and electors. Coronations and imperial assemblies form part of its history, while the building is an active Catholic cathedral.

Heunensäule

A plain cylindrical sandstone block called the Heunensäule stands in Mainz's market square beside the cathedral. It was quarried in the Roman period for an intended monumental project but never used, then presented to the city in 1975 during a cathedral anniversary. Bronze additions at the base summarize episodes of Mainz history, turning an ancient unfinished column into a modern civic marker.

Holzturm

Holzturm is an early fifteenth-century Gothic gate tower from Mainz's medieval walls, later used as a prison. One of the few surviving fortification fragments, it shows the scale of the former riverside defences. Stone floors and narrow openings recall its harsh secondary role.

Schloss Biebrich

Biebrich Palace faces the Rhine in Wiesbaden, across the river region from Mainz. Princes of Nassau developed the Baroque residence during the eighteenth century around a circular rotunda, long wings, ceremonial rooms, and a riverside garden later reshaped as a landscape park. Government use and events limit interior access, while the exterior and park remain the clearest way to understand its courtly scale.

Wiesbaden City Palace

Wiesbaden City Palace was built in the 1830s and 1840s as a restrained neoclassical residence for the dukes of Nassau. Courtyards and formal rooms later served Prussian rulers and now contain the Hessian State Parliament. Parliamentary work, security and scheduled guided visits control access, while the exterior remains integrated with Schlossplatz, the Marktkirche and the old civic center.

Kochbrunnen

Steam and a mineral smell rise from Wiesbaden's Kochbrunnen, one of the city's principal hot springs. Water emerges at roughly 66 degrees Celsius and feeds a public fountain beneath a neo-Baroque pavilion, although treatment systems and health guidance affect tasting. The spring records Wiesbaden's long spa economy more directly than a decorative fountain, while surfaces nearby may be wet or mineral-stained.

Roman Theatre

Mainz's Roman theater occupied a slope beside the ancient city and could accommodate a very large audience in provincial Mogontiacum. Railway construction and later excavation exposed curved seating foundations, radial walls, passages, and parts of the stage area near the modern station; the open archaeological site is fragmentary, so plans and interpretation help reconstruct a monument once far larger than the surviving masonry.
