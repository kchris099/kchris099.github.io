Explore Mainz

A Brief History

Mainz stands where the Main River joins the Rhine and was a Roman military base known as Mogontiacum. Archbishop Willigis began the cathedral around 975, and the city became an electorate of the Holy Roman Empire. Johannes Gutenberg developed movable type printing here around 1450. French sieges and annexations in the 18th and 19th centuries altered its borders. Today Mainz is the capital of Rhineland-Palatinate.

St. Augustine's Church

Augustine's Church is a visitor site in Mainz. Constructed between 1768 and 1771, this late Baroque church was originally built for Augustinian hermits. It survived the extensive bombings of World War II intact, preserving its ornate interior and ceiling frescoes as a monument of 18th-century religious architecture.

Kirschgarten

Kirschgarten is a visitor site in Mainz. This historic square is known for its well-preserved half-timbered houses dating back to the 15th and 16th centuries. Historically characterized by its dense medieval layout, it served as a residential center for local craftsmen and captures the authentic architectural atmosphere of the old town.

St. Stephan's Church

St. Stephan's Church is a religious building in Mainz, Germany. Stephan's Church is a visitor site in Mainz. Founded in 990, this Gothic church has a history intertwined with the city's ecclesiastical power. The exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside service times.

Fastnachtsbrunnen

Fastnachtsbrunnen is a visitor site in Mainz. Unveiled in 1967, this bronze fountain celebrates the rich tradition of the local Carnival, a cultural institution dating back to the 19th century. Featuring over 200 figures from carnival lore, it represents the city's historical resistance to authority through satire and humor.

Mainz Cathedral

Construction of this Romanesque cathedral began in 975, reflecting the historical power of the Archbishops who served as Prince-Electors of the Holy Roman Empire. Over centuries of restorations, it accumulated Gothic and Baroque additions, standing as a important site of imperial coronations.

Heunensäule

Heunensäule is a visitor site in Mainz. This cylindrical sandstone pillar dates back to the 4th century, originally quarried by the Romans. In 1975, it was presented to the city to commemorate its 1000th anniversary as a cathedral city, connecting the modern area to its ancient Roman origins.

Holzturm

Holzturm is a visitor site in Mainz. Dating back to the early 15th century, this Gothic stone tower was an integral part of the medieval city walls. Historically functioning as a severe prison, it is one of the few surviving remnants of the ancient fortifications, providing a stark reminder of medieval defensive architecture.

Schloss Biebrich

Schloss Biebrich is a visitor site in Mainz, Germany. Records associated with the site include 1841. Kurhaus Wiesbaden GmbH

Kurhaus Wiesbaden GmbH is a Neoclassical building with Art Nouveau elements, including a metal and glass dome. The venue hosts high-profile events, conventions, and traditional performing arts shows. It also houses Wiesbaden's prestigious casino and an upscale restaurant. Adjacent to the Kurhaus is the Kurpark, a public park known for its English landscape design, featuring a lake with boat rentals and an 6-meter tall fountain.

Hessisches Staatstheater Wiesbaden

Hessisches Staatstheater Wiesbaden, located on Christian-Zais-Strasse 3, is a historic theater dating back to 1894 and is considered theaters in Germany. This opulent destination hosts a diverse program from September to June, featuring operettas, musicals, ballets, and plays. The International May Festival is a highlight with concerts, classical in-house drama productions, and guest performances by known theater companies.

Evangelical Market church Wiesbaden

The Evangelical Market Church in Wiesbaden, Germany is a neo-Gothic brick church with a landmark main spire housing a 49-bell carillon. Despite significant damage during WWII, the church has undergone multiple restoration projects and is the tallest building in Wiesbaden. Its five red towers dominate the skyline and the sound of its hand-pulled bells resonates throughout the city.

Wiesbaden City Palace

Wiesbaden City Palace, a 19th-century Neoclassical palace In Wiesbaden, was originally intended to be the residence of Duke William of Nassau. Although it was completed after his death in 1841, the palace has had a rich history, serving as a government office and even becoming a second home for Prussian Kaisers. Today, it houses the Hessian State Parliament and offers guided tours for travellers interested in its historical significance.

Kochbrunnen

Kochbrunnen is a known hot spring in Wiesbaden, known for its open-air fountain located on a public square. The spring emerges at a scorching 66°C and has been recorded since the 14th century. The water is filtered through a processing plant before being channeled to the square, where travellers can also find a drinking water fountain under a Neo-Baroque pavilion.

It offers full event management services and has gained a reputation as an venue for conferences. Travellers have praised its impeccable organization and cleanliness over the years. St Elizabeth's Church

St Elizabeth's Church, built in 1855, is the only Russian Orthodox church in the city and is known for its striking gilt domes. Situated atop Wiesbaden's Neroberg hill, it offers a view of the city. The church was constructed by Duke Adolf of Nassau as a tribute to his late wife, Grand Duchess Elizabeth Mikhailovna of Russia. The church also houses her grave and is surrounded by the largest Russian cemetery in Europe outside of Russia.

It's not a traditional zoo but rather a place for families to enjoy nature walks, visit the petting zoo, and observe animals like sheep, bunnies, roosters, deer, birds, and other wildlife.

Sanctuary of Isis and Magna Mater

The Sanctuary of Isis and Magna Mater is an unexpected gem located in the basement of a shopping mall in Mainz. Unearthed during the mall's construction, this ancient temple dates back to 300-400 AD and houses a fascinating collection of artifacts including clay lamps, tablets, and statuettes displayed in glass cases for travellers to admire. A small museum has been set up around the ruins, complete with a raised glass walkway offering a unique perspective on the sanctuary.
