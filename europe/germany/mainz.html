Explore Mainz

A Brief History

Mainz stands where the Main River joins the Rhine and was a Roman military base known as Mogontiacum. Archbishop Willigis began the cathedral around 975, and the city became an electorate of the Holy Roman Empire. Johannes Gutenberg developed movable type printing here around 1450. French sieges and annexations in the 18th and 19th centuries altered its borders. Today Mainz is the capital of Rhineland-Palatinate.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

St. Augustine's Church

Constructed between 1768 and 1771, this late Baroque church was originally built for Augustinian hermits. It survived the extensive bombings of World War II intact, preserving its ornate interior and ceiling frescoes as a monument of 18th-century religious architecture.

Free

Kirschgarten

This historic square is known for its well-preserved half-timbered houses dating back to the 15th and 16th centuries. Historically characterized by its dense medieval layout, it served as a residential center for local craftsmen and captures the authentic architectural atmosphere of the old town.

Free

St. Stephan's Church

Founded in 990, this Gothic church has a history intertwined with the city's ecclesiastical power. Rebuilt several times and damaged during World War II, it features radiant blue stained-glass windows created by Marc Chagall in the post-war era as a gesture of Jewish-German reconciliation.

Free

Fastnachtsbrunnen

Unveiled in 1967, this bronze fountain celebrates the rich tradition of the local Carnival, a cultural institution dating back to the 19th century. Featuring over 200 figures from carnival lore, it represents the city's historical resistance to authority through satire and humor.

Free

Mainz Cathedral

Construction of this Romanesque cathedral began in 975, reflecting the historical power of the Archbishops who served as Prince-Electors of the Holy Roman Empire. Over centuries of restorations, it accumulated Gothic and Baroque additions, standing as a pivotal site of imperial coronations.

Free

Heunensäule

This cylindrical sandstone pillar dates back to the 4th century, originally quarried by the Romans. In 1975, it was presented to the city to commemorate its 1000th anniversary as a cathedral city, connecting the modern area to its ancient Roman origins.

Free

Holzturm

Dating back to the early 15th century, this Gothic stone tower was an integral part of the medieval city walls. Historically functioning as a severe prison, it stands as one of the few surviving remnants of the ancient fortifications, providing a stark reminder of medieval defensive architecture.
