Bad Reichenhall

Bad Reichenhall's salt springs financed medieval trade and later a spa economy in Bavaria's Berchtesgadener Land. Wittelsbach rulers guarded the brine works; 19th-century Kurhaus buildings and saltwater graduation towers turned the town into a resort. Alpine hinterland links to Salzburg and Berchtesgaden shaped traffic. Today the old saltworks museum, spa parks, and mountain railways record how brine wealth and health tourism built the town.

Alte Saline

Alte Saline in Bad Reichenhall is a nineteenth-century saltworks complex now interpreted as an industrial museum. Salt production shaped the town from Celtic times through modern spa use. Engine houses, brine infrastructure, and exhibits explain how the saline industry funded local growth.

Königlicher Kurgarten

Curving paths, mature trees, flower beds, fountains, and a concert rotunda define the Royal Spa Garden in Bad Reichenhall. Carl von Effner laid out the grounds in the nineteenth century beside spa and salt-related buildings, creating a formal setting for promenades and music. The nearby graduation house demonstrates brine evaporation, while the garden itself remains a public landscape.

Gradierhaus

The Gradierhaus in Bad Reichenhall is a long timber graduation tower where brine drips over blackthorn bundles to enrich salt air for open-air inhalation. A garden path runs beside the structure. Spa visitors walk the length of the wall as part of the town's traditional Kur landscape.

Wandelhalle

Wandelhalle in Bad Reichenhall is the covered spa promenade within the Kurpark, linking drinking fountains, seating, and access toward the Gradierhaus. The hall belongs to the classic German spa-town pattern of sheltered walks between treatment buildings. Concerts and Kur rituals still animate the arcade.

RupertusTherme

Thermal pools and saunas look toward the Alps beside Rupertuspark. Family zones with slides sit apart from quieter adult wellness areas. The complex continues Bad Reichenhall's identity as a salt-and-spa resort pressed against the mountain edge, with indoor and outdoor water shared through the day.

Burgruine Karlstein

Castle ruins stand a short walk from the Baroque Wallfahrtskirche St. Pankraz. Open masonry and interpretive signs mark the hilltop excavation. The pairing of ruin and pilgrimage church defines the ridge above the spa valley, where walkers move between the two landmarks on the same outing.

Almbachklamm

Almbachklamm near Marktschellenberg is a roughly two-kilometre gorge walk with bridges and rock passages above the stream. Viewpoints open between narrow walls, and some stretches lack full railings. The hike is often used on warm days as a shaded route into the Berchtesgaden foothills.

Salt Mine Berchtesgaden

The Berchtesgaden Salt Mine operates in the Bavarian Alps southeast of Bad Reichenhall. Guided underground visits use protective clothing and begin with a miners' train. Wooden slides, extraction chambers and machinery explain salt production, while a boat crossing on a subterranean lake adds another view of the brine-filled workings.

47.6378° N / 13.0179° E

09 AdmissionFree

Schloss Berchtesgaden

Berchtesgaden's royal palace developed from an Augustinian monastery founded in the Middle Ages. Bavarian rulers later adapted the cloister, chapels, halls and residential rooms, filling them with furniture, paintings, weapons and decorative arts. Guided tours connect monastic architecture with Wittelsbach use, while the adjacent collegiate church remains a separate active religious building.

47.6332° N / 13.0034° E

Nationalparkzentrum Haus der Berge

Also called the House of Mountains, this Berchtesgaden National Park centre works as a visitor hub and museum on the Alpine landscape. Exhibits explain the ranges, wildlife, and trails of the protected area. Indoor galleries and outdoor approaches orient day trips into the park.
