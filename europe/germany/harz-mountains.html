Harz Mountains

The Harz range in northern Germany was mined for silver and copper from the Middle Ages, supporting towns such as Goslar and Quedlinburg. Dense forests and narrow valleys carried trade routes and later steam railways, including the Brocken line. Legends of witches and spirits appear in local folklore tied to Brocken peak. National park designation in 1990 protected forests and reservoirs used for hydropower since the 19th century.

Rappbode Dam & Suspension Bridge

Two separate engineering works define this part of the Rappbode valley. The concrete dam impounds a major Harz reservoir, while the nearby pedestrian suspension bridge crosses high above the downstream gorge. Forested slopes, service roads and reservoir infrastructure frame the views, and the exposed bridge gives a direct sense of the valley's depth and scale.

Hexentanzplatz (Witches' Dance Floor)

The Hexentanzplatz, or Witches' Dance Floor, is a plateau above the Bode Gorge associated with local folklore about Walpurgis Night gatherings. A cable car connects the site to Thale, and an open-air theater stages seasonal performances. Trails lead to viewpoints over the gorge and the Brocken massif.

51.7310° N / 11.0264° E

The Brocken

Mist, wind, peatland and subalpine vegetation shape the Brocken, the Harz's high summit at 1,141 metres. A narrow-gauge railway, hiking routes, weather station, museum and observation facilities occupy a peak once restricted as a Cold War border zone, and folklore about witches and Goethe overlays a sensitive natural environment where visibility and temperature can differ sharply from valleys below.

51.7991° N / 10.6156° E

Harz Narrow Gauge Railways

The Harz Narrow Gauge Railways operate a network of steam trains through forests, valleys, and mountain towns. Lines connect Wernigerode, Brocken, Quedlinburg, and Nordhausen among other stations. The rides are a practical way to reach trailheads and a draw for visitors interested in historic rail travel.

51.8388° N / 10.7870° E
