Wiesbaden

Wiesbaden grew from Roman spa springs into one of Europe's leading resort cities in the 19th century, when aristocrats and bourgeois visitors filled Kurhaus, colonnades, and villa quarters. The dukes of Nassau added Biebrich Palace and landscaped parks along the Rhine before Prussian administration and later state capital functions followed. Wartime bombing and postwar rebuilding altered parts of the center while preserving major spa architecture.

Schloss Biebrich

Schloss Biebrich is a Baroque residence on the Rhine built from 1698 for the counts and later dukes of Nassau-Usingen before the court moved to Wiesbaden. Wings enclose courtyards facing the river and park. The palace now hosts events and recalls the ducal chapter that preceded the spa city's prominence.

Schlosspark Biebrich

The Schlosspark Biebrich extends north from the palace with meadows, tree groups, and paths shaped in the 19th century into an English-style landscape park. Joggers, walkers, and picnickers use the open grounds beside the Rhine. The park shows how residence estates were opened for leisure and public access.

50.0426° N / 8.2351° E

03 AdmissionFree

Mosburg

The Mosburg is a romantic artificial ruin in the Biebrich park, erected in 1804 on older foundations beside a pond. Gothicizing fragments were fashionable in landscaped parks seeking historical atmosphere. The structure belongs to the 19th-century redesign of the ducal grounds north of the palace.

50.0460° N / 8.2371° E
