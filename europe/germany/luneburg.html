Luneburg

Lüneburg's medieval wealth came from salt extracted beneath the town and shipped to Hanseatic markets. Brick warehouses, the town hall, and St. John's Church record that boom. When the saltworks declined, the university and later administrative roles sustained the city. Subsidence from mining tilted some streets. Half-timbered and brick façades around Am Sande still read as a compact Hanseatic salt capital on the edge of the Lüneburg Heath.

Old crane in the Lüneburg harbor

Lüneburg's old harbour crane, documented from 1346 and rebuilt in later centuries, once lifted salt and timber at the Ilmenau quay. The timber-clad machine still anchors the waterfront promenade. Embankment paths run past warehouses and cafés that replaced the medieval loading yards.

Am Sande

Am Sande forms a long market square lined with stepped and curved gables beneath the leaning spire of St. John's Church, and salt merchants once moved carts across its sandy surface before paving replaced the original ground. Shops, restaurants, buses, festivals, and daily pedestrian traffic now occupy a space that still reveals Lüneburg's prosperity through its brick townhouses.

St. John's Church

St John's in Lüneburg stands in the historic centre as Brick Gothic fabric with roots traced to the tenth century and a fourteenth-century steeple that leans noticeably. Local legend grew around the tilt of the tower. Outside services the high nave and side aisles are usually open.

05 AdmissionFree

St. Michaelis Church

St. Michaelis Church, dating back to the 14th-15th century, holds historical significance as it was where Johann Sebastian Bach attended school and sang in the church choir. The church's acoustic qualities are praised, making it a popular venue for events like Christmas services. Travellers can explore both the interior and exterior of this centuries-old church while enjoying its medieval surroundings.

06 AdmissionFree

Kalkberg

Gypsum mining reduced Lüneburg's Kalkberg from a much larger hill to the rocky remnant visible today, and the site helps explain both the natural salt-related deposits beneath the city and the long extraction history that altered its landscape. Paths cross a protected urban nature reserve with exposed geology, dry grassland, and viewpoints over red-tiled roofs and church towers.

53.2494° N / 10.3970° E

German Salt Museum

The German Salt Museum (Salzmuseum) occupies former saline works in Luneburg and covers roughly a thousand years of local salt production. Exhibits explain brine extraction, boiling houses, and Luneburg's role supplying the Hanseatic League. Surviving industrial buildings and tools show how salt wealth funded the town's brick Gothic civic architecture.

53.2441° N / 10.4020° E

Theater Lüneburg

Theater Lüneburg maintains a repertory that includes drama, opera, musical theater, dance, concerts and work for younger audiences. Resident performers and guest artists use the main auditorium and smaller stages across a changing season. The venue's cultural role comes from live production and regional ensemble work, with each program offering a different reason to enter the otherwise functional theater building.

09 AdmissionFree

Lüneburg Town Hall

Lüneburg Town Hall grew from medieval beginnings around 1230 into a complex civic pile with a later Baroque facade on Am Sande. Interiors retain halls from successive building phases. The ensemble ranks among northern Germany's most elaborate historic town halls.

53.2503° N / 10.4076° E

Liebesgrund

Former moat ground of the Lüneburg Wall now holds open lawn for soccer and frisbee, with wall remnants visible nearby. A publicly accessible kindergarten playground sits on the edge. After shopping around Am Sande, the grass offers a pause close to the historic core.
