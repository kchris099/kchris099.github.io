Luneburg

Lüneburg's medieval wealth came from salt extracted beneath the town and shipped to Hanseatic markets. Brick warehouses, the town hall, and St. John's Church record that boom. When the saltworks declined, the university and later administrative roles sustained the city. Subsidence from mining tilted some streets. Half-timbered and brick façades around Am Sande still read as a compact Hanseatic salt capital on the edge of the Lüneburg Heath.

Am Sande

Am Sande is a market square located in the historic old town of Luneburg, Germany. The square is surrounded by gabled buildings that house various shops and eateries, adding to its medieval charm. St John's Church with its leaning spire overlooks the cobblestone plaza, creating a backdrop. In the Middle Ages, Am Sande was covered in sand rather than cobbles, making it easier for merchants to navigate their carts to the market.

German Salt Museum

The German Salt Museum (Salzmuseum) occupies former saline works in Luneburg and covers roughly a thousand years of local salt production. Exhibits explain brine extraction, boiling houses, and Luneburg's role supplying the Hanseatic League. Surviving industrial buildings and tools show how salt wealth funded the town's brick Gothic civic architecture.
