Cologne

Cologne, one of Germany's oldest cities, was founded by the Romans in the 1st century AD. It flourished throughout the Middle Ages as a major religious and commercial center, famously anchored by its monumental Gothic Cathedral. Despite extensive World War II devastation, the city has careful restored its historic landmarks and active Rhineland culture.

Cologne Cathedral

Cologne Cathedral rose over more than six centuries after construction began in 1248 to house the Shrine of the Three Kings. Twin towers, flying buttresses, stained glass, choir stalls, chapels, sculpture. Tombs and the treasury fill a building completed in the nineteenth century from medieval plans. Bomb damage, pollution, constant conservation, pilgrimage and active Catholic worship all remain visible parts of its history.

Hohenzollern Bridge

Built between 1907 and 1911 as a Rhine railway link, the span was rebuilt after World War II for trains and pedestrians. Thousands of love padlocks cover the walkway railings. Pedestrian decks look toward the cathedral and the river traffic below, especially busy at dusk.

Hahnentorburg

Hahnentorburg is an early thirteenth-century gate of Cologne's medieval walls on Rudolfplatz. Holy Roman emperors traditionally entered the city here after coronation ceremonies farther afield. The twin towers remain a rare standing fragment of the former defensive ring.

Alter Markt

Alter Markt has been Cologne's civic and market square since the Middle Ages, beside the town hall tower. Heavy wartime damage led to reconstruction that kept the historic footprint. Festivals, carnival gatherings, and outdoor seating still fill the irregular plaza.

Cologne's Chocolate Museum opened in 1993 on the Rheinauhafen to trace cacao from Mesoamerican origins through European trade to modern production. Displays cover growing, processing, and industrial chocolate-making inside a contemporary building on the former harbour edge.

Kranhauser im Rheinauhafen

The Kranhäuser in Cologne's Rheinauhafen are three crane-shaped towers completed around 2010, echoing historic harbour cranes along the Rhine. Offices and apartments fill the cantilevered forms. They signal the port district's shift from industry to mixed waterfront use.

Flora und Botanischer Garten Koln

Flora und Botanischer Garten Köln opened in 1864 as a formal park for recreation and plant study after older city gardens were lost. Glasshouses, themed beds, and tree collections fill the grounds in Riehl. The gardens remain a major green refuge beside Cologne's northern districts.

Deer and other native species occupy woodland enclosures in Cologne's Lindenthal district. Families treat the lawns and shaded walks as a neighbourhood green space. The layout stays informal compared with the city's larger zoo, so visits feel like a local park outing with animals.

Heinzelmännchenbrunnen

Heinzelmännchenbrunnen in Cologne is an ornate 19th-century fountain that commemorates the local legend of house gnomes known as Heinzelmannchen. According to the tale, these gnome-like creatures used to do all the work in Cologne at night, allowing the citizens to relax during the day. The fountain depicts a curious tailor's wife trying to catch a glimpse of the Heinzelmannchen and ends up chasing them away.

Great St. Martin Church

Groß St. Martin rises above Cologne's Altstadt with a trefoil choir and Romanesque tower group built mainly between about 1150 and 1250. The church sits on former Rhine island ground near the Alter Markt. Stone exteriors and the spacious interior are typically accessible outside Mass.

Japanese Garden Leverkusen

The Japanese Garden in Leverkusen, established in 1913 by a local industrialist, lays out ponds, stone lanterns, and planted islands in a compact park. Paths circle water and maple plantings rather than open sports lawns. The garden sits north of Cologne proper but is commonly grouped with day trips from the city.

BayArena

BayArena, the home of Bayer Leverkusen, is a must-visit for football enthusiasts. With a seating capacity of around 30,000 spectators, it offers an immersive behind-the-scenes tour that includes walking through the tunnel onto the pitch and visiting various lounges and rooms. The stadium's design was based on the Ruhrstadium in Bochum and was constructed section by section to accommodate the club's growing demands.

Cologne Triangle is a high-rise on the Deutz bank of the Rhine with a public viewing platform over the cathedral, old town, and river bends. Lifts and stairs reach the deck for a full circle of the urban basin. Evening light across the bridges is a frequent reason for the ascent.

Rheinboulevard is a stepped riverside promenade about 500 metres long on Cologne's Deutz shore opposite the old town. Broad stone terraces face the cathedral skyline across the Rhine. Walkers and cyclists use the bank as an open viewing platform rather than a commercial strip.

Claudius Therme in Cologne's Rheinpark offers indoor and outdoor thermal pools, saunas, rest rooms, and garden areas. Mineral water and warm baths continue a modern spa tradition beside the Rhine fairgrounds. The complex sits within easy reach of the cable-car park landscape.

Dufthaus 4711 is the urban flagship store of the cologne house, offering guided history tours and fragrance seminars. Established in 1792, this historic location at Glockengasse provides travellers with a unique olfactory experience. Guests can partake in guided tours, explore the fragrance fountain of Original Eau de Cologne, and even create their own personalized scent by learning about different ingredients.

Severinstorburg

Severinstorburg once guarded the southern road through Cologne's medieval wall. Built mainly in the thirteenth century, the gate combines a central tower, passage, flanking structures and later alterations made after the surrounding fortifications disappeared. Traffic and neighborhood life now pass around it, while interior rooms are used for events.

Volksgarten Köln

Nineteenth-century lawns, a pond, tree avenues, and play areas fill this southern municipal park. Joggers and families use the paths away from cathedral crowds. It remains one of Cologne's older public green spaces, still used daily for exercise and informal gatherings.

St. Gereon

St. Gereon's distinctive decagonal dome rises over a church whose core reaches back to late antiquity, with major medieval work completed around 1227. Later centuries altered interiors while much of the unusual exterior volume survived. The church stands slightly apart from Cologne's denser parish cluster.

Scala Theater

Scala Theater stages comedy, musical entertainment and popular productions in Cologne, often using local dialect and regional humor. The private venue has an informal atmosphere and a program aimed at broad local audiences. Its relatively small auditorium keeps performers close to spectators and supports the direct style associated with Cologne's popular theater tradition. Regional humor gives many productions a local voice.
