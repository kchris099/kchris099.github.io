Explore Münster

A Brief History

Münster served as the capital of a prince-bishopric from the Middle Ages until secularisation in 1803, with the St. Paulus Cathedral crowning its walled old town. The city hosted Catholic and Protestant delegations in 1648 when the Peace of Westphalia ended the Thirty Years' War, a settlement commemorated in the Rathaus Friedenssaal. Rebuilt after wartime bombing, Münster's arcaded Prinzipalmarkt and Schloss now anchor a university city known for cycling culture.

Top Sights & Activities

Burg Hülshoff

Burg Hülshoff stands in Havixbeck west of Münster as a moated Renaissance castle that was home to the Droste-Hülshoff noble family from the 15th century. Poet Annette von Droste-Hülshoff was born here in 1797. The Droste Museum in the castle documents her life and Westphalian aristocratic culture, while the surrounding park and café remain open to visitors without charge.

Free

Prinzipalmarkt

The Prinzipalmarkt is Münster's arcaded main street, lined with gabled merchant houses rebuilt after the Second World War to match medieval proportions. Iron arcades shelter shopfronts, cafés, and the Gothic town hall on the cobbled thoroughfare. The street forms the commercial and ceremonial heart of the Altstadt and links the Lambertikirche to the Erbdrostenhof palace.

€3

Historic City Hall

Münster's Historic City Hall on the Prinzipalmarkt dates from the 14th century and gained world significance when the Peace of Westphalia was proclaimed here in 1648. The Friedenssaal chamber displays portraits of the negotiating diplomats and carved wood panelling from the peace congress. The gabled façade and adjoining Stadtweinhaus were reconstructed after wartime damage.

Free

St. Lamberti

St. Lamberti is a late Gothic hall church on the north side of the Prinzipalmarkt, built from 1375 with a distinctive tower completed in the 19th century. Three iron cages on the steeple once displayed the bodies of Anabaptist leaders executed after the Münster Rebellion of 1535. The church houses a notable organ and stained-glass windows from several building phases.

Free

Schloss Münster

Schloss Münster is a Baroque palace built between 1767 and 1787 as the residence of Prince-Bishop Maximilian Friedrich von Königsegg-Rothenfels. The yellow sandstone building with its central dome now houses administrative offices of the University of Münster. The Schlossplatz lawn in front hosts markets and university events beneath plane trees.

Free

Schlossplatz

Schlossplatz is the open square before Schloss Münster in the city centre, framed by the palace façade and university buildings. The lawn and gravel paths provide a gathering space for students and visitors between the Altstadt and the Aasee lake shore. Seasonal markets and outdoor events use the plaza throughout the year.

Free

St.-Paulus-Dom

St. Paulus Cathedral on Domplatz combines a Romanesque westwork and crypt from the 13th century with Gothic choir and nave additions. The cathedral served as the church of the prince-bishops of Münster and houses the astronomical clock dating from the late Middle Ages. Its twin towers dominate the skyline of the old town.

Free

Domplatz Münster

Domplatz spreads before St. Paulus Cathedral in the centre of Münster's Altstadt, bordered by the Domherrenhaus and chapter buildings. The cobbled square hosts the weekly farmers' market and forms the setting for religious processions and civic ceremonies. Stone benches and plane trees line the open space between cathedral westwork and surrounding lanes.

Free

Kiepenkerl-Denkmal

The Kiepenkerl monument depicts a travelling merchant carrying a wicker basket, or Kiepe, on his back in the Spiekerhof lane near the cathedral quarter. The bronze figure by August Schmiemann from 1896 commemorates itinerant traders who carried salt and goods across Westphalia. The statue lends its name to the surrounding Kiepenkerlviertel neighbourhood of lanes and courtyards.

Free

Giant Pool Balls

The Giant Pool Balls sculpture by Guillaume Bijl stands on the shore of the Aasee lake in Münster, consisting of oversized billiard balls arranged on a lawn near the promenade. Installed as part of the city's public art programme, the work plays with scale and leisure imagery beside the popular walking and cycling path around the artificial lake.
