Explore Baden-Baden

A Brief History

Baden-Baden developed around thermal springs used since Roman times in the northern Black Forest. Medieval margraves of Baden built a residence here, and 19th-century spa culture drew European visitors to its Kurhaus and colonnaded baths. The town remained part of the Grand Duchy of Baden until 1918. Postwar years preserved its parks, casino, and belle-époque villas while rail links connected it to Karlsruhe and Strasbourg.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Altstadt (Old Town)

Altstadt (Old Town) is a visitor site in Baden-Baden, Germany. The Altstadt of Celle is known for its concentration of beautifully preserved half-timbered (Fachwerk) houses. The pedestrianized streets offer a atmosphere filled with shops and cafes. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

€39

Friedrichsbad (Spa)

Of course, a visit to Baden-Baden would not be complete without a bathing for oneself. After visitors see how the Romans did it, visitors can pop upstairs to Friedrichsbad to it in the 21st century. Built in 1877, visitors can bath in style in what was considered the most modern bathhouse in Europe when it opened.

Free

Florentinerberg

The Florentinerberg is a terraced hillside in Baden-Baden, known for its very mild microclimate. The area is planted with Mediterranean and subtropical flora, including lemon, orange, and palm trees, which thrive due to the heat radiating from the town's thermal springs. A scenic walking path offers views of the town and the surrounding Black Forest.

Free

Stiftskirche (Collegiate Church)

Stiftskirche (Collegiate Church) sits within the Baden-Baden area of Baden-Baden, documented in local heritage registers for its civic or cultural role. Visitors approach from the main access road and combine the visit with neighbouring heritage buildings and public spaces in the district.

€5

Römische Badruinen (Roman Bath Ruins)

Visitors can go back to where it all began with this one of the sightseeing activities in Baden-Baden. The area offers the ruins of the old Roman baths. While the Friedrichsbad (coming up next) was built on top of these ruins, some of them have been preserved as a museum and open to visitors them.
