A Brief History

Berlin, the vibrant capital of Germany, has a cultural and political history as tumultuous as it is fascinating. From its origins as a small 13th-century trading post, it grew into the heart of the Prussian Empire, the epicenter of the roaring 1920s avant-garde, and the divided focal point of the Cold War. Following the historic fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989, the city was reborn, seamlessly weaving its stark, powerful historical monuments with cutting-edge art, alternative culture, and relentless architectural modernity.

Brandenburg Gate

As dawn casts the first golden rays upon the city, begin your Berlin adventure at the Brandenburg Gate. This neoclassical monument, with its imposing columns and the goddess of victory atop her chariot, has been a witness to Berlin’s history for over two centuries. Its presence evokes the myriad events that have shaped the city, from the peace marches to the tumultuous moments when Berlin was torn in two.

€ 12.00

Neues Museum

Your next destination is the world-renowned Neues Museum, situated on the UNESCO-listed Museum Island. This museum showcases an exquisite collection spanning from prehistory through early history, making it a treasure trove for enthusiasts of human civilization. You can marvel at the extensive array of artifacts from the Egyptian, Prehistoric, and Protohistoric collections.

Varies

Checkpoint Charlie

From the spiritual respite of the Berliner Dom, take a short bus trip near to Checkpoint Charlie, the most famous crossing point between East and West Berlin during the Cold War. You can visit a replica of the original checkpoint and there is also the Museum Haus am Checkpoint Charlie where you can learn more about the Cold War years, the crossing, the Wall and escape attempts. It reflects a bygone era when the world seemed on the brink of nuclear war.

€ 12.00

East Side Gallery

To complete your one-day itinerary, take the U-Bahn (U6) to Hallesches Tor and transfer to the U1 or U3 to Warschauer Straße station, leading you to the East Side Gallery. This remarkable segment of the Berlin Wall, standing along the banks of the Spree River, is the longest open-air gallery in the world. Artists from around the globe have used this former symbol of division as a canvas for their murals, depicting messages of peace, hope, and political commentary.

Varies

Reichstag Building

A brief walk from the Brandenburg Gate is the iconic Reichstag Building, the seat of Germany’s Bundestag, or federal parliament. Famous for its striking glass dome designed by the renowned architect Sir Norman Foster, this architectural marvel symbolizes the reunification of Germany and is a testament to transparency in politics. Register in advance for a visit to the dome, where you’ll gain a bird’s eye view of both Berlin and the workings of its government.

Free

Berliner Dom

As you leave the ancient world behind at the Neues Museum, the Berlin Cathedral (Berliner Dom) beckons. A short walk across Museum Island takes you to this grand structure, which stands as the largest church in Berlin. Venture up to the dome for a breathtaking panoramic view of the city, and inside, witness the lavish baroque and renaissance interiors that narrate stories of Berlin’s ecclesiastical history and the Hohenzollern family’s influence.
