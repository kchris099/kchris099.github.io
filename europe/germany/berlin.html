Destination Guide — Eastern Germany

Explore Berlin

A Brief History

Berlin became the capital of Prussia and later the German Empire after unification in 1871. Boulevards, museums, and the Reichstag expanded through industrial growth. The city was divided after 1945, with the Berlin Wall separating East and West from 1961 until 1989. Reunification restored it as the federal capital. Museumsinsel, Brandenburg Gate, and surviving neighborhoods record Prussian, Weimar, Nazi, and Cold War layers.

Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church

The Kaiser Wilhelm Memorial Church on Breitscheidplatz combines the ruined tower of a neo-Romanesque church destroyed in 1943 with a modern belfry and chapel added in the 1960s. The damaged spire was deliberately left unrepaired as a memorial against war. The new buildings use blue glass and concrete in a striking contrast to the old stonework.

Free

Brandenburg Gate

The Brandenburg Gate is an 18th-century neoclassical monument designed by Carl Gotthard Langhans at the western end of Unter den Linden. It marked the entrance to the city on the road to Brandenburg an der Havel. After standing in the shadow of the Berlin Wall, it became a focal point for celebrations of German reunification in 1989.

€24

Berliner Fernsehturm

Berliner Fernsehturm is a visitor site in Berlin. The Berlin TV Tower was built in 1969 in Alexanderplatz by the East German government as a visible symbol of socialist modernity. At 368 meters it remains the tallest structure in Germany. An observation deck and revolving restaurant draw visitors for views across both former East and West Berlin districts.

Free

Marx-Engels Forum

The Marx-Engels Forum is a public square near the Spree featuring bronze statues of Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels seated on a bench. Created in the 1980s by the East German state, it reflects the ideological landscape of the former capital. The square was redesigned after reunification but the monuments remain.

€4

Siegessaule

Siegessaule is a visitor site in Berlin. The Victory Column, or Siegessäule, stands in the Tiergarten and commemorates Prussian military victories in the 19th century. A gilded Victoria figure crowns the 67-meter shaft. The column was moved to its current location by the Nazis and now sits at the center of a major traffic circle.

Free

Grosser Tiergarten

The Großer Tiergarten is a 210-hectare park in the city center, originally a royal hunting ground laid out in the 16th century. Paths, lawns, and waterways connect monuments including the Victory Column and the Soviet War Memorial. The park provides open green space amid government buildings and embassies.

Free

Richard Wagner Monument

The Richard Wagner Monument in the Tiergarten is a marble statue of the composer erected in 1908. It was removed during the Nazi era and restored to its original site after the war. The monument honors Wagner's influence on German opera and his years spent in Berlin during the 19th century.

Free

Soviet War Memorial Tiergarten

The Soviet War Memorial in the Tiergarten was built in 1945 to honor Red Army soldiers who died in the Battle of Berlin. A curved colonnade frames a central statue of a Soviet soldier, and the grounds contain graves of fallen troops. It was one of the first memorials erected in the defeated city.

Free

Reichstag Building

The Reichstag building houses the German parliament, recognizable by its Renaissance-style facade and the glass dome added by Norman support after reunification. A fire in 1933 and wartime bombing damaged the structure, which was fully restored for the move of the capital from Bonn to Berlin in 1999. Dome visits require advance registration.

Free

Friedrichswerdersche Church

The Friedrichswerdersche Church was designed by Karl Friedrich Schinkel and completed in 1831 as Berlin's first neo-Gothic church. After wartime damage and periods of disuse, it was restored and now is a museum of 19th-century sculpture. The interior's slender columns and ribbed vaulting illustrate Schinkel's architectural approach.

€15

Berliner Dom

The Berlin Cathedral, or Berliner Dom, is a Protestant church on Museum Island built as the court church of the Hohenzollern dynasty in the early 20th century. Its large dome and ornate interior were restored after wartime damage. The Hohenzollern crypt below the church holds tombs of princes and emperors.

Free

Checkpoint Charlie

In Berlin, Checkpoint Charlie is a historic landmark that once served as the third checkpoint for immigration control during the Cold War. It separated the Soviet zone from the American sector and was a significant point for political visits and espionage activities. Today, travellers can explore its documentation center to learn about dramatic escapes, the Berlin Tank Crisis, and other historical events.

Free

Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe

The memorial is located in Berlin, Germany, and is dedicated to the thousands of Jewish victims of the Nazi genocide. The blocks of concrete are arranged in a mazelike fashion and are said to represent the unending cycle of violence inflicted on the Jews. The information office below the memorial holds records, letters, diaries, and photographs from Holocaust victims.

Free

Potsdamer Platz

Potsdamer Platz is a visitor site in Berlin, Germany. The revitalization began when major corporations like Sony and Daimler established their headquarters here, breathing new life into the area. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Charlottenburg Palace

Charlottenburg Palace is a fortified historic site in Berlin, Germany. Charlottenburg Palace, a Baroque summer residence in Berlin, is a treasure trove of history and art. Walls, courtyards, or towers form the main visitor route when the grounds are open.

Funkturm Berlin

The 150 metre high Funkturm Berlin, built in the 1920s, is now a tourist attraction with an observation deck and art-nouveau restaurant. The tower is based on the Eiffel Tower and was inaugurated during the great German Radio Exhibition in 1926. Albert Einstein gave a speech from the tower's base in 1924.

Free

World Time Clock

The World Time Clock (Weltzeituhr) is a prominent landmark located in Alexanderplatz, Berlin. Erected in 1969 during the East German era, the large, rotating cylinder displays the current time in major cities across the globe. It remains a popular meeting point and a symbol of the city's international connections and Cold War history.

Free

Alexanderplatz

Alexanderplatz is a historic meeting and market place in Berlin that has been rebuilt with modern buildings, including the 365-meter TV tower. The area is busy with over 100 stalls selling a variety of items, from handbags to handicrafts, as well as an array of Christmassy snacks. Travellers can also enjoy fairground rides and glide across a huge ice rink. The location is within walking distance to Mitte and offers easy access to the airport.

Bellevue Palace

Bellevue Palace, a neoclassical white mansion with three wings, has been the official residence of the President of Germany since 1994. Situated on the banks of River Spree in Berlin, it was originally built for Prince August Ferdinand of Prussia in 1785. Over time, it served various purposes including as a private residence and an art gallery before becoming the presidential residence.

Französischer Dom

The Französischer Dom, In Berlin at Gendarmenmarkt, is a stately 17th-century French church known for its domed tower offering panoramic views of the city. Despite its name, which translates to 'French Cathedral,' it's actually just the tower that bears this title. Climbing 284 steps to the top rewards travellers with a 360-degree view of Berlin from an observation deck situated 40 meters above street level.

Friedrichstadt-Palast

Friedrichstadt-Palast is a historic theater in Berlin with a rich heritage dating back to the 19th century. It has evolved from a circus and vaudeville theatre into one of Europe's most modern and spacious theatrical venues. The theater offers a diverse range of performances, including revue shows, children's shows, ballets, opera, and dance presentations, it hosts the popular Quatsch Comedy Club in its basement area.

Tränenpalast

Located Friedrichstrasse, the Tränenpalast, or Palace of Tears, is a reminder of Berlin's divided past. Once an active border crossing point between East and West Berlin, this unassuming 1960s building now is a museum that captures the essence of daily life during the Cold War. Travellers can explore heartfelt stories from those who traversed this route between 1962 and 1990, revealing the emotional farewells that took place here.

German Historical Museum

German Historical Museum is a museum in Berlin, Germany. The German Historical Museum, also known as the Deutsches Historisches Museum (DHM), was established in 1987 to commemorate Berlin's 750th anniversary. It offers a full journey through Germany's rich history, featuring artifacts from various periods and events up to the fall of the Berlin Wall. Permanent galleries and temporary displays are open on posted hours.

Grunewald Tower

Grunewald Tower, a 19th-century brick tower standing over 180 feet tall, offers an observation deck with views of the surrounding forest. Travellers can began on a bike trip through Grunewald Forest, passing by the Devil's Lake and encountering the historical landmark of Grunewald Tower. The tower is situated in Berlin's Grunewald Forest and is a lookout point and recreational destination.

Flakturm Humboldthain

Flakturm Humboldthain is a visitor site in Berlin. The Flak Tower was built in 1938 to protect the city of Berlin from air attacks. It is now a popular tourist spot with tours available that allow travellers to explore the three floors of the bunker and stare deep down into the building's underground landscape.

Olympiastadion Berlin

Olympiastadion Berlin, originally constructed for the 1936 Olympics during Adolf Hitler's reign, is Germany's largest stadium with a capacity of 74,649 seats. The venue retains its original architectural style, featuring imposing columns made from stones brought from the Alps. After much debate about its preservation, the stadium underwent renovations in 2004 and now hosts major sporting events and concerts.

Teufelsberg

Teufelsberg, located in the western part of Berlin, is a man-made hill that was once home to a former U.S. Listening station during the Cold War. The site is now covered in eye-catching street art and offers panoramic views of the city. Travellers can explore the remnants of large satellite dishes and domed towers while immersing themselves in Cold War history. The area's official archives are set to open in 2020, shedding light on its mysterious past.

Kurfürstendamm, also known as Ku'damm, is a legendary boulevard in Berlin that is a must-visit for fashion enthusiasts and shoppers. Berlin Zoological Garden is a public park in Berlin, Germany. Berlin Zoological Garden, located in the city's Tiergarten, spans over 85 acres and houses more than 15,000 animals from around 2,000 species. The zoo is home to a diverse range of creatures including lions, giant pandas, Bactrian camels, giraffes, kangaroos, penguins and even a polar bear named Knut. Paths, lawns, and shaded areas make up the main public facilities.
