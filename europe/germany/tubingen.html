Tübingen

A Hohenzollern castle overlooks the Neckar; the university founded in 1477 turned the hillside into a reformers' and poets' town. Hölderlin's tower, collegiate churches, and student pubs along the river punt route come from centuries of learning rather than from mills or mines. Hegel, Schelling, and Hölderlin studied in the same Stift that trained Württemberg clergy.

Hohentübingen Castle

Hohentübingen Castle crowns a hill above the old town and was expanded into a Renaissance residence in the 16th century. It now houses the University Museum with archaeological collections, including early figurative sculptures from the Swabian Jura. The ramparts offer views across the Neckar valley.

Tübingen Old Town & Market Square

Tübingen's old town survived World War II largely intact, with half-timbered houses and narrow lanes climbing the hillside. The Marktplatz is anchored by a painted 15th-century town hall with an ornate astronomical clock. Student pubs and bookshops fill the streets around the university buildings.

St. George's Collegiate Church

St. George's Collegiate Church is a late-Gothic hall church with stained glass windows from the 15th and 16th centuries. The crypt contains the tombs of several dukes of Württemberg. The choir stalls and carved pulpit are notable examples of late medieval woodwork in the region.

Botanical Garden

The Botanical Garden is maintained by the University of Tübingen just outside the old city walls. Established in the early 19th century, it contains greenhouses and outdoor beds organized by geographic regions. The garden is open to the public and is a teaching and research facility.

Neckar Riverfront & Hölderlin Tower

The Neckar riverfront is lined with tall, colorful half-timbered houses rising directly from the water's edge. The yellow Hölderlin Tower, where the poet Friedrich Hölderlin lived from 1807 to 1843, is the most photographed building on the bank. Weeping willows and punting boats define the scene.

06 AdmissionFree

Stocherkahn Punting

Stocherkahn punting is a Tübingen tradition in which flat wooden boats are propelled along the Neckar by a pole pushed against the riverbed. Students and visitors rent boats in summer for leisurely trips beneath overhanging trees. The practice dates to the 19th century and is central to the city's social life.
