Explore Freiburg

A Brief History

Freiburg im Breisgau was founded around 1120 as a market town in the Black Forest foothills. Its Münster cathedral, begun around 1200, carries a Gothic spire and medieval stained glass. The city housed one of Germany's earliest universities in 1457. Bächle, small water channels in the streets, date from the 13th century. Freiburg sustained bombing in 1944 but retained much of its historic core and later pursued solar-energy research.

Top Sights & Activities

Free

Freiburger Münster (Cathedral)

Located in the central Münsterplatz, you won’t want to miss Freiburg’s 13th century Münster. Even set against the gorgeous Old Town, it has an impressive presence. It took 300 years to build this Romanesque church which withstood even the battering Freiburg took in World War II.

Free

Altstadt (Old Town)

The Altstadt of Celle is renowned for its exceptional concentration of beautifully preserved half-timbered (Fachwerk) houses. The historic center features over 400 of these traditional buildings, dating from the 16th to the 18th centuries, many adorned with intricate carvings and colorful inscriptions. The pedestrianized streets offer a charming atmosphere filled with shops and cafes.

€12

Augustinermuseum

Art lovers will not want to miss the Augustiner Museum particularly if you are looking for things to do in Freiburg on a rainy day as this is a great option. This museum showcases work from the Middle Ages to the 19th century in a former Augustinian monastery. The building itself has been well renovated making a great home for this collection.

Free

Rathausplatz (Town Hall Square)

One of the most charming areas to visit in Freiburg is Rathausplatz or Town Hall Square. It’s a great spot to take a time out from exploring, have a drink and soak in the atmosphere. Don’t miss the Old Town Hall which dates back to the 16th century and is Built in the Renaissance style in red.

Free

Bächle

As you walk around the Old Town, you won’t be able to miss Bächle, the little waterways that flow through the streets. These were created in 1170 from the Dreisam River to provide industrial and cleaning water to the town as well as a way to stop fires, although they were also used as open sewers. These days, there are still 15.5 kilometres of these channels, some of which are underground.
