Koblenz

Koblenz stands at the confluence of the Rhine and Moselle, a site settled in Roman times and fortified by electors, Prussians, and later German governments. Ehrenbreitstein Fortress, churches, waterfront promenades, and the Deutsches Eck monument record military, ecclesiastical, and national commemoration along the rivers. The city hosted the 2011 Bundesgartenschau, renewing parkland and cable-car links across the Rhine.

Schloss Koblenz

The Kurfürstliches Schloss on the Rhine embankment was built in the 18th century for the electors of Trier after their move to Koblenz. Classical facades and riverside gardens face one of Germany's great waterways. The palace marks the formal courtly side of a city defined by confluence and fortification.

Konrad-Adenauer-Ufer/Kastorhof

Konrad-Adenauer-Ufer and the Kastorhof square frame the riverfront approach to St Castor's church and the Deutsches Eck. Embankment paths, plane trees, and open space structure movement at the confluence. The area links old town streets with Rhine and Moselle promenades.

50.3618° N / 7.6048° E

03 AdmissionFree

Basilica of St. Castor

A church has stood near the Rhine-Moselle confluence since the early Middle Ages, and today's Basilica of St. Castor preserves Romanesque fabric with later alterations. Imperial negotiations and ecclesiastical gatherings once used the site, while towers, nave, choir, artworks, and gardens remain beside the Deutsches Eck. Active Catholic worship continues within a setting shaped by repeated rebuilding and restoration.

The riverside station of the Koblenz cable car lifts visitors across the Rhine to Ehrenbreitstein Fortress in minutes. Gondolas provide views over the Deutsches Eck and both rivers. The line, opened for the 2011 Bundesgartenschau, integrates modern visitor access into the historic river landscape.

Ehrenbreitstein Fortress

Prussia rebuilt Ehrenbreitstein Fortress after 1817 on a high rock above the Rhine and Moselle. Bastions, ditches, gates, barracks, tunnels, museums; memorials and terraces form a vast military complex connected to Koblenz by cable car and road. Earlier fortifications occupied the hill, but most visible architecture reflects nineteenth-century strategic planning.

50.3647° N / 7.6151° E

06 AdmissionFree

Deutsches Eck

The Deutsches Eck is the headland where the Moselle joins the Rhine, marked by a monument to Emperor William I erected in 1897 and rebuilt after wartime loss. Flags and embankment paths draw visitors to the tip of the peninsula. The setting turns the natural confluence into a place of regional historical symbolism.

50.3650° N / 7.6065° E

07 AdmissionFree

Schängelbrunnen

The Schängelbrunnen in the old town depicts the Koblenz nickname Schängel, a boy figure who spits water at intervals into the fountain basin. Karl-Jonas cast the bronze in 1940 outside the town hall. The fountain adds a playful civic reference to a city known for churches and fortifications.

50.3602° N / 7.5981° E

08 AdmissionFree

Liebfrauenkirche

Twin onion-capped towers rise above Koblenz's old town at Liebfrauenkirche. The parish church developed from medieval predecessors into a Gothic building with later Baroque tower roofs, furnishings and extensive postwar repair. Its interior, stained glass, sculpture and position among market streets reveal a living neighborhood church.

50.3608° N / 7.5960° E
