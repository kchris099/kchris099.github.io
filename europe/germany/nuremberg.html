Nuremberg

Nuremberg developed as a free imperial city with major commercial, artistic, and political importance in the Holy Roman Empire from the 11th century. The castle hill, market square, and parish churches recall medieval prosperity and Albrecht Dürer's workshop. The city hosted imperial diets and later 19th-century national commemorations before the Nazi party rallies of the 1930s left a difficult 20th-century legacy addressed by documentation centers and memorials.

Nuremberg Castle (Kaiserburg)

Nuremberg Castle crowns the north edge of the old city and served emperors of the Holy Roman Empire during their stays in the imperial city. The Palas, chapel, and Sinwell Tower preserve medieval and later fortifications. Guided tours explain Nuremberg's role in imperial administration and justice.

Hauptmarkt and Schöner Brunnen

The Hauptmarkt is Nuremberg's main market square, framed by the Frauenkirche and gabled townhouses. The Schöner Brunnen, a Gothic fountain shrine of the 14th century, stands at its center. Weekly markets and the Christkindlesmarkt continue centuries of commerce and civic ceremony here.

Germanisches Nationalmuseum

The Germanisches Nationalmuseum occupies a former Carthusian monastery and holds one of the broadest collections of German cultural history, from prehistory to modern art. Dürer holdings, folk art, and scientific instruments anchor the displays. The museum connects Nuremberg with national narratives of craft, art, and daily life.

Weißgerbergasse

Weißgerbergasse preserves a run of medieval and early modern timber-framed houses that recall the tanners and craftsmen who worked in this quarter. Cafés and small shops now line the pedestrian street. It offers one of the clearest views of the old city's domestic architecture away from the main squares.

Nuremberg Frauenkirche

The Frauenkirche was built on the Hauptmarkt in the 14th century at the behest of Emperor Charles IV. Its west facade includes the Männleinlaufen clock with moving figures from 1509. The church remains a major Gothic monument tied directly to the civic life of the square.

Maxbrücke

Maxbrücke crosses the Pegnitz at the eastern edge of the old town, linking quarters around the river islands. Stone arches and adjacent half-timbered houses reflect the water-based topography of central Nuremberg. The bridge is a short walk from the castle and Hauptmarkt along the river.

Sebalduskirche Nürnberg

Sebalduskirche is one of Nuremberg's two great parish churches, Romanesque in origin and enlarged in Gothic form near the castle. The shrine of Saint Sebald and rich sculpture drew pilgrims. Its position maps the religious geography of the medieval imperial city.

08 AdmissionFree

Kongresshalle

The unfinished Congress Hall stands within Nuremberg's former Nazi Party Rally Grounds as a massive brick and stone shell. Nazi planners began it in 1935 for propaganda gatherings modeled loosely on Roman forms, but construction stopped during the war. Documentation, exhibitions, courtyards and surrounding terrain confront dictatorship, forced labor, spectacle.

Zeppelinfeld

Part of the Nazi Party Rally Grounds, the field held annual party rallies in the 1930s. Albert Speer's monolithic grandstand still faces the open ground. The concrete mass documents that propaganda architecture without soft framing, and interpretive visits treat it as a historical warning site.

Nürnberg Zoo

Nuremberg Zoo occupies wooded slopes and former sandstone quarry terrain east of the city center. Animal habitats, rock formations, ponds, an aquatic complex, primate areas, playgrounds and conservation programs spread across a hilly site used by the zoo since 1939. Species and facilities change as enclosures are renovated and animal-care priorities develop.

St. Lorenz

St. Lorenz is Nuremberg's major Gothic parish church on the south side of the Pegnitz, heavily damaged in World War II and carefully rebuilt. The west facade's rose window and twin towers dominate Lorenzer Platz. Carved portals and surviving artworks repay time inside the nave.

Nuremberg State Theater

Staatstheater Nürnberg uses several venues for opera, ballet, drama, concerts and musical theater. The main opera house opened in 1905 to designs by Heinrich Seeling, not Gottfried Semper and combines late historicist architecture with later rebuilding and technical upgrades. Performance schedules determine access.

Ehekarussell

Jurgen Weber's Ehekarussell fountain circles a ventilation shaft near Nuremberg's White Tower with six dramatic scenes of married life. Installed in the 1980s, its bronze figures draw on a satirical poem by Hans Sachs and move from attraction and family to conflict, aging and death. The deliberately grotesque imagery has made the work both a public landmark and a continuing subject of debate about its treatment of marriage.

Sinwellturm

Sinwell Tower forms the round keep of Nuremberg's Imperial Castle, where its thick medieval masonry provided surveillance and a final defensive refuge. The thirteenth-century structure rises above the palace roofs and old town. When climbing access is available, interior stairs pass construction displays before reaching an upper level with broad views across the castle complex and city.

49.4578° N / 11.0765° E

Stadtpark

Flower beds and alcoves bloom through the seasons across this large municipal green. Paths and lawns suit picnics and quiet walks away from the old-town walls. Locals treat it as everyday parkland rather than a formal court garden, with shade for summer afternoons.

Memorium Nuremberg Trials

Courtroom 600 and an adjoining exhibition document the International Military Tribunal and subsequent Nuremberg trials after the Second World War; court use can restrict entry to the room itself. Films, documents, photographs, biographies, legal questions, and the original judicial setting examine prosecution of Nazi leaders and the development of international criminal law.
