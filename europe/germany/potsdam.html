Destination Guide — Eastern Germany

Explore Potsdam

A Brief History

Potsdam served as a residence of the Prussian kings, with Sanssouci Palace built for Frederick the Great in the 1740s amid terraced vineyards. Dutch Quarter and Russian Colony quarters reflect 18th- and 19th-century immigration. The city hosted the 1945 Potsdam Conference after Allied victory in Europe. Palaces and parks along the Havel River were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1990.

Brandenburger Tor in Potsdam

The Brandenburg Gate in Potsdam was built in 1770 as a triumphal arch marking the road to Brandenburg an der Havel. Designed in the Roman style, it predates the Berlin gate of the same name by several decades. It originally served as a customs and city gate at the western entrance to the old town.

Free

Friedenskirche

The Friedenskirche, or Church of Peace, stands in the palace park of Sanssouci and was inspired by early Christian basilicas in Italy. Built in the 1840s, it holds the tombs of several Hohenzollern rulers, including Frederick William IV. The marble interior and mosaic apse reflect 19th-century Prussian royal patronage.

Free

Schlosspark Sanssouci

Sanssouci Park covers nearly 300 hectares of terraced vineyards, temples, fountains, and tree-lined avenues laid out for Frederick the Great in the 18th century. Paths connect the main palace to the New Palace, the Orangery, and the Chinese House. The park is a major example of Prussian landscape design.

€19

Schloss Sanssouci

Schloss Sanssouci was Frederick the Great's summer residence, completed in 1747 in Rococo style on a terraced vineyard slope. The single-story palace was designed as a private retreat rather than a ceremonial seat of power. Rooms include the king's study, concert hall, and intimate guest apartments.

€4

Historic Mill of Sanssouci

The Historic Mill of Sanssouci is a smock mill rebuilt near the palace that became the subject of a legend about a miller who resisted the king's order to remove it. The current structure is a reconstruction; the original was destroyed in the 19th century. It stands in deliberate contrast to the royal architecture nearby.

Free

Sizilianischer Garten

The Sizilianischer Garten, or Sicilian Garden, is a section of Sanssouci Park planted with Mediterranean species to evoke the flora of Sicily. Created in the 19th century, it reflects the botanical interests of the Prussian court. Palm houses and exotic plantings sit within a formal layout of paths and beds.

€14

Neues Palais

The Neues Palais, or New Palace, was built at the far end of Sanssouci Park after the Seven Years' War to demonstrate Prussian recovery and wealth. Completed in 1769, its Baroque facades and over 200 rooms were used to host visiting dignitaries. Frederick the Great preferred the smaller Sanssouci for daily living.
