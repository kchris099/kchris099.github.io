Explore Rhine Valley

A Brief History

The Upper Middle Rhine Valley is a UNESCO World Heritage stretch of the Rhine between Bingen and Koblenz. Its gorge, terraced vineyards, and medieval castles developed around river trade, tolls, and viticulture from the Roman period onward. Romantic writers and artists later drew wider attention to the Lorelei rock and the valley's fortified heights.

Marksburg Castle

The Marksburg rises above Braubach on a Rhine bend and is the only Middle Rhine hill castle never destroyed. Built from the 12th century, it served as a fortress rather than a princely residence. Guided tours cover courtyards, armour, and chapel spaces within the UNESCO valley.

€6

Rheinfels Castle

Burg Rheinfels above Sankt Goar was begun around 1245 and became one of the largest Rhine fortresses between Koblenz and Mainz. French troops damaged it heavily in 1797. Visitors can explore ruins, terraces, and underground mine galleries; a torch helps in darker passages.

Free

Rüdesheim am Rhein

Rüdesheim stands at the southern entrance to the Middle Rhine Valley in the Rheingau wine region. Timber-framed houses, church towers, and vineyard slopes frame the waterfront. The Drosselgasse lane is open to walk through; trains, KD boats, and the Niederwald cable car connect from here.

Free

Lorelei Rock

The Lorelei is a 132-metre slate cliff above Sankt Goarshausen where the Rhine narrows and currents run strong. Shipping hazards here were recorded from medieval times. Brentano and Heine later linked the rock to siren legend; today paths and viewing platforms on the plateau are free to enter.

€20

Rhine River Cruise

KD liner boats run the Middle Rhine gorge between April and October, linking more than thirty landing stages from Cologne to Mainz. The Rüdesheim–St. Goar section passes castles, vineyards, and the Lorelei narrows. Tickets are sold at Rheinstraße landing stages and online; arrive about fifteen minutes early.

Free (cable car €12)

Niederwald Monument

The Niederwalddenkmal above Rüdesheim was built from 1877 to 1883 to mark German unification after the Franco-Prussian War. A 38-metre ensemble crowned by the figure Germania overlooks the Rhine Gorge toward Bingen. The monument grounds are free; most visitors use the Rüdesheim cable car.
