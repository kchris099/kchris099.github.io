Rhine Valley

The Upper Middle Rhine Valley is a UNESCO World Heritage stretch of the Rhine between Bingen and Koblenz. Its gorge, terraced vineyards, and medieval castles developed around river trade, tolls, and viticulture from the Roman period onward. Romantic writers and artists later drew wider attention to the Lorelei rock and the valley's fortified heights.

Marksburg Castle

Marksburg stands above Braubach as a major Middle Rhine hill castle to survive without destruction. Construction from the twelfth century onward produced walls, gates, halls, chapel, kitchen, armory and defensive works adapted by successive owners. Guided tours interpret daily life and fortification within the historic valley, while steep access and uneven surfaces reflect the steep hilltop setting.

Rheinfels Castle

Rheinfels Castle began above Sankt Goar in the thirteenth century and expanded into a vast fortress controlling Rhine traffic. French revolutionary forces captured it, and later demolition left extensive walls, cellars, tunnels, terraces; some underground passages are dark and confined, while modern hotel use occupies part of the broader ruined complex.

Rüdesheim am Rhein

Rüdesheim stands at the southern entrance to the Middle Rhine Valley in the Rheingau wine region. Timber-framed houses, church towers, and vineyard slopes frame the waterfront. The Drosselgasse lane is open to walk through; trains, KD boats, and the Niederwald cable car connect from here.

Lorelei Rock

The Lorelei is a 132-metre slate cliff above Sankt Goarshausen where the Rhine narrows and currents run strong. Shipping hazards here were recorded from medieval times. Brentano and Heine later linked the rock to siren legend; today paths and viewing platforms on the plateau are free to enter.

50.1394° N / 7.7289° E

Rhine River Cruise

Seasonal passenger boats follow the Rhine through the gorge between Rudesheim and Koblenz, stopping at towns such as St. Goar. Open decks reveal vineyards and castles above the narrow bends. Railway lines and riverside villages remain close to the water, especially around the Loreley passage. Route lengths differ, but each trip traces the Middle Rhine's historic transport corridor.

Niederwald Monument

The Niederwalddenkmal above Rüdesheim was built from 1877 to 1883 to mark German unification after the Franco-Prussian War. A 38-metre ensemble crowned by the figure Germania overlooks the Rhine Gorge toward Bingen. The monument grounds are free; most visitors use the Rüdesheim cable car.
