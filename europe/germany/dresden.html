Dresden

Dresden was the capital of Saxony under the Wettin dynasty, with August the Strong patronizing baroque architecture such as the Zwinger and Frauenkirche in the 18th century. The city was heavily bombed in February 1945, destroying much of the historic center. East German reconstruction and post-1990 rebuilding restored the church and royal buildings. Museums on the Elbe hold collections of porcelain, painting, and scientific instruments.

Brühl's Terrace

Brühl's Terrace is a raised promenade above the Elbe, built on the remains of the city's 16th-century fortifications. Nicknamed the 'Balcony of Europe,' it offers views across the river toward the Neustadt. Statues, balustrades, and the Albertinum museum line the walkway.

Frauenkirche Dresden

The Frauenkirche is a Baroque Lutheran church in the Neumarkt, destroyed in the 1945 bombing and rebuilt between 1994 and 2005. Original stones were incorporated into the new structure, and the golden cross atop the dome was a gift from Britain. The interior holds one of Europe's largest stone domes.

Fürstenzug

The Fürstenzug is a 102-meter porcelain mural on the outer wall of the Stallhof, depicting 35 rulers of the House of Wettin from 1127 to 1904. Created from over 23,000 Meissen tiles between 1904 and 1907, it is one of the largest porcelain artworks in the world.

Dresden Suspension Railway

The Dresden Suspension Railway is a unique and historic monorail system that gives visitors a one-of-a-kind experience. As one of the oldest suspension railways in the world, it ascends to an observation deck where travelers can enjoy panoramic views of the Dresden Elbe Valley. The 280-meter-long railway climbs over 84 meters to Oberloschwitz, providing a quick but scenic four-minute trip. For those who prefer hiking, there's also the option to make the ascent on foot.
