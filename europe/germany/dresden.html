Dresden

Dresden was the capital of Saxony under the Wettin dynasty, with August the Strong patronizing baroque architecture such as the Zwinger and Frauenkirche in the 18th century. The city was heavily bombed in February 1945, destroying much of the historic center. East German reconstruction and post-1990 rebuilding restored the church and royal buildings. Museums on the Elbe hold collections of porcelain, painting, and scientific instruments.

Brühl's Terrace

Brühl's Terrace is a raised promenade above the Elbe, built on the remains of the city's 16th-century fortifications. Nicknamed the 'Balcony of Europe,' it offers views across the river toward the Neustadt. Statues, balustrades, and the Albertinum museum line the walkway.

Frauenkirche Dresden

Dresden rebuilt the Frauenkirche from its wartime ruins between 1994 and 2005, returning the Baroque church to Neumarkt after decades as a memorial mound. Dark original stones remain visible among lighter new masonry beneath George Bähr's great dome. The interior, altar, galleries, organ, and viewing route interpret both eighteenth-century design and the international reconciliation effort behind reconstruction.

Martin Luther Statue

The Martin Luther statue in Neumarkt was erected in 1885 to commemorate the Reformation. The bronze figure survived the wartime destruction of the square and was restored after reunification. It stands near the Frauenkirche, linking Dresden to the wider Protestant history of Saxony.

Stallhof Dresden

The Stallhof is a Renaissance courtyard within the Residenzschloss, once used for tournaments and court festivities in the 16th century. Its arcaded galleries and sgraffito decoration illustrate Saxon equestrian culture. Today it hosts events including the annual Medieval Christmas Market.

Fürstenzug

More than one hundred metres of Meissen porcelain tiles carry the Fürstenzug along Dresden's Stallhof wall, and its durable ceramic surface survived the 1945 bombing better than many surrounding buildings, although individual tiles have required repair. The early twentieth-century mural shows mounted rulers of the House of Wettin in chronological procession, accompanied by soldiers, scholars, artists, and craftsmen.

Cathedral St. Trinitatis

The Cathedral of the Holy Trinity, also called the Hofkirche, was built in the 18th century as Dresden's Catholic court church under Augustus III. The crypt holds the remains of many Wettin rulers. Silbermann organs and a Rococo interior make it a notable stop on the historic city walk.

Theaterplatz

Theaterplatz is Dresden's main civic square, bordered by the Semperoper, the Zwinger, and the Residenzschloss. Equestrian statues and open space give it a formal character. Most of the surrounding buildings were rebuilt after wartime damage while preserving their original facades.

Dresdener Zwinger

Augustus the Strong developed Dresden's Zwinger as an orangery, festival court and architectural setting for court ceremony in the early eighteenth century. Matthäus Daniel Pöppelmann and sculptor Balthasar Permoser shaped pavilions, galleries, fountains and the Crown Gate around a broad courtyard; museums now occupy the complex, while restoration can alter access to the Old Masters, porcelain and scientific-instrument collections.

Kronentor

The Kronentor, or Crown Gate, is the southeastern entrance to the Zwinger, topped by a replica of the Polish royal crown. It symbolizes Augustus the Strong's election as King of Poland in 1697. Sculptural groups on the gate represent the four seasons and four elements.

Augustusbrücke

The Augustusbrücke spans the Elbe between the Altstadt and Neustadt. A bridge has stood at this site since the 12th century; the current stone structure largely dates from the 19th century. From the bridge, walkers can see the skyline of spires, domes, and the riverfront terraces.

Goldener Reiter

A gilded Augustus the Strong rides eastward across Dresden's Neustädter Markt in the Goldener Reiter monument. Installed in 1736, the bronze ruler appears in Roman armor on a rearing horse, oriented toward the Polish-Lithuanian lands he also governed as king. The statue marks the approach from the Augustus Bridge and survived through relocation, wartime damage, and later restoration.

The Grand Garden Palace

At the center of Dresden's Grosser Garten, the Palais im Grossen Garten rises as an early Baroque pleasure palace built for court festivities; symmetrical facades, corner pavilions, a ceremonial hall and surrounding avenues establish the formal axis of the park. Wartime destruction left the interior largely unfinished, so exhibitions, concerts and restoration projects use spaces that remain more architectural shell than furnished residence.

The Grand Garden of Dresden

Long avenues cross the Grosser Garten, Dresden's principal historic park southeast of the old center. Court planning in the seventeenth century created formal axes around the central palace, later softened by lawns, groves, ponds, sculpture and recreational paths. The zoo, botanical garden, miniature railway, sports areas and seasonal events now share the grounds with surviving Baroque structure.

Zoo Dresden

Dresden Zoo occupies the southern edge of the Grosser Garten and has operated since 1861. Animal areas, historic structures, modern houses, play zones, and conservation displays cover species from several continents, while the children's railway passes nearby but belongs to the wider park.

Kunsthofpassage in Dresden's Neustadt links courtyards refurbished around 2001 with artist studios, cafés, and small shops. Dresdner Molkerei Gebrüder Pfund GmbH

The Pfund brothers opened this dairy shop in 1879; hand-painted tiles still cover the interior. Destroyed in World War II, the store was rebuilt to match the ornate 19th-century decor. Milk and souvenirs sell under the reconstructed tile work that made the shop famous.

Loschwitz Bridge

Known as the Blue Wonder, the historic steel span crosses the Elbe between Blasewitz and Loschwitz. Pedestrians, cyclists, and cars share views of the river and villa slopes. Decks and approach streets give the usual vantage points on either bank for photos and short pauses.

Dresden Suspension Railway

Dresden's Schwebebahn climbs the steep Loschwitz hillside on suspended cars running beneath an elevated rail. Opened in 1901, the short line links the lower district with Oberloschwitz and an upper station that includes a viewing platform. Its engineering differs from an ordinary monorail, while maintenance, weather, and operating hours determine whether the railway and observation deck are available.

Albrechtsberg Palace

Prince Albert of Prussia commissioned Albrechtsberg Palace above the Elbe in the mid-nineteenth century. Adolf Lohse designed an Italian Renaissance-inspired residence with terraces, formal rooms, service wings and landscaped grounds forming part of Dresden's group of Elbe castles. The building now hosts events and limited tours, so interior access is less predictable than views of the exterior, park and river slope.

Residenzschloss

Dresden's Residenzschloss, the former royal palace, has roots more than six centuries deep and was rebuilt after severe World War II damage. It now holds the Historic Green Vault, New Green Vault, and other State Art Collections. Courtyards and museum wings restore the castle's role at the city's core.

Hausmannsturm

Hausmannsturm is the tall watchtower of Dresden's Residenzschloss, long used for fire lookout and civic signalling above the castle roofs. After wartime ruin and reconstruction it again rises over the Zwinger and Elbe skyline. Climbing routes and viewpoints reopen when restoration schedules allow.

Schlossplatz

An 18th-century cobbled square looks toward Dresden Castle, the cathedral, Augustus Bridge, and the Elbe promenade. Heavy wartime damage was followed by careful restoration of the paving and surrounding facades. The open stone field sits at the civic core of the rebuilt old town.

Semperoper Dresden

Situated in Dresden Old Town, the Semperoper is a known opera house known for its opulent interior and acoustics. Originally built in 1841 by architect Gottfried Semper, it has a rich history including destruction by fire in 1869 and bombing in 1945. Despite these setbacks, the opera house was rebuilt and continues to host performances. The 1,300-seat auditorium offers an intimate setting with excellent views from every seat.

Holy Cross Church

Kreuzkirche has been rebuilt repeatedly after fires, warfare and the destruction of Dresden in 1945. The present late Baroque church contains a broad preaching hall, restrained postwar interior, organ and traditions associated with the Kreuzchor boys' choir. A tower route may open for city views, while worship, concerts and choir performances remain central to the building's purpose.

Altmarkt

Altmarkt has been Dresden's main market square since the Middle Ages, documented as a trading ground by 1434. Post-war rebuilding frames the plaza with shops, hotels, and civic fronts. In winter a large Christmas market fills the open pavement with stalls and seasonal lights.

Filmnächte am Elbufer

Each summer, Filmnachte am Elbufer installs a temporary screen and concert stage on the north bank of the Elbe opposite Dresden's old town. Film screenings, live music and special broadcasts use the cathedral, castle and dome as a backdrop after dark. Dates, performers, seating arrangements and weather policies change with each seasonal program.

Pillnitz Castle

A riverside palace restored after fire holds a botanical palm house and a decorative arts museum within the Staatliche Kunstsammlungen Dresden. Collections gathered by Saxon princes and Polish kings fill the galleries. Gardens and riverside terraces frame the rebuilt pavilions.
