Essen

Essen grew around Essen Abbey, founded in the 9th century, whose abbesses ruled a wealthy territory on the Ruhr River for centuries. Coal mining from the 19th century turned the city into a core of the Ruhr industrial belt, culminating in the Zollverein colliery complex. Post-war decline and 1980s pit closures prompted conversion of mines and steelworks into museums, parks, and the Zollverein UNESCO World Heritage Site that now anchors Ruhr cultural tourism.

Zollverein UNESCO World Heritage Site

Zollverein preserves the linked remains of a major Essen coal mine and coking plant within a historic industrial landscape; museums, design institutions, walks, events and guided technical routes now occupy the complex without erasing its labor and environmental history. Shaft XII's geometric red-steel architecture, coal-washing structures, conveyors, rail lines and vast production spaces show how the Ruhr organized extraction at scale.

Shaft XII

Shaft XII at Zollverein, completed in 1932 to designs by Fritz Schupp and Martin Kremmer, became the emblem of the Ruhr coal industry with its central headframe and symmetrical Bauhaus layout. The preserved above-ground plant illustrates how coal was hoisted, sorted, and dispatched. Guided tours along the Denkmalpfad explain extraction technology and the daily work of miners before the pit closed in 1986.

Kokerei

Rows of ovens, quenching structures and conveyors reveal the industrial process at Zollverein's coking plant. Built from the late 1950s, the facility converted coal into coke for steel production until 1993. Chimneys and service buildings trace the movement of material. Preserved machinery supports guided interpretation of hazardous work across the large site.

Limbecker Platz

Retail floors make Limbecker Platz a large indoor shopping center on the western edge of Essen's central district. Multiple retail levels contain chain stores, restaurants, services, and parking beneath a contemporary curved facade completed through redevelopment in the late 2000s.

05 AdmissionFree

Colosseum Theater

The Colosseum Theater occupies a former factory hall built in 1901 for the Krupp steelworks in Essen's inner city. After industrial use ended, the brick and steel structure was converted into a musical and concert venue while retaining its industrial character. The auditorium hosts touring musicals and live performances, and the exterior remains a visible example of Ruhr conversion architecture.

06 AdmissionFree (treasury €6)

Essen Minster

Essen Minster grew from the church of a powerful women's religious foundation whose abbesses governed extensive lands. The present complex combines an Ottonian westwork, later Gothic hall church, crypt, cloister remains and postwar reconstruction. The treasury preserves the Golden Madonna, crosses, manuscripts and liturgical objects that reveal female patronage and imperial connections beyond the building's current role as a Catholic cathedral.

07 AdmissionFree

Philharmonic Essen

The Philharmonie Essen, opened in 2004 beside the Aalto Theatre, is the home of the Essen Philharmonic Orchestra. Architect Alvaro Siza designed the white cubic concert hall with wraparound seating and a central organ. The building anchors the city's Kulturviertel cultural quarter and hosts classical concerts, recitals, and guest ensembles throughout the season.

08 AdmissionFree

Ruine Neue Isenburg

The ruins of Neue Isenburg stand on a rocky spur above the Baldeneysee reservoir in Essen's Stadtwald forest. The castle was built around 1240 and served regional lords until it fell into disrepair after the Thirty Years' War. Restored foundations, information panels, and a woodland trail explain the fortification's medieval role and offer views across the lake and surrounding woodland.

Schloß Borbeck

Prince-abbesses of Essen used Schloss Borbeck as a residence and administrative center northwest of the city. Medieval origins lie beneath a Baroque rebuilding completed in the eighteenth century, while a landscaped park, moat traces, and later additions surround the pale stone house. Cultural programs and local exhibitions occupy parts of the interior, with opening schedules determining whether the palace can be seen beyond its grounds.

51.4684° N / 6.9422° E

10 AdmissionFree

Historische Altstadt

The historic old town of Kettwig lines the Ruhr River in southern Essen, with half-timbered houses, a riverside promenade, and a preserved crane on the waterfront. Kettwig held town rights from 1372 and remained a separate municipality until incorporation into Essen in 1975. Cobbled lanes, the Altstadt bridge, and former textile mills record the settlement's trading and craft history.
