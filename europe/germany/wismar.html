Wismar

Wismar joined the Hanse in the thirteenth century, shipping beer, salt, and herring from brick warehouses. Swedish rule from 1648 to 1903 added fortifications and the Fürstenhof. UNESCO listing later recognised the surviving Hanseatic street grid on the Mecklenburg coast after wartime damage and GDR-era neglect. The Wasserpforte and old harbour cranes recall bulk goods moving between warehouse and ship.

Historic Market Place (Markt)

Wismar's Markt is a broad Hanseatic market square edged by gabled brick houses and the Wasserkunst fountain. Market stalls still sell food and goods on regular trading days. The open paving links harbour streets to the remaining church towers and the reconstructed St. George's silhouette of the old town.

St. Mary's Church (Marienkirche)

Most of Wismar's Marienkirche was lost to wartime bombing and later demolition, leaving the great steeple of 1339 as the principal remnant. The roughly eighty-metre tower can be climbed for views over the brick-built harbour town. Foundations and adjacent open ground mark where the nave once stood.

53.8864° N / 11.4651° E

03 AdmissionFree

St. Nicholas Church (Nikolaikirche)

St. Nicholas Church rises near Wismar's harbor with one of the tallest surviving Brick Gothic naves in the Baltic region; wartime survival distinguishes it from the city's more heavily damaged churches, although restoration and active worship continue to shape the interior. Merchants and seafarers supported its medieval construction, while chapels, vaults, artworks, ship models, and later repairs reflect a maritime parish.

53.8939° N / 11.4634° E

04 AdmissionFree

St. George's Church (Georgenkirche)

Wismar's St. George's Church was rebuilt over decades after severe wartime damage left much of the vast Brick Gothic structure in ruins, and a viewing platform may offer city perspectives, while visible reconstruction makes twentieth-century preservation part of the building's story. The restored nave, tower, chapels, and open interior now support exhibitions, concerts, and civic events alongside historical interpretation.

53.8917° N / 11.4693° E

05 AdmissionFree

Town Hall (Rathaus)

Wismar's Town Hall is a large white and red building that is a symbol of the town's rich history and governance. Built in the late Gothic style from 1817-1819, it's home to the Rathaus Historical Exhibition in its basement that narrate Wismar's evolution from a busy trade center to a modern town. Exhibits include a mural from the 15th century and a tomb.

Wismar City Museum

The Schabbell houses Wismar's city-history museum in adjoining Renaissance merchant residences near the old harbor. Archaeology, maritime trade, Hanseatic connections, household objects, art, models and urban displays explain how residents lived and worked across centuries. The buildings themselves, with courtyards and restored interiors.
