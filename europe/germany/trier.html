Explore Trier

A Brief History

Trier, founded as Augusta Treverorum under Augustus around 16 BC, is often cited as Germany's oldest city. It grew into a major Roman provincial capital with gates, baths, an amphitheatre, and imperial residence buildings. Later an archbishopric and electoral court shaped the medieval and Baroque cityscape. UNESCO-listed Roman monuments alongside the cathedral, Liebfrauenkirche, and the electoral palace district trace this long civic and religious history along the Moselle.

Porta Nigra

The Porta Nigra is a Roman city gate from about AD 170, built from large sandstone blocks darkened with age. It guarded the northern entrance to Augusta Treverorum and is part of Trier's UNESCO World Heritage ensemble. Visitors can walk upper levels on paid admission; the exterior can be viewed from the street at any time.

Free

Hauptmarkt

Hauptmarkt is Trier's central market square east of the cathedral quarter. Timber-framed and Baroque townhouses, the market cross, and the Steipe guild hall frame a space used for markets and civic events since the Middle Ages. The square links pedestrian routes to St Gangolf, the Dom, and shopping streets.

Free

Trier Cathedral

Trier Cathedral (Dom St Peter) incorporates Roman and late antique masonry within a cathedral rebuilt and extended from the 11th century onward. It houses the Holy Tunic relic and forms a UNESCO site with the adjacent Liebfrauenkirche. The interior mixes Romanesque, Gothic, and Baroque work from over a thousand years of liturgical use.

Free

Liebfrauenkirche

Liebfrauenkirche, begun around 1230, is an early French Gothic church in Germany attached to the cathedral by a cloister. Its central plan with twelve pillars around one nave column gave the building the name Church of Our Lady. UNESCO lists it with Trier's Roman monuments as part of one World Heritage ensemble.

Free

Konstantin-Basilika

Konstantin-Basilika, the Aula Palatina, was erected about AD 310 as a throne hall in Constantine's palace district. The hall spans roughly 67 by 27 metres without interior columns, among the largest surviving Roman audience buildings north of the Alps. Today it serves the Protestant parish; entry is free outside concert times.

€10

Rheinisches Landesmuseum Trier

Rheinisches Landesmuseum Trier on Weimarer Allee holds Roman mosaics, sculpture, and Trier finds from prehistory to the Middle Ages. Highlights include the Neumagen wine ship and cathedral excavation material. Permanent and temporary exhibitions document Augusta Treverorum and the Rhineland; audioguides are included in standard admission.

Free

Palastgarten

Palastgarten lies beside the Electoral Palace and Konstantin-Basilika in Trier's former residence quarter. Laid out as a public park in the 1930s on ground once used as a parade square, it has lawns, a pond, sports courts, and rococo sculptures. Paths connect the palace, Kaiserthermen, and museum district on foot.

€6

Kaiserthermen

Kaiserthermen are extensive Roman imperial baths begun around AD 293 during Trier's palace expansion. Vaulted substructures and a hot-water basin survive though construction halted when the court moved east. The ruins adjoin the Palastgarten and are managed by the Centre of Antiquity; paid entry covers exploration of the underground halls.

€6

Trier Amphitheater

Trier Amphitheater, from about the 2nd century AD, seated roughly 18,000 spectators for games and assemblies. Later medieval fortifications incorporated its walls. Subterranean passages and the arena floor can be visited on admission; the bowl sits south of the centre on the edge of the Barbarossa quarter.

Free

Benediktinerabtei St. Matthias

Benediktinerabtei St. Matthias is a Benedictine monastery with a Romanesque basilica consecrated in 1148. The crypt holds relics of the Apostle Matthias, one of few apostolic graves north of the Alps, drawing pilgrims since the Middle Ages. The active community opens the church daily for prayer and visitors until evening.

Free

Mariensäule

Mariensäule crowns the Pulsberg above the Moselle west of central Trier. The neo-Gothic column, completed in 1866, commemorates the 1854 Immaculate Conception dogma and rises about 40 metres with a Marian statue. Forest paths lead to the base; views extend across the city and river valley though the viewing gallery is usually closed.
