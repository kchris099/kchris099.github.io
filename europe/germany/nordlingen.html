Explore Nördlingen

A Brief History

Nördlingen lies inside a meteorite crater formed about 15 million years ago, its round wall still visible in the landscape. The town received imperial privileges and fortified its center with walls walkers can trace today. St. George's Church carries a late Gothic hall and a tower climb. Nördlingen remained an independent imperial city until 1802. Its preserved street plan records centuries of regional trade in grain and cloth.

Nördlingen Old Town

Nördlingen's old town sits inside a circular layout formed by a meteorite impact about 15 million years ago. Narrow streets radiate from the central church toward the ring-shaped wall. Patrician houses with red roofs and timber frames line the main thoroughfares, preserving a medieval street plan.

Bavarian Railway Museum

The Bavarian Railway Museum next to the station displays more than a hundred historic locomotives and carriages in open-air sheds and indoor halls. Steam engines, dining cars, and royal saloons illustrate the development of rail travel in Bavaria from the 19th century to the postwar period.

Free

City Walls and Gates

Nördlingen is one of the few German towns whose entire medieval wall can still be walked without interruption, a circuit of about 2.6 kilometers. Covered wooden galleries and guard towers punctuate the route. The elevated walk gives a continuous view of rooftops and the surrounding Ries crater landscape.

€12

Stadtmuseum Nördlingen

The Stadtmuseum occupies a former hospital building near the city wall. Collections cover Nördlingen's history as a Free Imperial City, its role in the Thirty Years' War, and the geology of the Ries crater. Models and artifacts explain how the town's economy shifted from trade to agriculture over the centuries.

Free

St. George's Church and the 'Daniel

St. George's Church dominates the town center with a 90-meter tower known locally as the Daniel. Climbing about 350 steps to the viewing platform reveals the full circular shape of the old town and the flat crater rim beyond. The late-Gothic nave is one of the largest hall churches in southern Germany.

€12

Ries Crater Museum

The Ries Crater Museum explains the meteorite impact that created the bowl-shaped landscape around Nördlingen. Displays include impact rocks, fossils, and a moon rock sample donated by NASA, which used the crater for Apollo astronaut training in the 1970s because its terrain resembled lunar surfaces.
