Explore Weimar

A Brief History

Weimar is the undisputed intellectual, artistic, and cultural soul of Germany. During the late 18th and early 19th centuries, it became the vibrant epicenter of the German Enlightenment (Weimar Classicism), hosting unparalleled literary and philosophical giants like Johann Wolfgang von Goethe and Friedrich Schiller. In the 20th century, it birthed the revolutionary, highly influential Bauhaus design movement and the democratic Weimar Republic. Today, this quiet, leafy city stands as a massive, breathtaking monument to European intellectual history, boasting multiple UNESCO World Heritage sites.

Goethe’s Gartenhaus

Goethe’s Gartenhaus is not just a quaint garden retreat; it’s an emblem of Goethe’s deep bond with nature which profoundly influenced his writing. After moving to Weimar in 1775, Goethe was also gifted this garden and house by Duke Carl August and it quickly became a haven for his literary creativity. This simple cottage amidst verdant surroundings offered him solitude and simplicity, contrasting with the complexities of courtly life.

Fürstengruft

Resting in the peaceful backdrop of the Historical Cemetery, the Fürstengruft, or the Ducal Vault, holds a special significance within Weimar’s cultural panorama. A mausoleum for Weimar’s dukes, the Fürstengruft also astonishingly came to house the remains of the iconic Goethe and what was thought to be Schiller for a time. This quiet sepulcher, Built in the neoclassical style, allows you to reflect on mortality and the enduring legacies individuals can leave behind.

€12

Goethe National Museum (Goethe-Nationalmuseum)

A pilgrimage to the Goethe National Museum is indispensable when visiting Weimar, as it encapsulates the legacy of Germany’s great literary master, Johann Wolfgang von Goethe. Located in Goethe’s former residence, where he lived for almost 50 years, the museum is more than just a window into his life; it’s a complete immersion into his world. This UNESCO-listed Baroque house, given to Goethe in 1782 to keep him in Weimar, was transformed according to his ideas.

Free

Park an der Ilm

UNESCO-listed Park an der Ilm is the green heart of Weimar, sprawling across 48 hectares along the river Ilm. Historically significant and a masterpiece of landscape design, it was transformed into an English-style park in the 1770s under the guidance of Duke Carl August, with Goethe participating in its design. Here, you can connect with nature, meander through its many pathways, discover hidden groves, and stumble upon classical temples and sculptures reminiscent of past eras.

Liszt House

Franz Liszt, the celebrated 19th-century composer, chose Weimar as his home twice, contributing to its reputation as a cultural hub. His residence for his second stint from 1869 to 1886, the Liszt House, is fundamental to understanding Weimar’s musical heritage and is thus a must-visit for music aficionados. Also located at Park an der Ilm, it is easy to visit here.
