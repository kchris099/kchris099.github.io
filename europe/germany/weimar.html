Weimar

Weimar served as residence of the dukes of Saxe-Weimar-Eisenach, who employed Goethe and Schiller and funded the Bauhaus after 1919 in a small Thuringian town. The National Assembly adopted the first German republic's constitution here in 1919, giving the state its name before Nazi concentration camp Buchenwald opened nearby. Palaces, theaters, and modernist housing estates record how court patronage, classical literature, and twentieth-century political rupture layered Weimar's identity.

Goethe's Gartenhaus

Goethe's Gartenhaus is a visitor site in Weimar, Germany. Goethe’s Gartenhaus is not just a quaint garden retreat; it’s an emblem of Goethe’s deep bond with nature which influenced his writing. After moving to Weimar in 1775, Goethe was also gifted this garden and house by Duke Carl August and it quickly became a haven for his literary creativity. The main structures and grounds are the principal points of interest.

Fürstengruft

Fürstengruft is a visitor site in Weimar, Germany. Resting in the peaceful backdrop of the Historical Cemetery, the Fürstengruft, or the Ducal Vault, holds a special significance within Weimar’s cultural panorama. A mausoleum for Weimar’s dukes, the Fürstengruft also astonishingly came to house the remains of the Goethe and what was thought to be Schiller for a time. The main structures and grounds are the principal points of interest.

Goethe National Museum (Goethe-Nationalmuseum)

A pilgrimage to the Goethe National Museum is indispensable when visiting Weimar, as it encapsulates the legacy of Germany’s great literary master, Johann Wolfgang von Goethe. Located in Goethe’s former residence, where he lived for almost 50 years, the museum is more than just a window into his life; it’s a complete immersion into his world. This UNESCO-listed Baroque house, given to Goethe in 1782 to keep him in Weimar, was transformed according to his ideas.

Park an der Ilm

UNESCO-listed Park an der Ilm is the green Weimar, large across 48 hectares along the river Ilm. Historically significant and a masterpiece of landscape design, it was transformed into an English-style park in the 1770s under the guidance of Duke Carl August, with Goethe participating in its design. Here, can connect with nature, meander through its many pathways, discover hidden groves, and stumble upon classical temples and sculptures reminiscent of past eras.

Liszt House

Franz Liszt, the celebrated 19th-century composer, chose Weimar as his home twice, contributing to its reputation as a cultural hub. His residence for his second stint from 1869 to 1886, the Liszt House, is fundamental to understanding Weimar’s musical heritage and is thus a must-visit for music aficionados. Also located at Park an der Ilm, it is easy to visit here.
