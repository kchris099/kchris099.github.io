Karlsruhe

Karlsruhe was planned in 1715 as a residence for Margrave Charles III William of Baden-Durlach, with streets radiating from the palace tower. The city housed the Baden court and, from 1818, Germany's first technical university. Federal institutions including the Constitutional Court sit here today. Trams have operated since 1881. Allied bombing in 1944 damaged the center, though the fan-shaped layout was largely preserved in rebuilding.

Schloss Karlsruhe

Schloss Karlsruhe is a visitor site in Karlsruhe. Completed in 1718, this grand Baroque palace was built by Margrave Charles III William as his seat of government. It marks the center of a unique 'fan city' layout, with streets radiating outward to reflect political order, now housing a primary cultural institution dedicated to regional history.

Grossherzog-Karl-Friedrich-Denkmal

Grossherzog-Karl-Friedrich-Denkmal is a visitor site in Karlsruhe. Built in 1844, this bronze monument honors a defining ruler who expanded his territory and modernized the state in the late 18th century. The statue reflects the 19th-century practice of memorializing enlightened leadership, commemorating the political consolidation of the region.

Karlsruher Pyramide

Karlsruher Pyramide is a visitor site in Karlsruhe. Built in 1823, this red sandstone pyramid stands over the vault of the city's founder. Designed by Friedrich Weinbrenner, it brought an Egyptian revival style to the neoclassical square, serving as both an active tomb and a respected historical monument at the the city.
