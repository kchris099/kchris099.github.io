Ulm

First recorded in 854 as a royal domain, Ulm became a free imperial city in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries on linen and Danube river trade. World War II destroyed much of the centre; reconstruction kept the Gothic Münster, whose spire remains the world's tallest church tower. Fishermen's quarter lanes and postwar blocks now share the riverbank opposite Neu-Ulm.

Ulm Minster (Ulmer Münster)

Ulm Minster's west tower rises 161.5 metres above a vast Gothic church begun by the city's citizens in 1377; a long stair reaches the tower when open, but height, weather, restoration and physical effort affect access. Construction paused for centuries before nineteenth-century completion, leaving medieval sculpture, choir stalls, stained glass, chapels and later engineering within one fabric.

Ulm City Hall (Rathaus) & Marktplatz

Painted facades and an astronomical clock distinguish Ulm Town Hall on Marktplatz. Medieval commercial buildings were adapted for civic government, then received Renaissance-era decoration and later reconstruction after wartime damage. The exterior scenes, clock, arcades, council functions, and surrounding market connect municipal authority with everyday urban life, while interior access remains limited by administration.

Fishermen’s Quarter (Fischerviertel)

The Fishermen's Quarter lies along the Blau river with cobbled lanes, canals, and half-timbered houses. The Schiefes Haus, or Crooked House, tilts noticeably due to its timber frame settling over centuries. Small bridges and outdoor cafés give the area a quieter character than the busy Marktplatz.

04 AdmissionFree

Danube River Promenade & City Walls

The Danube promenade follows the river through Ulm with tree-lined paths and views of the minster spire. Remnants of the medieval city walls stand near Friedrichsau park at the eastern edge of the old town. The river marked the city's southern boundary and trade route toward the Black Sea.

48.3948° N / 9.9940° E

05 AdmissionFree

The Einstein Fountain

The Einstein Fountain on Zeughausgasse commemorates Albert Einstein, who was born in Ulm in 1879. The bronze sculpture by Jürgen Goertz shows a rocket emerging from a snail shell, referencing Einstein's theory of relativity in playful form. It was installed in 1984 near the site of his birth house.

The Ulm Museum

Ulm Museum brings archaeology, medieval and modern art, design, and city history together in buildings near Marktplatz. Its most important archaeological object is the Ice Age Lion Man, carved from mammoth ivory tens of thousands of years ago, alongside finds from the Danube region.
