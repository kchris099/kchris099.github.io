Nafplio

Venetian and Ottoman forts ring the Argolic harbour that became the first capital of independent Greece after 1821. Neoclassical mansions from that short capital period stand below Palamidi fortress. Nafplio's significance is national-founding and military, compressed into a few early-nineteenth-century decades. Kapodistrias governed from here before his assassination ended the first capital experiment.

Syntagma Square - Nafplio

Nafplio's Syntagma Square is framed by Venetian, Ottoman and neoclassical buildings that reflect the town's changing rulers and early role in the Greek state. The archaeological museum, former mosque, warehouses, cafes, residences and public events occupy the paved center. Morning and evening atmosphere varies, but the square's historical substance comes from architecture and civic use.

Nafplio Port

On the Argolic Gulf, quays and embankment paths support leisure and dining along a historic harbor front. Boats and waterfront tables reflect the town's maritime past as a former capital. The waterfront works as Nafplio's main evening promenade for residents and guests.

Mycenean Acropolis of Tiryns

The Mycenaean acropolis of Tiryns near Nafplio is a Bronze Age citadel famous for cyclopean walls built roughly between the seventeenth and twelfth centuries BC. Palace remains and galleries sit within the massive stone circuit. Homeric tradition and archaeology both mark the site as a major Argolid centre.

37.5995° N / 22.8003° E

Fortress of Acronauplía

Acronauplia spreads across the rocky peninsula above Nafplio, where fortifications were altered by ancient, Byzantine, Frankish, Venetian and Ottoman authorities. Walls, gates and bastions remain among later roads and hotel development. Ruined structures and former prison areas add further layers to the headland. The surviving defenses reveal repeated changes in control, while present access follows several separate routes.

37.5641° N / 22.7965° E

Lion of the Bavarians

A reclining lion carved into Nafplio's rock commemorates Bavarian soldiers who died during the early reign of King Otto, and the quiet roadside setting and inscription support remembrance. Christian Siegel created the monument in the 1840s after a model associated with neoclassical funerary sculpture, placing it near the soldiers' burial area.

Bourtzi Fortress

Bourtzi Fortress, situated on the islet of St. Theodoroi near Nafplio Harbor, was constructed by the Venetians in 1473 as a defense against invaders. Over time, it served various purposes including a prison, executioner's residence, hotel, and restaurant. Today, it is a popular tourist attraction with boat trips available during summer months and weekends from October to March.

37.5697° N / 22.7904° E

Church of Saint Spyridon

Agios Spyridon is a small eighteenth-century church in Nafplio's old town where Governor Ioannis Kapodistrias was assassinated in 1831. A mark near the entrance is associated with the fatal shot, while icons, a compact nave, and surrounding lanes preserve the immediate setting.

37.5653° N / 22.7981° E

Kolokotronis Park

Kolokotronis Park provides a shaded public garden near Nafplio's old railway station. An equestrian statue honors independence leader Theodoros Kolokotronis, while lawns, paths, trees, benches, and nearby rail displays support everyday recreation. A monument to Kolokotronis stands by a small lake and old train displays used for photos.

37.5659° N / 22.8022° E

09 AdmissionFree

Paralia Arvanitias

Arvanitia Beach lies directly below Nafplio's Acronauplia peninsula, close to the old town. A small pebble-and-rock shore, seasonal facilities and the coastal path occupy a sheltered cove beneath the fortifications. The beach is distinct from Tolo, a separate resort farther along the Argolid coast, and its compact setting connects swimming with views of Nafplio's walls.

37.5631° N / 22.7993° E

Five Brothers

The Five Brothers are a battery of five cannons on Nafplio's seaside fortifications, part of the harbour defences below the Palamidi and Akronafplia heights. The guns face the Argolic Gulf along the waterfront walk. They recall the town's early modern military role as a fortified port.
