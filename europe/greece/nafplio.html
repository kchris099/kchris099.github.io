Explore Nafplio

A Brief History

Serving as the first capital of modern Greece following independence in 1821, Nafplio bears the architectural scars of successive Venetian and Ottoman occupations. Its formidable coastal fortresses and neoclassical mansions reflect its brief but vital role in national formation. Museums, markets, and street plans in Nafplio still show layers from those periods.

Syntagma Square - Nafplio

Syntagma Square in Nafplio, Greece is the the city, surrounded by busy cafes, restaurants, and shops. It's to visit in the morning for a peaceful stroll before the crowds arrive or in the evening for a livelier atmosphere. The square offers a taste of local life and is perfect for enjoying a coffee or beverage after exploring the historical center.

Free

Nafplio Port

Nafplio Port is a waterfront area in Nafplio, Greece. The Port of Nafplio is a historic and harbor located on the Argolic Gulf. The port area is a central hub for leisure and dining, reflecting the town's maritime history and relaxed coastal atmosphere. Quays and embankment paths form the main walking route.

€10

Mycenean Acropolis of Tiryns

Mycenean Acropolis of Tiryns is a visitor site in Nafplio, Greece. Records associated with the site include 1750, 1050. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest. Mycenean Acropolis of Tiryns remains part of the public visitor circuit in Nafplio.

Free

Fortress of Acronauplía

The Fortress of Akronauplia, also known as "Its Kale," is a historic moated fortress located on a rocky peninsula in Nafplio, Greece. It has a rich history dating back to ancient times and has been influenced by various civilizations including the Romans, Venetians, Byzantines, Franks, and Ottomans. The fortress offers lift access and is the oldest fortification in Nafplio.

Free

Lion of the Bavarians

The Lion of the Bavarians, also known as Agouroon by locals, is a famous attraction located in Nafplio, Argolis. Carved into the rock with fine artistry, this monument was created in 1836 by German sculptor Siegel at the request of King Ludovik to honor Bavarian soldiers who succumbed to typhoid fever between 1833 and 1834.

€7

Bourtzi Fortress

Bourtzi Fortress, situated on the islet of St. Theodoroi near Nafplio Harbor, was constructed by the Venetians in 1473 as a defense against invaders. Over time, it served various purposes including a prison, executioner's residence, hotel, and restaurant. Today, it is a popular tourist attraction with boat trips available during summer months and weekends from October to March.

Free

Church of Saint Spyridon

The Church of Saint Spyridon, located in Nafplio Old Town, is a significant landmark with a rich historical background. Built in 1702 during the Second Venetian domination, it holds great importance in Greek history as it was the site of the assassination of Ioannis Kapodistrias, Greece's first governor. This small yet important church witnessed key events such as the establishment of Greece's first parliament and military school.

Free

Kolokotronis Park

Kolokotronis Park is a public park in Nafplio, Greece. The park houses the historical monument of Kolokotronis, along with a small lake and old trains that make for great photo opportunities. Visitors can explore the prestigious Peloponnesian Folk Art Museum, The Children's Museum, and the unique Komboloi Museum nearby, the park offers shaded areas and a clean, safe environment for families to enjoy. Paths, lawns, and shaded areas make up the main public facilities.

Free

Paralia Arvanitias

Paralia Arvanitias, also known as Tolo beach, is a popular spot for swimming and sunbathing just 9 km outside of Nafplio. The small sandy and rocky shoreline can get crowded due to its proximity to hotels and campings. Visitors can enjoy free entrance and ample parking space. The area offers a view, with benches in the shade along the road for resting. However, there is a locked fence that may confuse some visitors.

Free

Five Brothers

Five Brothers is a visitor site in Nafplio. Located Nafplion, the neighborhood of Psaromachalas offers a glimpse into the city's rich history, dating back to the Byzantine era. This quaint area, named after its fishing heritage (with "psari" meaning fish in Greek), was a refuge for Greeks during Ottoman rule.
