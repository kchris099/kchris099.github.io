Delphi

Greeks treated Delphi as the world's navel; the oracle's counsel shaped interstate politics for centuries. Christian closure and landslides buried the sanctuary until nineteenth-century excavators uncovered the temple terrace and stadium. The archaeological site on Mount Parnassus is therefore a recovered classical religious capital, not a continuous living city.

Temple of Apollo

Six restored Doric columns from the 300s BCE mark the sanctuary where legend ties Leto's flight from Hera to the birth of Apollo and Artemis. The platform overlooks the sacred precinct and valley. Exterior and main foundations stay viewable on the hillside terrace.

Tholos of Athena Pronaia

The circular Tholos stands in the Sanctuary of Athena Pronaia below Delphi's main sacred precinct. Built in the fourth century BC, its restored columns, ring-shaped plan, sculpted fragments, and mountain setting have made it one of the site's most recognizable monuments.

Ancient Stadium of Delphi

Delphi's stadium lies high above the sanctuary at the end of an ascending archaeological route. Stone seating, starting lines, track and later Roman alterations survive from a venue used for the Pythian Games and other contests. The climb, heat, uneven ground and possible closures affect access.

The Ancient Theater of Delphi

The ancient theater occupies a slope above Apollo's sanctuary at Delphi, with stone seating facing the temple terrace and valley. It hosted music, drama and festival events connected with the Pythian Games and later rebuilding altered the original form.

The Athenian Treasury

The Athenian Treasury at Delphi is a small marble building beside the Sacred Way to the Temple of Apollo. Athenians dedicated it around 510–480 BCE after victories over the Persians, using it to house votive offerings from the city. Its sculpted metopes and tidy rectangular plan make it one of the clearer reconstructed treasuries on the sanctuary terraces.

Corycian Cave

High on Mount Parnassus, Corycian Cave combines limestone formations with a long record of human use. Ancient worshippers associated the cavern with Pan and the nymphs; archaeological finds point to ritual activity over several periods. A mountain approach reaches the cool interior. Broad chambers and stalactites contrast with the open slopes above Delphi.

38.5156° N / 22.5216° E

Delphi Archaeological Museum

Delphi Archaeological Museum arranges sculpture, architectural fragments, bronzes, votive offerings, inscriptions and everyday objects from the sanctuary and surrounding area. The Charioteer, Siphnian Treasury frieze, Sphinx of Naxos and finds from successive sacred buildings trace changing artistic and religious practice; galleries provide essential context before or after the exposed ruins, while crowd flow and renovation can affect the route.

European Cultural Centre of Delphi

The European Cultural Centre of Delphi operates a modern conference and arts campus outside the ancient sanctuary. Residences, meeting halls, sculpture, exhibitions, archives and performance programs support international work in culture, education and dialogue.

Maritime Museum of Galaxidi

Galaxidi's Maritime Museum occupies a historic building in a town once active in Greek sailing and shipowning. Ship portraits, models and figureheads trace the local merchant fleet. Navigation instruments, documents and maps explain its trading routes. Photographs and archaeological finds add evidence of daily life at sea and ashore, linking the collection closely to Galaxidi's nineteenth-century maritime economy.
