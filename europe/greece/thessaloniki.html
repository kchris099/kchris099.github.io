Thessaloniki

Thessaloniki was founded in 315 BCE and became a major Byzantine second capital after Constantinople. Roman forums, Ottoman markets, and waterfront warehouses record Macedonian, Balkan, and Mediterranean trade. A devastating fire in 1917 reshaped the centre before 20th-century reconstruction and the 1997 European Capital of Culture programme. Municipal archives, parish records, and surviving architecture help explain how the city connected to wider regional trade routes, court patronage, and modern civic rebuilding after industrial and wartime change.

Arch of Galerius

Built around AD 300, the Arch of Galerius celebrated the emperor's campaigns and formed part of a processional route between his palace and the Rotunda. Relief-covered piers stand beside a busy Thessaloniki crossing known as Kamara. Part of the original arch survives, with carved battle and imperial scenes still legible amid shops, traffic and daily meeting activity.

Rotonda

The Rotonda is a massive circular Roman building from the early fourth century, long tied to Emperor Galerius's palace complex and later used as a church and mosque. High brick walls and a brick dome enclose a dim interior where mosaic fragments survive. Scholars still debate whether Constantine intended it as a mausoleum; visitors see the layered Christian and imperial imagery that remains.

Heptapyrgion of Thessaloniki

Heptapyrgion crowns Thessaloniki's upper city as a fortress with Byzantine, Ottoman and modern layers; despite its name, the complex has more than seven towers and later served as a prison until the twentieth century. Walls, courtyards, cells, gates, restoration areas and views require historical context on confinement and military control.

Thessaloniki Acropolis

Also called Ano Poli or the Upper City, Thessaloniki's Acropolis occupies the high northern slopes founded in the era of King Cassander. Byzantine and Ottoman walls, narrow lanes, and timber-framed houses climb toward the Heptapyrgion fortress. From the ramparts the Thermaic Gulf and lower city open out below the old fortifications.

The Umbrellas by Zongolopoulos

George Zongolopoulos's Umbrellas sculpture rises on Thessaloniki's New Waterfront as a row of tall metal umbrellas open to the sky. The piece sits on the promenade where people gather toward evening for views across the Thermaic Gulf. It functions as a public meeting marker more than a museum exhibit, with open space around the poles for walking and photographs.

Alexander the Great Statue

An equestrian bronze of Alexander the Great stands on Thessaloniki's waterfront facing the Thermaic Gulf. Installed in the 1970s, the monument depicts Alexander on Bucephalus beside shields, spears and reliefs associated with Macedonian campaigns. Its scale and coastal position express modern civic identification with ancient Macedonia, while the open promenade gives the sculpture a broad ceremonial setting.

Aristotelous Square

Aristotelous Square opens from central Thessaloniki toward the Thermaic Gulf along a broad planned axis. Ernest Hebrard's post-1917 reconstruction plan shaped its curved facades, arcades, upper stories and relationship with the waterfront, although completion took decades. Cafes, hotels, demonstrations, celebrations and daily movement keep it active as the city's main civic space. The sea remains visible at the end of the axis.

The Metropolitan Church of Saint Gregory Palamas stands near Thessaloniki's waterfront as the seat of the local Orthodox metropolis. Built around the turn of the twentieth century after an earlier church burned, it combines Byzantine-inspired forms, a dome, bell towers, icons, frescoes and relics associated with the theologian Gregory Palamas. Services and ecclesiastical use govern access to the active cathedral.

Holy Cathedral Church of Hagia Sophia

Hagia Sophia in Thessaloniki is an early medieval church built in a form inspired by Constantinople's great cathedral. A broad dome, mosaics, columns, wall paintings and later Ottoman alterations record Byzantine worship, mosque use and restoration; the church belongs to Thessaloniki's historic early Christian and Byzantine monuments and remains active, making service schedules part of any visit.

Trigonion Tower

Trigonion Tower, sometimes called the Triangle Tower, forms part of the Ottoman-reinforced walls that guarded medieval Thessaloniki. Its angular stone mass stands on the eastern circuit, with interior levels that once served defense. From the upper platform the city and gulf spread outward, making the tower both a fortification remnant and a viewpoint.

Roman Forum of Thessaloniki

The Roman Forum of Thessaloniki occupies an excavated terrace in the city center, with remains of a two-level agora, stoas, and a small odeon. Stone paving and foundations show how civic business and entertainment sat together in the imperial period. Walkways let visitors look down into the dig without treating the site as a reconstructed plaza.

Thessaloniki Waterfront

Thessaloniki's waterfront follows the Thermaic Gulf from the port past the White Tower and into the landscaped New Waterfront. Promenades and cycle paths connect gardens, public art, playgrounds, cafes and boat piers across several urban sections. Views toward Mount Olympus change with weather and light. Festivals and heavy evening use make the route an active civic space along the water.
