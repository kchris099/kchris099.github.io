Kefalonia

Kefalonia, the largest Ionian island, held Mycenaean and classical settlements before Venetian and British rule left fortresses such as Assos and ports like Argostoli. The 1953 earthquake destroyed most towns; concrete rebuilding followed while some villages were abandoned. Olive groves, Melissani cave, and Myrtos cliffs define the postwar landscape. Ferry links to the mainland and fellow Ionian islands frame a history of quakes, empires, and recovery.

Platia Kampanas

Platia Kampanas in Argostoli centres on a clock tower rebuilt after the 1953 Ionian earthquake, surrounded by cafés and shops. Evening walks fill the paved space between harbour streets and civic buildings. The square functions as Argostoli's main open-air gathering ground after the postwar reconstruction of the town.

Vallianos Square

In Argostoli, restaurants and cafes ring this modern central plaza that brightens at night with bars and outdoor tables. Open paving and surrounding facades define the civic room. Locals use it as a rest and meal stop while moving through the island capital.

03 AdmissionFree

Monument to the Fallen of the Acqui Division

A memorial above Argostoli commemorates soldiers of the Italian Acqui Division killed by German forces on Kefalonia after Italy's 1943 armistice. Stone forms, inscriptions, plaques and a hillside setting address a wartime massacre and its contested memory.

04 AdmissionFree

Saint Theodore Lighthouse

Saint Theodore Lighthouse stands on a spit near Argostoli, a white tower marking entrance approaches to Kefalonia's main harbour gulf. A coastal walk reaches the light along calm inland water used by local boats. Sea and mountain views open across the gulf toward the island's western ridges at sunset.

38.1914° N / 20.4678° E

05 AdmissionFree

Old Vlachata

Old Vlachata refers to the pre-earthquake settlement area above Kefalonia's south coast, where ruins and lanes recall villages shattered in 1953. Newer Vlachata nearby absorbed much resettlement. Walking the older ground shows how the quake redrew habitation across the island's hills.

38.2560° N / 20.6024° E

06 AdmissionFree

Karavomilos Lake

Karavomilos Lake forms a small brackish pool beside the coast near Sami and belongs to Kefalonia's unusual karst-water system. Water entering sinkholes near Argostoli travels underground across the island before emerging around Sami and Melissani. Trees, a path, a bridge and the nearby shore frame a site whose main scientific interest lies in the island's hydrology. A waterwheel recalls the lake's former mill connection.

38.2550° N / 20.6298° E

08 AdmissionFree

Paralia Petani

Paralia Petani is a west-coast Kefalonia beach of pebbles set below green slopes, with clear water and seasonal sunbeds. Afternoon light draws sunset watchers, while waves can run strong on windy days. The bay remains one of the island's more dramatic swimming coves.

38.2608° N / 20.3766° E

09 AdmissionFree

Makris Gialos Beach

Coastal road and boardwalk approaches reach a shoreline tied to nearby harbor districts and seaside markets. Sand and shallows stay reachable year-round on foot from adjacent paths. The beach works as a simple swimming strip rather than a developed resort core.

38.1543° N / 20.4809° E

10 AdmissionFree

Platis Gialos Beach

Platis Gialos Beach is a coastal spot located 3 km from Argostoli, adjacent to Makrys Gialos beach. The area is adopted by verdant hills and abundant vegetation. This beach has pristine white sand and clear waters, earning it the prestigious Blue Flag for cleanliness and excellent facilities. It's not overly crowded, making it perfect for snorkeling enthusiasts.
