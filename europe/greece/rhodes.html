Rhodes

Rhodes town preserves a Gothic and Ottoman layered harbour capital built by the Knights of Saint John after 1309. Palace of the Grand Master, Street of the Knights, and mosques record crusader fortification and later Ottoman Aegean governance. Harbour mandraki walks and medieval inns extend crusader street grids along the Aegean waterfront. Walking routes through the historic centre help visitors relate these monuments to wider patterns of settlement and civic life in Greece.

Medieval City of Rhodes

The walled medieval town of Rhodes retains Hospitaller streets, gates, and the Palace of the Grand Master above the harbour. The Street of the Knights climbs toward the palace, while the Archaeological Museum occupies the former Knights' hospital nearby. Ottoman mosques, small squares, and the old harbour fortifications sit inside the same circuit of walls that still enclose the old city.

Street of the Knights of Rhodes

The Street of the Knights climbs through Rhodes's medieval city between the former hospital and the Palace of the Grand Master. Stone facades line a narrow paved route where the Knights Hospitaller maintained inns for members grouped by language and region; later restoration reinforced its austere appearance while preserving evidence of the order's administrative organization.

Palace of the Grand Master of the Knights of Rhodes

The Palace of the Grand Master anchors the upper end of Rhodes's Street of the Knights. A medieval Hospitaller fortress and residence was damaged, altered and extensively reconstructed under Italian rule in the twentieth century, producing monumental halls around older fabric. Courtyards, mosaics, exhibitions, towers and state rooms therefore combine medieval remains with a politically motivated modern rebuilding.

Medieval Clock Tower

The Medieval Clock Tower above Rhodes Town traces to early fortifications and was rebuilt in 1852 with Baroque touches after an explosion. Stairs climb to a deck looking over the Hospitaller street grid and harbour. From the platform the red roofs of the old town spread toward the sea walls.

Saint Athanasios Gate

Saint Athanasios Gate pierces the southern walls of Rhodes's medieval city near the church of Saint Francis. Hospitaller fortifications, a round tower, moat, and later Ottoman associations frame an entrance linked by tradition with Suleiman's conquest in 1522; the gate's names and historical accounts vary.

06 AdmissionFree

Sea Gate

Sea Gate is a historic stone entryway from the Middle Ages, featuring double crenellated towers and Christian reliefs. Built in the 14th century by Grand Master Juan Fernandez de Heredia, it bears his coat of arms on top. The Ottoman administration later demolished its side towers to widen access to Kolona harbour. Today, it facilitates swift vehicle flow between the harbour and the new town through direct connection with Liberty Gate.

Hippocrates Square

Hippocrates Square (Plateia Ippokratous) is the busy central square of the medieval Old Town of Rhodes. It is characterized by the Castellania building, a historic fountain, and numerous cafes and shops. The square is a major meeting point and a active hub for visitors exploring the cobblestone streets and historic architecture of the walled city.

Acropolis of Rhodes

On Monte Smith above Rhodes Town, the Acropolis of Rhodes spreads across a hillside of excavated temples, a stadium, and a restored odeon. Columns and foundations mark the ancient upper city rather than a single standing palace. Paths climb through open ground with views back to the harbor and the medieval quarter below.

Ancient Stadium of Rhodes

Hellenistic temple remains dedicated to Zeus, Athena, Apollo, and Helios stand near the stadium field on the acropolis hill. A renovated theater section includes newer marble seating for performances. Event access follows posted gate procedures on the archaeological terrace.

10 AdmissionFree

Colossus of Rhodes

The Myth Colossus of Rhodes, once a towering 33-meter statue of the Greek sun-god Helios, stood at the entrance to Mandraki Harbour. It was built in 280 BC to commemorate Rhodes' successful defense against an attack. Unfortunately, it collapsed during an earthquake in 226 BC. Today, two bronze statues of a stag and a doe stand as memorials at the harbor entrance.

Windmills of Rhodes

Three preserved windmills stand on the Mandraki harbor breakwater in Rhodes, recalling a larger group once used to grind grain delivered by ship. Their cylindrical stone towers and conical roofs line the approach toward Fort Saint Nicholas, with harbor traffic and old-city walls beyond. They are primarily exterior landmarks tied to the port's commercial history.

Nea Agora

Nea Agora stands beside the Mandraki harbor in Rhodes Town as a covered market of shops and food stalls under a circular arcade. Vendors sell produce, cheeses, spices, honey, and everyday goods used by residents as much as by travellers. The building's courtyard is a practical stop between the Old Town walls and the waterfront.

Kallithea Springs

Kallithea Springs faces the sea on Rhodes with Italian-era spa pavilions, mosaic floors, and a small beach below the cliffs. Thermal bathing here goes back to antiquity, though the present buildings date from the early twentieth-century spa complex. Domed halls, shaded courtyards, and stone steps down to the water define the visit.

Paralia Antoni Kouin

Paralia Antoni Kouin is a shoreline stretch on Rhodes near the Kallithea coast, used for swimming and sunbathing rather than monumental sightseeing. Clear water meets a mix of sand and pebbles along a relatively open bay. The beach sits within easy reach of the spa grounds inland, so many people combine a coastal stop with nearby walks.

Saint Nectarios Church

Saint Nectarios Church is a modern Orthodox parish building on Rhodes, completed during the twentieth century. Sand-colored masonry, arcaded walkways and red-tiled roofs surround a paved courtyard, while a tall bell tower gives the low complex a clear vertical marker. Icons and devotional furnishings support continuing worship, distinguishing the church from the island's medieval harbor monuments.

Filerimos Monastery

Filerimos Monastery crowns Filerimos Hill west of Rhodes Town, a stone religious complex among pines with domed arches and a church dedicated to the Virgin. The hill held sanctuaries long before the medieval monastery; Knights of St. John later shaped the buildings visitors see today. Early twentieth-century restoration left the cloisters and viewpoints open for quiet walks.

17 AdmissionFree

The ruins of Church of the Virgin Mary of the Burgh

Located at the eastern edge of Rhodes Old City, in the Jewish Quarter, the Church of the Virgin Mary of the Burgh is a significant example of ecclesiastical architecture in Dodecanese and Asia Minor. Dating back to the 14th century, it was originally a three-aisled basilica with Gothic ribbed cross vaults. Although it suffered damage during World War II and was later converted into houses, its grand original Gothic style still impresses visitors.

18 AdmissionFree

Jewish Martyrs Square

In Rhodes's former Jewish quarter, Jewish Martyrs Square commemorates the islanders deported during the Holocaust and the wider history of the local Sephardic community. A memorial stands among cafes and medieval streets near Kahal Shalom Synagogue. The open civic space links present-day neighborhood activity with the destruction of a community that had lived on Rhodes for centuries.

Elli Beach

Elli Beach runs along Rhodes Town's northern shore below the casino and aquarium headland, with a mix of pebbles and sand. Swimmers use the clear water beside diving platforms and seasonal beach concessions. The shoreline walk links the medieval town walls to the modern hotel strip facing the Anatolian sea.
