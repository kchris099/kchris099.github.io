A Brief History

Athens stands as one of the world's oldest cities, with a recorded history spanning over three millennia. During its classical period in the 5th century BC, it became a highly powerful city-state and the foundational center for the arts, learning, and philosophy, giving rise to the concept of democracy. Its monumental architectural legacy, profoundly symbolized by the Acropolis, continues to exert an unparalleled influence on Western civilization.

Acropolis of Athens

An ancient citadel located on a rocky outcrop above Athens, containing the remains of several historically significant buildings. It reached its peak construction in the 5th century BC under Pericles.

Free (Acropolis: €20)

Parthenon

A former temple on the Athenian Acropolis dedicated to the goddess Athena, completed in 438 BC. It is considered the most important surviving building of Classical Greece and the zenith of the Doric order.

Free (Acropolis: €20)

Erechtheion

An ancient Greek temple on the north side of the Acropolis, dedicated to both Athena and Poseidon. Built between 421 and 406 BC, it is famous for its Porch of the Caryatids.

Free (Acropolis: €20)

Greek Flag of Athens Acropolis

A historically significant flagpole site where Evzones raise and lower the Greek flag daily. It marks the location where Manolis Glezos and Apostolos Santas tore down the Nazi flag in 1941.

Free

Philopappos Hill

A prominent hill in central Athens featuring the Philopappos Monument, a mausoleum dedicated to a commagene prince in 114 AD. It offers a strategic historical vantage point over the Attica basin.

€12

National Archaeological Museum

The largest archaeological museum in Greece, established in 1829. It houses some of the most important artifacts from various archaeological locations around Greece from prehistory to late antiquity.

Free

Arch of Hadrian

A monumental gateway built in 131 AD to celebrate the arrival of the Roman Emperor Hadrian and honor him for his benefactions to the city. It spans the ancient road from the center of Athens.

Free

Zappeion

A building in the National Gardens of Athens, constructed in the 1880s by Evangelos Zappas for the revival of the Olympic Games in the modern world. It has served as an exhibition hall and fencing venue.

Free

Monastiraki Square

A central commercial neighborhood and square, named after the Pantanassa church monastery. It has been a continuous marketplace area since the Ottoman period and features the Tzistarakis Mosque.

€10

Panathenaic Stadium

A multi-purpose stadium built entirely of marble, constructed on the site of an ancient Greek stadium. It was refurbished to host the opening and closing ceremonies of the first modern Olympics in 1896.

€6

Daphni Monastery

An 11th-century Byzantine monastery built on the site of the Sanctuary of Apollo Daphnaios, which was destroyed by the Goths in 395 AD. It is known for its well-preserved middle Byzantine mosaics.
