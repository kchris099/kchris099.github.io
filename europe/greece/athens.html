Athens

Athens has been inhabited for millennia, with the Acropolis bearing temples such as the Parthenon, built in the 5th century BCE under Pericles. The city served as a cultural center in antiquity and later under Roman, Byzantine, and Ottoman rule. It became the capital of independent Greece in 1834. Neoclassical quarters expanded in the 19th century. The Acropolis and its slopes are inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site.

Acropolis of Athens

A rocky citadel above Athens holds the Parthenon, Erechtheion, Propylaea, and Temple of Athena Nike. Major construction under Pericles in the 5th century BC defined classical Greek architecture. Ongoing conservation addresses pollution damage and structural stress on the marble temples.

Parthenon

The Parthenon on Athens's Acropolis is the Doric temple of Athena Parthenos completed around 438 BC under Ictinus and Callicrates, with Phidias directing the sculptural programme. Surviving columns and pediment fragments still define classical Greek architecture. The temple crowns the sacred rock above the city.

Erechtheion

The Erechtheion on the Acropolis north side is an Ionic temple of 421–406 BC honouring Athena Polias and Poseidon-Erechtheus on uneven sacred ground. Six Caryatids carry the south porch; five originals stand in the Acropolis Museum. Multiple shrines share one irregular plan.

National Archaeological Museum

Founded in 1829, Greece's national archaeological museum displays finds from excavations across the country, from Neolithic figurines to Roman bronzes. Highlights include Mycenaean gold masks, the Antikythera mechanism, and frescoes from Thera. The neoclassical building stands in the Exarchia district north of the center.

Arch of Hadrian

Hadrian dedicated this marble arch in 131-132 AD to mark the boundary between old Athens and his new quarter. Inscriptions on opposite sides address Theseus and Hadrian as founders. The structure stands east of the Temple of Olympian Zeus along a Roman processional route.

Evangelos Zappas funded the Zappeion as a neoclassical hall for the revival of the Olympic Games and national exhibitions. Theatre of Dionysus lies on the south slope of the Acropolis where Athenian dramatic festivals developed from the sixth century BC onward, and plays by Aeschylus, Sophocles, Euripides and Aristophanes were associated with this civic setting, although capacity estimates vary. Choragic Monument of Lysicrates

Lysicrates erected his choragic monument in 334 BC to display a bronze tripod awarded for sponsoring a winning dramatic chorus. Slender Corinthian columns, a circular marble form, sculpted frieze, and high pedestal survive near the ancient Street of the Tripods in Plaka; later incorporation into a monastery helped preserve it, while the nickname Diogenes' Lantern has no connection to its original function.

Lycabettus Hill

Construction of the Temple of Olympian Zeus began under Athenian tyrants in the sixth century BC and was completed under Emperor Hadrian in the second century AD. Ancient Agora of Athens

Athens's Ancient Agora functioned as a center of assembly, law, religion, commerce and public life below the Acropolis. Foundations of civic buildings, temples, stoas, monuments, roads and drainage reveal centuries of change, while the reconstructed Stoa of Attalos houses the site museum. Democratic institutions developed here, but the broad archaeological area also reflects Roman rebuilding and ordinary market activity.

Mikrolimano

Mikrolimano curves around a sheltered harbor in Piraeus historically known as Munichia. The basin once supported ancient naval activity and later developed around fishing, small craft and dense housing on the surrounding slopes. Modern quays are lined with restaurants and moored yachts, yet the compact shape of the harbor still reveals its value as a protected anchorage close to Athens' main port.

Technopolis City of Athens

Athens converted the former Gazi gasworks into Technopolis, retaining chimneys, furnaces, tanks, machinery and industrial buildings from a plant that supplied the city from the nineteenth century. The Industrial Gas Museum explains the production process and working conditions. Concerts, exhibitions, festivals and workshops now occupy the adapted complex, placing contemporary cultural use within surviving industrial infrastructure.

The Pnyx is a rocky assembly site west of the Acropolis where Athenian citizens met to debate and vote during the Classical period. Cut terraces, a speaker's platform, retaining walls and changing orientations reveal how the gathering place developed. Views toward the Acropolis help explain its civic geography, while sunset, pet access and nearby landmarks are secondary to its role in direct democracy.
