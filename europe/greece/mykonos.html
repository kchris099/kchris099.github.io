Mykonos

Mykonos was historically a modest agricultural and maritime island, overshadowed by the nearby sacred island of Delos in antiquity. It experienced periods of Venetian and Ottoman rule, which influenced its distinctive Cycladic architecture and labyrinthine streets designed to thwart pirates. In the mid-twentieth century, Mykonos transformed into a cosmopolitan destination, attracting international artists and travelers. Today, it is known for its well known windmills, active nightlife, and pristine beaches.

Windmills of Mykonos

The windmills of Mykonos stand on a rise above the harbour, a line of sixteenth- and seventeenth-century mills once used to grind grain. Several in the Kato Mili group have been restored as whitewashed landmarks. They remain the island's classic silhouette against Aegean sunsets.

Armenistis Lighthouse

Armenistis Lighthouse stands on the exposed northwestern coast of Mykonos above the channel toward Tinos. Built in the nineteenth century after a maritime disaster, the stone tower served navigation around a difficult passage, while its original lens is preserved elsewhere. The remote exterior setting, wind, rough roads, and lack of formal visitor facilities matter more than guaranteed sunset views.

Archaeological Site of Delos

The island sanctuary opposite Mykonos holds excavated streets, houses, and sanctuaries from the ancient sacred and commercial center. Visitors reach it by boat and walk among foundations visible from adjacent viewpoints. The open ruins remain a protected archaeological field.

Mykonos Harbour View

Mykonos's old harbor curves along the edge of Chora with fishing boats, excursion vessels, waterfront buildings, cafes, and access toward the archaeological museum. It remains distinct from the newer ferry port at Tourlos and from Little Venice or the windmills farther south, and commercial activity and changing marine traffic define the scene.

Monastery of Tourliani

The Monastery of Tourliani, dating back to the 16th century, is a whitewashed church and monastery located southeast of Mykonos Town. It is known for detailed marble carvings and a grand Italian baroque altar screen. Visiting this monastery offers a glimpse into the more authentic side of Mykonos, away from the touristy areas.

07 AdmissionFree

Holy Church of Panagia Paraportiani

Panagia Paraportiani is a whitewashed complex of five interconnected churches in Mykonos's Kastro quarter. Construction across several centuries produced irregular walls, domes, vaults, chapels, and an upper church above earlier structures near a former castle gate. The sculptural exterior is usually easier to see than the limited interior, while active religious use and conservation govern access.

37.4471° N / 25.3257° E

08 AdmissionFree

View of Alefkandra (Little Venice)

Alefkandra, widely called Little Venice, lines a Mykonos waterfront where houses project balconies directly above the sea. Merchants and ship captains developed much of the district in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries and bars, restaurants, residences and galleries now occupy the narrow lanes. Waves, crowding and commercial use shape the experience beyond the familiar sunset view.

37.4454° N / 25.3260° E

09 AdmissionFree

Paradise Beach

Paradise Beach lies on Mykonos's south coast as a sandy bay reached by road, path, or seasonal boat from other beaches. Shoreline clubs and sunbeds line parts of the sand while open stretches remain for swimming. Hills behind the cove cut the beach off from the island's stronger northern wind corridors.

37.4103° N / 25.3560° E

Ornos Beach

Ornos Beach, located just 2 km southwest of Mykonos Town, is a popular and trendy destination known for its family-friendly atmosphere. Unlike some of the other beaches on the island, Ornos offers calm waters and soft sand, making it ideal for young visitors. The area is lined with rental chairs and umbrellas, beach bars, restaurants, and hotels. It's a setting with plenty of beach hotel rooms, cafes, taverns, and activities like diving.
