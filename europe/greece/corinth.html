Corinth

Ancient Corinth controlled the narrow isthmus connecting the Peloponnese to mainland Greece, amassing large wealth through the portage of ships between the Aegean and Ionian seas. Destroyed by the Romans in 146 BC, it was rebuilt by Julius Caesar to become the prosperous capital of Roman Greece and an early site of Christian preaching by the Apostle Paul. Subsequent Byzantine, Frankish, and Ottoman rulers fortified the looming Acrocorinth. Visitors explore the extensive Roman forum, the archaic Temple of Apollo, and the massive fortifications of the upper citadel.

Temple of Apollo

Built around the sixth century BC, the heavy Doric structure predates much of the Roman city later established around it. Seven monolithic limestone columns survive from the Archaic Temple of Apollo above Ancient Corinth. Foundations and column spacing reveal the temple's original form, while the surrounding archaeological site provides the commercial, civic, and religious context missing from the isolated ruin.

Archaeological Museum of Ancient Corinth

The Archaeological Museum of Ancient Corinth displays finds from the Greek, Roman, Byzantine and later settlement beside the excavation site. Sculpture, mosaics, pottery, inscriptions, domestic objects and material from major monuments help reconstruct a city repeatedly rebuilt across centuries.

Acrocorinth

Acrocorinth is the fortified acropolis rock rising about 575 metres above ancient Corinth, with medieval walls enclosing earlier sanctuaries. Paths climb to views over the Corinthian and Saronic gulfs. Successive Byzantine, Frankish, and Ottoman phases shaped the fortress gates and keeps.

37.8910° N / 22.8704° E

04 AdmissionFree

Corinth Canal

The Corinth Canal, a feat of engineering completed in 1893, separates the Peloponnese from mainland Greece. The canal offers views of its sheer rock walls and blue waters, enjoyed from a sturdy footbridge or on a tour boat. For the adventurous, bungee jumping provides an entirely different perspective. Nearby attractions include the Gulf of Corinth and traditional Greek villages offering local food and wine experiences.

37.9342° N / 22.9844° E

Splash Waterpark Isthmos

Seasonal operations define Splash Waterpark Isthmos, a commercial water park near the Corinth Canal area. Pools, slides, children's zones, food service, changing facilities and supervised attractions operate according to weather and an annually changing schedule. Safety limits, height rules, maintenance and crowding matter more than customer-review praise, while the venue has no direct connection to ancient Corinth's archaeological history.

Pegasus Statue

The Pegasus statue in Corinth recalls the mythical winged horse linked to the spring of Peirene and Corinthian foundation stories. The modern sculpture stands in a public setting within the contemporary city. It offers a quick mythological marker amid streets rebuilt after earthquakes.

Πλατεία Παναγή Τσαλδάρη, Κόρινθος

Plateia Panagi Tsaldari takes its name from statesman Panagis Tsaldaris and forms a paved downtown focus in modern Corinth. Cafés and civic buildings frame the open space used for everyday meeting and events. The square sits well away from the inland archaeological site of ancient Corinth.

Kalamia Beach

Kalamia Beach follows Corinth's urban Gulf shoreline, where pebbles and coarse sand meet shallow water near the waterfront road, and cafes, seasonal facilities and residential streets keep the beach closely tied to the modern city. Wind, water quality and local maintenance shape swimming conditions along this open stretch of coast.
