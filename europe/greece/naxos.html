Naxos

Early Cycladic communities and, later, a Venetian duchy made Naxos the largest and most fertile of the central Cyclades. Emery exports and marble quarries funded island wealth; the hilltop kastro contains the Latin cathedral and tower houses. Portara's unfinished temple doorway on the islet opposite the harbour is the most visited ancient fragment.

Temple of Apollo

Naxos, the largest of the Cyclades islands, is a treasure trove of history and natural beauty. The island has seven museums showing artifacts from various periods, including the Archaeological Museum with exhibits dating back to the Neolithic period. Visitors can explore ancient ruins like the doorlike remains of the Temple of Apollo and wander through Chora's Venetian castle.

Bazeos Tower

Bazeos Tower, originally a 17th-century monastery, now is a cultural hub and historical landmark on the island of Naxos. After being abandoned by monks in the 19th century, it became home to several families of potters. Today, the tower hosts various cultural activities and is an art gallery featuring works by both local and international artists. Visitors can explore its well-preserved chapel and learn about its rich history from friendly staff members.

Ancient Temple of Demeter

The Ancient Temple of Demeter, located near the village of Sangri on Naxos, is a significant archaeological site dating back to the 6th century BC. Built entirely of Naxian marble, the partially restored temple provides early examples of classical Greek architecture. It was dedicated to the goddess of agriculture, reflecting the fertile nature of the surrounding valley.

Apollonas Kouros

The Apollonas Kouros is a massive, unfinished marble statue dating from the 8th century BC, located in an ancient quarry near the village of Apollonas on Naxos. Measuring over 10 meters in length, the statue depicts a male figure, possibly the god Dionysus or Apollo. It offers a fascinating glimpse into the techniques and scale of ancient Greek marble carving.
