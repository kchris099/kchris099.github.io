Malmo

Malmö lies at the eastern end of the Öresund strait opposite Copenhagen and was a Danish town until the Treaty of Roskilde in 1658 transferred Skåne to Sweden. St. Peter's Church and the Malmöhus castle date from the Middle Ages. Shipbuilding and industry grew in the 19th century. The Öresund Bridge opened in 2000, linking the city by rail and road to Denmark. Turning Torso tower marks recent waterfront development.

Malmo Castle

Malmo Castle is a visitor site in Malmo. Founded in 1434 and rebuilt by Christian III of Denmark in the 1530s, this waterfront fortress is among Scandinavia's oldest surviving Renaissance castles. Its mint, dungeons, and cannons recall wars between Denmark and Sweden. Museums inside cover city history, shipping, and natural science.

Malmo City Hall

Built in Dutch Renaissance style and completed around 1546, Malmo's town hall faces Stortorget with a stepped gable and ornate entrance. Council chambers and courtrooms still operate inside. The facade reflects the city's Hanseatic trade wealth when Skåne belonged to Denmark.

Stortorget Malmo

Stortorget has served as Malmo's main square since the 16th century, surrounded by town hall, merchants' houses, and cafes. Markets and public events still fill the cobbled space. A bronze equestrian statue of Karl X Gustav recalls the Swedish conquest of Skåne in 1658.

Optimistorkestern is a visitor site in Malmo. Yngve Lundell's 1985 sculpture shows seven bronze musicians marching across Gustav Adolfs torg. Centralposthuset

Centralposthuset is a visitor site in Malmo. Designed by Ferdinand Boberg and opened in 1906, this National post office faces the central station with a tower and granite base. Telegraph and sorting halls have given way to shops and offices, but arched windows and brick ornament remain landmarks of early 20th-century civic design.

Turning Torso

Turning Torso is a visitor site in Malmo, Sweden. Designed by Santiago Calatrava, this neo-futurist skyscraper stands at 190 meters with 54 stories, making it the tallest building in the Nordics. Its distinctive twisted shell turns 90 degrees from bottom to top, creating a sight for travellers. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.
