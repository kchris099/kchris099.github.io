Hallstatt

Hallstatt's prosperity originated in prehistoric times with the exploitation of its extensive salt deposits, giving its name to the early Iron Age Hallstatt culture. The settlement remained an important salt mining center under the Habsburgs, with wealth driving the construction of its distinctive Alpine architecture squeezed between the lake and steep mountain slopes. Before modern road access in the late nineteenth century, the town relied almost entirely on boat transport.

Panoramic Viewpoint - Hallstatt Skywalk

A platform projects from the mountainside above Hallstatt at the World Heritage View lookout near the salt mine. Reached by funicular or a steep walking route, it looks down on the village roofs, Hallstätter See, and the narrow valley between the Dachstein slopes. The suspended point emphasizes the settlement's limited strip of land between lake and mountain.

Hallstätter See

Hallstätter See fills a narrow alpine basin below Hallstatt and Obertraun, with steep wooded slopes descending directly to the water. Ferries link the lakeside village with the railway station across the lake, while small boats, swimming areas, and shore paths operate seasonally. The lake's transport role and constrained shoreline have strongly shaped settlement in the valley.

47.5786° N / 13.6629° E

Dachstein-Gletscher (Schladming-Dachstein) - Talstation der Bergbahn

The lower station of the Dachstein cableway stands above Ramsau am Dachstein and begins the ascent to the high plateau. Parking, equipment areas and boarding platforms concentrate around the terminal before cabins rise over forest and exposed rock. The route leads toward glacier viewpoints, mountain trails and the upper station near the Dachstein's limestone summits. The terminal also marks a shift from valley roads to high-mountain travel.

Dachstein Skywalk

Near the upper Dachstein cable-car station, the Dachstein Skywalk extends over the south face of the mountain with glass sections above the drop. The terrace frames the glacier, limestone plateau, and distant Alpine ranges, while nearby routes lead to the suspension bridge and Ice Palace. High-altitude weather and snow can limit visibility or close exposed structures.

Five narrow platforms extend from the Krippenstein ridge above Hallstätter See, each ending with a different viewing detail such as glass flooring or a frame. Cable cars from Obertraun reach the high plateau, followed by a marked walk across exposed alpine terrain. The outlook covers Hallstatt, the lake, and the Dachstein massif from above the northern escarpment.

World Heritage Spiral (Welterbespirale)

The World Heritage Spiral sits near the summit of the Dachstein massif, reached by the Dachstein cable car above Hallstatt. A 360-degree platform looks over the lake town and alpine ridges at about 2,100 metres. From the same mountain station, visitors continue on to the Giant Ice Cave and Mammoth Cave.

Dachstein Rieseneishöhle

Dachstein Giant Ice Cave occupies limestone passages high above Obertraun, reached by cable car and a guided underground route. Seasonal lighting reveals ice curtains, frozen floors, rock chambers and formations renewed by winter water and cold air; the approach and interior include stairs, uneven surfaces and low temperatures even when the valley is warm.

47.5349° N / 13.7186° E

10 AdmissionFree

Vorderer Gosausee

Vorderer Gosausee lies beneath the Dachstein massif, a mountain lake reached by road and walking paths from Gosau. Clear water mirrors limestone peaks, and lakeside trails suit short walks and longer hikes toward higher tarns. Photographers gather at the shore when the Dachstein glaciers show above the treeline. Boardwalk sections near the shore keep boots dry after snowmelt.

47.5283° N / 13.5071° E
