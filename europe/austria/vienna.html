Vienna

Vienna grew from a Roman camp and medieval ducal seat into the capital of the Habsburg monarchy. The Hofburg, Schönbrunn, and baroque churches expanded as the court patronized music and the arts from the 17th century onward. After 1918 the city became the capital of the Austrian Republic. Postwar reconstruction preserved the Ringstrasse boulevard and historic center, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage site in 2001.

Schloss Belvedere

Prince Eugene of Savoy commissioned the Belvedere as a summer palace complex on rising ground outside old Vienna, and Gustav Klimt's paintings are displayed in the Upper Palace alongside works by Schiele, Kokoschka and earlier masters. Upper and Lower Belvedere frame formal gardens, fountains and terraces, while former state rooms now hold Austrian art from the Middle Ages onward.

Schloss Schönbrunn

Schonbrunn Palace in Hietzing was the Habsburgs' principal summer residence west of central Vienna. Baroque state rooms, a formal garden axis, and outbuildings fill a vast imperial park. The name refers to a 'beautiful spring' once found on the grounds that became a court retreat. Coach tours and audio guides move through state apartments in timed groups.

Gloriette Schönbrunn

The Gloriette, built in 1775, is a neoclassical colonnade on the hill above Schonbrunn Palace. From the terrace the formal gardens and palace facade align on a single axis. Karlskirche

Karlskirche faces Karlsplatz on the edge of Vienna's Innere Stadt and the Wieden district. Emperor Charles VI ordered the Baroque church after the 1713 plague, dedicating it to Saint Charles Borromeo. Twin columns, a green dome, and an oval nave define the exterior that still anchors the square.

Johann Strauss monument

Edmund Hellmer's gilded bronze figure of Johann Strauss II has stood in Vienna's Stadtpark since 1921. The composer appears with violin and bow before a curved marble relief populated by dancing figures. Lawns, flower beds, and paths surround the monument near the Wien River, placing the memorial within the nineteenth-century park closely associated with public music and leisure.

Vienna City Hall

Friedrich von Schmidt designed Vienna City Hall in a Gothic Revival style for the expanding municipal government of the late nineteenth century, and the building houses both city and state administration and opens selected interiors through scheduled tours and events. Its arcaded facade and tall central tower face Rathausplatz, while courtyards and ceremonial rooms fill the large block behind.

Austria's Parliament on the Ringstrasse is a neoclassical complex completed around 1883. Athena's fountain and a broad ramp face the boulevard between the Hofburg and City Hall. Debating chambers and committee rooms occupy the Pallas-Athena-framed wings. Guided routes explain how the chambers map onto Austria's bicameral system.

Kunsthistorisches Museum Wien

Twin museum buildings face each other across Maria-Theresien-Platz, with the Kunsthistorisches Museum holding the Habsburg imperial art collections, and the domed Ringstrasse building, opened in 1891, is itself richly finished with marble, murals and monumental stairways. Galleries include Egyptian and Near Eastern antiquities, Greek and Roman objects, European paintings, sculpture, decorative arts and the Kunstkammer.

Hofburg

Centuries of Habsburg expansion turned the Hofburg from a medieval stronghold into a connected complex of wings, courtyards, chapels, libraries and ceremonial halls. Public sections include the Imperial Apartments, Sisi Museum, Silver Collection, Spanish Riding School and imperial treasury. Other parts remain offices, museums or the official workplace of Austria's federal president.

Michaelerplatz

Michaelerplatz brings several periods of Vienna together at the northern entrance to the Hofburg. The curved Baroque facade of the Michaelertrakt faces St Michael's Church and restrained buildings by Adolf Loos, while an open excavation reveals Roman and medieval foundations below street level. Carriages, pedestrians, and palace traffic still pass through the square's central route.

Finished in the early eighteenth century, Peterskirche packs an oval Baroque plan and dome just off Vienna's Graben, often compared to St. Peter's in Rome on a smaller footprint. Gilt altars and frescoes fill the interior. Organ concerts sometimes occupy the nave outside Mass.

Column of Pest

Emperor Leopold I commissioned the Plague Column on the Graben after Vienna's 1679 epidemic. A dense Baroque composition rises from kneeling imperial figures through clouds, angels, saints, and representations of faith triumphing over disease. The monument's changing designs and long construction period reflect both public devotion and Habsburg political imagery in the heart of the old city.

A patterned tile roof and the tall south tower make St Stephen's Cathedral the dominant structure on Stephansplatz, and guided routes reach the catacombs and towers, while the main nave is an active place of Catholic worship and major civic ceremony. Romanesque doorways survive within a largely Gothic church containing chapels, tombs, altars, and a carved stone pulpit.

Viennese Giant Ferris Wheel

Built in 1897 for Emperor Franz Joseph's jubilee, the Wiener Riesenrad is the surviving historic wheel of the Prater amusement district. Enclosed red cabins turn slowly above the park, opening views across Vienna and the Danube basin. Fire destroyed much of the structure during the Second World War, and the rebuilt wheel returned with fewer cabins than the original design.

The Hundertwasser House, completed in 1985, is a social-housing block designed with irregular windows, color patches, and rooftop trees. Prater

The Prater combines a large wooded public park with the Wurstelprater amusement district in Vienna's second district. Emperor Joseph II opened the former imperial hunting ground to the public in 1766, after which restaurants, rides, sports grounds and exhibition venues developed around its avenues. The long Hauptallee remains a major route for walking, cycling and running beyond the Ferris wheel area.

Ankeruhr

Franz Matsch designed the Anker Clock between 1911 and 1914 as a bridge linking two insurance-company buildings above Hoher Markt. Twelve historical figures cross the mosaic face over the course of each day, accompanied by music at noon; copper, marble, and Art Nouveau ornament turn a functional connection between offices into a public mechanical display above one of Vienna's historic squares.

The present Vienna State Opera house opened in 1869 in French Early Renaissance style after earlier court theatre traditions. Ticketed performances and daytime visits govern public access. The Ringstrasse landmark remains Austria's principal opera stage.

Schönbrunn Zoo

Franz Stephan of Lorraine founded the imperial menagerie at Schönbrunn in 1752 around a central breakfast pavilion; research, breeding and conservation programs operate alongside the public collection at a historic continuously operating zoo. The modern zoo retains this Baroque core while extending through wooded palace grounds with habitats for mammals, birds, reptiles, fish and insects.

Schönbrunn Palace

Schönbrunn Palace is an 18th-century summer residence that offers tours of its opulent rococo ceremonial rooms and lavish gardens with a maze. The palace has Baroque and Neo-Classical architecture, reflecting Vienna's rich history as the town of Habsburgs and Maria Theresa. It is Vienna's most visited attraction, featuring 45 luxurious state rooms, ceremonial halls, and private apartments once inhabited by Empress Elisabeth and Emperor Franz Joseph.

The Wiener Konzerthaus opened in 1913 with four halls ranging from a large auditorium to intimate rooms. Orchestras, chamber groups, and festival programs share the complex through the season. Burgtheater

Opened in 1888, the Burgtheater was designed by Gottfried Semper and Karl von Hasenauer as the imperial court theater on the Ringstrasse, and wartime damage required major reconstruction, but the restored auditorium and resident company continue Vienna's German-language theater tradition. Grand staircases carry early paintings by Gustav Klimt, Ernst Klimt, and Franz Matsch beneath ornate ceilings.

Palm House

Iron ribs and long glass walls enclose the Palm House on the edge of Vienna's Burggarten. Built in the early twentieth century on the site of an earlier greenhouse, the Jugendstil structure is divided into climate zones for tropical and Mediterranean plants. Parts of the building also contain a cafe and butterfly house, while the exterior forms a formal backdrop to the palace garden.

Imperial Crypt

The Imperial Crypt beneath the Capuchin Church holds sarcophagi of Habsburg emperors and family from the seventeenth century onward. Ornate metal tombs fill vaulted chambers under the friary. Visitors descend from the church square into a quiet sequence of burial rooms. Metal wreaths and nameplates identify successive Habsburg generations.

Österreichische Postsparkasse

Otto Wagner's Postal Savings Bank near the Ring, finished in 1906, wraps marble slabs with aluminum rivets. The banking hall's glass canopy and functional detailing mark an early modernist public interior. A small museum upstairs explains Wagner's design for the Austrian postal savings system. Aluminum details inside still look sharp more than a century after opening.

Time Travel Vienna

Time Travel Vienna is an indoor multimedia attraction that walks through roughly two thousand years of city history. Sets, films, and guided scenes move from Roman Vindobona to imperial and twentieth-century Vienna. The show suits mixed-age groups looking for a condensed narrative before walking the old town. Animatronics and film clips compress centuries into a single timed walk-through.
