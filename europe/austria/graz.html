Graz

Graz served as a Habsburg residence and later capital of Inner Austria, with its Schlossberg fortifications controlling the Mur valley on routes from the Balkans to the Alps. Renaissance arcaded courtyards, the Landhaus, and the cathedral reflect patronage by archdukes who rivalled Vienna before the nineteenth century brought industry and universities. Designated a UNESCO City of Design, Graz layers baroque façades, modernist exhibition halls, and Styrian wine culture on a historic trade crossroads.

Uhrturm

The Uhrturm has stood above Graz on the Schlossberg since the sixteenth century, surviving the demolition of much of the hilltop fortress. Its large clock faces use an unusual historic arrangement in which the long hand marks hours and the shorter hand marks minutes; terraces around the stone tower look over the red roofs of the old town and the Mur valley.

Schlossberglift

The Schlossberglift and related funicular links rise from Graz's center to Schlossberg near the clock tower. Modern cabins replace earlier climbs, delivering visitors to ramparts, cafes, and hilltop paths. Return trips drop back into the lanes beside the Mur within a few minutes. Return tickets drop riders back near the Hauptplatz shopping lanes within minutes.

Hauptplatz der Stadt Graz

Hauptplatz is the main square of Graz with the Rathaus and town houses around a central equestrian monument. Markets and civic events have filled the space since the medieval period. Arcades and façades illustrate the city's commercial and political center on the Mur.

Opera Graz

Completed in 1899, Graz Opera was designed by Ferdinand Fellner and Hermann Helmer as a richly decorated theater for opera, ballet, musicals and concerts. A Second World War bomb damaged the building and later restoration and modernization reshaped parts of the interior. The auditorium, foyers and resident ensembles continue the city's formal performance tradition.

05 AdmissionFree

Dreifaltigkeits-Pestsäule

The Trinity Plague Column on Graz's Hauptplatz was raised in the late seventeenth century after plague and fire. Baroque saints and the Trinity group rise on a sculpted column above the market square paving. Town hall and arcaded houses frame the monument that still anchors the plaza's center. Market stalls sometimes ring the column on busy trading mornings.

Mausoleum Kaiser Ferdinands II.

The mausoleum of Emperor Ferdinand II adjoins the cathedral and was built in the 17th century as a dynastic burial church. Its dome and interior decoration reflect early Baroque court patronage in Graz. The building forms part of the ecclesiastical ensemble near the Landeszeughaus.

Grazer Landhaus

Domenico dell'Allio designed the Grazer Landhaus in the sixteenth century for the Styrian estates. Three levels of Renaissance arcades surround a quiet courtyard behind the Herrengasse facade, while ceremonial rooms and an armory connection reflect provincial government. The building still houses the Styrian parliament, linking its historic architecture with continuing civic use.

47.0701° N / 15.4394° E

08 AdmissionFree

Stadtpfarrkirche zum Heiligen Blut

The City Parish Church of the Holy Blood stands on Graz's Hauptplatz with a Gothic core and later Baroque fittings. Its name refers to a relic once venerated here, and stained glass and side altars fill the interior. Market stalls outside contrast with the quieter nave just beyond the portal. Side altars and choir stalls keep a parish scale despite the plaza setting.
