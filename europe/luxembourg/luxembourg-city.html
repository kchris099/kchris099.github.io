A Brief History

Luxembourg City grew from a modest Roman watchtower on the Bock promontory, first documented when Count Siegfried acquired the site in 963 and built a castle that became the seed of a formidable fortress. Over centuries the Spanish, Austrian, and French each reinforced its defences, carving kilometres of casemates into the sandstone cliffs and earning it the nickname 'Gibraltar of the North.' The 1867 Treaty of London ordered the fortress dismantled, but the underground galleries survived. The Grand Duchy gained full independence in 1890 under the House of Nassau-Weilburg, and the city evolved from a garrison town into a cosmopolitan financial and European institutional centre, all while preserving its layered medieval and 19th-century streetscapes above the Alzette and Pétrusse valleys.

Casemates du Bock

Casemates du Bock is a network of underground tunnels and galleries initially constructed in 1644 as part of Luxembourg's legendary fortress defences. During both World Wars the tunnels sheltered thousands of civilians. Now a UNESCO World Heritage site, the casemates reveal atmospheric passages carved through sandstone, multi-level galleries, and steep rock stairways that open onto dramatic views of the Alzette valley below.

€15

Palais Grand-Ducal

The Palais Grand-Ducal stands in Luxembourg City's old town as the official residence of the Grand Duke. Built in 1572 as the city hall, this Flemish Renaissance palace acquired its present role in 1890 when the House of Nassau-Weilburg ascended to the grand-ducal throne. Its ornate façade, wrought-iron balconies, and sentry boxes make it the ceremonial centrepiece of the monarchy. Guided tours run during the summer parliamentary recess when the Grand Duke is in residence elsewhere.

Free

Monument of Remembrance

The Monument of Remembrance, locally called Gëlle Fra (Golden Lady), is a 21-metre granite obelisk on Constitution Square. Erected in 1923, it honours the Luxembourgish volunteers who served and died in the First World War. At its summit stands a gilded bronze Nike, the Greek goddess of victory, holding a laurel wreath aloft. The monument was dismantled by the Nazi occupation in 1940 and painstakingly restored after the war.

Free

Place Guillaume II

Place Guillaume II, known locally as Knuedler, is the civic Luxembourg City. The 13th-century square hosts weekly farmers' markets, open-air concerts, and the annual Christmas market. An equestrian bronze statue of Grand Duke William II, who ruled both Luxembourg and the Netherlands in the 1840s, dominates its centre. The neo-classical Hôtel de Ville (city hall) and the lion fountain stand at its edges.

Free

Cathedral Notre-Dame of Luxembourg

Cathedral Notre-Dame of Luxembourg is a religious building in Luxembourg, Luxembourg. Construction and early use date to around 1613. Cathédrale Notre-Dame is a Catholic cathedral on Rue Notre-Dame, built between 1613 and 1621 by the Jesuit order. It was elevated to cathedral in 1870 when the Diocese of Luxembourg was refounded. The exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside service times.

Free

Pont Adolphe

Pont Adolphe is a stone-arch road bridge in Luxembourg City, built 1900 to 1903 and named for Grand Duke Adolphe. Designer Paul Séjourné engineered it as a 153-metre twin arch over the Pétrusse valley, with the deck rising 42 metres above the river. The structure links the Ville Haute quarter to the Gare district and was for decades the country's largest stone bridge. After years of increasing traffic it was refurbished from 2016 to 2018 with extra lanes and a glass walkway.

Free

Philharmonie Luxembourg, also known as The Grande-Duchesse Josephine-Charlotte Concert Hall, is a visitor site in Luxembourg, Luxembourg. Construction and early use date to around 1997. The Philharmonie Luxembourg, also known as The Grande-Duchesse Josephine-Charlotte Concert Hall, is a known concert venue situated in the Kirchberg Plateau. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

La Passerelle

La Passerelle, also known as the Luxembourg Viaduct or Old Bridge, is a 19th-century stone viaduct that spans the Petrusse Valley in Luxembourg City. Built between 1859 and 1861 by the Waring Brothers, this historic bridge connects the districts of Gare and Ville Haute, offering visitors panoramic views of the city and its surroundings.

Parc Merveilleux

Parc Merveilleux is a visitor site in Luxembourg, Luxembourg. It opened or was completed around 1957. Parc Merveilleux is a family-oriented animal and amusement park in Bettembourg, south of Luxembourg City. Opened in 1957 as a private zoo and amusement park, it has an enclosed zoo, tropical aviary, walk-through terrarium, and outdoor rides including a small-train tour. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.

Children's playground - Pirate Ship is a visitor site in Luxembourg, Luxembourg. Located Luxembourg City, the Pirate Ship Playground is a play area inspired by the region's historical sieges and fortifications. Children can enable their creativity as they navigate through this expansive wooden pirate ship, engaging in activities like climbing, sliding, and steering. The grounds and principal structures are the main points of interest.
