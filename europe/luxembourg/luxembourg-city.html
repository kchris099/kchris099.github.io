Luxembourg

Luxembourg City grew from a modest Roman watchtower on the Bock promontory, first documented when Count Siegfried acquired the site in 963 and built a castle that became the seed of a formidable fortress. Over centuries the Spanish, Austrian, and French each reinforced its defences, carving kilometres of casemates into the sandstone cliffs and earning it the nickname 'Gibraltar of the North.' The 1867 Treaty of London ordered the fortress dismantled, but the underground galleries survived. The Grand Duchy gained full independence in 1890 under the House of Nassau-Weilburg, and the city evolved from a garrison town into a cosmopolitan financial and European institutional centre, all while preserving its layered medieval and 19th-century streetscapes above the Alzette and Pétrusse valleys.

Casemates du Bock

Tunnels, gun positions, stairs, wells, and openings overlook the Alzette valley beneath the former fortress. Spanish engineers began cutting the Bock Casemates into Luxembourg's sandstone in 1644, and later occupiers expanded the underground defense network, and parts sheltered civilians during twentieth-century wars; today designated sections open, with steep passages and closures governed by safety and conservation.

Palais Grand-Ducal

The Palais Grand-Ducal stands in Luxembourg City's old town as the official residence of the Grand Duke. Built in 1572 as the city hall, this Flemish Renaissance palace acquired its present role in 1890 when the House of Nassau-Weilburg ascended to the grand-ducal throne. Its ornate façade, wrought-iron balconies, and sentry boxes make it the ceremonial centrepiece of the monarchy. Guided tours run during the summer parliamentary recess when the Grand Duke is in residence elsewhere.

Monument of Remembrance

A gilded female figure holding a wreath crowns the Monument of Remembrance on Constitution Square. Luxembourg erected the memorial in 1923 for citizens who volunteered with Allied forces during the First World War. Nazi occupiers dismantled it in 1940, and recovered pieces enabled restoration decades later; additional inscriptions now broaden its commemorative role to later conflicts.

Place Guillaume II

Place Guillaume II, known locally as Knuedler, is the civic Luxembourg City. The 13th-century square hosts weekly farmers' markets, open-air concerts, and the annual Christmas market. An equestrian bronze statue of Grand Duke William II, who ruled both Luxembourg and the Netherlands in the 1840s, dominates its centre. The neo-classical Hôtel de Ville (city hall) and the lion fountain stand at its edges.

Cathedral Notre-Dame of Luxembourg

Jesuits built Cathédrale Notre-Dame on Rue Notre-Dame between 1613 and 1621; it became a cathedral in 1870 when the Diocese of Luxembourg was restored. Stone vaults and side chapels define the Catholic heart of the upper town. Exterior and main interior spaces are usually viewable outside Mass times.

Pont Adolphe

Two great stone arches carry Adolphe Bridge across the Petrusse valley between Ville Haute and the station district. Engineer Paul Sejourne and local engineer Albert Rodange designed the bridge, completed from 1900 to 1903 as a national infrastructure project; later restoration added a suspended route for cyclists and pedestrians beneath the road deck, creating a second perspective on its masonry structure.

Philharmonie Luxembourg

The Philharmonie Luxembourg, formally the Grande-Duchesse Josephine-Charlotte Concert Hall, stands on the Kirchberg Plateau as the country's principal concert venue. Planning advanced in the late 1990s toward a white, colonnaded hall for orchestral and contemporary programs. Kirchberg's EU institutions surround the cultural campus.

La Passerelle

Twenty-four stone arches form La Passerelle, the railway-era viaduct completed in 1861 across Luxembourg's Petrusse valley. The bridge linked the new station district with the fortified upper city after rail service arrived. Road and pedestrian traffic still use the deck, while views from adjacent streets and valley paths show its repeated arches more clearly than the crossing itself.

Parc Merveilleux

Parc Merveilleux in Bettembourg opened in 1957 as a private zoo and amusement park south of Luxembourg City. An enclosed zoo, tropical aviary, walk-through terrarium, and small-train rides share wooded grounds. Generations of local families treat it as a standard weekend outing.

Children's playground - Pirate Ship

The Pirate Ship playground in Luxembourg City is a large wooden play structure themed around the fortresses that once ringed the town. Climbing nets, slides, and decks let children move through a ship-shaped frame. Parents watch from benches at the edge of the public park lawn.
