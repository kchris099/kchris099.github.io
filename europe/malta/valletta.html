Valletta

Valletta was founded in 1566 by the Knights of St. John after the Great Siege of Malta and named for Grand Master Jean Parisot de Valette. The grid plan on the Sciberras peninsula includes fortifications, auberges, and St. John's Co-Cathedral. British rule from 1800 to 1964 added institutions and harbor works. Heavy bombing in 1942 damaged parts of the city. UNESCO inscribed Valletta as a World Heritage site in 1980.

Tritons' Fountain

Three bronze tritons support a broad basin at the entrance to Valletta, where the fountain was inaugurated in 1959; sculptor Vincent Apap and designer Victor Anastasi created the modernist composition for a major traffic and civic space in Floriana. Restoration before Valletta's 2018 cultural year renewed the figures, waterworks, and surrounding pedestrian plaza.

Saluting Battery

The Saluting Battery was built in the sixteenth century by the Order of Saint John on Valletta's fortifications, near ground used by Ottoman guns in the Great Siege. Cannons still fire ceremonial salutes from the lower tier of St. Peter's Bastion. Views open across Grand Harbour toward the Three Cities.

35.8946° N / 14.5126° E

St. George's Square

The Grandmaster's Palace faces St. George's Square at the political center of Valletta. Government buildings and historic facades surround paving used for ceremonies, public events and everyday passage along Republic Street. The square has carried several names and functions across the Knights, British and independent periods, while fountains add a modern public element.
