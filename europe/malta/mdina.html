Explore Mdina

A Brief History

Mdina was Malta's capital from antiquity through the medieval period, situated on a hill in the island's interior. Phoenician and Roman Melite lay beneath its walls. The Knights of St. John used the fortified city as a refuge after the 1565 Great Siege. Narrow lanes and baroque palaces reflect noble residences. After Valletta became the capital in 1571, Mdina remained an administrative and religious center with its cathedral.

Mdina Old City Fortress

Mdina served as Malta's capital from antiquity through the medieval period until the Knights moved government to Valletta. Narrow lanes and limestone palaces sit within fortified walls on a central plateau. Known as the Silent City for its quiet streets, it overlooks fields and villages toward the coast.

Free

Mdina Gate

The main gate into Mdina was rebuilt in Baroque style in 1724 under Grand Master Manoel de Vilhena. A bridge crosses the dry moat to an arched portal flanked by reliefs and trophies. The design reflects the wealth of the Knights of St. John when Mdina remained the island's noble residence.

Free

Observation Deck Mdina

Bastion walls on Mdina's northern edge provide a raised walkway above the surrounding plain. Built for artillery defense, the ramparts now serve as a viewpoint over Mosta dome, village rooftops, and the distant sea. Interpretive panels explain how the fortifications were strengthened under the Knights.
